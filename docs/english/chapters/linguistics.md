### What Is Linguistics?

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.what-is-linguistics` · Difficulty: advanced · Bloom level: understand · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 2h*

**Learning objective.** Characterize linguistics as the scientific, descriptive study of human language, distinguish description from prescription with evidence, and name the field's core levels of analysis and the design features that make human language unique.

A survey introduction to linguistics as the scientific, descriptive study of human language.

Linguistics is the scientific study of human language: it observes what speakers actually do, forms hypotheses about the systems underlying that behaviour, and tests them against data. Its founding stance is the descriptive/prescriptive distinction. School grammar is largely prescriptive — rules about what speakers should say ('don't split infinitives'); linguistics is descriptive — an account of the rules speakers demonstrably follow, including rules no one was ever taught. Every native speaker who says 'the big old iron gate' rather than 'the iron old big gate' is obeying an adjective-ordering rule they could not state; that silent, systematic knowledge is the discipline's object. Descriptivism does not mean 'anything goes': nonstandard varieties are rule-governed systems of their own, and the descriptive claim is that their rules differ, not that rules are absent. The field divides language into levels of analysis, each with its own survey concept in this domain: phonetics (physical sounds, already studied in this curriculum) and phonology (the sound system), morphology (word structure), syntax (sentence structure), semantics (meaning), and pragmatics (meaning in context) — with discourse, social variation, history, acquisition, the mind, and computation as further branches. What makes human language worth a science of its own is captured in its design features, classically catalogued by Charles Hockett: arbitrariness (the sound of 'gate' has no natural connection to gates — signs are conventional), duality of patterning (meaningless sounds combine into meaningful units, which recombine again), productivity (speakers produce and understand sentences never uttered before), and displacement (language can refer to what is absent, past, future, or imaginary). No animal communication system robustly combines all four. The survey's working habit, applied throughout this domain, is the linguist's triple move: collect data, find the pattern, state the rule — then test it against more data.

**Key ideas**

- Linguistics is descriptive science: it states the rules speakers actually follow — including unconscious ones like English adjective ordering — not the rules an authority prefers.
- Descriptivism is not 'anything goes': nonstandard varieties are rule-governed systems whose rules differ from the standard's, and every variety is equally an object of study.
- The field analyzes language at stacked levels — phonetics/phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, pragmatics — each the subject of a later survey concept in this domain.
- Hockett's design features distinguish human language: arbitrariness, duality of patterning, productivity, and displacement jointly separate it from animal signalling.
- The discipline's method is the triple move: collect data, find the pattern, state the rule, then test the rule against new data — linguistics earns the name 'science' by this loop.

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Linguistics is about learning to speak many languages.
  *Correction:* That is polyglotism. A linguist studies how language works — sound systems, structures, meaning, variation — and may analyze a language fluently without speaking it. The field's object is the human language faculty and its products, not personal fluency.
- *Misconception:* Descriptive linguistics claims there are no rules and any way of speaking is correct.
  *Correction:* Descriptivism finds more rules than prescriptivism, not fewer: it documents the intricate systems every variety follows — including nonstandard ones. What it declines to do is rank varieties morally. 'He don't know' breaks standard English's agreement rule while perfectly obeying the agreement rule of the varieties it belongs to.
- *Misconception:* Some languages or dialects are primitive — simpler and less capable than others.
  *Correction:* No natural language is primitive: every documented language has full grammatical structure and unlimited expressive capacity in its speakers' domains of use, and 'simple' features in one area (English's sparse inflection) pair with complexity elsewhere (English's rigid word order and its auxiliary system). The primitive-language claim has no linguistic evidence behind it.
- *Misconception:* Writing is language, and languages without writing lack real grammar.
  *Correction:* Speech (and sign) is primary: children acquire spoken or signed language without instruction, thousands of fully grammatical languages have never been written, and writing is a secondary technology invented late and acquired only by teaching. Linguistics studies the system underlying speech and sign; orthography is one application of it.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Equating linguistics with speaking many languages.
- Reading 'descriptive' as 'no rules exist' instead of 'the real rules are the object of study'.
- Ranking dialects or languages as primitive versus advanced.
- Treating writing as the primary form of language and unwritten languages as grammarless.

**Worked example**

*Setup:* A single ordinary utterance overheard at a village gate — 'Could you unlock the gate?' — used as the domain's running specimen. The task: apply the linguist's triple move to one hidden rule inside it, and preview how each level of analysis will dissect the same utterance in later concepts.

1. Collect data on one puzzle: native speakers say 'the big old iron gate' but reject 'the iron old big gate'; gather three more phrases ('a lovely little wooden box', not 'a wooden little lovely box') to establish the pattern is general, not idiomatic.
2. State the discovered rule: English orders prenominal adjectives roughly opinion > size > age > material — a rule fluent speakers obey reliably yet almost never can state; classify it as descriptive knowledge (what speakers do), not prescriptive doctrine (what they're told).
3. Test the rule against new data: 'an old big house' sounds marked but occurs with contrastive stress — refine the rule to a default ordering overridable under contrast, demonstrating the science loop: rule, counterexample, revision.
4. Preview the levels on the specimen: phonology will ask why the /k/ of 'could' is pronounced differently from the /k/ in 'skate'; morphology will dissect un-lock; syntax will ask how a question is built from 'you could unlock the gate'; semantics will compute the literal meaning; pragmatics will explain why the utterance is a request, not a question about ability.
5. Run the design features on the specimen: arbitrariness (nothing gate-like in the sound of 'gate'); duality (meaningless /g/, /eɪ/, /t/ compose the meaningful 'gate'); productivity (the sentence is understood even if never heard before); displacement (the same sentence can be uttered about a remembered or imagined gate).
6. Close with the stance test: a prescriptive commentator objects that the speaker should have said 'May you...' — evaluate the objection descriptively: 'Could you...?' is the productive, rule-governed English polite request form; the correction describes no fact about English, only a preference.

*Outcome:* The learner executes the collect-pattern-rule-test loop on a genuine hidden rule of English, correctly classifies descriptive versus prescriptive claims, locates all five analysis levels on one specimen utterance, and verifies the four design features against it.

**Real-world intuition**

- Language technology — search, speech recognition, machine translation, and AI assistants — is applied descriptive linguistics: systems must model what speakers actually say, not what style guides prefer.
- Education and literacy policy depend on the descriptive stance: understanding that a child's home variety is rule-governed changes how teachers diagnose 'errors' and teach the standard as an additional register.
- Forensic linguistics, speech therapy, and language documentation (thousands of languages are endangered) all rest on the discipline's core skill of analyzing a variety on its own terms.

### Introduction to Phonology

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.phonology-intro` · Difficulty: advanced · Bloom level: understand · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Distinguish phonology (the abstract sound system) from phonetics (physical sound), analyze the phoneme/allophone relationship using minimal pairs and complementary distribution, and state simple phonotactic rules of English from data.

A survey of phonology as the study of the abstract sound system of a language, distinct from phonetics' study of physical sound.

Phonology studies the sound system of a language: not the physical facts of articulation and acoustics — that is phonetics, already covered in this curriculum — but the abstract organization a language imposes on sound. The pivotal concept is the phoneme: a category of sound that distinguishes meaning in a particular language. English /p/ and /b/ are separate phonemes because 'pin' and 'bin' differ in meaning by that sound alone — such a pair, differing in exactly one sound, is a minimal pair, phonology's basic discovery instrument. Beneath each phoneme sit its allophones: physically different pronunciations that a language treats as the same sound. The /k/ in 'could' is aspirated — released with a puff of air, [kʰ] — while the /k/ in 'skate' is unaspirated [k]; English speakers neither hear nor intend the difference, because the two sounds never distinguish English words and occur in mutually exclusive environments (aspirated syllable-initially before a stressed vowel, unaspirated after /s/). That mutual exclusivity is complementary distribution, the standard evidence that two sounds are allophones of one phoneme. The same physical difference can be phonemic in another language — Hindi and Thai distinguish words by aspiration alone — which is the deepest lesson of the concept: languages carve the same acoustic continuum into different category systems, and 'hearing' is partly a phonological act. Phonology also governs combinations: phonotactics are a language's rules for permissible sound sequences — English allows /st/ to begin a syllable but not /ts/ ('tsunami' gets repaired in casual speech), and loanwords are systematically reshaped to fit native phonotactics. Alternations round out the survey: the English plural is pronounced /s/ after voiceless sounds ('gates'), /z/ after voiced ('wells'), /ɪz/ after sibilants ('latches') — one abstract element with rule-governed surface variants, a pattern morphology will meet again as allomorphy.

**Key ideas**

- Phonology studies the abstract sound system; phonetics studies physical sound — the same difference can be all-important in one language and inaudible-in-practice in another.
- A phoneme is a meaning-distinguishing sound category, discovered by minimal pairs ('pin'/'bin'); allophones are its context-determined pronunciations, diagnosed by complementary distribution.
- English aspiration is the classic case: [kʰ] in 'could' versus [k] in 'skate' are one phoneme /k/ in English but contrast phonemically in Hindi or Thai.
- Phonotactics are the language's sequencing rules — which sound strings are legal syllables — and loanword repair reveals them in action.
- Rule-governed alternations (plural /s/~/z/~/ɪz/) show one abstract element with predictable surface variants: the phonology/morphology interface in miniature.
- Perception is category-shaped: speakers literally hear through their phoneme system, which is why foreign contrasts are hard to perceive.

**Vocabulary**

- **phonology**
- **phoneme**
- **allophone**
- **minimal pair**
- **complementary distribution**
- **aspiration**
- **phonotactics**
- **alternation**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Phonology and phonetics are the same subject — both are just 'the sounds of language'.
  *Correction:* Phonetics measures physical events (articulation, acoustics); phonology analyzes the categories and rules a language builds over them. Aspiration is a phonetic fact in every language that has it; whether it distinguishes words — its phonological status — differs by language. One discipline describes the clay, the other the pottery.
- *Misconception:* If two sounds are physically different, speakers will hear them as different.
  *Correction:* Perception is filtered through the phoneme system: English speakers cannot easily hear the [kʰ]/[k] difference their own mouths produce systematically, while Hindi speakers hear it instantly because it separates words in Hindi. Discriminability tracks phonemic status, not acoustic size.
- *Misconception:* A letter of the alphabet corresponds to a phoneme.
  *Correction:* Spelling and phonology diverge constantly: 'gate' has four letters and three phonemes; the single letter x in 'exit' spells two phonemes /ks/; the phoneme /f/ is spelled f, ph, and gh ('fun', 'photo', 'laugh'). Phonological analysis works on sound categories and must set the orthography aside.
- *Misconception:* Phonotactic gaps are just words that happen not to exist.
  *Correction:* There are two kinds of non-words: accidental gaps ('blick' — legal but unused) and systematic gaps ('bnick' — barred by English phonotactics). Speakers judge the difference instantly, which shows the sequencing rules are real knowledge, not statistics over the dictionary.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Confusing phonetics (physical sound) with phonology (the sound system).
- Reasoning from spelling — counting letters instead of phonemes.
- Calling any two similar sounds allophones without checking distribution.
- Treating unpronounceable non-words and merely unused words as the same kind of gap.

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A phoneme-umbrella diagram: /k/ as the canopy with allophones [kʰ] and [k] beneath, each tagged with its environment — and beside it the Hindi version where the umbrella splits into two phonemes.
- A minimal-pair ladder: word pairs stacked with the single differing sound highlighted, building the phoneme inventory rung by rung.
- A plural-rule flowchart: final sound of the noun → voiceless/voiced/sibilant branches → [s]/[z]/[ɪz], with a novel-word test lane proving productivity.

**Worked example**

*Setup:* The running specimen 'Could you unlock the gate?' submitted to phonological analysis, plus a mini-corpus: the English pairs 'pin/bin', 'could/good'; the pronunciations [kʰʊd] 'could' and [skeɪt] 'skate'; the Hindi contrast /kʰiil/ 'parched grain' versus /kiil/ 'nail'; and the plural forms 'gates' [s], 'wells' [z], 'latches' [ɪz]. The task: run the full phonemic workup.

1. Establish phonemes by minimal pair: 'could'/'good' differ only in /k/ versus /g/ and mean different things — /k/ and /g/ are distinct English phonemes; state the logic explicitly: one sound difference, meaning difference, therefore category difference.
2. Diagnose the allophones: transcribe the specimen's 'could' as [kʰʊd] (aspirated, syllable-initial before a stressed vowel) against 'skate' [skeɪt] (unaspirated after /s/); show the environments never overlap — complementary distribution — and conclude [kʰ] and [k] are allophones of one English phoneme /k/.
3. Flip the analysis cross-linguistically: in Hindi, /kʰiil/ and /kiil/ form a minimal pair, so the same physical difference is phonemic there; state the moral — phonological status is language-relative, and an English learner of Hindi must learn to hear a difference their system files as identity.
4. State a phonotactic rule from the specimen: 'unlock' syllabifies as un.lock because English permits /l/ + vowel but the sequence /nl/ cannot begin a syllable; contrast the legal onset /kl/ ('clock') with barred /nl/, and classify 'blick' versus 'bnick' as accidental versus systematic gap.
5. Analyze the alternation: the plural of 'gate' is [s], of 'well' is [z], of 'latch' is [ɪz] — extract the rule (voiceless → [s], voiced → [z], sibilant → [ɪz]) and note it applies automatically to new words (the plural of the invented 'blick' is [s]), proving the rule is productive knowledge.
6. Close the level: summarize what phonology extracted from the specimen that phonetics alone could not — the categories (/k/ as one phoneme), the rules (aspiration by position, plural by voicing), and the language-relativity of both.

*Outcome:* The learner establishes phonemes by minimal pair, diagnoses allophony by complementary distribution, demonstrates the language-relativity of the same phonetic contrast, states a phonotactic constraint distinguishing systematic from accidental gaps, and extracts the plural alternation rule from data.

**Real-world intuition**

- Second-language pronunciation difficulty is phonology in action: learners struggle precisely with contrasts their native system files as identity (English /l/–/r/ for Japanese speakers), and effective training targets category perception, not just articulation.
- Speech technology — recognition and synthesis — must model phonemes, allophonic rules, and phonotactics; a synthesizer that ignores aspiration or plural alternation sounds immediately non-native.
- Literacy instruction and spelling reform debates depend on the phoneme/letter distinction: English spelling's notorious difficulty is largely a mismatch between a phonological system and a historically frozen orthography.

**Practice progression**

Item types: Minimal-pair identification task (find or verify pairs establishing given phoneme contrasts); Allophone-versus-phoneme adjudication task (given distribution data from English or another language, rule same-phoneme or different-phoneme with the evidence named); Phonotactics and alternation task (classify non-words as accidental versus systematic gaps; predict plural/past-tense variants for novel words). Suggested item count: 6.

Begin with minimal-pair verification in English; progress to distribution-based adjudication including one non-English data set where the English allophone pattern is phonemic; end with combined problems requiring a stated rule (phonotactic or alternation) tested against novel items.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Minimal-pair reasoning task, Distribution-analysis task, Rule-extraction-and-prediction task. Bloom alignment: Understand — learners must comprehend the phoneme/allophone architecture and the evidence types that establish it; the survey level demands secure reasoning with minimal pairs and distributions rather than original phonological theory.

**Teacher notes**

The hand-in-front-of-the-mouth aspiration demonstration ('pin' puffs, 'spin' doesn't) is compulsory — it converts the phoneme/allophone distinction from definition to felt experience in ten seconds. Insist on the two evidence types as a discipline: minimal pair ⇒ different phonemes; complementary distribution ⇒ same phoneme. The Hindi flip is the concept's intellectual payoff; present it as data, not trivia, and let students derive the language-relativity moral themselves. Keep orthography rigorously out of the analysis — letters are this concept's main saboteur.

**Student notes**

Phonetics is the physics; phonology is the filing system. Two tools do almost all the work: if swapping two sounds changes the word ('pin'/'bin'), they're different phonemes; if two sounds never show up in the same position ([kʰ] and [k]), they're costumes of one phoneme. Try the puff test: hand before your mouth, say 'pin' then 'spin' — you make two different p-sounds and have never noticed, because English files them together. Hindi files them apart. That's the whole lesson: languages carve sound differently, and your ears follow your language's carving.

### Introduction to Morphology

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.morphology-intro` · Difficulty: advanced · Bloom level: understand · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Analyze word-internal structure by segmenting words into morphemes, classify morphemes as free or bound and affixes as derivational or inflectional, identify allomorphy, and draw simple hierarchical word structures.

A survey of morphology as the study of word-internal structure and the morpheme as the smallest unit of meaning.

Morphology studies the internal structure of words, and its atom is the morpheme: the smallest unit that carries meaning or grammatical function. 'Unlockable' contains three — un-, lock, -able — and its meaning is composed from them. Morphemes divide into free (standing alone as words: 'lock', 'gate') and bound (occurring only attached: un-, -able, -ed, -s). Affixes — bound morphemes attached to a base — do two very different jobs, and the derivational/inflectional distinction organizes the whole level. Derivational affixes build new words: they typically change meaning or word class ('lock' → 'unlock' reverses the action; 'lock' → 'lockable' makes an adjective), and their output can feed further derivation. Inflectional affixes adapt a word to its grammatical context without creating a new dictionary word: English has exactly eight inflectional suffixes — plural -s, possessive -'s, 3rd-person singular -s, past -ed, past participle -en/-ed, progressive -ing, comparative -er, superlative -est — a strikingly small set (a legacy this domain's historical concept will explain). Inflection is the outermost layer: 'unlockables' must be un-lock-able-s, never *un-lock-s-able, and such ordering facts show that words have hierarchical structure, not bead-string structure. The structure can branch ambiguously: 'unlockable' is either [un-[lockable]] 'not able to be locked' or [[unlock]-able] 'able to be unlocked' — a structural ambiguity resolved by drawing the two trees, syntax's central insight arriving one level early. Morphemes also vary in shape: allomorphy gives one morpheme several context-determined forms — the plural's /s/~/z/~/ɪz/ (met in phonology), the indefinite article's a~an, the negative prefix's in-~im-~ir- ('intolerant', 'impossible', 'irregular'). And beyond affixation, the curriculum's word-formation processes (compounding, conversion, clipping, blending, borrowing — already studied in the vocabulary domain) are here reframed as the morphological toolkit by which the lexicon grows.

**Key ideas**

- The morpheme is the smallest meaningful unit; words are composed of one or more, and composition is largely regular ('un-lock-able').
- Morphemes are free (standalone words) or bound (affixes and other attached elements); affixes attach to a base.
- Derivational affixes create new words and often change class ('lock'→'lockable'); inflectional affixes adapt words to grammar — English has exactly eight inflectional suffixes.
- Inflection is the outer layer (un-lock-able-s, never *un-lock-s-able): affix ordering evidence shows word structure is hierarchical.
- Structural ambiguity exists inside words: 'unlockable' has two meanings from two trees — [un[lockable]] versus [[unlock]able].
- Allomorphy gives one morpheme several conditioned shapes (a~an; in-~im-~ir-; plural /s/~/z/~/ɪz/), the morphological twin of phonology's alternations.

**Vocabulary**

- **morphology**
- **morpheme**
- **free vs bound morpheme**
- **affix, prefix, suffix**
- **derivation vs inflection**
- **root and base**
- **allomorph**
- **suppletion**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Morphemes are the same thing as syllables.
  *Correction:* The units cross-cut: 'crocodile' has three syllables but one morpheme; 'locked' has one syllable but two morphemes (lock + past -ed). Syllables are sound-organization units; morphemes are meaning units — a word's two anatomies rarely align.
- *Misconception:* Every word can be decomposed — if 'unlock' has un-, then 'uncle' must too.
  *Correction:* Segmentation requires that each part carry its meaning across contexts: un- in 'unlock', 'untie', 'undo' contributes reversal systematically; 'uncle' shares a sound sequence, not a morpheme. The test is recombinability with constant meaning, not spelling resemblance.
- *Misconception:* Derivational and inflectional affixes are just two names for suffixes.
  *Correction:* The distinction is functional, not positional: derivation makes new lexemes (often changing word class, sometimes by prefix: 'unlock'); inflection adapts one lexeme to its sentence (never changing class, always suffixal in English, and always outermost). 'Runner' and 'runs' both add a suffix to 'run', but only the first creates a new dictionary word.
- *Misconception:* Irregular forms like 'went' or 'children' have no morphological analysis.
  *Correction:* Irregulars realize the same grammatical morphemes by unusual means: 'went' is go + past just as 'locked' is lock + past — suppletion and irregular plurals are alternative spell-outs, not exceptions to the system. The proof is acquisition: children briefly regularize ('goed'), showing they command the abstract morpheme before mastering its irregular shape.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Cutting words at syllable boundaries instead of morpheme boundaries.
- Segmenting look-alikes ('uncle', 'under') that fail the constant-meaning test.
- Classifying affixes by position instead of function, and missing that inflection never changes word class.
- Treating irregulars as analysis-free exceptions rather than alternative realizations of the same morphemes.

**Visual teaching opportunities**

### Introduction to Syntactic Theory

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.syntax-theory-intro` · Difficulty: advanced · Bloom level: understand · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Analyze sentence structure as hierarchical constituency rather than word sequence, apply constituency tests, draw simple phrase-structure trees including both readings of a structurally ambiguous sentence, and explain recursion and the grammaticality/meaningfulness distinction.

A survey of syntax as the formal study of sentence structure, distinct from prescriptive school grammar.

Syntax is the formal study of sentence structure: how words combine into phrases and phrases into sentences. Its founding discovery is that sentences are not word-strings but hierarchies. In the specimen 'Could you unlock the old gate?', the words 'the old gate' form a unit — a constituent — that behaves as a block: it can be replaced by one word ('Could you unlock it?'), moved ('The old gate, you could unlock'), and questioned ('What could you unlock?'). These substitution and movement operations are constituency tests, syntax's experimental instruments; strings that fail them ('unlock the') are not units no matter how adjacent they are. Constituents are organized into phrases built around heads — a noun phrase around a noun, a verb phrase around a verb — and drawn as trees whose branches encode the grouping. Trees earn their keep on structural ambiguity: 'She watched the man with the telescope' has two meanings from one word-string because 'with the telescope' can attach to the verb phrase (she used the telescope) or inside the noun phrase (the man has it) — the ambiguity is geometric, not lexical, and drawing both trees is the analysis. Two further results define the modern field, both associated with Noam Chomsky's mid-twentieth-century reframing of syntax as a science of speakers' internal knowledge. Recursion: phrase types can contain their own type ('the gate at the well behind the house of...'), so the grammar's finite rules generate an unbounded set of sentences — productivity, mechanized. And the grammaticality/meaningfulness split: 'Colorless green ideas sleep furiously' is nonsense but perfectly grammatical English, while 'Furiously sleep ideas green colorless' is word salad — speakers judge the difference instantly, showing syntactic knowledge is a system of its own, not a byproduct of meaning. Throughout, the descriptive stance holds: syntactic theory models the grammar speakers demonstrably possess — their ability to produce, parse, and judge — not the school grammar they were taught.

**Key ideas**

- Sentences are hierarchies of constituents, not word-strings; adjacency is not structure.
- Constituency tests — substitution by a pro-form, movement, question-answer fragments — are the experimental evidence for units.
- Phrases are built around heads (noun phrase, verb phrase, prepositional phrase) and drawn as trees encoding the grouping.
- Structural ambiguity is geometric: one string, two trees, two meanings ('the man with the telescope') — attachment decides.
- Recursion lets rules apply inside their own output, generating unbounded sentences from finite means — the engine of productivity.
- Grammaticality is independent of meaningfulness ('Colorless green ideas sleep furiously'): speakers' instant judgments are data about an internal system, the object syntax models.

**Vocabulary**

- **syntax**
- **constituent**
- **constituency test**
- **phrase and head**
- **tree diagram**
- **structural ambiguity**
- **attachment**
- **recursion**
- **grammaticality**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Syntax is the list of grammar rules taught at school — the linguist's job is enforcing them.
  *Correction:* School grammar is largely prescriptive and partial; syntactic theory describes the system speakers already command — the one that lets them build and judge sentences they've never heard. School rules like 'don't end with a preposition' aren't part of that system, as speakers' own productions show.
- *Misconception:* A sentence's structure is just its word order — adjacent words go together.
  *Correction:* Adjacency misleads: in 'unlock the old gate', the words 'unlock the' are adjacent but form no unit, while 'the old gate' does — as substitution ('unlock it') and movement prove. Structure is grouping, discovered by tests, and only trees, not strings, represent it.
- *Misconception:* Ambiguous sentences are ambiguous because some word in them has two meanings.
  *Correction:* That is lexical ambiguity ('bank'). Structural ambiguity needs no ambiguous word: every word in 'She watched the man with the telescope' is univocal, yet two attachments yield two meanings. The diagnosis is two trees, and paraphrase pairs — not dictionaries — reveal it.
- *Misconception:* If a sentence is meaningless, it is ungrammatical.
  *Correction:* The two dimensions are independent: 'Colorless green ideas sleep furiously' is semantically absurd yet syntactically perfect (compare its scrambled version, which is neither). Grammaticality is well-formedness by the syntactic system; meaningfulness is a separate, semantic verdict — and speakers deliver the two judgments separately.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Judging constituency by adjacency instead of by tests.
- Running only positive tests and never checking that non-units fail.
- Diagnosing structural ambiguity as a two-meaning word problem.
- Collapsing grammaticality into meaningfulness and calling absurd-but-well-formed sentences ungrammatical.

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Two sentences: the running specimen 'Could you unlock the old gate?' and the ambiguity classic 'She watched the man with the telescope.' The task: a full constituency workup of the first and a two-tree analysis of the second, ending with recursion and the grammaticality split demonstrated on specimen-derived material.

### Introduction to Semantics

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.semantics-intro` · Difficulty: advanced · Bloom level: understand · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Analyze linguistic meaning at word and sentence level: distinguish sense from reference, apply sense relations and semantic feature analysis, use entailment and truth conditions as sentence-meaning instruments, and demonstrate compositionality over syntactic structure.

A survey of semantics as the study of linguistic meaning at the word and sentence level.

Semantics studies literal linguistic meaning — the meaning a language assigns to its words and sentences by convention, before context does its work (that remainder is pragmatics, the next concept). At word level, the founding distinction is sense versus reference: an expression's reference is what it picks out in the world; its sense is the conceptual route by which it does so. 'The village's last working well' and 'the well behind Amara's gate' may refer to the same object while differing in sense — which is why 'The last working well is the well behind the gate' is informative rather than trivial. Word meanings relate systematically: synonymy (near-sameness of sense: 'fasten'/'secure'), antonymy in kinds — gradable opposites with a scale between ('deep'/'shallow'), complementary opposites that exhaust the options ('locked'/'unlocked'), and converses that describe one relation from two ends ('lend'/'borrow') — and hyponymy, the subtype relation ('padlock' is a hyponym of 'lock') that organizes the lexicon into taxonomies and licenses inference. Classical semantic analysis decomposes senses into features ('mare' = HORSE + FEMALE), which illuminates relations and contradictions but meets its limits at fuzzy categories; prototype theory (associated with Eleanor Rosch's experiments) answers with graded membership — a sparrow is a better bird than a penguin — capturing how real categories radiate from central cases. At sentence level, the instruments are truth-conditional: to know a sentence's meaning is, at minimum, to know the conditions under which it would be true. Entailment is the workhorse relation: A entails B when B must be true whenever A is ('Amara broke the lock' entails 'the lock broke') — distinguished from presupposition, the background a sentence takes for granted ('Amara stopped locking the gate' presupposes she used to), which characteristically survives negation. Governing everything is the principle of compositionality, associated with Gottlob Frege: the meaning of a complex expression is determined by the meanings of its parts and their syntactic arrangement — which is why the two trees of 'She watched the man with the telescope' deliver two meanings, and why semantics reads its instructions off syntax's structures. Idioms ('kick the bucket'), whose meanings are not composed from their parts, mark the principle's stored exceptions — the vocabulary domain's idiom inventory reframed as semantics' boundary cases.

**Key ideas**

- Sense versus reference: two expressions can pick out the same thing by different conceptual routes — which is why identity statements can be informative.
- Sense relations structure the lexicon: synonymy, three kinds of antonymy (gradable, complementary, converse), and hyponymy's subtype taxonomies that license inference.
- Feature decomposition explains relations and contradictions but fails at fuzzy boundaries; prototype theory adds graded, centre-outward category structure.
- Sentence meaning is truth-conditional at core: knowing meaning includes knowing what the world must be like for the sentence to be true.
- Entailment (guaranteed consequence) versus presupposition (backgrounded assumption that survives negation) are the two load-bearing sentence relations.
- Compositionality (Frege's principle): complex meaning is computed from part-meanings plus syntactic structure — idioms are the stored exceptions that prove the rule.

**Vocabulary**

- **semantics**
- **sense vs reference**
- **synonymy**
- **antonymy (gradable, complementary, converse)**
- **hyponymy**
- **prototype**
- **truth conditions**
- **entailment**
- **presupposition**
- **compositionality**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* A word's meaning is the thing it refers to.
  *Correction:* Reference alone cannot be the whole story: 'the last working well' and 'the well behind the gate' can share a referent while differing in meaning, and words like 'unicorn' or 'nobody' mean something while referring to nothing actual. Sense — the conceptual route — is the further ingredient.
- *Misconception:* Opposites are all one relation — every word has 'an opposite'.
  *Correction:* Antonymy divides into kinds with different logic: gradables ('deep'/'shallow') admit middles and comparison; complementaries ('locked'/'unlocked') exhaust the space — not-locked means unlocked; converses ('lend'/'borrow') describe one event from two perspectives. Inference patterns differ by kind, so lumping them breeds errors.
- *Misconception:* If a sentence is true, everything it suggests is entailed.
  *Correction:* Entailment is only what cannot be false if the sentence is true. 'Amara broke the lock' entails the lock broke; it merely suggests (implicates) she did so deliberately — deniable without contradiction ('...by accident'). And 'Amara stopped locking the gate' presupposes prior locking — a background that survives negation, unlike entailment. Three relations, three behaviours.
- *Misconception:* Compositionality means sentence meaning is the sum of the word meanings.
  *Correction:* The principle includes structure: 'the dog bit the stranger' and 'the stranger bit the dog' share every word and differ in meaning because arrangement matters. Meaning is computed over the syntactic tree, not over the word bag — and idioms, which resist computation altogether, must be stored whole.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Equating meaning with reference and stalling on empty or co-referring expressions.
- Treating all opposites as one relation and misfiring inferences accordingly.
- Promoting suggestions to entailments — reading 'up to 50%' as a guarantee.
- Forgetting structure in composition and computing meaning from the word bag.

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A three-test bench for sentence relations: entailment (must-be-true check), presupposition (negation survival), implicature (cancellation) — one example processed through all three lanes side by side.

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Specimen material from the running gate narrative: the expressions 'the village's last working well' and 'the well behind Amara's gate' (same referent); the sentence pair 'Amara broke the lock' / 'Amara stopped locking the gate'; and the word set 'lock, padlock, fasten, secure, locked/unlocked, deep/shallow, lend/borrow'. The task: a full word-level and sentence-level semantic workup.

1. Run sense versus reference: confirm the two well-descriptions co-refer in the story world, then show 'The last working well is the well behind Amara's gate' is informative — a hearer can learn from it — which sense-difference explains and pure reference cannot.
2. Close with compositionality over structure: recall the telescope sentence's two trees from syntax and compute the two meanings by attaching 'with the telescope' as instrument versus modifier; then contrast the idiom 'under lock and key', whose meaning composition cannot deliver — stored whole, the principle's boundary marked.

### Introduction to Pragmatics

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.pragmatics-intro` · Difficulty: advanced · Bloom level: understand · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Explain how context converts literal meaning into speaker meaning: analyze deixis, apply Grice's cooperative principle and maxims to derive conversational implicatures, classify speech acts including indirect ones, and connect politeness phenomena to indirectness.

A survey of pragmatics as the study of how context shapes meaning in language use.

Pragmatics studies meaning in use: the systematic ways context turns what a sentence literally means (semantics' territory) into what a speaker means by uttering it here, now, to this hearer. The running specimen makes the gap vivid: 'Could you unlock the gate?' is literally a question about ability, yet every competent speaker hears a request — and answering 'Yes' without moving is a joke precisely because it honours the semantics while defying the pragmatics. Four instruments organize the survey. Deixis: expressions like 'I', 'you', 'here', 'now', 'this gate' have meanings that only context can fill in — the same sentence uttered by different speakers at different gates picks out different things, which is why a note reading 'Meet me here tomorrow' found on the ground is uninterpretable. Implicature: H. P. Grice's foundational account holds that conversation runs on a cooperative principle elaborated by maxims — of quantity (be as informative as required), quality (say what you believe true), relation (be relevant), and manner (be clear) — and that hearers derive extra meaning by assuming the speaker is cooperating. If Tesfay asks 'Is the well open?' and Amara answers 'The key is in my kitchen', the reply is literally about a key's location; its relevance-driven implicature is 'no, but access is obtainable'. Implicatures are diagnostically cancellable ('...but the gate's actually unlocked') without contradiction — the test that separates them from entailments. Speech acts: following J. L. Austin's discovery that utterances do things — promise, warn, apologize, name — and John Searle's systematization, utterances carry force as well as content; 'I'll bring the key at dawn' performs a promise, and saying it is the doing. Indirect speech acts perform one act via another ('Could you...?' — question in form, request in force), and their ubiquity connects to the fourth instrument: politeness. Indirectness gives hearers room to refuse gracefully, and politeness theory (Brown and Levinson's face framework) treats requests as threats to the hearer's autonomy that speakers routinely soften — explaining why 'Could you possibly...' feels different from 'Unlock the gate', and why the calibration differs across cultures and relationships.

**Key ideas**

- Pragmatics studies speaker meaning: what utterances mean in context, over and above the sentence's literal, conventional meaning.
- Deixis anchors language to the speech situation — person ('I'/'you'), place ('here'/'this'), and time ('now'/'tomorrow') expressions are uninterpretable without contextual coordinates.
- Grice's cooperative principle and maxims (quantity, quality, relation, manner) explain how hearers derive implicatures — extra meanings computed from the assumption of cooperation.
- Implicatures are cancellable without contradiction; entailments are not — the diagnostic separating pragmatic from semantic content.
- Speech acts: utterances have force as well as content (Austin, Searle) — promising, warning, requesting are things done in words, and indirect speech acts perform one act via another.
- Politeness motivates indirectness: softened requests protect the hearer's freedom to refuse (face), and the calibration is socially and culturally variable.

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Pragmatic meaning is vague guesswork — only literal meaning is systematic.
  *Correction:* Implicature derivation is rule-governed and replicable: hearers across a speech community compute the same 'no, but obtainable' from 'The key is in my kitchen', and the maxims predict which inferences arise and when they cancel. Pragmatics is systematic meaning-computation over context, not free association.
- *Misconception:* Indirect forms like 'Could you unlock the gate?' are ambiguous, and hearers must guess between the question and the request.
  *Correction:* Conventionalized indirect speech acts are heard as their force directly — no felt guessing occurs, and answering the literal question alone ('Yes', without acting) registers as deliberate non-cooperation or humour. The form-force gap is real, but its resolution is fast, shared, and systematic.
- *Misconception:* Violating a maxim is simply lying or bad conversation.
  *Correction:* Grice distinguished quiet violation (deception) from flouting — blatant, mutually obvious 'violation' designed to trigger an implicature: replying 'Lovely weather for a harvest' over a dust-dead field flouts quality to convey irony. Flouting is the engine of much implicature, metaphor, and humour — a use of the maxims, not a breakdown.
- *Misconception:* Politeness is decorative etiquette, external to language structure.
  *Correction:* Politeness systematically shapes grammar and form choice: it selects interrogatives over imperatives, past tense over present ('I wanted to ask...'), hedges, and honorifics — in some languages as obligatory morphology. Face management is a load-bearing explanation for why indirect forms exist and when they are chosen.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Treating pragmatic inference as unsystematic guessing instead of maxim-driven computation.
- Confusing implicature with entailment and skipping the cancellability test.
- Conflating flouting (signal) with violating (deception).
- Reading indirect refusals literally ('I'm in the middle of dinner' filed as a dinner report).

**Worked example**

*Setup:* One exchange at the gate, dusk. Tesfay: 'Is the well open?' Amara: 'The key is in my kitchen.' Tesfay: 'Could you get it?' Amara: 'I'm in the middle of dinner.' The task: a complete pragmatic workup — deixis, implicature derivation with maxim analysis, speech-act classification, and politeness accounting.

1. Fix the deictic coordinates: 'my kitchen' (speaker-anchored possessor), 'I' (shifts reference across the two speakers' turns), 'in the middle of dinner' (time anchored to utterance moment); demonstrate the anchor-dependence by transplanting the dialogue to other speakers and watching every deictic re-point.
2. Derive implicature one: Amara's 'The key is in my kitchen' is literally irrelevant to 'Is the well open?'; assuming cooperation (relation maxim), Tesfay computes: the well is locked (else the key is irrelevant), and unlocking is possible (else why mention the key's location) — reconstruct the derivation chain explicitly.
3. Run the cancellability test: extend the reply with '...but the gate's actually unlocked tonight' and observe no contradiction — confirming the 'well is locked' content was implicature, not entailment; contrast with attempting to cancel a true entailment and hitting contradiction.
4. Classify the speech acts: 'Is the well open?' — direct question; 'Could you get it?' — indirect request (interrogative form, directive force); 'I'm in the middle of dinner' — literal assertion functioning as an indirect refusal; note that neither the request nor the refusal is ever named as such by either speaker.
5. Account for the indirection via politeness: the request avoids the bare imperative ('Get it'), preserving Amara's freedom to refuse; her refusal avoids the bare 'No', preserving the relationship — map both onto face protection and observe the exchange completes successfully with zero direct forms.
6. Close with the division of labour: list what semantics delivered (literal contents, the ability-question reading) and what pragmatics added (reference fixing, two implicatures, two indirect forces, the politeness calculus) — the two-level pipeline of meaning made explicit on four short turns.

*Outcome:* The learner resolves deixis by anchoring, derives implicatures via named maxims and verifies them by cancellation, classifies direct and indirect speech acts including an indirect refusal, and explains the systematic role of politeness in the choice of indirect forms.

**Real-world intuition**

- Conversational AI fails most visibly at pragmatics — literal readings of indirect requests, missed implicatures — making this level the active frontier of assistant design and evaluation.
- Cross-cultural professional communication runs on politeness calibration: directness norms differ sharply across cultures, and misread indirectness (an 'I'll try' that meant 'no') is a documented source of business and diplomatic failure.
- Law courts routinely adjudicate the implicature/assertion boundary — what a defendant literally said versus what they meant a hearer to infer — and testimony analysis is applied Gricean reasoning.

Politeness is why: indirectness leaves the other person room.

### Introduction to Discourse Analysis

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.discourse-analysis-intro` · Difficulty: expert · Bloom level: analyze · Mastery threshold: 0.7 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Analyze how meaning and coherence are built across stretches of text and talk: distinguish cohesion devices from underlying coherence, track information structure and reference chains, and apply conversation-analytic tools (turn-taking, adjacency pairs, repair) to real talk.

A survey of how meaning and coherence are constructed across stretches of connected text or talk beyond the single sentence.

Discourse analysis studies language beyond the sentence: how sequences of sentences become texts and how sequences of turns become conversations. Its first distinction is cohesion versus coherence. Cohesion is the visible wiring — the grammatical and lexical devices that tie sentences together, catalogued classically by Halliday and Hasan: reference chains (Amara ... she ... her), ellipsis and substitution ('Tesfay brought a bucket; Amara, two'), conjunction ('however', 'meanwhile'), and lexical cohesion (repetition and related words: well, water, drought). Coherence is the underlying sense-connectedness that makes a stretch interpretable as being about something — and the two dissociate in both directions: a text can be cohesively wired yet incoherent (pronouns and connectives linking non sequiturs), and coherent while barely cohesive ('The gate stood open. Nobody came.' — no tie, full sense, supplied by the reader's inference). Coherence, in other words, is partly built by hearers using the pragmatic machinery of the previous concept. Second, discourse manages information flow: sentences package content as given (recoverable from context) before new, and English uses word order, intonation, and constructions (passives, clefts: 'It was Amara who broke the lock') to control what arrives as topic and what as news — explaining why grammatically perfect sentences can still be 'wrong' in context. Third, spoken interaction has its own architecture, the territory of conversation analysis (founded by Sacks, Schegloff, and Jefferson): turn-taking runs on a precise economy (one speaker at a time, transitions at recognizable completion points, gaps and overlaps meaningful); utterances come in adjacency pairs (question/answer, greeting/greeting, offer/acceptance) whose first parts make seconds expectable — and noticeably absent when withheld; preference organization makes agreements fast and plain while dispreferred responses (refusals, disagreements) arrive delayed, hedged, and accounted for; and repair mechanisms fix trouble in real time, with self-repair systematically preferred over other-correction. The survey's method is empirical throughout: real texts and transcribed talk, examined for the machinery that ordinary fluency conceals.

**Key ideas**

- Discourse analysis studies language above the sentence: texts and conversations as structured objects.
- Cohesion (visible ties: reference chains, ellipsis, conjunction, lexical chains) is distinct from coherence (underlying interpretability) — and the two dissociate in both directions.
- Coherence is partly hearer-built: readers bridge unlinked sentences by pragmatic inference, so texture is a joint achievement.
- Information structure packages content as given-before-new; constructions like passives and clefts exist largely to manage this flow, which is why context can veto grammatical sentences.
- Conversation has precise architecture: turn-taking at completion points, adjacency pairs whose second parts are expectable, and preference organization that marks refusals with delay and hedging.
- Repair — self-initiated where possible — keeps talk on the rails; its systematic patterning is evidence that conversation is an orderly, analyzable machine.

- **discourse**
- **cohesion**
- **coherence**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* A text is just a sequence of grammatical sentences — sentence-level analysis covers everything.
  *Correction:* Sentence-perfect sequences can fail as text: wrong given/new packaging, broken reference chains, and missing relevance links produce grammatical gibberish. Textuality has its own machinery — cohesion, coherence, information flow — invisible at sentence scale.
- *Misconception:* Cohesive devices guarantee coherence: add enough 'however' and 'therefore' and the text hangs together.
  *Correction:* Cohesion without coherence is wiring without current: 'The well is deep. Therefore Tuesdays are long. However, she sneezed.' is richly tied and meaningless. Conversely, sparse texts cohere through inference. The devices signal connections; they cannot manufacture them.
- *Misconception:* Everyday conversation is unstructured — casual talk has no rules to analyze.
  *Correction:* Transcribed conversation reveals split-second precision: speakers project completion points and transition with minimal gap or overlap; withheld answers are heard as meaningful; refusals are systematically delayed and hedged. Conversation analysis exists because casual talk turned out to be among the most tightly ordered behaviour we have.
- *Misconception:* Passives and clefts are just wordy stylistic variants that good writing avoids.
  *Correction:* They are information-structure tools: the passive lets a text keep an established topic in subject position ('The lock was broken that night' in a lock-topic paragraph); the cleft marks focus ('It was Amara who...' answering who-questions). Blanket bans mistake discourse machinery for bad style.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Equating cohesive-device density with textual quality.
- Ignoring given/new packaging and producing grammatical but contextually broken sentences.
- Treating conversational pauses, hedges, and trail-offs as disfluent noise rather than meaningful structure.
- Banning passives and clefts on stylistic principle instead of deploying them for information flow.

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A cohesion-map overlay: the specimen paragraph with reference chains drawn as arcs, lexical chains colour-grouped, and conjunctions flagged — the text's wiring diagram.
- A two-axis grid (cohesion high/low × coherence high/low) with a passage specimen pinned in each quadrant, making the dissociation a visible space.
- A transcript timing strip for the dialogue: turns as blocks on a timeline, the 0.8-second gap drawn to scale, and the dispreferred turn exploded into its delay/repair/account/trail-off anatomy.

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Two specimens from the running narrative: (A) a written paragraph — 'Amara kept the key. She oiled the lock each week, and her neighbour watched from the path. The well stayed shut. However, the rains were coming.' — and (B) a transcribed exchange: Tesfay: 'Any water today?' / (0.8-second pause) / Amara: 'Well — I mean — the level's low, and I've got the field to think of, so...' The task: full discourse workups of text and talk.

1. Map A's cohesion: the reference chain (Amara → she → her), lexical chain (key → lock → well → rains, the water field), and the conjunction 'However'; then interrogate the wiring: what exactly does 'However' contrast? (the shut well versus coming rains — the tie is real but the reader must build the bridge: rain will make the lock pointless).
2. Demonstrate the dissociation both ways: rewrite A cohesively-but-incoherently (keep every tie, scramble the propositions) and coherently-but-tie-free ('The gate stood open. Nobody came.'), verifying that interpretability tracks coherence, not device count.
3. Analyze A's information flow: each sentence opens with given material (Amara/she/the well — established topics) and closes with news; then break it — reorder to 'The lock was oiled each week by her' in a paragraph about Amara and feel the packaging misfire; state the moral: context assigns jobs that grammar alone cannot see.
4. Turn to B and analyze the pause: 0.8 seconds after a yes/no question is a noticeably absent answer — Tesfay can already hear the 'no' coming before a word arrives; name the machinery: adjacency pair (question/answer) plus preference organization (dispreferred second delayed).
5. Dissect the dispreferred turn's anatomy: delay ('Well —'), self-repair ('I mean —'), account (the level, the field), and a trail-off ('so...') that lets the refusal remain unsaid — map each component and note the politeness continuity with the pragmatics concept's indirect refusal.
6. Close by contrasting the two modes: the written text achieves texture through cohesion and packaging; the talk achieves order through timing, pairs, and preference — one discipline, two architectures, both fully systematic.

*Outcome:* The learner maps cohesion devices and separates them from coherence with both dissociation directions demonstrated, analyzes given/new packaging and its context-sensitivity, and performs a conversation-analytic reading of a dispreferred response — pause, delay markers, account, and trail-off.

**Real-world intuition**

- Professional editing and technical writing are applied discourse analysis: paragraph flow problems are almost always given/new packaging and reference-chain failures, and the cohesion map is the diagnostic.
- Medical, legal, and service interaction studies use conversation analysis to improve outcomes — how doctors deliver bad news, how emergency calls are opened, where interpreters intervene — with turn and preference structure as the working variables.
- Chatbot and voice-assistant design confronts turn-taking, repair, and dispreferred responses directly; systems that miss a 0.8-second pause's meaning talk over exactly the moments humans read most precisely.

**Student notes**

Texts hold together at two levels: the visible ties (pronouns pointing back, 'however', repeated key words) and the sense underneath. Ties without sense is still nonsense — and sense can survive without ties, because you build bridges as you read. When you write, watch the packaging: start sentences with what's already on the table, end with the news. When you listen to real talk, listen to the timing: a pause before an answer is an answer, and refusals arrive wrapped — delay, 'well...', a reason, a trail-off. None of that is sloppiness; it's structure.

### Introduction to Sociolinguistics

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.sociolinguistics-intro` · Difficulty: expert · Bloom level: analyze · Mastery threshold: 0.7 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Analyze how language varies systematically with social factors — class, gender, age, identity, and situation — using the sociolinguistic variable as the measuring instrument, and explain register, style-shifting, code-switching, and language attitudes with evidence.

A survey of how language varies and functions in relation to social factors such as class, gender, and identity.

Sociolinguistics studies language in its social life: how variation in speech is structured by who speakers are, whom they address, and what the moment demands. Its founding methodological insight, established by William Labov's pioneering studies (notably the New York City department-store study of postvocalic /r/), is the sociolinguistic variable: a feature with identifiable variants — pronouncing or dropping the /r/ in 'fourth floor', saying '-ing' or '-in'', 'he doesn't' or 'he don't' — whose frequencies pattern systematically by social class, age, gender, formality, and identity. The discovery that variation is structured, not random ('orderly heterogeneity'), created the field: speakers do not have one way of talking but a repertoire, and their choices are socially meaningful. Key patterns organize the survey. Stratification: variant frequencies grade with class and with attention to speech — the same speaker produces more standard variants reading a word list than chatting, which is style-shifting along the formality dimension. Register: language varies by situation and purpose (the courtroom, the playground, the lab report), and command of registers — not abandonment of one for another — is the sociolinguistic skill the curriculum's own 'formal versus informal' instruction has been building. Code-switching: bilingual and bidialectal speakers alternate languages or varieties within and across utterances, and decades of research show the switching is grammatically constrained and socially strategic (marking solidarity, quoting, signalling identity) — competence, not confusion. Attitudes: listeners attach prestige judgments to varieties within seconds, and matched-guise experiments (the same speaker judged differently in different guises) prove the judgments target the variety's social standing, not the speech's intrinsic quality — there is overt prestige (the standard's institutional authority) and covert prestige (the local variety's solidarity value, which keeps nonstandard forms robustly alive). The descriptive stance from the domain's opening here meets its social test: every variety is rule-governed, and the standard is a variety with an army of institutions, not a linguistically superior system.

**Key ideas**

- The sociolinguistic variable — a feature with countable variants (/r/ presence, -ing/-in', negative concord) — turns social variation into measurable science (Labov's founding method).
- Variation is orderly heterogeneity: variant frequencies stratify systematically by class, age, gender, and formality — never randomly.
- Speakers own repertoires, not single styles: style-shifting tracks attention and audience; register tracks situation and purpose.
- Code-switching between languages or varieties is grammatically constrained and socially strategic — a bilingual skill, not a deficiency.
- Language attitudes are judgments of speakers' social standing displaced onto speech; matched-guise experiments expose the displacement.
- Overt prestige sustains the standard; covert prestige sustains local varieties — which is why 'stigmatized' forms persist for centuries.

**Vocabulary**

- **sociolinguistics**
- **sociolinguistic variable**
- **stratification**
- **style-shifting**
- **register**
- **code-switching**
- **matched guise**
- **overt vs covert prestige**
- **repertoire**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Nonstandard speech is careless error — speakers who say 'he don't' just haven't learned the rule.
  *Correction:* Nonstandard varieties follow their own consistent rules ('he don't' is regular agreement in those systems, and multiple negation was standard in earlier English); their speakers typically also command the standard and shift by situation. The variation is systematic choice within a repertoire, not failed standard.
- *Misconception:* Each person has one authentic way of speaking; changing your speech is fakery.
  *Correction:* Repertoire is the human norm: every speaker style-shifts with audience and situation, and always has. The interview voice, the playground voice, and the family voice are all authentic — sociolinguistic competence is precisely the command of the range.
- *Misconception:* Code-switching shows a bilingual can't keep the languages apart.
  *Correction:* Switching obeys grammatical constraints (switches cluster at permissible boundaries), requires proficiency in both varieties, and does identity and interactional work — quotation, emphasis, solidarity. The most fluent bilinguals switch the most skilfully; confusion produces different, distinguishable patterns.
- *Misconception:* The standard language is linguistically superior — clearer, more logical, more grammatical than dialects.
  *Correction:* Standards are varieties that history gave institutions: print, schools, courts. Linguistically they are unexceptional — French's standard lost negative 'ne' informally, English's standard lost 'thou'; 'I didn't see nothing' is as rule-governed as standard French's obligatory double negative. The superiority judgment is social, as matched-guise results demonstrate.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Reading nonstandard forms as errors instead of variants within rule-governed systems.
- Expecting categorical dialect membership and missing that frequencies carry the signal.
- Treating code-switching as confusion rather than constrained, strategic competence.
- Mistaking prestige judgments for linguistic quality judgments.

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A style-shifting line graph: -in' percentage across the three recording settings, one line for Amara, dotted comparison lines for hypothetical class groups — stratification and shifting in one image.
- A two-prestige balance diagram: overt prestige (institutions, standard variants) on one pan, covert prestige (solidarity, local variants) on the other, with situations sliding the fulcrum.
- An annotated code-switching passage: the nephew's turn with switch points flagged at clause boundaries and function labels (solidarity/quotation/precision) in the margin.

**Worked example**

*Setup:* A village fieldwork scenario built on the running narrative: the researcher records Amara in three settings — negotiating with the district water officer (formal), talking with Tesfay at the gate (casual), and reading the tanker notice aloud (maximal attention) — counting two variables: (ing) ('locking' vs 'lockin'') and (h) in 'help'. Supplementary data: the officer judges a recorded 'lockin'' speaker 'less competent'; Tesfay's nephew alternates village dialect and school English across one turn. The task: a full sociolinguistic workup.

1. Define the variables and count: tabulate Amara's -in' rate per setting — highest at the gate, lower with the officer, lowest reading aloud; state the finding as style-shifting along the attention-to-speech continuum, and note the crucial fact: no setting shows 0% or 100% — frequencies, not switches, carry the signal.
2. Interpret the pattern: Amara commands both variants and deploys them by situation — classify this as repertoire and reject the deficit reading (if she 'didn't know' the standard form, the word-list rate could not approach categorical).
3. Analyze the attitude datum: the officer's 'less competent' judgment of a voice saying 'lockin'' — connect to matched-guise logic (same content, different variants, different judgment) and name the mechanism: social standing read off speech features and misfiled as ability.
4. Split prestige two ways: with the officer, standard variants buy overt prestige (institutional credibility); at the gate, they would cost covert prestige (distance, airs) — show that Amara's shifting is rational under both economies simultaneously, which is why 'why don't people just talk properly?' misdescribes the incentive structure.
5. Analyze the nephew's switch: village dialect for the greeting and the joke, school English for the tanker schedule's technical detail — identify the switch points (clause boundaries), the functions (solidarity, then precision-plus-authority), and the verdict: strategic, constrained, bilingual competence.
6. Close with the descriptive-stance payoff: every variety in the scenario did rule-governed work; the variables measured social meaning, not linguistic merit — the domain's opening claim, now carrying data.

*Outcome:* The learner operationalizes sociolinguistic variables, reads frequency stratification as style-shifting within a repertoire, applies matched-guise logic to an attitude judgment, explains behaviour under overt and covert prestige jointly, and analyzes a code-switch for boundaries and functions.

**Real-world intuition**

- Hiring, education, and courtroom outcomes are documentably affected by accent and dialect judgments; matched-guise literacy is bias literacy, with direct fairness applications.
- Marketing, political speechwriting, and public health messaging tune register and variety to audience — style-shifting engineered at scale, for solidarity or authority as the campaign requires.
- Speech technology inherits the attitude problem: recognizers trained on standard varieties fail nonstandard speakers, and voice interfaces encode prestige choices — sociolinguistics names the stakes.

Suggested item count: 6.

Begin with single-variable frequency reading; progress to multi-setting and multi-group tables requiring the repertoire interpretation and prestige analysis; end with integrated scenarios combining variation data, attitude judgments, and switching passages into one evidenced account.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Data-interpretation task, Judgment-analysis task, Annotated-passage task. Bloom alignment: Analyze — learners must decompose variation data into social patterning, attribute attitude effects to their social sources, and dissect switching behaviour for structure and strategy.

Mastery signal: The learner reads frequency (not presence/absence) as the signal, interprets shifting as repertoire rather than inconsistency, explains persistence of stigmatized forms via covert prestige, and analyzes code-switching as constrained, functional competence.

### Introduction to Historical Linguistics

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.historical-linguistics-intro` · Difficulty: expert · Bloom level: analyze · Mastery threshold: 0.7 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Explain how languages change systematically over time in sound, structure, and meaning; apply the regularity of sound change and the comparative method to cognate data; and read English's own history — Old, Middle, and Modern — as a worked case of documented change.

A survey of how languages change over time in sound, structure, and meaning.

Historical linguistics studies language change: the universal, ongoing process by which every living language continually becomes something else. Its founding discovery, the nineteenth century's great result, is that sound change is regular: when a sound changes in a language, it changes across the vocabulary in phonetically defined environments, not word by word at random. The showcase is Grimm's Law, the systematic consonant shift separating the Germanic family (English among them) from the rest of Indo-European: where Latin preserves p, t, k, Germanic shows f, θ (th), h — pater/father, tres/three, cornu/horn — correspondence sets so orderly they can be stated as rules. Regularity powers the comparative method: by assembling cognates (words descended from a common ancestor, identified by systematic sound correspondences, not surface similarity) across related languages, linguists reconstruct unattested proto-languages — most famously Proto-Indo-European, ancestor of English, Latin, Greek, Sanskrit, Russian, and hundreds more (the family the next concept surveys). Change runs through every level. Sound: English's Great Vowel Shift (roughly 1400–1700) systematically raised and diphthongized the long vowels — which is why 'name', 'see', and 'house' are spelled for their medieval pronunciations, and much of English spelling is a fossil record. Structure: Old English was richly inflected with four cases and freer word order; the inflections eroded (sound change grinding down unstressed endings) and Modern English compensated with rigid word order and prepositions — morphology's eight-suffix remnant, explained. Meaning: semantic change follows recurring pathways — broadening ('dog', once a specific breed), narrowing ('meat', once food generally; 'deer', once any animal), amelioration ('knight', once a boy/servant), pejoration ('silly', once blessed/innocent) — and borrowing floods the lexicon at cultural contact (English's triple vocabulary of Anglo-Saxon, Norse, and French/Latin layers, already met in the etymology work this concept builds on). The governing correction to folk belief: change is not decay. Every stage of a language is a complete, fully functional system; today's 'errors' are tomorrow's standard — as English's own documented history proves at every period boundary.

**Key ideas**

- All living languages change continuously at every level — sound, structure, meaning — and change is the normal condition, not corruption.
- Sound change is regular: it applies across the vocabulary in phonetically defined environments, which makes historical linguistics a predictive science.
- Grimm's Law's correspondence sets (pater/father, tres/three) define the Germanic branch and exemplify statable, systematic change.
- The comparative method reconstructs proto-languages from cognate sets identified by systematic correspondence — surface similarity is not evidence; recurring correspondence is.
- English's documented arc is the worked case: the Great Vowel Shift explains the spelling-sound mismatch; inflectional erosion explains the shift to fixed word order.
- Semantic change follows recurrent pathways — broadening, narrowing, amelioration, pejoration — and borrowing layers the lexicon at every cultural contact.

**Vocabulary**

- **historical linguistics**
- **sound change**
- **Grimm's Law**
- **cognate**
- **comparative method**
- **proto-language**
- **Great Vowel Shift**
- **broadening / narrowing**
- **amelioration / pejoration**
- **borrowing**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Language change is decay — English is deteriorating from a better past state.
  *Correction:* Every attested stage is a complete, expressive system, and today's standard is yesterday's condemned innovation ('you' for singular, 'aren't I', dropped 'thou' — each once an error). Change redistributes a language's machinery; it does not degrade it, and no linguistic measure of 'better' distinguishes stages.
- *Misconception:* Words that sound alike across languages are related.
  *Correction:* Chance resemblance is common (English 'much' and Spanish 'mucho' are unrelated) and borrowing mimics kinship. Cognacy is established only by systematic correspondence across many words — Latin p : English f recurring in pater/father, pes/foot, piscis/fish. The method demands sets, not lookalikes.
- *Misconception:* English spelling is irrational — the mismatches are random chaos.
  *Correction:* The spelling is largely a snapshot of late-medieval pronunciation overtaken by regular change: the Great Vowel Shift moved the vowels while print froze the letters, and silent letters ('knight', 'gnaw') were once pronounced. The system is historical, not random — the mismatches are datable fossils.
- *Misconception:* Old English is just modern English with old-fashioned flavour — Shakespeare, roughly.
  *Correction:* Shakespeare writes Early Modern English, mostly readable today. Old English (Beowulf's language) is a different grammatical system — four cases, grammatical gender, free word order — that requires study like a foreign language. The Middle English between (Chaucer) is the transitional stage. Three periods, three grammars.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Declaring words related from surface similarity without correspondence sets.
- Treating English spelling as random rather than historically explicable.
- Confusing Shakespeare's Early Modern English with Old English.
- Framing ongoing changes as decay instead of reorganization.

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A correspondence matrix: Latin and English cognates in rows, the p→f / t→θ / k→h rules extracted in a highlighted rule column — Grimm's Law as a completed worksheet.
- A Great Vowel Shift wheel: the long vowels arranged by mouth height with arrows showing the raising/diphthongization circuit, and 'name/see/house' pinned at their before/after stations.
- A word-history timeline card for 'gate': Old English form, Norse contact note, Middle English spelling, the frozen orthography, and today's pronunciation — one word carrying the whole method.

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Three data sets: (1) cognate table — Latin pater, tres, cornu, piscis versus English father, three, horn, fish; (2) the running specimen's key words traced — 'gate' (Old English gæt/geat, with a Norse-influenced hard-g doublet), 'lock' (OE loc), 'water' (OE wæter, cognate with German Wasser, Latin unda-side kin set aside); (3) a semantic-change set — 'meat', 'deer', 'silly', 'knight'. The task: run the historical toolkit end to end.

1. Extract the correspondences from set (1): Latin p : English f (pater/father, piscis/fish), t : θ (tres/three), k : h (cornu/horn) — state them as recurring rules, name the pattern (Grimm's Law), and draw the conclusion: English and Latin descend from a common ancestor with English having undergone the shift.
2. Show why the method needs sets: test the lookalike trap with a chance resemblance and with a loan ('very' from French vrai-family) — neither shows recurring correspondence across native vocabulary; only the systematic sets certify kinship.
3. Trace the specimen words in (2): 'lock' and 'gate' from Old English with continuous form and sense; use 'water'/Wasser to add a second branch (West Germanic) inside the family tree — the learner's first hand-drawn piece of the genealogy the next concept completes.
4. Explain a spelling fossil with the Great Vowel Shift: 'gate' spells a vowel that medieval speakers pronounced roughly as written; the shift raised/diphthongized it while the spelling froze — generalize to 'name', 'see', 'house', and reclassify English orthography as a historical record.
5. Classify the semantic changes in (3): 'meat' food→flesh (narrowing), 'deer' animal→cervid (narrowing), 'silly' blessed→foolish (pejoration), 'knight' servant-boy→noble warrior (amelioration); note each pathway is recurrent cross-linguistically — semantic change has typology, not anarchy.
6. Close with the no-decay argument on English's own arc: Old English's case system did real work; its erosion was compensated by word order and prepositions; nothing expressible then is inexpressible now — change as reorganization, demonstrated across a millennium of documentation.

*Outcome:* The learner states sound correspondences as rules from cognate data, distinguishes cognates from loans and lookalikes by the recurrence criterion, explains English spelling mismatches via the Great Vowel Shift, classifies semantic changes by pathway, and argues the change-is-not-decay position from documented English history.

**Real-world intuition**

- Etymology-informed vocabulary learning (the curriculum's own strategy) rests on this machinery: recognizing Latin/Greek/Germanic layers and regular correspondences multiplies word-learning efficiency.
- Spelling instruction and reform debates need the fossil analysis: knowing why 'knight' looks as it does converts orthographic despair into decodable history.
- Language reconstruction underpins archaeology and population history — Proto-Indo-European vocabulary informs hypotheses about homeland and migration — and the same comparative method authenticates or debunks claimed language relationships in the public sphere.

**Practice progression**

Item types: Correspondence-extraction task (given small cognate tables, state the sound correspondences and identify the odd item — loan or lookalike); Spelling-fossil explanation task (account for given spelling-pronunciation mismatches historically); Semantic-pathway classification task (label changes as broadening, narrowing, amelioration, or pejoration, with the older sense evidenced). Suggested item count: 6.

Begin with two-language correspondence tables containing one planted intruder; progress to fossil explanations and period identification (Old/Middle/Early Modern passages by feature); end with mixed problems integrating correspondence, borrowing layers, and semantic pathways in one word-history dossier.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Correspondence-rule task, Historical-explanation task, Pathway-classification task. Bloom alignment: Analyze — learners must decompose cognate data into systematic correspondences, discriminate inheritance from borrowing and chance, and attribute present-day irregularities to identified historical processes.

Mastery signal: The learner certifies kinship only by recurring correspondence, explains at least the Great Vowel Shift and inflectional erosion as causes of modern English's profile, classifies semantic changes by the four pathways, and rebuts the decay narrative with documented evidence.

**Teacher notes**

Let students extract Grimm's Law themselves from the cognate table before naming it — the correspondence-discovery experience is the concept. Enforce the sets-not-lookalikes rule with planted traps in every exercise; it is the field's core epistemic discipline. The Great Vowel Shift sells best as the answer to a grievance students already hold (English spelling), so frame it as the explanation they were owed. Reserve explicit time for the decay debate: bring one condemned-then-standardized form per century and let the pattern make the argument.

**Student notes**

Languages change the way coastlines change — continuously, and by rules. Three tools to keep: (1) sound change is regular, so relatives show recurring correspondences (Latin p = English f, again and again) — one lookalike proves nothing, a set proves descent; (2) English spelling is a museum: the letters record how words sounded before the Great Vowel Shift moved the vowels; (3) meanings drift along four well-worn paths — wider, narrower, upward, downward ('meat' narrowed, 'silly' fell). And drop the decay story: every 'error' your grandparents' teachers condemned that you now say plainly is how the whole language got here.

### Language Families

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.language-families` · Difficulty: expert · Bloom level: understand · Mastery threshold: 0.7 · Estimated study time: 2h*

**Learning objective.** Explain the genealogical classification of languages into families descended from common ancestors, read and reason over family trees — especially Indo-European and English's Germanic line — and distinguish genealogical relatedness from typological similarity and contact influence.

A survey of the genealogical classification of the world's languages into related families, including the Indo-European family.

Language families are the genealogical order of the world's roughly 7,000 languages: groups descended, by the continuous change surveyed in the previous concept, from a common ancestral language. The evidence is the comparative method's systematic correspondences; the representation is the family tree, with a proto-language at the root, branches for the lineages that diverged as speaker communities separated, and attested languages at the leaves. The showcase family is Indo-European — identified in the foundational observation of Sir William Jones (1786) that Sanskrit, Greek, and Latin shared resemblances 'so strong... that no philologer could examine them all three, without believing them to have sprung from some common source'. Its major branches: Germanic (English, German, Dutch, the Scandinavian languages), Romance/Italic (Latin's descendants: Spanish, French, Portuguese, Italian, Romanian), Slavic (Russian, Polish, Czech...), Indo-Iranian (Hindi-Urdu, Bengali, Persian...), plus Celtic, Hellenic (Greek), Baltic, Armenian, Albanian, and extinct branches like Anatolian and Tocharian. English's pedigree reads: Proto-Indo-European → Germanic (marked by Grimm's Law) → West Germanic → Anglo-Frisian → English; its closest living relative is Frisian, and its heavy French and Latin vocabulary is contact overlay, not ancestry — English remains Germanic in core vocabulary, morphology, and descent even though a majority of dictionary entries are borrowed. That distinction — inheritance versus borrowing versus mere similarity — is the concept's central discipline. Beyond Indo-European, the world's major families include Sino-Tibetan (Mandarin and the Chinese languages, Burmese, Tibetan), Afro-Asiatic (Arabic, Hebrew, Amharic, ancient Egyptian), Niger-Congo (Swahili, Yoruba, Zulu — sub-Saharan Africa's vast family), Austronesian (Malay/Indonesian, Tagalog, Māori, Malagasy — the great seafaring family), Dravidian (Tamil, Telugu — South India, unrelated to Indo-Aryan Hindi), Uralic (Finnish, Hungarian — famously inside Europe but outside Indo-European), and language isolates like Basque, with no demonstrated relatives at all. Two cautions complete the survey: family trees idealize (dialect continua and contact blur clean branching, which wave models supplement), and deep proposals linking established families (Nostratic and kin) remain unproven — the comparative method's power decays with time depth, and the professional stance is disciplined agnosticism beyond it.

**Key ideas**

- A language family is a set of languages descended from a common proto-language, established by systematic correspondences and represented as a tree of branching lineages.
- Indo-European — from Jones's 1786 'common source' observation — spans Germanic, Romance, Slavic, Indo-Iranian, Celtic, Hellenic and more, from Iceland to Bangladesh.
- English's line runs Proto-Indo-European → Germanic → West Germanic → Anglo-Frisian → English: Germanic by descent, with French/Latin vocabulary as contact overlay, and Frisian as closest kin.
- Inheritance, borrowing, and chance similarity are three different relations — only recurring correspondence in core vocabulary certifies descent.
- The world's other giants include Sino-Tibetan, Afro-Asiatic, Niger-Congo, Austronesian, Dravidian, and Uralic; isolates like Basque have no demonstrated relatives.
- Trees idealize (contact and continua blur branches) and the method has a time-depth horizon — proposed super-families beyond it remain unproven.

**Vocabulary**

- **language family**
- **proto-language**
- **family tree / branch**
- **Indo-European**
- **Germanic**
- **cognate vs borrowing**
- **isolate**
- **dialect continuum**
- **time depth**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* English is basically a Romance language — most of its words come from French and Latin.
  *Correction:* Descent is measured by core vocabulary, grammar, and systematic correspondence, not dictionary counts: English's function words, irregular verbs, numerals, and kinship terms are Germanic, and it shows Grimm's Law throughout. The French/Latin mass is borrowed overlay from contact (1066 and scholarship) — a Germanic tree wearing Romance foliage.
- *Misconception:* Languages spoken near each other, or sharing features, belong to the same family.
  *Correction:* Geography and typology both mislead: Hungarian sits surrounded by Indo-European but is Uralic; Tamil shares the subcontinent with Hindi but is Dravidian; Basque neighbours Romance and relates to nothing known. Family membership is established only by correspondence evidence, and unrelated neighbours routinely converge through contact.
- *Misconception:* Some of today's languages are 'older' than others — Greek is ancient, English is young.
  *Correction:* Every living language has an equally long pedigree reaching back through unbroken transmission; what differs is documentation age and how long a name has been attached. Modern Greek is not ancient Greek any more than English is Old English — all today's languages are the current stage of equally old lineages.
- *Misconception:* The family tree is the whole truth — every language has exactly one clean ancestral line.
  *Correction:* Trees idealize a messier history: dialect continua lack sharp branch points, contact produces convergence areas and mixed systems (creoles challenge single-parent ancestry), and borrowing crosses branches freely. The tree captures descent; wave and contact models capture the rest — professionals read them together.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Classifying English as Romance from dictionary counts instead of core vocabulary and correspondences.
- Inferring kinship from geography or surface similarity.
- Ranking living languages as older and younger.
- Taking the tree as literal total history and ignoring contact, continua, and the method's time horizon.

- The Indo-European master tree with English's path highlighted root-to-leaf, major branches labelled with two member languages each, and extinct branches greyed.

- A world families map: the six giant families colour-zoned with Basque, Hungarian, and Tamil pinned as caution flags where the map defies geographic expectation.

**Worked example**

*Setup:* A classification dossier built around the specimen word 'water' and three placement puzzles: (1) the cognate row — English water, German Wasser, Dutch water versus Latin aqua, Spanish agua; (2) a mixed word list for English — 'water, three, mother, gate' versus 'aqua-tic, trio, maternal, portal'; (3) placement candidates: Hungarian (in Europe, unrelated?), Tamil versus Hindi, and Basque. The task: build English's pedigree and adjudicate the puzzles by evidence.

1. Read the cognate row: water/Wasser/water correspond systematically; aqua/agua correspond with each other but not with the Germanic set — draw the two sub-trees (West Germanic; Italic/Romance) and join them only at the Proto-Indo-European root, stating what each junction claims.
2. Place English precisely: Proto-Indo-European → Germanic (cite Grimm's Law from the previous concept) → West Germanic (the water/Wasser sub-tree) → Anglo-Frisian → English; add Frisian as the nearest living branch and defend each node with its evidence type.
3. Adjudicate the mixed list: 'water, three, mother, gate' are inherited (core vocabulary, Germanic correspondences); 'aquatic, trio, maternal, portal' are Latin-side borrowings of the same concepts — conclude that English contains cognate pairs by two routes (inherited 'three' beside borrowed 'trio'), the inheritance/borrowing distinction embodied in single word pairs.
4. Solve placement puzzle one: Hungarian — geographically central-European, but its core vocabulary and morphology correspond with Finnish and Estonian, not its neighbours: Uralic, and the moral that geography is not genealogy.
5. Solve puzzle two: Tamil versus Hindi — same subcontinent, deep mutual borrowing, but Hindi's correspondences run to Sanskrit and thence Indo-European while Tamil's run to Telugu and Malayalam (Dravidian): two families in contact, convergence without kinship; then puzzle three: Basque — no recurring correspondences with anything documented: an isolate, and the honest classification is 'unrelated so far as evidence shows'.
6. Close with the horizon caution: sketch why the method fades with time depth (regular correspondences erode beyond recognition; chance and borrowing noise swamp signal) and file super-family proposals (Nostratic-style) as unproven — the disciplined boundary of a rigorous method, ending the survey where evidence ends.

*Outcome:* The learner constructs English's full genealogical line with evidence at each node, distinguishes inherited from borrowed doublets within English, correctly places Hungarian, Tamil/Hindi, and Basque against the geography and similarity traps, and states the comparative method's time-depth limits.

**Real-world intuition**

- Language learning transfers run along family lines: a Spanish speaker's shortcut into Portuguese, an English speaker's head start in German core vocabulary — and the borrowing overlay explains why French feels deceptively easy for English readers.
- Population history and archaeology triangulate with family trees: Indo-European's branches inform migration hypotheses, and Austronesian's tree tracks the peopling of the Pacific.
- Public claims about language kinship (nationalist etymologies, 'X is the oldest language' assertions) are testable against the correspondence standard — family-tree literacy is a working defence against linguistic mythology.

Item types: Tree-reading and construction task (build or complete family sub-trees from cognate tables; state what each node claims); Relation-classification task (label word pairs and language pairs as inherited/cognate, borrowed, or chance/typological resemblance, with evidence); Placement-puzzle task (assign languages to families from correspondence data against geographic and similarity distractors). Suggested item count: 6.

### Introduction to Language Acquisition

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.language-acquisition-intro` · Difficulty: expert · Bloom level: understand · Mastery threshold: 0.7 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Describe the milestones and mechanisms of first-language acquisition, evaluate the evidence that children build rule systems rather than imitate (overregularization, poverty of the stimulus debates), and contrast first- with second-language acquisition including critical-period evidence and interlanguage.

A survey of how children and learners acquire first and additional languages.

Language acquisition studies how humans come to command a language — the child's feat of building a full grammatical system from ambient speech, without instruction, in a few years. First-language (L1) acquisition follows robust milestones across languages and cultures: cooing (from around 2 months), babbling (repetitive syllables, around 6 months — including in the manual mode for deaf infants exposed to sign), first words (around 12 months), two-word combinations with consistent order ('more water', 'Amara gate', around 18–24 months), and then an explosive elaboration of morphology and syntax through the preschool years. The survey's central finding is that children are rule-builders, not recorders. The showcase evidence is overregularization: children say 'goed', 'breaked', and 'foots' — forms they have never heard — precisely because they have extracted the regular rules (past = -ed, plural = -s) and applied them beyond their limits; the error is the fingerprint of a productive system, and its developmental curve (correct irregular → overregularized → correct again) is the famous U-shape. Jean Berko's wug test made the point experimental: shown a novel creature called a 'wug', preschoolers pluralize 'wugs' — with the right allomorph — proving productive rules over memorized forms. Children also acquire through characteristic stages regardless of correction, and direct correction famously does little (the 'nobody don't like me' anecdote of the child impervious to eight corrections). What explains the feat is the field's great debate: nativist accounts (associated with Chomsky) argue the input underdetermines the grammar attained — the poverty-of-the-stimulus argument — so children must bring language-specific expectations; usage-based accounts (associated with Tomasello) argue powerful statistical learning plus intention-reading over rich input suffices. The survey presents both honestly: the debate is live, and the data (babbling universality, milestone robustness, statistical learning demonstrations in infants) constrain both sides. Second-language (L2) acquisition differs systematically: later starters build an interlanguage — a learner system with its own evolving rules, showing transfer from the L1 and developmental sequences partly shared with L1 learners — and ultimate attainment, especially in phonology, declines with starting age. The critical (or sensitive) period evidence — from cases of late L1 exposure and from age-of-arrival studies of immigrants — indicates a biological window during which acquisition is most complete, closing gradually rather than abruptly; adult learners compensate with explicit strategies the child never needed.

**Key ideas**

- L1 acquisition follows universal milestones — cooing, babbling (vocal or manual), first words (~12 months), two-word grammar (~18–24 months), preschool elaboration — without instruction.
- Children build rules, not recordings: overregularization ('goed', 'foots') produces never-heard forms, and its U-shaped curve is the fingerprint of productive grammar.
- Berko's wug test proves productivity experimentally: children inflect novel words with the correct allomorphs.
- Explicit correction has little effect on the child's developing system — stages unfold on the system's own schedule.
- The mechanism debate is live: poverty-of-the-stimulus nativism (Chomsky) versus statistical, usage-based learning (Tomasello), with evidence constraining both.
- L2 acquisition builds an interlanguage shaped by transfer and shared developmental sequences; critical-period evidence shows completeness — especially phonological — declines with starting age.

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Children learn language by imitating adults and being corrected.
  *Correction:* Imitation cannot produce 'goed' or 'wugs' — forms no adult models — and correction studies show the child's system resists direct repair until its own schedule matures. Children extract and over-apply rules from input; imitation and correction are marginal to the engine.
- *Misconception:* A child's 'goed' and 'foots' are signs of confusion or slow development.
  *Correction:* Overregularization is progress: it appears after correct irregulars, precisely when the child discovers the rule, and resolves as exceptions are relearned atop it (the U-shape). The error announces a productive grammar coming online — its absence, not its presence, would be the worry.
- *Misconception:* Deaf children don't really acquire language unless taught speech.
  *Correction:* Sign languages are complete natural languages, and deaf infants exposed to them hit the same milestones — manual babbling included — on the same schedule. Acquisition is modality-independent; what matters is accessible input during the sensitive years, which is why early sign exposure is the linguistically urgent variable.
- *Misconception:* Adults learn second languages the same way children learn their first, just less willingly.
  *Correction:* The processes differ systematically: adults bring an entrenched L1 (transfer), rely on explicit learning children never use, and face declining ultimate attainment — sharpest in phonology — as starting age rises. Adult advantages exist (faster early vocabulary, metalinguistic insight), but the child's implicit route is not simply available on demand.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Reading overregularization as regression instead of rule discovery.
- Crediting imitation and correction with the acquisition the child's system actually performs.
- Treating sign-exposed deaf infants as languageless rather than on-schedule acquirers in another modality.
- Expecting adult L2 learning to replicate child L1 acquisition and misreading interlanguage as carelessness.

### Introduction to Psycholinguistics

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.psycholinguistics-intro` · Difficulty: research · Bloom level: understand · Mastery threshold: 0.7 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Describe the mental processes underlying language comprehension, production, and storage: incremental parsing and garden-path effects, lexical access and priming in the mental lexicon, the speech-production pipeline evidenced by error patterns, and the experimental methods that reveal each.

A survey of the mental processes underlying language comprehension, production, and storage.

Psycholinguistics studies language as real-time mental activity: what the mind does, in milliseconds, to understand, produce, and store language. Comprehension is incremental: hearers and readers build structure word by word rather than waiting for sentence end — the decisive evidence being garden-path sentences like 'The old man the boats', where the parser commits early to 'old man' as a noun phrase and must reanalyze when 'the boats' arrives (the intended reading: the old [people] man the boats). Reading-time and eye-tracking studies show exactly where processing stalls, and ambiguity resolution draws continuously on frequency, plausibility, and context. Storage is the mental lexicon: tens of thousands of words organized not alphabetically but by connected networks of sound, meaning, and frequency. Its signature phenomena: frequency effects (common words recognized faster), semantic priming (hearing 'water' speeds recognition of 'well' — activation spreads through meaning neighbourhoods, measured in lexical-decision experiments), and tip-of-the-tongue states, where meaning is retrieved while form access fails partially (speakers often know the first sound or syllable count) — evidence that meaning and form are stored and accessed separably. Production runs a pipeline from message to articulation — conceptualization, formulation (selecting words and building structure), articulation — and its architecture shows in speech errors, which are strikingly lawful: exchanges swap units of the same type (sounds with sounds — 'lock the gate' → 'gock the late', a spoonerism; words with words), and morphemes strand ('she unlocked the gates' → 'she ungated the locks' keeps -ed and -s in place), revealing that sounds, stems, and inflections are handled at distinct stages. The neuroscience layer, surveyed briefly: the classical aphasia pattern — Broca's aphasia (effortful, telegraphic production with relatively preserved comprehension) versus Wernicke's aphasia (fluent but often meaning-impaired speech with comprehension deficits) — established that language function is differentiated in the brain, a picture modern imaging refines into networks rather than two centres. Throughout, the survey's emphasis is methodological: reaction times, eye-tracking, priming paradigms, and error corpora are how invisible processes are made measurable — the domain's collect-pattern-rule loop, now run on the mind itself.

**Key ideas**

- Comprehension is incremental: structure is built word by word, and garden-path sentences expose the parser's early commitments and forced reanalysis.
- The mental lexicon is a network organized by sound, meaning, and frequency — evidenced by frequency effects, semantic priming, and tip-of-the-tongue states.
- Tip-of-the-tongue shows meaning and form are separably stored: retrieval can deliver one while the other stalls.
- Production is a staged pipeline (conceptualize → formulate → articulate) whose architecture is visible in lawful speech errors: same-type exchanges and stranded morphology.
- Aphasia patterns (Broca's effortful production versus Wernicke's fluent-but-impaired comprehension) demonstrate differentiated brain organization for language, now understood as networks.
- Method makes the field: reaction times, eye-tracking, priming, and error corpora convert millisecond-scale invisible processing into data.

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* We understand sentences by waiting for the end and then working out the meaning.
  *Correction:* Processing is radically incremental: eye-tracking shows commitments within a couple of hundred milliseconds per word, and garden-path stalls occur mid-sentence exactly where an early analysis fails. There is no end-of-sentence assembly point — comprehension is built, and sometimes rebuilt, on the fly.
- *Misconception:* Speech errors are random noise from sloppy speakers.
  *Correction:* Errors are lawful: sounds exchange with sounds, words with words, almost never across types; inflections strand in place while stems move ('ungated the locks'). The regularities are strong enough to map the production pipeline — errors are the system photographed mid-operation, which is why psycholinguists collect them.
- *Misconception:* Words are stored like a dictionary — an alphabetical list the mind looks up.
  *Correction:* Access patterns refute the list: frequency speeds recognition, semantic neighbours prime each other, and tip-of-the-tongue retrieves meaning with partial form. The lexicon behaves as a spreading-activation network keyed simultaneously by sound, meaning, and use — nothing like alphabetical search.
- *Misconception:* Language lives in one brain spot, and damage there removes it entirely.
  *Correction:* The classical two-area picture itself showed differentiation (production-weighted versus comprehension-weighted impairments), and modern imaging replaces centres with distributed, interacting networks — damage produces selective, patterned deficits, not all-or-nothing loss, and recovery reorganizes across the network.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Assuming end-of-sentence interpretation instead of incremental build-and-revise.
- Dismissing speech errors and slips as unanalyzable noise.
- Picturing the lexicon as an alphabetical dictionary.
- Retaining the strict two-centre brain model without the modern network qualification.

### Introduction to Applied Linguistics

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.applied-linguistics-intro` · Difficulty: expert · Bloom level: apply · Mastery threshold: 0.7 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Apply linguistic findings to practical language problems: evaluate language-teaching methods against acquisition evidence, assess language tests for validity and reliability and their consequences, analyze language-policy choices including medium-of-instruction decisions, and survey the field's further branches.

A survey of practical applications of linguistic theory, including language teaching, testing, and policy.

Applied linguistics puts the discipline's findings to work on real-world language problems — the survey covers its three central arenas and the professional stance that unites them. Language teaching: the field's history is a succession of methods answering to (or ignoring) acquisition evidence — grammar-translation (rules and written translation, strong on analysis, weak on communication), audiolingualism (drill and habit-formation, undermined when acquisition research showed rule-building rather than imitation), and the modern communicative family (communicative language teaching and task-based learning: meaning-focused use with form attended to in context), whose rationale rests directly on the previous concepts' findings — developmental sequences that syllabi cannot reorder, transfer profiles predictable from the L1, the input's central role, and the critical-period gradient that calibrates pronunciation expectations. The applied linguist's craft includes needs analysis (what will this learner actually do with the language?) and error analysis read through interlanguage: errors as windows on the learner's current system, not moral failures. Language testing: tests are instruments with measurable virtues — validity (does it measure what it claims? a grammar quiz is not a speaking test), reliability (are scores stable across occasions and raters?), and washback (tests reshape the teaching that precedes them, for better or worse) — and with consequences that make testing an ethical field: gatekeeping exams (citizenship, university entry, professional licensure) allocate life chances, so bias, accent-penalizing rubrics, and construct-irrelevant difficulty are matters of justice as well as psychometrics. Language policy and planning: societies decide, explicitly or by drift, which varieties are official, taught, and tested — status planning (which language for law, school, media), corpus planning (standardization, orthography design, terminology creation), and acquisition planning (who learns what, and how resources flow). The survey's flagship policy question, continuous with the sociolinguistics concept, is medium of instruction: robust findings support early literacy in a child's home language with well-planned transition, against the intuitive-but-costly straight-for-the-prestige-language default; and the standard-variety question in classrooms — additive bidialectalism (adding the standard as a register while respecting the home variety) versus subtractive correction — applies the descriptive stance where it matters most. Around these arenas the field's further branches radiate: forensic linguistics (authorship, courtroom language), clinical linguistics (assessment and therapy), lexicography, translation and interpreting (surveyed fully in a later concept), and language documentation and revitalization for the world's thousands of endangered languages.

**Key ideas**

- Applied linguistics converts findings into practice across teaching, testing, and policy — with evidence, not tradition, as the design criterion.
- Teaching methods form an evidence-answerable sequence: grammar-translation → audiolingual drill (undermined by rule-building findings) → communicative and task-based approaches grounded in acquisition research.
- Learner errors are interlanguage data: error analysis diagnoses the current system and predicts development, replacing the deficit reading.
- Tests are instruments judged by validity, reliability, and washback — and gatekeeping tests allocate life chances, making bias a justice issue.
- Language policy divides into status, corpus, and acquisition planning; medium-of-instruction evidence favours home-language early literacy with planned transition.
- The field's branches — forensic, clinical, lexicographic, documentation and revitalization — share one method: linguistic analysis in service of a real-world decision.

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Teaching a language just means teaching its grammar rules — applied linguistics is grammar pedagogy.
  *Correction:* Rule presentation is one tool among many, and acquisition research shows developmental sequences that explicit rules cannot reorder. Modern practice is meaning-focused with form attended in context, driven by needs analysis — what the learner must do — not by the grammar book's chapter order.
- *Misconception:* A difficult test is a good test — rigour equals quality.
  *Correction:* Quality is validity (measuring the intended construct), reliability (stable scoring), and healthy washback. Difficulty from construct-irrelevant sources — cultural assumptions, accent-penalizing rubrics, trick formats — makes a test worse and, in gatekeeping contexts, unjust. Hard and good are independent axes.
- *Misconception:* Children learn best if schooling starts immediately and exclusively in the prestige language.
  *Correction:* The evidence runs the other way: initial literacy in the home language builds skills that transfer, while submersion in an unknown language delays literacy and content learning together. Well-designed programs add the target language from a home-language foundation — additive, sequenced, and better on the prestige language's own outcomes.
- *Misconception:* Correcting every error is the teacher's core job — uncorrected errors fossilize immediately.
  *Correction:* Errors are interlanguage stages, many of which resolve on the developmental schedule regardless of correction, and blanket correction consumes classroom time while raising anxiety. Effective feedback is selective — targeted at learnable-now items — and error analysis is diagnosis, not moral accounting.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Equating language teaching with rule presentation and ignoring needs analysis.
- Judging tests by difficulty instead of validity, reliability, and washback.
- Correcting all errors indiscriminately instead of reading interlanguage diagnostically.
- Endorsing prestige-language submersion against the medium-of-instruction evidence.

6. Close with the stance synthesis: every recommendation traced to a finding from earlier in the domain (interlanguage, variation, descriptivism, acquisition sequences) — applied linguistics as the domain's evidence, cashed.

### Dialectology

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.dialectology` · Difficulty: expert · Bloom level: analyze · Mastery threshold: 0.7 · Estimated study time: 2h*

**Learning objective.** Apply dialectology's methods — isoglosses, dialect atlases, and the apparent-time construct — to map regional and social variation; analyze dialect continua, levelling, and new-dialect formation; and evaluate dialect data against the standard-versus-dialect ideology.

The systematic study of regional and social dialects as a specialized branch of sociolinguistics.

Dialectology is the systematic study of dialects — regionally and socially patterned varieties of a language — as a specialization within sociolinguistics. The traditional core is regional: fieldworkers survey how features vary across space — lexical items (the fizzy-drink map: 'soda'/'pop'/'coke'; British 'bread roll'/'bap'/'cob'), pronunciations (rhotic versus non-rhotic /r/; the north/south English split in 'bath' and 'strut' vowels), and grammatical constructions — and plot each feature's boundary as an isogloss. Isoglosses rarely coincide neatly; where several bundle, dialectologists draw major dialect boundaries, and where they fan out, varieties shade into one another as a dialect continuum — the ground truth behind the previous concepts' caution that language/dialect boundaries are conventional: across continua like the West Germanic or Romance areas, neighbouring villages understand each other while the endpoints do not, and where 'a language' stops is a political fact, not a linguistic one (the aphorism that a language is 'a dialect with an army and a navy' summarizes the point). Classical dialect geography — the great atlas projects begun in nineteenth-century Germany and France (Wenker's postal surveys; Gilliéron's trained-fieldworker atlas) — sought NORMs (nonmobile, older, rural males) as the most conservative speakers; modern dialectology corrects the sampling bias, embraces cities (urban dialectology is sociolinguistics' birthplace), and adds perceptual dialectology: mapping where non-linguists believe dialects begin, and how those beliefs load attitudes. Change over time is read through apparent time: comparing age groups at one moment — if the old say one form and the young reliably another, change is likely in progress, a construct validated by real-time restudies. Contemporary dynamics complete the survey: dialect levelling (mobility and contact eroding locally distinctive forms toward regional koinés), new-dialect formation (settlements mixing input dialects into novel stable varieties — New Zealand English is the documented showcase), and the media question, where evidence supports vocabulary spreading through broadcast exposure while accent systems change mainly through face-to-face interaction. Throughout, the field's findings dissolve the standard-versus-dialect hierarchy: the standard is one dialect among many whose social career the sociolinguistics concept already explained — everyone speaks a dialect, and the atlas maps them all with the same pen.

**Key ideas**

- Dialectology maps regionally and socially patterned variation: lexical, phonological, and grammatical features surveyed and plotted as isoglosses.
- Isoglosses bundle into dialect boundaries or fan into continua — where mutual intelligibility grades village by village, and language/dialect lines are political conventions.
- Classical atlases (Wenker, Gilliéron) founded the method but sampled conservatively (NORMs); modern work corrects the bias, goes urban, and adds perceptual dialectology.
- Apparent time reads change from age-grading at one moment: systematic old/young differences signal change in progress, validated by real-time restudies.
- Levelling erodes local forms toward regional koinés under mobility and contact; new-dialect formation (New Zealand English) shows mixture settling into novel stable systems.
- Media spreads vocabulary readily, but accent systems change mainly through face-to-face interaction — and everyone, standard speakers included, speaks a dialect.

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Only other people speak dialects — standard speakers speak 'the language' itself.
  *Correction:* The standard is a dialect with a prestigious history: it has regional roots, distinctive features, and an isogloss geography like any variety. 'Dialect' names any systematically patterned variety, and every speaker of the language speaks at least one — the atlas maps the standard with the same instruments.
- *Misconception:* Dialects have sharp borders — cross a line and the speech changes.
  *Correction:* Individual features have isoglosses, but different features draw different lines; boundaries are bundles at best, and vast areas are continua where each village understands the next while endpoints diverge beyond intelligibility. Sharp dialect borders on maps are cartographic conveniences, not field reality.
- *Misconception:* Television and the internet are making everyone speak the same, so dialects are dying out.
  *Correction:* The evidence is layered: broadcast media spread words and catchphrases effectively, but sound-system change tracks face-to-face networks — and while levelling erodes some local forms, regional koinés emerge, cities generate new varieties, and apparent-time studies keep finding vigorous change in progress. Dialect diversity is reorganizing, not vanishing.
- *Misconception:* The oldest rural speakers preserve the 'pure' dialect that younger and urban speech corrupts.
  *Correction:* Every generation's speech is a system, and the NORM-focused sampling that treated conservative rural speech as the 'real' dialect was method bias, not linguistics: varieties have always changed continuously, cities have always innovated, and the young's forms are tomorrow's dialect exactly as the elders' forms were yesterday's innovations.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Exempting the standard from dialect status.
- Drawing sharp single-line dialect borders that the feature data doesn't support.
- Attributing all change to media and predicting dialect extinction.
- Treating elderly rural speech as the 'pure' dialect and younger urban speech as corruption.

**Worked example**

*Setup:* A fieldwork dossier for the running narrative's district and its neighbouring valley: survey data for three features — the word for the well-bucket ('pail' upslope, 'bucket' downslope), rhoticity (older speakers pronounce final /r/, younger increasingly do not), and a grammatical form ('it wants oiled' in three upslope villages) — plus a perceptual sketch-map task where residents drew where 'the valley accent' begins. The task: run the dialectological toolkit end to end.

1. Draw the isoglosses: plot the pail/bucket line, the 'wants oiled' area, and the (variable) rhoticity zone on the district sketch-map; observe that the three lines do not coincide — state the bundling principle and refuse to draw a single 'dialect border' the data doesn't support.
2. Read the continuum: village-by-village intelligibility is complete while the district's endpoints differ noticeably — connect to the language/dialect convention and the army-and-navy aphorism, and classify the district as a micro-continuum.
3. Apply apparent time to rhoticity: tabulate /r/ rates by age group (elders high, middle variable, young low) — infer change in progress toward non-rhoticity; add the validation caveat (age-grading versus change) and note what a real-time restudy in twenty years would settle.
4. Diagnose levelling: 'pail' is retreating among younger upslope speakers toward the regional 'bucket' — attribute to schooling and market-town contact (face-to-face), not media alone; contrast with a broadcast catchphrase all ages use: vocabulary via media, sound change via interaction.
5. Analyze the perceptual maps: residents place 'the valley accent' boundary at the old district line — where no isogloss bundle runs; state the finding: perceived boundaries track social geography and identity, and load the attitudes the sociolinguistics concept measured.
6. Close with the ideology audit: the schoolteacher calls upslope 'wants oiled' 'broken grammar' — answer with the toolkit: the form is regionally systematic, isogloss-mappable, historically ordinary — a dialect feature exactly as standard forms are; everyone in the district, teacher included, speaks a mapped variety.

*Outcome:* The learner plots non-coinciding isoglosses and refuses unsupported borders, reads a continuum correctly, applies apparent time with its caveat, distinguishes levelling mechanisms from media vocabulary spread, interprets perceptual maps as attitude data, and dissolves a 'broken grammar' judgment with the field's instruments.

**Real-world intuition**

- Forensic speaker profiling draws on dialect geography — feature bundles can indicate a speaker's regional background, within the field's carefully stated limits.
- Speech technology must handle regional variation: recognizers trained on one variety fail others, and dialect-aware modelling is an active engineering requirement.
- Marketing, dubbing, and localization use dialect maps operationally — which lexical variant, which accent for which market — and perceptual-dialectology findings predict audience reactions.

### Bilingualism and Multilingualism

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.bilingualism-and-multilingualism` · Difficulty: expert · Bloom level: analyze · Mastery threshold: 0.7 · Estimated study time: 2h*

**Learning objective.** Analyze bilingualism at individual and societal levels: characterize bilingual competence against the two-monolinguals myth, evaluate cognitive and social findings with their debates stated honestly, and analyze societal multilingualism — diglossia, language maintenance and shift, and endangerment — with its policy stakes.

A survey of how individuals and societies acquire, use, and are shaped by more than one language.

Bilingualism and multilingualism — command and use of more than one language — are the human norm: most of the world's population uses multiple languages routinely, and the monolingual default assumed by much early research is the special case. At the individual level, the founding correction is Grosjean's principle that a bilingual is not two monolinguals in one person: bilinguals acquire and use their languages in different domains, for different purposes, with proficiency profiles that differ by skill (a speaker may read best in one language, joke best in another) — so measuring each language against a monolingual yardstick systematically misdescribes the competence. Bilinguals' languages are simultaneously active (both systems show activation even in single-language settings, a robust psycholinguistic finding), which makes code-switching — analyzed in the sociolinguistics concept as grammatically constrained and socially strategic — the natural behaviour of a doubly-live system rather than interference. Types are distinguished by onset and route: simultaneous bilingualism (two languages from infancy — children separate the systems early, and mixing is developmentally ordinary, not confusion) versus sequential (a second language after the first is established, connecting to the acquisition concept's interlanguage and critical-period findings). On cognition, the survey reports honestly: bilingual children reliably show earlier metalinguistic awareness (understanding that names are conventions — the domain's arbitrariness feature, discovered early by children who own two names for one thing); claims of a general executive-function 'bilingual advantage' generated an enormous literature and an ongoing replication debate, and the responsible summary is contested-and-open; what is settled is that bilingualism carries no cognitive cost — the deficit view that shaped early twentieth-century testing and policy is decisively refuted. At the societal level: multilingual societies organize their languages functionally — Ferguson's diglossia names the stable arrangement where a High variety serves formal/written domains and a Low variety everyday talk (Arabic's standard/colloquial pairing; Swiss German alongside Standard German) — and communities undergo language maintenance or shift across generations, with the canonical three-generation pattern in migration settings (monolingual grandparents, bilingual parents, majority-language-dominant grandchildren). At the far end of shift lies endangerment: roughly half the world's ~7,000 languages are at risk, intergenerational transmission is the diagnostic variable (Fishman's insight that schools and media cannot substitute for the home), and revitalization ranges from documentation through immersion schooling to the benchmark case of Hebrew's revernacularization — with Māori and Welsh among the instructive partial successes. The policy stakes connect back to applied linguistics: additive versus subtractive schooling decides whether children's home languages are resources kept or costs discarded.

**Key ideas**

- Multilingualism is the global norm; the monolingual baseline is the special case, and research design has had to correct for it.
- A bilingual is not two monolinguals (Grosjean): proficiency is domain- and skill-differentiated, and both languages remain active even in single-language use.
- Simultaneous bilingual children separate their systems early — mixing is developmentally ordinary; sequential bilingualism runs through interlanguage and the critical-period gradient.
- Cognitive findings, stated honestly: earlier metalinguistic awareness is robust; the executive-function 'bilingual advantage' is contested and unresolved; cognitive-deficit claims are decisively refuted.
- Societies organize multilingualism functionally: diglossia (Ferguson's High/Low domains), and communities maintain or shift languages — canonically across three migration generations.
- Endangerment threatens roughly half of the world's languages; intergenerational home transmission is the decisive variable (Fishman), and revitalization outcomes range from Hebrew's success through Māori and Welsh's partial gains.

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Raising a child with two languages causes confusion and delays language development.
  *Correction:* Simultaneous bilinguals hit the same milestones on the same schedule (vocabulary totals across both languages match monolingual totals), separate their systems early, and their mixing is ordinary developmental behaviour that resolves — the same verdict acquisition research gave code-switching in adults: competence, not confusion. The confusion claim descends from discredited early testing, not evidence.
- *Misconception:* A real bilingual speaks both languages perfectly and equally — anything less is failed bilingualism.
  *Correction:* Balanced-and-perfect is the two-monolinguals myth in disguise: actual bilinguals distribute their languages across domains and skills by the lives they lead, and profiles shift over a lifetime. Bilingual competence is the integrated repertoire doing its jobs, and measuring each half against a monolingual yardstick misses the whole.
- *Misconception:* The bilingual cognitive advantage is settled science — bilingualism makes you smarter.
  *Correction:* The honest report is layered: earlier metalinguistic awareness is well supported; the broader executive-function advantage is the subject of a live replication debate with evidence on both sides; and 'no cognitive cost' is settled. Overselling the advantage invites the same evidential carelessness as the old deficit claims — the field's credibility lies in reporting the layers.
- *Misconception:* Endangered languages die because parents freely choose a more useful language — schools and apps can save them anyway.
  *Correction:* Shift typically happens under economic and institutional pressure and stigma — the 'choice' is structured — and the revitalization evidence (Fishman's) is that nothing substitutes for intergenerational home transmission: schools and media support it but cannot replace it. Where communities re-secured the home domain (Hebrew; Māori language nests), revitalization gained ground; where efforts stayed institutional, it mostly did not.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Measuring each of a bilingual's languages against monolingual norms.
- Reading child language mixing as confusion instead of ordinary development.
- Citing the executive-function advantage as settled science.
- Expecting schools or media alone to reverse language shift without home transmission.

### Introduction to Translation Studies

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.translation-studies-intro` · Difficulty: research · Bloom level: evaluate · Mastery threshold: 0.7 · Estimated study time: 2h*

**Learning objective.** Analyze translation as decision-making under constraint: apply the equivalence spectrum from literal to dynamic, diagnose classic translation problems (idioms, culture-bound terms, register, untranslatables), distinguish translation from interpreting, and evaluate machine translation's capabilities and limits.

A survey of the theory and practice of transferring meaning between languages.

Translation studies examines the theory and practice of transferring meaning between languages — and its founding insight is that transfer is never mechanical substitution, because languages do not partition meaning identically. The organizing instrument is the equivalence spectrum. At one end, formal (literal) equivalence tracks the source's words and structures as closely as the target language permits; at the other, dynamic (functional) equivalence — the terms are Eugene Nida's, from the Bible-translation tradition that founded much of the field — seeks the same effect on the target reader that the source had on its readers. Neither end is 'correct': the right position depends on the text's function, a principle German functionalists generalized as skopos theory — the translation's purpose governs its strategy (a court document, a poem, and an advertising slogan for the same product demand different equivalences). The spectrum becomes vivid at the classic problem sites. Idioms: 'kick the bucket' translated word-for-word produces nonsense — the semantics concept's non-compositionality made practical; the translator must find the target idiom with the same function, not the same imagery. Culture-bound terms: words like English 'privacy', Danish 'hygge', or kinship systems that split where English lumps (many languages distinguish maternal from paternal uncles) lack ready equivalents — strategies range from borrowing and calquing to explanatory paraphrase, each with costs. Register and dialect: a source text's formality, politeness calibration (the pragmatics concept's face-work, which languages grammaticalize differently — tu/vous distinctions English lacks), and dialect voices force choices with no neutral option. 'Untranslatability' is thus better stated precisely: anything sayable in one language is expressible in another (the expressibility principle), but not always with the same compactness, connotation, and form — poetry, where sound and meaning fuse (the literature domain's meter and rhyme), marks the practical limit, which is why verse translation is called re-creation. Interpreting — real-time oral translation, simultaneous or consecutive — adds cognitive constraints translation escapes: memory load, speed, and no revision, a genuinely distinct professional skill. Machine translation completes the survey: from rule-based systems through statistical to neural MT, quality has risen dramatically on high-resource language pairs, yet the failure modes track this concept's problem sites exactly — idioms, register, culture-bound terms, low-resource languages — and professional practice has reorganized around post-editing, with the human translator as quality authority. The comparative-literature concept's discipline holds here as the consumer's rule: every translation is an interpretive performance, and comparing two translations of one passage reveals both the original's possibilities and each translator's decisions.

**Key ideas**

- Translation is decision-making under constraint, not substitution: languages partition meaning differently, so every rendering selects.
- The equivalence spectrum runs from formal (structure-tracking) to dynamic (effect-matching) — Nida's distinction — and skopos (purpose) decides the right position per text.
- Classic problem sites: idioms (non-compositional, translate by function), culture-bound terms (borrow, calque, or paraphrase — each with costs), and register/politeness systems languages grammaticalize differently.
- Untranslatability, stated precisely: everything is expressible across languages, but not always with equal compactness, connotation, or form — poetry marks the practical limit.
- Interpreting is translation under real-time cognitive constraint (simultaneous/consecutive) — a distinct skill with no revision pass.
- Neural machine translation is strong on high-resource pairs and fails precisely at this concept's problem sites; professional practice centres on human post-editing and quality authority.

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Translation is word substitution — a big enough dictionary solves it.
  *Correction:* Word-for-word rendering breaks on the first idiom, the first register choice, the first term the target language splits differently. Translators translate texts-in-purpose, not words: the unit of fidelity is function and meaning in context, which is why the same sentence translates differently in a contract and a novel.
- *Misconception:* A good translation is always the most literal one — departures are liberties.
  *Correction:* Literalism is one strategy on a spectrum, right for some purposes (legal parallel texts, scholarly cribs) and wrong for others: the literal rendering of an idiom is a mistranslation of its meaning, and the literal politeness form can be a register error. Skopos decides: fidelity to function sometimes requires formal departure.
- *Misconception:* Some things simply cannot be translated — untranslatable words prove languages are sealed worlds.
  *Correction:* The expressibility principle holds: any meaning can be conveyed in any language, though perhaps by phrase rather than word and with connotative and formal losses. 'Untranslatable' words are compactness gaps, not meaning walls — 'hygge' takes English a sentence instead of a syllable. The real limit case is form-meaning fusion (verse), where translation becomes acknowledged re-creation.
- *Misconception:* Machine translation has solved translation — human translators are obsolete.
  *Correction:* Neural MT is impressively fluent on high-resource pairs and routine text, but its failures cluster exactly at the field's known problem sites — idioms rendered literally, register flattened, culture-bound terms mishandled, low-resource languages poorly served — and fluent output masks errors, raising the stakes. The profession has shifted toward post-editing and quality authority, not disappearance; for consequential texts, human judgment remains the standard.

### Introduction to Corpus Linguistics

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.corpus-linguistics-intro` · Difficulty: research · Bloom level: apply · Mastery threshold: 0.7 · Estimated study time: 2h*

**Learning objective.** Use corpora — large, principled collections of authentic text — to study language empirically: read concordances and collocation statistics, compare frequencies across registers with normalization, respect representativeness limits, and state what corpus evidence can and cannot settle.

A survey of using large, structured collections of authentic text to study language empirically.

Corpus linguistics studies language through corpora: large, structured collections of authentic text and transcribed speech, sampled on principle and queried by software. It is a methodology serving every level of the domain rather than a level of its own — and its founding demonstration is that intuition about usage is systematically unreliable: speakers confidently misreport how often they use words, which senses dominate, and which combinations are normal, while corpus counts settle such questions in seconds. The toolkit's core instruments: the concordance (every occurrence of a query word displayed in context — KWIC, 'key word in context' — turning reading into pattern-scanning across hundreds of instances); frequency lists (raw counts, meaningful only after normalization to a common base such as per million words when corpora differ in size); collocation statistics (measures like mutual information and log-likelihood that separate a word's habitual partners from chance co-occurrence — the vocabulary domain's collocations, now measurable: 'strong tea' but 'powerful engine', and around the running specimen, 'unlock' partnering 'door', 'gate', 'potential', 'phone'); and register comparison (the same feature counted across sub-corpora of conversation, fiction, news, and academic prose — the design of major reference corpora from the pioneering million-word Brown Corpus of 1961 through the 100-million-word British National Corpus — revealing, in Douglas Biber's tradition of register studies, that grammar varies by situation as systematically as vocabulary: passives cluster in academic prose, contractions in conversation). Two disciplines govern inference. Representativeness: a corpus is evidence about the language varieties it samples — a newspaper corpus licenses claims about newspaper English, and 'the corpus shows X' silently means 'in these registers, in this period'. And the absence caveat: corpora prove presence and measure frequency, but absence proves little (rare ≠ impossible — the syntax concept's grammaticality judgments remain a distinct evidence type that corpus data complements rather than replaces). The method's reach is wide: lexicography rebuilt itself on corpora (modern dictionaries rank senses and choose examples by corpus evidence); language teaching draws frequency-informed syllabi and data-driven learning (students reading concordances directly); historical corpora track change quantitatively (the historical concept's semantic pathways, now countable decade by decade); and forensic and stylistic applications compare disputed texts against reference norms. Corpus linguistics is the domain's collect-pattern-rule loop industrialized — and the bridge to the computational concept that closes the domain, where the same corpora train language technology.

**Key ideas**

- A corpus is a principled sample of authentic language queried by software — and intuition about usage is demonstrably unreliable where corpus counts are decisive.
- The concordance (KWIC) turns hundreds of contexts into scannable pattern evidence; frequency comparisons require normalization to a common base.
- Collocation statistics separate habitual partnerships ('strong tea') from chance co-occurrence, making the vocabulary domain's collocations measurable.
- Register comparison across sub-corpora (conversation, fiction, news, academic) shows grammar varying by situation as systematically as vocabulary (Biber's tradition; Brown and BNC as landmark designs).
- Representativeness bounds every claim: a corpus evidences the varieties it samples, and 'the corpus shows' means 'in these registers, this period'.
- Corpora prove presence and frequency, not impossibility: absence is weak evidence, and grammaticality judgments remain a complementary data type.

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Native-speaker intuition already knows how the language is used — corpora just confirm it expensively.
  *Correction:* Intuition is reliable for grammaticality but demonstrably poor at frequency and distribution: speakers misestimate common senses, miss their own most-used collocations, and misreport register differences. Corpus counts routinely overturn confident introspection — which is precisely why lexicography and testing rebuilt themselves on corpus evidence.
- *Misconception:* Bigger is always better — the largest corpus wins.
  *Correction:* Design beats raw size for most questions: a claim about conversation needs conversational data however large a news crawl is, and an unbalanced giant licenses only claims about what it contains. Size buys statistical power for rare items; representativeness decides what the power is evidence about — the two virtues are independent.
- *Misconception:* If a form doesn't occur in the corpus, it isn't part of the language.
  *Correction:* Absence conflates the impossible with the merely rare and the unsampled: perfectly grammatical sentences have zero corpus frequency (productivity guarantees it — most possible sentences have never been uttered). Corpora measure what happens, not what can happen; possibility questions still need judgment data.
- *Misconception:* Corpus work is just counting words — it can't touch grammar or meaning.
  *Correction:* Annotated corpora tag parts of speech and parse structure, enabling grammatical queries (passive rates by register, relative-clause types); concordance sense-sorting quantifies meaning distinctions; and collocation profiles distinguish near-synonyms ('strong'/'powerful') no definition separates cleanly. Counting is the entry point; the queries reach every level of the domain.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Comparing raw frequencies across corpora of different sizes.
- Reading three concordance lines anecdotally instead of scanning the pattern.
- Claiming impossibility from corpus absence.
- Generalizing beyond the corpus's sampled registers and period.

### Introduction to Computational Linguistics

*Concept ID: `eng.linguistics.computational-linguistics-intro` · Difficulty: research · Bloom level: apply · Mastery threshold: 0.7 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Survey computational approaches to language: trace the field's arc from rules through statistics to neural models, explain core tasks and the ambiguity problem that unifies them, describe how n-gram and neural language models work in principle, and evaluate modern systems' capabilities and limits with the domain's linguistic instruments.

A survey of computational and statistical approaches to processing and modeling human language.

Computational linguistics — with its engineering face, natural language processing (NLP) — studies how to process and model human language computationally. The survey closes the domain because it inherits everything: the levels tower returns as the field's task list — speech recognition and synthesis (phonetics/phonology), morphological analysis and stemming (morphology), part-of-speech tagging and parsing (syntax), word-sense disambiguation and semantic similarity (semantics), dialogue management (pragmatics/discourse), and machine translation (the previous concept's application flagship). One problem unifies them: ambiguity, everywhere the domain found it — the syntax concept's attachment ambiguities ('She watched the man with the telescope') are a parser's daily fare, the garden paths of psycholinguistics are an incremental system's hazard, and resolving ambiguity requires exactly the frequency, plausibility, and context information humans deploy. The field's history is three eras. Rule-based systems (from the 1950s) hand-coded grammars and lexicons: linguistically transparent, brittle against real text's variety — and the era's cautionary emblem is the early machine-translation overpromise that ended in the funding winter of the 1960s. Statistical NLP (from the late 1980s) learned probabilities from corpora — the previous concept's data industrialized: n-gram language models estimate each word's probability from the preceding few words (bigrams, trigrams), enough to power the era's speech recognizers and translators, with hidden Markov models tagging parts of speech; the limitation is horizon — an n-gram cannot see past its window, missing long-distance dependencies the syntax concept's recursion guarantees. Neural language models (dominant from the 2010s) replaced symbol-counting with learned vector representations: word embeddings place words in a space where distributional neighbours ('lock', 'key', 'bolt') cluster — the distributional hypothesis, 'you shall know a word by the company it keeps' (Firth), which is the corpus concept's collocation insight become geometry; sequence models and then transformer architectures with attention mechanisms learn long-range dependencies at scale, and today's large language models, trained on vast corpora to predict the next word, generate fluent text and perform many of the classical tasks at once. The survey's evaluative discipline is the domain's own: fluency is not understanding (the systems model the distribution of text, and their confident errors — 'hallucinations' — betray the difference); performance concentrates where data is (high-resource languages and registers — the corpus concept's representativeness limit at planetary scale, with most of the world's languages under-served); bias in training text becomes bias in systems; and evaluation needs linguistic instruments — the levels, the ambiguity types, the register and variation findings — to probe what fluent output conceals. Computational linguistics is thus the domain's mirror: every concept surveyed — phonology to pragmatics, acquisition to corpora — names both a capability the machines must reproduce and a test they must pass.

**Key ideas**

- Computational linguistics/NLP inherits the levels tower as its task list — speech, morphology, tagging and parsing, sense disambiguation, dialogue, translation — with ambiguity as the unifying hard problem.
- Three eras: rule-based systems (transparent, brittle; the early MT overpromise as cautionary tale), statistical NLP (probabilities from corpora; n-grams and HMMs), and neural models (embeddings, attention, large language models).
- N-gram models predict from a fixed window and cannot see long-distance dependencies; recursion guarantees language contains them — a linguistic fact with architectural consequences.
- Word embeddings geometrize the distributional hypothesis (Firth's 'company it keeps'): corpus collocation becomes vector proximity.
- Large language models are next-word predictors over vast corpora: fluent and multi-task, but fluency is not understanding — confident errors reveal distribution-modelling beneath.
- Evaluation is applied linguistics: data concentration (high-resource bias), encoded social bias, and ambiguity/register probes are the domain's findings turned into test instruments.

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Computers process language by looking up dictionary meanings and applying grammar rules.
  *Correction:* That describes the rule-based era, which broke against real text's variety and ambiguity. Modern systems learn statistical and vector representations from corpora; their 'knowledge' is distributional patterning, not consulted definitions — which explains both their breadth and their characteristic failure modes.
- *Misconception:* A system that produces fluent, grammatical text understands language.
  *Correction:* Fluency demonstrates mastery of textual distribution, not comprehension: the same systems confidently generate false statements and miss inferences a child makes. The domain's own distinction applies — modelling the form of the signal is not possessing its meaning and use; evaluation must probe beneath surface fluency.
- *Misconception:* Language technology works equally well for all languages.
  *Correction:* Performance tracks training data, and data tracks wealth, population, and web presence: a handful of high-resource languages enjoy strong systems while thousands of languages — most of the world's linguistic diversity — are barely served. This is the corpus representativeness limit at global scale, and it is an equity issue as much as an engineering one.
- *Misconception:* Since machines now generate language, linguistics is obsolete — the data does the science.
  *Correction:* The dependency runs the other way: linguistic categories define the tasks (what is a parse, a sense, a register?), linguistic phenomena supply the hard cases (ambiguity, long-distance dependency, politeness), and linguistic analysis builds the evaluations that expose what fluency conceals. The better the systems, the more the probing needs the science.

The task: trace each system's handling and evaluate with the domain's instruments.

1. Run the rule-based trace: tokenize, tag ('could' modal, 'unlock' verb...), parse by hand-coded rules — transparent at every step; then break it: feed dialect input ('Could you unlock them gates?') and watch coverage fail — the brittleness that ended era one, and the sociolinguistics concept's variation as the breaking data.
2. Run the trigram view: the model scores 'unlock the gate' highly (frequent trigram) and prefers it over 'unlock the gaze' — real power from mere counts; then expose the horizon: in 'the gate that the rains the villagers had prayed for finally opened', the dependency between 'gate' and 'opened' spans past any trigram window — recursion's guarantee, met by architecture failure.
3. Inspect the embedding space: 'lock', 'key', 'bolt', 'gate' cluster; 'unlock' sits near 'open' but their neighbourhoods differ exactly as the corpus concept's collocation profiles differed — state the identity: distributional hypothesis, geometrized.
4. Probe the neural model with the attachment ambiguity: it resolves 'with the telescope' by learned plausibility (watching-instruments are telescope-like) — often humanlike, and wrong the same way humans garden-path when plausibility misleads; connect to psycholinguistics: the systems inherit the ambiguity problem, not just the tasks.
5. Probe pragmatics on the specimen: does the system respond to 'Could you unlock the gate?' with action-oriented content or a literal ability answer? — test the indirect speech act; note that handling it requires exactly the pragmatics concept's machinery, learned or engineered, and that failures here are failures of the highest floor of the levels tower.
6. Close with the evaluation brief: fluency observed everywhere, so probe instead — one ambiguity battery (syntax), one register/dialect battery (sociolinguistics), one low-resource check (corpus representativeness), one confident-error hunt (fluency ≠ understanding); verdict template: capabilities named, limits named, both with linguistic instruments — the domain closing as the toolkit it promised to be.
