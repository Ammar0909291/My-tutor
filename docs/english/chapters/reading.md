### From Print to Meaning

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.print-to-meaning` · Difficulty: developing · Bloom level: understand · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 2h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to explain how decoding and vocabulary knowledge combine to produce comprehended meaning, and identify breakdowns when decoding succeeds but comprehension fails.

The bridging skill of connecting accurately decoded print to comprehended meaning, uniting decoding and vocabulary knowledge.

FROM PRINT TO MEANING is the bridging skill connecting accurately decoded print (from eng.phonics.decoding-fluency) to comprehended meaning, uniting it with vocabulary knowledge (from eng.vocab.context-clues) — the foundational insight that decoding alone is not reading. A reader can correctly sound out every word in a sentence (decode accurately) and still fail to understand its meaning, if the vocabulary is unfamiliar or the decoding process consumes too much cognitive effort to leave capacity for comprehension. This concept formalizes the Simple View of Reading: Reading Comprehension = Decoding × Language Comprehension — both factors must be present, since a zero in either one produces zero overall comprehension, and this multiplicative (not additive) relationship is the domain's opening organizing principle.

**Key ideas**

- Decoding (accurately converting print to sound, from eng.phonics.decoding-fluency) and language comprehension (understanding word/sentence meaning, drawing on eng.vocab.context-clues) are two distinct skills that must combine to produce reading comprehension
- The Simple View of Reading formalizes this as multiplicative: Reading Comprehension = Decoding × Language Comprehension — a zero in either factor produces zero comprehension overall, not partial comprehension
- A reader can decode perfectly (correctly sound out every word) yet still fail to comprehend, if vocabulary is unfamiliar or too much cognitive effort is consumed by the decoding process itself, leaving too little capacity for meaning-making
- Conversely, a reader with strong language comprehension but weak decoding will also fail to comprehend written text, even though they could understand the same content if it were spoken aloud to them
- This bridging concept is the direct reason accurate, fluent decoding (developed through the entire Foundational Literacy & Phonics domain) is a necessary but not sufficient condition for genuine reading comprehension

**Vocabulary**

- **Simple View of Reading**
- **decoding**
- **language comprehension**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* A student who can accurately sound out every word in a passage has, by definition, read and understood it.
  *Correction:* Accurate decoding alone does not guarantee comprehension — the Simple View of Reading formalizes this as a MULTIPLICATIVE relationship (Decoding × Language Comprehension), meaning a student can decode perfectly yet still fail to comprehend if their language comprehension (vocabulary, background knowledge) is insufficient for that specific text.
- *Misconception:* Reading comprehension and decoding are the same skill, just described with different words.
  *Correction:* Decoding and language comprehension are two genuinely distinct cognitive skills, each independently developed across different parts of the curriculum (decoding through Foundational Literacy & Phonics; language comprehension through Vocabulary Development and beyond) — a struggling reader might have a decoding-specific weakness, a comprehension-specific weakness, or both, and correctly diagnosing which is essential for effective intervention.
- *Misconception:* The Decoding × Language Comprehension relationship is additive, so being very strong in one area can fully compensate for being very weak in the other.
  *Correction:* The relationship is explicitly MULTIPLICATIVE, not additive — a very high decoding score multiplied by a near-zero language comprehension score still produces a near-zero overall comprehension result, meaning strength in one factor cannot fully substitute for severe weakness in the other, unlike an additive average would suggest.
- *Misconception:* Once decoding fluency is achieved, cognitive effort is no longer a relevant factor in reading comprehension.
  *Correction:* Even after decoding fluency is achieved, allocating adequate cognitive effort to meaning-making remains essential — if decoding a particularly difficult or unfamiliar word still consumes significant cognitive resources in a specific instance, less capacity remains available for comprehension of that sentence, which is why increasing automaticity (the focus of eng.phonics.decoding-fluency and eng.reading.reading-fluency) directly supports comprehension by freeing up this cognitive capacity.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Assuming accurate decoding automatically means comprehension has occurred
- Treating the decoding-comprehension relationship as additive rather than multiplicative
- Misdiagnosing a vocabulary-based comprehension gap as a decoding problem, or vice versa

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A multiplication-equation diagram: Reading Comprehension = Decoding × Language Comprehension, with a zero-value example on one side producing a zero result
- A cognitive-capacity 'bucket' illustration showing effort divided between decoding and comprehension, with the decoding portion shrinking as fluency increases
- A two-axis diagram plotting decoding skill against language comprehension skill, with reading comprehension only strong in the top-right quadrant where both are strong

**Worked example**

*Setup:* A student accurately decodes every word in the sentence 'The mitochondria is the powerhouse of the cell's cellular respiration process' but cannot explain what the sentence means. Diagnose the breakdown using the Simple View of Reading, and explain what kind of support this student needs.

1. Confirm decoding is not the issue: the student accurately sounded out every word, including multisyllabic scientific terms — decoding is strong.
2. Identify the likely breakdown location: language comprehension, specifically vocabulary knowledge — the student likely lacks familiarity with 'mitochondria,' 'powerhouse,' and 'cellular respiration' as concepts, even though they could pronounce the words.
3. Apply the Simple View equation: strong decoding × weak vocabulary/background knowledge for this topic = weak overall comprehension for this specific sentence.
4. Determine the appropriate support: this student needs vocabulary and background-knowledge instruction (connecting to eng.vocab's academic-vocabulary concept), not additional decoding practice, since decoding is already strong.
5. Contrast with a different diagnosis: a student who struggles to sound out the words at all, despite understanding the biology concept when explained aloud, would need decoding support instead — the same comprehension failure can have opposite underlying causes.

*Outcome:* The learner correctly applies the Simple View of Reading to diagnose that this specific student's comprehension breakdown stems from language comprehension (vocabulary), not decoding, and correctly identifies the appropriate type of instructional support.

**Real-world intuition**

- The Simple View of Reading is a foundational, evidence-based framework used by reading specialists and educators to diagnose and target specific reading difficulties
- Understanding this bridging skill explains why English language learners with strong decoding (from phonics instruction) may still struggle with comprehension due to vocabulary gaps, informing targeted instructional design
- This framework helps parents and teachers distinguish 'sounds like reading' (fluent-sounding decoding) from genuine comprehension, guiding more effective at-home and classroom support

**Practice progression**

Item types: Diagnose whether a comprehension breakdown stems from decoding or language comprehension given a described reading scenario; Explain the multiplicative (not additive) nature of the Simple View of Reading using a numeric or conceptual example; Identify the appropriate type of support (decoding practice vs. vocabulary/background-knowledge instruction) for a given diagnosed breakdown; Explain why fluent decoding frees up cognitive capacity for comprehension. Suggested item count: 6.

Begin with clearly one-sided diagnostic scenarios (strong decoding, weak vocabulary or vice versa), progress to explaining the multiplicative relationship conceptually, then to selecting appropriate instructional responses for mixed or ambiguous diagnostic scenarios.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Diagnostic scenario task, Short answer, Application task. Bloom alignment: Understand — learners must comprehend the multiplicative relationship between decoding and language comprehension and correctly diagnose which factor is responsible for a given comprehension breakdown.

Mastery signal: The learner can correctly diagnose whether a comprehension breakdown stems from decoding or language comprehension using the Simple View of Reading, and correctly explain the equation's multiplicative nature, at 75% accuracy or better.

*Note:* Sample item shapes: Diagnostic scenario: a student reads fluently aloud but cannot answer questions about the passage — diagnose the likely breakdown. | Short answer: explain why a 90% decoding score and a 10% language comprehension score do not average to 50% overall comprehension. | Application: recommend an instructional intervention for a diagnosed vocabulary-based comprehension gap.

**Teacher notes**

This concept is the domain's essential organizing principle — every later concept in Reading builds on the assumption that decoding and language comprehension are already integrating successfully. The multiplicative-versus-additive framing is the single most important, most easily misunderstood point and deserves a concrete numeric example, not just a verbal description.

**Student notes**

Reading isn't just sounding out words correctly — it's sounding out words AND understanding what they mean, together. The Simple View of Reading says: Comprehension = Decoding × Language Comprehension. If either one is zero, your total understanding is zero too, no matter how strong the other one is.

### Reading Fluency

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.reading-fluency` · Difficulty: developing · Bloom level: apply · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 2h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to define reading fluency as the combination of accuracy, rate, and prosody, and evaluate a reading sample against all three dimensions.

Reading connected text accurately, at an appropriate rate, and with expression that reflects meaning.

READING FLUENCY is reading connected text accurately, at an appropriate rate, and with expression (PROSODY) that reflects meaning — directly extending print-to-meaning's cognitive-capacity insight into a measurable, three-dimensional skill. ACCURACY means correctly reading words with minimal errors; RATE means reading at a pace appropriate to the text and purpose, neither too slow (which taxes working memory) nor unnaturally fast (which can sacrifice comprehension); and PROSODY means reading with appropriate phrasing, stress, and intonation that reflects the text's meaning and punctuation, rather than a flat, word-by-word monotone. Fluency is not a goal in itself but a means to an end: the automaticity that fluent reading provides is precisely what frees the cognitive capacity that print-to-meaning identified as necessary for comprehension.

**Key ideas**

- Reading fluency has three dimensions: accuracy (correctly reading words), rate (appropriate pace), and prosody (expression reflecting meaning) — often summarized as reading 'accurately, quickly, and with expression'
- Fluency is not simply reading FAST — an inappropriately fast reading rate can sacrifice both accuracy and comprehension, just as an inappropriately slow rate taxes working memory and disrupts comprehension
- Prosody (the 'e' in accurate-rate-expression) reflects understanding: a reader who pauses at commas, drops pitch at periods, and raises pitch for questions demonstrates that they are processing meaning, not simply decoding words in a monotone
- Fluency directly serves comprehension by building automaticity in decoding (from decoding-fluency), which frees the cognitive capacity print-to-meaning identified as necessary for meaning-making — fluency is a means to comprehension, not an end in itself
- Fluency is typically measured and monitored using words-correct-per-minute (accuracy and rate combined) alongside a qualitative prosody rating scale, both used by reading specialists to track progress

**Vocabulary**

- **reading fluency**
- **accuracy**
- **rate**
- **prosody**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Reading fluency simply means reading as fast as possible.
  *Correction:* Fluency requires accuracy and appropriate prosody alongside rate, not speed alone — a student who reads very quickly but inaccurately, or in a flat monotone with no expression reflecting meaning, is not truly fluent, even if their words-per-minute count is high; genuine fluency serves comprehension, and excessive speed can actually undermine it.
- *Misconception:* Prosody (expressive reading) is a nice bonus for fluent reading, but not connected to genuine comprehension.
  *Correction:* Prosody is a meaningful INDICATOR of comprehension, not merely a stylistic bonus — a reader who correctly pauses at commas, uses rising intonation for questions, and stresses appropriate words demonstrates that they are actively processing the text's meaning and grammatical structure, not just mechanically decoding word by word.
- *Misconception:* A student who reads slowly and carefully, with excellent accuracy, has no fluency concerns since accuracy is the most important dimension.
  *Correction:* While accuracy is essential, an inappropriately slow reading rate taxes working memory and can itself disrupt comprehension — by the time a very slow reader finishes a long sentence, they may have lost track of its beginning, showing that all three dimensions (accuracy, rate, AND prosody) genuinely matter together, not just accuracy in isolation.
- *Misconception:* Fluency is an end goal of reading instruction, valuable for its own sake.
  *Correction:* Fluency is a MEANS to comprehension, not an end in itself — its value comes entirely from the cognitive capacity it frees up for meaning-making, exactly as established at print-to-meaning; a student could theoretically become fluent at reading a text in a language they don't understand at all, which would demonstrate that fluency alone, without comprehension, is not the true instructional goal.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Equating fluency with reading speed alone
- Undervaluing prosody as a genuine comprehension indicator
- Assuming a slow, careful reader has no fluency concerns as long as accuracy is high

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A three-dimension fluency triangle (accuracy/rate/prosody) with a brief description at each vertex
- A words-correct-per-minute progress-tracking graph example showing improvement over several reading sessions
- An audio-waveform-style illustration contrasting flat, monotone prosody with expressive, meaning-reflecting prosody for the same sentence

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Evaluate two reading samples of the same paragraph against all three fluency dimensions: Reader A reads very quickly with several word-substitution errors and no vocal expression. Reader B reads at a moderate pace with no errors and appropriate pausing/intonation. Determine which reader demonstrates stronger fluency and why.

1. Evaluate Reader A on accuracy: several word-substitution errors indicate weak accuracy.
2. Evaluate Reader A on rate: very fast, but this speed may be contributing to the accuracy errors rather than reflecting genuine automaticity.
3. Evaluate Reader A on prosody: no vocal expression indicates the reader may be focused entirely on speed rather than meaning, suggesting weak comprehension monitoring.
4. Evaluate Reader B on all three dimensions: no errors (strong accuracy), moderate appropriate pace (strong rate), and appropriate pausing/intonation (strong prosody).
5. Conclude: Reader B demonstrates significantly stronger overall fluency despite reading more slowly than Reader A, since fluency requires all three dimensions together, not speed alone.

*Outcome:* The learner correctly evaluates both readers across all three fluency dimensions and correctly concludes that Reader B's balanced, accurate, expressive reading demonstrates stronger fluency than Reader A's fast but error-prone, expressionless reading.

**Real-world intuition**

- Reading fluency assessments (words-correct-per-minute with prosody rating) are standard diagnostic tools used by teachers and reading specialists to monitor reading development
- Fluent, expressive oral reading is essential for reading aloud effectively in professional, educational, and public-speaking contexts
- Understanding fluency's three dimensions helps parents and tutors provide more targeted at-home reading support than simply encouraging a child to 'read faster'

**Practice progression**

Item types: Evaluate a described reading sample against the three fluency dimensions; Identify which fluency dimension is weakest in a given reading scenario; Explain why an excessively fast reading rate can undermine fluency and comprehension; Explain the connection between prosody and comprehension using a specific example. Suggested item count: 6.

Begin with clearly one-dimensional fluency weaknesses (accuracy only, or rate only), progress to evaluating multiple dimensions simultaneously, then to explaining the deeper connection between fluency and comprehension via cognitive capacity.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Evaluation task, Short answer, Diagnostic scenario. Bloom alignment: Apply — learners must apply the three-dimensional fluency framework to evaluate and compare described reading samples, going beyond simply recalling the three dimension names.

Mastery signal: The learner can correctly evaluate a reading sample against all three fluency dimensions and correctly explain why fluency serves comprehension rather than being a goal in itself, at 75% accuracy or better.

*Note:* Sample item shapes: Evaluation: rate a described reading sample on accuracy, rate, and prosody. | Short answer: explain why reading too quickly can undermine fluency despite a high words-per-minute count. | Diagnostic scenario: identify the weakest fluency dimension for a student who reads accurately but in a flat monotone.

**Teacher notes**

The 'fluency is not just speed' misconception is extremely common and worth addressing immediately and memorably — the two-reader comparison worked example is the strongest tool for this, since it makes the tradeoff concrete rather than abstract. Explicitly connect prosody to comprehension monitoring, since students (and sometimes teachers) undervalue this dimension relative to accuracy and rate.

**Student notes**

Fluency means reading ACCURATELY, at an appropriate RATE, and with PROSODY (expression that shows you understand what you're reading) — all three together, not just speed. A fast reader who makes mistakes or reads in a flat monotone isn't truly fluent. Fluency matters because it frees up your brain's attention for understanding meaning, not because speed itself is the goal.

### Literal Comprehension

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.literal-comprehension` · Difficulty: developing · Bloom level: understand · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 2h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to accurately locate and restate information that is explicitly stated in a text, distinguishing it from inferred or interpreted information.

Understanding information that is explicitly stated in a text.

LITERAL COMPREHENSION is understanding information explicitly stated in a text — building directly on reading-fluency's foundation of automatic, accurate reading by applying that automaticity to the most basic level of comprehension: extracting facts the text directly states. Literal comprehension answers who/what/when/where questions with information found directly in the text, requiring no inference or interpretation beyond correctly understanding the stated words and sentences. This concept establishes the essential baseline the entire rest of the domain builds upon: main-idea-and-details, inference-in-reading, and every subsequent comprehension skill all require literal comprehension as their starting foundation, since a reader must first accurately understand what a text directly says before analyzing, inferring, or evaluating anything about it.

**Key ideas**

- Literal comprehension is understanding information explicitly stated in a text — answering who/what/when/where questions directly answerable from the text's own words
- Literal comprehension requires no inference or interpretation — the answer is directly present in the text, needing only accurate reading and understanding of what is stated
- Literal comprehension is the essential foundation for every higher-order comprehension skill developed later in the domain (main idea, inference, summarizing, analysis) — a reader must accurately grasp what a text directly says before they can meaningfully analyze, infer, or evaluate beyond it
- A common diagnostic test for literal comprehension: can the answer to a question be pointed to directly, word-for-word or near word-for-word, in the text? If yes, it is a literal comprehension question, not an inferential one
- Literal comprehension questions are typically the most straightforward comprehension-check format, but weak literal comprehension (misreading or misremembering explicitly stated facts) undermines every subsequent, more sophisticated comprehension skill built on top of it

**Vocabulary**

- **literal comprehension**
- **explicitly stated information**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Literal comprehension is a trivial, unimportant skill not worth dedicated instructional attention, since 'obviously' students can find facts stated directly in a text.
  *Correction:* Literal comprehension errors are a genuine and common source of reading difficulty even among otherwise capable readers, and weak literal comprehension undermines every higher-order skill built on top of it — a reader who misremembers or misreads an explicitly stated fact will produce flawed inferences, summaries, and analyses despite those higher-order skills being intact, making accurate literal comprehension a genuinely essential, not trivial, foundation.
- *Misconception:* Any question about a text's content, including questions requiring some interpretation, counts as testing literal comprehension.
  *Correction:* Literal comprehension specifically means information stated directly and explicitly in the text, answerable by pointing to the text's own words — a question requiring the reader to connect two separately stated facts, infer an unstated cause, or interpret a character's unstated motivation is an INFERENTIAL question (developed at inference-in-reading), not a literal one, even though both are legitimate comprehension question types.
- *Misconception:* Literal comprehension and reading fluency are the same skill.
  *Correction:* Reading fluency (from reading-fluency) concerns HOW accurately, quickly, and expressively a text is read aloud or processed, while literal comprehension concerns WHAT explicitly stated content the reader accurately understands and can recall — a reader can be fluent (read smoothly and accurately) yet still fail to correctly grasp or remember literally stated facts, showing these are related but genuinely distinct skills.
- *Misconception:* Literal comprehension only applies to simple, short texts and becomes unnecessary for longer or more sophisticated reading.
  *Correction:* Literal comprehension remains foundational and necessary at every text complexity level, including advanced academic and literary texts — even sophisticated close-reading and critical-reading analysis (developed later in the domain) depends on first accurately grasping what a complex text explicitly states, before any deeper analysis can meaningfully proceed.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Confusing an inferential question with a literal one
- Misreading or misremembering explicitly stated details, which then undermines later analysis
- Treating literal comprehension as too simple to require careful attention

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A 'point to the answer' diagram showing a literal comprehension question with an arrow pointing directly to the exact sentence in the text containing the answer
- A literal-versus-inferential question comparison pair based on the same short passage, showing the structural difference in what each requires
- A foundation-building pyramid diagram showing literal comprehension as the base layer, with main idea, inference, and analysis stacked above it

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Given the short passage 'Maria arrived at the library at 3 PM. She had forgotten her library card, so she asked the librarian for help,' answer the literal comprehension questions: (a) What time did Maria arrive? (b) What had Maria forgotten? (c) Who did Maria ask for help?

1. (a) 'What time did Maria arrive?' — directly stated: 'at 3 PM.' Point to the exact phrase in the text.
2. (b) 'What had Maria forgotten?' — directly stated: 'her library card.' Point to the exact phrase.
3. (c) 'Who did Maria ask for help?' — directly stated: 'the librarian.' Point to the exact phrase.
4. Confirm all three answers are literal — each can be located word-for-word or near word-for-word directly in the text, requiring no inference about WHY Maria forgot her card or HOW she felt about it (which would be inferential questions instead).
5. Contrast with a hypothetical inferential question about the same passage: 'How did Maria likely feel when she realized she'd forgotten her card?' — this cannot be answered by pointing directly to the text, since the passage never states Maria's feelings explicitly.

*Outcome:* The learner correctly answers all three literal comprehension questions by locating explicitly stated information, and correctly explains why a parallel inferential question would require a different, non-literal approach.

**Real-world intuition**

- Literal comprehension is the foundational skill tested by nearly all reading comprehension assessments before any inferential or analytical questions are introduced
- Following written instructions, procedures, and factual reports in professional and everyday contexts depends directly on accurate literal comprehension
- Accurate literal comprehension of source material is the essential first step before any research, citation, or analytical writing task can be completed correctly

**Practice progression**

Item types: Answer literal comprehension questions by locating explicitly stated information in a passage; Distinguish a literal comprehension question from an inferential question given a shared passage; Identify and correct a literal comprehension error (misreading or misremembering an explicitly stated fact); Point to the exact textual evidence supporting a literal comprehension answer. Suggested item count: 8.

Begin with short, simple passages with clearly locatable facts, progress to distinguishing literal from inferential questions on the same passage, then to longer passages with multiple explicitly stated details requiring careful, accurate literal comprehension.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Comprehension question task, Classification task, Evidence-location task. Bloom alignment: Understand — learners must comprehend and accurately restate information explicitly stated in a text, distinguishing this from information requiring inference or interpretation.

Mastery signal: The learner can correctly answer literal comprehension questions by locating explicitly stated information and correctly distinguish literal from inferential questions, at 85% accuracy or better.

*Note:* Sample item shapes: Comprehension question: answer 'Where did the story take place?' using a given passage. | Classification: is 'What did the character say?' a literal or inferential question? | Evidence location: highlight the exact sentence answering a given literal comprehension question.

**Teacher notes**

Resist treating this concept as too basic to deserve real instructional time — the point-to-the-text diagnostic test is a genuinely valuable, transferable skill, and weak literal comprehension is a common, under-diagnosed cause of downstream reading struggles. The literal-versus-inferential contrast pair is the strongest tool for establishing this concept's boundary before inference-in-reading extends beyond it.

**Student notes**

Literal comprehension means understanding what a text DIRECTLY says — no guessing, no reading between the lines, just accurately finding and understanding the facts that are actually stated. A good test: can you point to the exact sentence that answers the question? If yes, it's literal comprehension.

### Main Idea and Supporting Details

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.main-idea-and-details` · Difficulty: developing · Bloom level: analyze · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 2h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to identify a text's central main idea and distinguish it from the supporting details that develop it.

Identifying a text's central idea and the specific details that develop or support it.

MAIN IDEA AND SUPPORTING DETAILS involves identifying a text's central idea and the specific details that develop or support it — the first concept requiring genuine ANALYSIS (moving beyond literal-comprehension's direct fact-location into synthesizing multiple pieces of literal information into one overarching idea). The MAIN IDEA is the central point a text conveys, often (but not always) stated in a topic sentence; SUPPORTING DETAILS are the specific facts, examples, and explanations that develop, illustrate, or prove that main idea. Unlike literal comprehension's directly-locatable facts, identifying the main idea sometimes requires synthesizing information across an entire paragraph or passage, especially when the main idea is IMPLIED rather than directly stated in a single sentence.

**Key ideas**

- The main idea is a text's central point — what the text is fundamentally about or arguing — while supporting details are the specific facts, examples, and explanations that develop or prove that main idea
- A main idea is sometimes directly STATED in an explicit topic sentence (often, but not always, the first sentence of a paragraph), and sometimes IMPLIED, requiring the reader to synthesize the collective point of several supporting details
- Identifying the main idea requires a genuine analytical step beyond literal comprehension: synthesizing multiple literally-comprehended facts into one overarching idea, rather than simply locating one directly stated answer
- A common test for distinguishing main idea from supporting detail: the main idea is broad enough to encompass and be supported by ALL the details, while any single supporting detail is too narrow to represent the whole passage
- Confusing a supporting detail (especially a vivid, memorable, or first-mentioned one) for the main idea is one of the most common comprehension errors at this level, since a compelling detail can feel more memorable than the broader, more abstract main idea it supports

**Vocabulary**

- **main idea**
- **supporting detail**
- **topic sentence**
- **implied main idea**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* The main idea is always the first sentence of a paragraph or passage.
  *Correction:* While a topic sentence stating the main idea often appears first, main ideas can also appear in the middle or at the end of a paragraph (as a concluding synthesis), or be entirely IMPLIED rather than directly stated anywhere, requiring the reader to synthesize it from the collective supporting details — assuming the first sentence is always the main idea leads to frequent misidentification.
- *Misconception:* The most interesting, vivid, or memorable detail in a passage is usually the main idea.
  *Correction:* A vivid or memorable detail is, by definition, a SUPPORTING detail — one specific piece of evidence — not the broader main idea it exists to support; mistaking an especially compelling detail for the main idea is one of the most common comprehension errors, since memorable details often feel more significant than the more abstract, encompassing idea they serve.
- *Misconception:* Identifying the main idea is the same skill as literal comprehension, just applied to a longer piece of text.
  *Correction:* Identifying the main idea requires a genuine analytical step beyond literal comprehension — synthesizing multiple separately, literally-comprehended facts into one overarching, often more abstract idea — rather than simply locating one directly stated answer, which is why this concept is classified at the analyze level of Bloom's taxonomy rather than the understand level of literal-comprehension.
- *Misconception:* Every paragraph has exactly one correct, universally agreed-upon main idea with no room for reasonable variation in phrasing.
  *Correction:* While a paragraph's main idea has a definite CONTENT (the central point being made), skilled readers may reasonably phrase or emphasize that main idea somewhat differently while still correctly capturing its essential substance — what matters is that the identified main idea is broad enough to encompass all the supporting details and accurately reflects the passage's central point, not that it matches one single memorized wording.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Mistaking a vivid supporting detail for the main idea
- Assuming the first sentence always states the main idea
- Stating a main idea too narrowly (matching only one detail) or too broadly (losing the passage's specific point)

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- An umbrella diagram with the main idea as the umbrella canopy and supporting details as raindrops falling beneath it, all connected to the central idea
- A stated-versus-implied main idea comparison, showing one paragraph with an explicit topic sentence and another requiring synthesis across all its sentences
- A 'too narrow vs. too broad vs. just right' main-idea-candidate exercise showing three possible main idea statements for one passage, only one of which correctly matches the details' scope

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Given the paragraph 'Bees pollinate roughly one-third of the food crops humans eat. Without bees, crops like almonds, apples, and blueberries would produce far less fruit. Bee populations have declined significantly in recent decades due to pesticide use and habitat loss. Protecting bee habitats is therefore essential for global food security,' identify the main idea and list the supporting details.

1. Scan for an explicit topic sentence: the paragraph does not open with one obvious single-sentence statement of the main idea, though the final sentence comes close.
2. Identify the supporting details: (1) bees pollinate about one-third of food crops, (2) specific crops (almonds, apples, blueberries) depend on bee pollination, (3) bee populations are declining due to pesticides and habitat loss.
3. Synthesize across all three details to identify the broader main idea they collectively support: bee population decline threatens global food security, making bee habitat protection essential.
4. Test the candidate main idea against the 'too narrow vs. too broad vs. just right' standard: it is broad enough to encompass all three supporting details (pollination importance, specific crop dependence, and population decline) without being so broad it loses the passage's specific point.
5. Confirm the main idea appears most explicitly (though not in isolation) in the final sentence, which functions as a synthesizing conclusion drawing together the details that preceded it.

*Outcome:* The learner correctly identifies the main idea as a synthesis of all three supporting details (bee decline threatens food security via pollination loss), rather than mistaking any single vivid detail (like the specific crop list) for the main idea itself.

**Real-world intuition**

- Identifying main idea and supporting details is one of the most heavily tested reading comprehension skills across standardized assessments at every education level
- Skimming professional documents, news articles, and reports for their main idea (developed further at skimming-and-scanning) is an essential real-world information-processing skill
- Effective summary writing (developed at summarizing) depends entirely on first correctly identifying a text's main idea and most essential supporting details

**Practice progression**

Item types: Identify a passage's stated or implied main idea; Distinguish supporting details from the main idea they develop; Evaluate whether a candidate main idea statement is too narrow, too broad, or accurately scoped; Synthesize an implied main idea from a set of supporting details with no explicit topic sentence. Suggested item count: 8.

Begin with passages containing an explicit topic sentence, progress to passages with the main idea in a non-initial position, then to passages with a fully implied main idea requiring synthesis across all details.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Identification task, Classification task, Synthesis task. Bloom alignment: Analyze — learners must analyze and synthesize multiple supporting details into a coherent main idea statement, going beyond simply locating a directly stated fact.

Mastery signal: The learner can correctly identify a passage's main idea (stated or implied) and correctly distinguish it from its supporting details, at 75% accuracy or better.

*Note:* Sample item shapes: Identification: identify the main idea of a 4-sentence paragraph with an implied main idea. | Classification: is 'Bee populations have declined' the main idea or a supporting detail? | Synthesis: write a main idea sentence synthesizing three given supporting details.

**Teacher notes**

The 'vivid detail mistaken for main idea' error is extremely common and deserves explicit, repeated attention — the umbrella diagram is the most effective visual for helping students feel the difference in scope between an encompassing main idea and a narrower supporting detail. Give real practice time to implied-main-idea passages, since these require the genuine synthesis skill that distinguishes this concept from literal-comprehension.

**Student notes**

The main idea is the big, overall point a passage is making. Supporting details are the specific facts and examples that back up that point. Watch out: the most interesting or memorable detail is NOT usually the main idea — it's just one piece of evidence supporting a broader point. Sometimes you have to figure out the main idea yourself by combining several details together (an implied main idea), since it isn't always stated directly.

### Inference in Reading

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.inference-in-reading` · Difficulty: proficient · Bloom level: analyze · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to draw logical, textually-supported inferences from information that is implied but not directly stated, and distinguish valid inferences from unsupported guesses.

Drawing logical conclusions from textual evidence that is implied but not directly stated.

INFERENCE IN READING is drawing logical conclusions from textual evidence that is implied but not directly stated — extending main-idea-and-details' synthesis skill into drawing genuinely NEW conclusions the text never explicitly states, but strongly and logically implies. An inference combines explicitly stated textual evidence with the reader's own background knowledge and logical reasoning to reach a conclusion the author intends but does not directly write ('The floor was covered in muddy footprints, and Maria's boots were soaked' implies it recently rained or she walked through mud, even though the text never states this directly). A VALID inference is always logically supported by specific textual evidence that can be cited, distinguishing it sharply from an unsupported GUESS, which has no clear textual grounding.

**Key ideas**

- An inference is a logical conclusion drawn from textual evidence that is implied but not directly stated, combining explicit text with the reader's background knowledge and reasoning
- A valid inference must be supported by specific, citable textual evidence — 'I think this because the text says...' is the essential justification structure that distinguishes inference from unsupported guessing
- Inference extends main-idea-and-details' synthesis skill (combining multiple stated facts) one step further: synthesizing stated facts to reach a NEW conclusion the text itself never explicitly states
- Multiple readers can draw a valid but slightly different inference from the same evidence, as long as each is logically supported by the text — inference is not a search for one single 'correct' unstated fact, but a disciplined, evidence-based reasoning process
- An invalid inference (or unsupported guess) either contradicts the text's actual evidence or relies on outside assumptions with no textual grounding at all — the key distinguishing test is always whether specific textual evidence can be cited to support the conclusion

**Vocabulary**

- **inference**
- **textual evidence**
- **implied meaning**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* An inference is just a guess, since the answer isn't directly stated in the text.
  *Correction:* A valid inference is fundamentally different from a guess — it is a logical conclusion built on specific, citable textual evidence combined with reasoning, not an arbitrary guess with no textual grounding; the key test is always 'what specific evidence in the text supports this conclusion?', and an answer that cannot cite such evidence is a guess, not a valid inference.
- *Misconception:* There is only one correct inference for any given piece of implied information, and any other conclusion is automatically wrong.
  *Correction:* Multiple readers can sometimes draw slightly different, equally valid inferences from the same textual evidence, as long as each is logically supported and doesn't contradict the text — inference is a disciplined reasoning process producing a well-supported conclusion, not a search for one single memorized 'correct' unstated fact.
- *Misconception:* Any conclusion a reader draws while reading, even one based on outside knowledge unrelated to the text's actual content, counts as a valid inference.
  *Correction:* A valid inference must be grounded in the TEXT's own specific evidence, even when combined with the reader's general background knowledge — a conclusion based purely on outside assumptions with no connection to what the text actually states or implies is not a valid inference, since it fails the citable-evidence test.
- *Misconception:* Inference is a completely separate skill from literal comprehension, unrelated to accurately understanding what the text directly states.
  *Correction:* Inference directly depends on accurate literal comprehension as its foundation — a reader must first correctly understand the text's explicitly stated evidence before they can validly reason beyond it; misreading the literal evidence (a literal-comprehension error) will produce a flawed inference, even if the reasoning process applied to that misread evidence is otherwise sound.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Treating inference as pure guesswork with no textual grounding required
- Overreaching beyond what the cited evidence actually supports
- Basing an 'inference' entirely on outside assumptions unconnected to the text's actual content

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- An evidence-plus-reasoning-equals-inference equation diagram showing two stated textual clues combining into one inferred conclusion
- A three-column comparison chart (stated fact / valid inference / invalid guess) using the same passage to show all three side by side
- A detective-magnifying-glass illustration showing a reader 'finding clues' in text before drawing a justified conclusion

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Given the sentence 'Maria slammed the door, threw her backpack on the floor, and didn't answer when her mother called her name,' draw a valid inference about Maria's emotional state, citing specific textual evidence, and explain why 'Maria hates her mother' would be an invalid inference from this same evidence.

1. Identify the stated textual evidence: 'slammed the door,' 'threw her backpack,' 'didn't answer when called.'
2. Combine this evidence with background knowledge about typical behavioral signals of emotion: these three actions together commonly signal frustration, anger, or upset feelings.
3. Draw the valid inference: Maria is likely upset or angry about something, citing all three pieces of behavioral evidence as support.
4. Evaluate 'Maria hates her mother' as a candidate inference: this claim goes far beyond what the cited evidence actually supports — slamming a door and not answering suggests immediate emotional upset, not a stable, lasting feeling of hatred toward her mother specifically.
5. Conclude: 'Maria is upset about something' is a valid, well-supported inference; 'Maria hates her mother' is an invalid, overreaching guess not adequately supported by this specific evidence.

*Outcome:* The learner draws a valid, textually-supported inference about Maria's emotional state and correctly explains, using the citable-evidence test, why the overreaching alternative claim is an invalid, unsupported guess rather than a valid inference.

**Real-world intuition**

- Inference skills are heavily tested on standardized reading comprehension assessments as a marker of higher-order reading ability beyond literal fact-finding
- Reading between the lines to infer an author's, character's, or speaker's unstated intentions or feelings is essential for literary analysis and social/emotional understanding in everyday communication
- Professional contexts (interpreting emails, reports, or contracts) frequently require valid, evidence-based inference about unstated implications, rather than relying only on explicitly stated content

**Practice progression**

Item types: Draw a valid inference from a short passage, citing specific supporting textual evidence; Distinguish a valid inference from an unsupported guess given a shared passage; Identify the specific textual evidence supporting a given inference; Evaluate a candidate inference for whether it overreaches beyond what the evidence actually supports. Suggested item count: 8.

Begin with passages containing strong, unambiguous implied evidence, progress to distinguishing valid inferences from overreaching or unsupported guesses, then to passages requiring inference from multiple combined, more subtly implied clues.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Inference task, Classification task, Evidence-citation task. Bloom alignment: Analyze — learners must analyze textual evidence and combine it with reasoning to draw a new, logically supported conclusion, going beyond both literal comprehension and simple main-idea synthesis.

Mastery signal: The learner can draw valid, textually-supported inferences and correctly distinguish them from unsupported guesses by citing specific evidence, at 75% accuracy or better.

*Note:* Sample item shapes: Inference task: what can you infer about the weather from 'Everyone arrived at the party in wet raincoats, shaking water off their umbrellas'? | Classification: is 'The character is nervous' a valid inference or an unsupported guess given a described scene? | Evidence citation: cite the specific text supporting a given inference.

**Teacher notes**

The valid-inference-versus-guess distinction is this concept's central, most testable point — drill the 'cite your evidence' discipline relentlessly, since it's the single skill that separates genuine inference from wild guessing. The three-column comparison chart (stated fact / valid inference / invalid guess) using one shared passage is the strongest single teaching tool for making this distinction concrete.

**Student notes**

Inference means figuring out something the text implies but never directly says — using clues from the text PLUS your own reasoning. The key rule: a valid inference must be backed up by specific evidence you can point to in the text. If you can't cite the evidence, it's just a guess, not a real inference.

### Summarizing

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.summarizing` · Difficulty: proficient · Bloom level: analyze · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 2h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to condense a text's essential content into a concise, accurate restatement in their own words, excluding minor details and personal opinion.

Condensing a text's essential content into a concise restatement in one's own words.

SUMMARIZING is condensing a text's essential content into a concise restatement in one's own words — directly applying main-idea-and-details' identification skill to the productive task of composing a new, shorter text. A good summary includes the main idea and the most essential supporting details, EXCLUDES minor details, examples, and repetition, and is written in the summarizer's OWN WORDS rather than copied phrases (avoiding plagiarism and demonstrating genuine understanding rather than mechanical copying). A summary is also strictly OBJECTIVE, reporting only what the original text says, without adding the summarizer's own opinions, interpretations, or evaluative commentary — a distinction that becomes especially important later when contrasted with critical-reading's evaluative stance.

**Key ideas**

- A summary condenses a text's essential content (main idea plus most essential supporting details, from main-idea-and-details) into a much shorter restatement
- A summary excludes minor details, illustrative examples, and repeated points, retaining only what is essential to understanding the text's core content
- A summary is written in the summarizer's OWN WORDS, not copied phrases from the original — genuine paraphrasing demonstrates real understanding and avoids plagiarism
- A summary is strictly OBJECTIVE: it reports only what the original text states, without adding the summarizer's own opinions, evaluations, or interpretations — a distinction developed further in contrast to critical-reading's deliberately evaluative stance
- Effective summarizing requires first correctly identifying the main idea and distinguishing it from supporting details (from main-idea-and-details) — an inaccurate main-idea identification will produce a flawed summary regardless of otherwise strong condensing and paraphrasing skill

**Vocabulary**

- **summary**
- **paraphrase**
- **objectivity**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* A summary should include the writer's own opinion about whether they agree with the text.
  *Correction:* A summary is strictly objective and reports only what the original text states — adding personal opinion, agreement, or disagreement crosses into a different genre (a response or review, or eventually critical-reading's deliberately evaluative analysis), not a summary, which must remain neutral and faithful to the original content alone.
- *Misconception:* A good summary copies the most important sentences directly from the original text.
  *Correction:* A genuine summary is written in the summarizer's OWN WORDS through paraphrasing, not copied directly from the original — copying phrases or sentences verbatim (even if they are objectively the 'most important' ones) constitutes plagiarism and also fails to demonstrate that the summarizer has actually understood and processed the content well enough to restate it independently.
- *Misconception:* A thorough summary should include as many details as possible to be complete and accurate.
  *Correction:* An effective summary deliberately EXCLUDES minor details, examples, and repetition, retaining only the main idea and most essential supporting details — a summary that tries to include everything is not actually condensing the text and fails at the core purpose of summarizing, which is significant, deliberate reduction while preserving essential meaning.
- *Misconception:* Summarizing is the same skill as identifying the main idea, just with extra writing involved.
  *Correction:* Summarizing requires main-idea identification as a necessary first step, but also requires the additional skills of selecting which supporting details are essential enough to include, condensing content significantly, and paraphrasing accurately in one's own words — these are genuinely additional productive skills beyond simply identifying the main idea receptively.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Adding personal opinion or evaluation to a summary
- Copying phrases or sentences verbatim instead of paraphrasing
- Including too many minor details instead of condensing to the essentials

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A funnel diagram showing a full text narrowing down through 'remove minor details' and 'paraphrase' stages into a concise summary
- A side-by-side comparison of an acceptable paraphrased summary sentence and an unacceptable copied-verbatim sentence from the same source text
- An objectivity checklist card showing 'reports what the text says' (correct) versus 'adds the writer's own opinion' (incorrect, belongs in a different genre)

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Summarize the paragraph 'The Amazon rainforest produces about 20% of the world's oxygen and is home to an estimated 10% of all known species on Earth. Despite its ecological importance, deforestation has destroyed roughly 17% of the Amazon over the past 50 years, primarily due to cattle ranching and agriculture. Scientists warn that continued deforestation could push the rainforest past an irreversible tipping point,' in one or two sentences.

1. Identify the main idea: the Amazon's ecological importance is threatened by ongoing deforestation, which scientists warn could reach an irreversible tipping point.
2. Identify the most essential supporting details: the deforestation cause (cattle ranching/agriculture) and the scale (17% over 50 years) are essential; the specific oxygen and species percentages are useful context but could be condensed or selectively included.
3. Paraphrase in original wording rather than copying: avoid reusing the exact phrases 'irreversible tipping point' or '17% over the past 50 years' verbatim where possible, restating the core content independently.
4. Exclude minor elaboration and maintain strict objectivity, with no added opinion about whether this is 'terrible' or 'concerning' (an evaluative stance that would belong in a different genre).
5. Draft summary: 'The ecologically vital Amazon rainforest has lost a significant portion of its area to deforestation from farming and ranching, and scientists warn this trend may soon become irreversible.'

*Outcome:* The learner produces a concise, accurate, objectively-worded summary in their own paraphrased language, correctly identifying and retaining the main idea and most essential details while excluding minor elaboration.

**Real-world intuition**

- Summarizing is an essential academic skill for research, literature reviews, and citing sources accurately without plagiarizing
- Professional contexts (executive summaries, meeting notes, briefing documents) depend heavily on the ability to condense lengthy information into essential, objective summaries
- Effective summarizing supports genuine reading comprehension retention, since the act of condensing and paraphrasing requires and reinforces real understanding

**Practice progression**

Item types: Write a one-to-two-sentence summary of a short passage; Identify and remove minor details from an overly long draft summary; Revise a summary that copies phrases verbatim into properly paraphrased language; Identify and remove inappropriate opinion or evaluation from a draft summary. Suggested item count: 8.

Begin with short, single-paragraph summarizing practice, progress to identifying and correcting verbatim-copying and opinion-inclusion errors in draft summaries, then to summarizing longer, multi-paragraph passages requiring more selective detail inclusion.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Summary-writing task, Error correction, Evaluation task. Bloom alignment: Analyze — learners must analyze a text to distinguish essential from nonessential content and synthesize that analysis into an accurate, objective, independently-worded summary.

Mastery signal: The learner can write a concise, accurate, objectively-worded summary in their own words, correctly excluding minor details and personal opinion, at 75% accuracy or better.

*Note:* Sample item shapes: Summary writing: summarize a given 4-sentence paragraph in one sentence. | Error correction: revise a summary that copies phrases verbatim from the source. | Evaluation: identify and remove an inappropriate opinion statement from a draft summary.

**Teacher notes**

The objectivity point deserves particular emphasis, since it sets up the deliberate contrast with critical-reading's evaluative stance much later in the domain — flag this forward connection explicitly. The verbatim-copying-versus-paraphrasing distinction connects directly to plagiarism awareness and is worth treating as seriously as an academic integrity issue, not just a stylistic preference.

**Student notes**

A summary condenses a text down to its main idea and most essential details, in YOUR OWN WORDS (not copied), and with NO added opinion — just an objective, shorter restatement of what the text actually says. Cut minor details, examples, and repetition; keep only what's essential.

### Predicting and Confirming

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.predicting-and-confirming` · Difficulty: developing · Bloom level: apply · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 1h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to form reasonable, evidence-based predictions about upcoming text content and revise those predictions as new information is read.

Forming reasonable predictions about upcoming text content and revising them as new information is read.

PREDICTING AND CONFIRMING is forming reasonable predictions about upcoming text content and revising them as new information is read — applying inference-in-reading's evidence-based reasoning forward in time, toward content not yet encountered, rather than backward toward already-read implied information. A reader uses titles, headings, illustrations, and content read so far to predict what will happen or be discussed next, then actively confirms, revises, or abandons that prediction as they continue reading and gather new evidence. This active, ongoing prediction-confirmation cycle is a hallmark of engaged, strategic reading, keeping the reader actively processing and anticipating meaning rather than passively decoding words in sequence.

**Key ideas**

- Predicting means forming a reasonable, evidence-based expectation about upcoming text content, using titles, headings, illustrations, and content already read — directly extending inference-in-reading's evidence-based reasoning forward in time
- Confirming means actively checking a prediction against new information as reading continues, and revising or abandoning the prediction if the new evidence contradicts it
- A good prediction, like a good inference, must be reasonably supported by available evidence (not a random guess) — but predictions are explicitly provisional and expected to be revised as more information becomes available, unlike a finalized inference
- This ongoing predict-read-confirm-revise cycle is a hallmark of active, strategic, engaged reading, keeping the reader continuously processing and anticipating meaning rather than passively decoding words in sequence
- Predicting incorrectly is not a failure — the VALUE of predicting lies in the active engagement and purposeful reading it generates, and the process of confirming or revising a prediction itself deepens comprehension, regardless of whether the original prediction turns out to be accurate

**Vocabulary**

- **prediction**
- **confirming**
- **revising a prediction**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* A prediction that turns out to be wrong means the reader failed at this skill.
  *Correction:* The value of predicting lies in the active engagement and purposeful reading it generates, not in guessing correctly — a reasonable, evidence-based prediction that later proves incorrect still demonstrates strong strategic reading, and the process of noticing the mismatch and revising the prediction actively deepens comprehension, which is the real instructional goal.
- *Misconception:* Predicting is the same skill as inference, just using different terminology.
  *Correction:* Predicting and inference both use evidence-based reasoning, but predicting specifically looks FORWARD to not-yet-read content and is explicitly provisional (expected to be revised), while inference (from inference-in-reading) draws conclusions about content already read that is implied but not stated — related skills applying the same underlying evidence-based reasoning process in different directions.
- *Misconception:* Once a prediction is made, a reader should stick with it regardless of what the text later reveals.
  *Correction:* Active, effective prediction explicitly requires ongoing CONFIRMATION and revision as new evidence emerges — a reader who forms a prediction and then ignores contradicting evidence in the text is not engaging in the full predicting-and-confirming cycle, only the first half of it, missing the equally important revision step.
- *Misconception:* Predictions should be based purely on imagination or personal preference for how the reader wants the text to unfold.
  *Correction:* Valid predictions, like valid inferences, must be reasonably grounded in available textual evidence (titles, headings, content read so far) plus genre and background knowledge — a prediction based purely on wishful thinking with no textual grounding is not a strategic reading skill, even if it happens to be entertaining or creative.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Treating an incorrect prediction as a failure rather than a normal part of active reading
- Forming predictions with no textual evidence grounding them
- Sticking rigidly to an initial prediction despite contradicting new evidence

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A cyclical diagram showing predict → read → confirm/revise → predict again, looping continuously through a text
- A prediction-evidence table with columns for 'clue used,' 'prediction made,' and 'confirmed/revised' filled in across several points in a sample text
- A title-and-illustration-based prediction example showing how cover art and headings generate a reasonable pre-reading prediction

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Given the title 'The Storm That Almost Sank the Ship' and the opening line 'Captain Reyes checked the barometer nervously as dark clouds gathered on the horizon,' form a reasonable prediction about the story's upcoming events, then explain how you would confirm or revise that prediction as you continue reading.

1. Use the title as evidence: 'The Storm That Almost Sank the Ship' strongly suggests a dangerous storm will occur and threaten the ship, though 'almost sank' implies the ship ultimately survives.
2. Use the opening line as additional evidence: the captain's nervousness and the gathering dark clouds support and reinforce the title's storm-danger prediction.
3. Form the prediction: the story will describe an intense, dangerous storm that seriously threatens the ship, but the crew will ultimately survive, based on the title's phrasing.
4. Describe the confirmation process: as the story continues, check whether the storm does intensify as predicted, and whether the ship indeed survives — if the story instead reveals a different central conflict (e.g., a mutiny, not a storm), the reader should revise the prediction to match this new evidence.
5. Note the value regardless of accuracy: even if the prediction turns out to be partially wrong (perhaps the ship doesn't survive, subverting the 'almost' framing), the act of predicting kept the reader actively anticipating and engaged with the unfolding plot.

*Outcome:* The learner forms a reasonable, evidence-based prediction using the title and opening line, and correctly describes an active confirmation process for checking and potentially revising that prediction as the story unfolds.

**Real-world intuition**

- Predicting and confirming is a widely taught active-reading strategy used by educators to increase student engagement and comprehension monitoring during independent reading
- Forming and revising predictions based on partial information is a transferable critical-thinking skill applicable to scientific hypothesis testing and everyday problem-solving
- Engaged, prediction-driven reading supports better retention and comprehension than passive, purely sequential reading

**Practice progression**

Item types: Form a prediction about upcoming text content using a title, heading, or opening lines as evidence; Confirm or revise a prediction based on new information provided partway through a passage; Explain the evidence used to justify a given prediction; Distinguish a reasonable, evidence-based prediction from an unsupported guess about future content. Suggested item count: 6.

Begin with prediction practice using clear title/heading evidence, progress to confirming or revising predictions as new text information is introduced, then to forming and tracking predictions across a longer, multi-part passage.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Prediction task, Confirmation/revision task, Short answer. Bloom alignment: Apply — learners must apply evidence-based reasoning to form reasonable predictions and actively confirm or revise them as new textual information is introduced.

Mastery signal: The learner can form reasonable, evidence-based predictions and correctly confirm or revise them based on new textual evidence, at 75% accuracy or better.

*Note:* Sample item shapes: Prediction: predict what will happen next given a story's title and opening paragraph. | Confirmation/revision: given new evidence partway through a passage, confirm or revise an earlier prediction. | Short answer: explain what evidence supported your original prediction.

**Teacher notes**

The 'wrong predictions are not failures' reframing is essential and should be stated explicitly and early, since students often feel anxious about 'guessing wrong' — normalize revision as the actual point of the exercise. The cyclical predict-read-confirm-revise diagram is the strongest tool for showing this as an ongoing process throughout reading, not a one-time pre-reading activity.

**Student notes**

Predicting means making a reasonable guess about what's coming next in a text, based on real evidence (titles, headings, what you've read so far) — not just imagination. As you keep reading, check your prediction: does the new evidence confirm it, or should you revise it? Being 'wrong' isn't a failure — the active thinking involved is what actually helps you understand the text better.

### Text Structure

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.text-structure` · Difficulty: proficient · Bloom level: analyze · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to identify a text's organizational pattern (narrative sequence, cause-effect, compare-contrast, problem-solution, or description) and explain how recognizing that structure supports comprehension.

Recognizing organizational patterns in text, including narrative sequence, cause-effect, compare-contrast, problem-solution, and description.

TEXT STRUCTURE is recognizing organizational patterns in text, extending main-idea-and-details' analytical synthesis into recognizing the underlying ARCHITECTURE that organizes an entire text, not just its content. Five common structural patterns recur across nonfiction and informational text: NARRATIVE SEQUENCE (chronological order of events); CAUSE-EFFECT (showing how one event leads to another); COMPARE-CONTRAST (examining similarities and differences between two or more things); PROBLEM-SOLUTION (presenting a problem followed by one or more proposed solutions); and DESCRIPTION (organizing details around a single topic's characteristics, without a strong sequential or logical progression). Recognizing which structure a text uses helps a reader anticipate what kind of information is coming and organize their own understanding accordingly — a skill directly transferable to writing's own paragraph-organization instruction.

**Key ideas**

- Narrative sequence structure organizes content chronologically, in the order events occurred, typically signaled by time-order transition words (first, then, next, finally)
- Cause-effect structure shows how one event or condition leads to another, typically signaled by causal transition words (because, therefore, as a result, consequently — connecting to eng.grammar's conjunction and connective-word instruction)
- Compare-contrast structure examines similarities and differences between two or more things, typically signaled by comparison transition words (similarly, however, in contrast, likewise)
- Problem-solution structure presents a problem followed by one or more proposed solutions, typically signaled by words like 'issue,' 'challenge,' 'solution,' or 'proposed'
- Description structure organizes details around a single topic's characteristics without a strong sequential or logical progression, often used for general informational or encyclopedic content

**Vocabulary**

- **narrative sequence**
- **cause-effect**
- **compare-contrast**
- **problem-solution**
- **description**
- **signal words**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Every text uses exactly one, single, clearly identifiable structural pattern throughout.
  *Correction:* While one structure often dominates a given passage, longer or more complex texts frequently combine multiple structures — a text might use cause-effect structure within an overall problem-solution framework, or narrative sequence within a larger compare-contrast piece — recognizing a dominant structure doesn't mean every sentence follows only that single pattern.
- *Misconception:* Text structure only applies to nonfiction, informational writing, not narrative fiction.
  *Correction:* While narrative sequence structure is the most obviously fiction-associated pattern, the other structures (cause-effect, compare-contrast, problem-solution, description) can also appear within fiction — a novel's plot might follow cause-effect logic, or a character description passage might use pure description structure, showing these patterns apply broadly across genres, not exclusively to nonfiction.
- *Misconception:* Identifying text structure is a purely academic exercise disconnected from actual reading comprehension.
  *Correction:* Recognizing a text's structure genuinely supports comprehension in practice — a reader who identifies compare-contrast structure knows to actively track similarities and differences as they read, and a reader who identifies problem-solution structure knows to look for a proposed solution following the stated problem, making structural awareness a practical comprehension strategy, not merely an academic label.
- *Misconception:* Transition words are the only reliable signal of a text's structure, and structure cannot be identified without them.
  *Correction:* While transition words (because, however, first) are strong, reliable signals of text structure, skilled readers can also identify structure through the logical relationship between ideas even when explicit transition words are sparse or absent — relying solely on transition-word-spotting without considering the underlying logical relationships can miss a text's actual organizational pattern.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Assuming every text uses exactly one structure with no combination
- Assuming text structure only applies to nonfiction
- Relying solely on transition words while missing the underlying logical relationship

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A five-pattern reference chart (narrative sequence/cause-effect/compare-contrast/problem-solution/description) with a graphic organizer shape and signal words for each
- A cause-effect chain diagram and a compare-contrast Venn diagram shown side by side as structure-specific graphic organizers
- A mixed-structure passage example showing a dominant problem-solution frame containing an embedded cause-effect explanation

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Identify the text structure of the passage 'Urban traffic congestion costs cities billions of dollars annually in lost productivity. As a result, many cities have introduced congestion pricing, charging drivers a fee to enter crowded downtown areas during peak hours. Early data from cities that adopted this policy show a 20% reduction in downtown traffic,' and identify the signal words supporting your answer.

1. Identify the opening content: a stated problem (urban traffic congestion causing significant economic cost).
2. Identify the signal word 'As a result,' indicating a causal or solution-oriented relationship connecting the problem to what follows.
3. Identify the following content: a proposed solution (congestion pricing) and evidence of its effectiveness (20% reduction in traffic).
4. Classify the overall structure: PROBLEM-SOLUTION, since the passage presents a clear problem followed by a proposed solution and supporting evidence of its impact.
5. Note the secondary cause-effect relationship embedded within: 'As a result' also signals a cause-effect link between adopting the policy and the traffic reduction, showing structures can combine within one passage.

*Outcome:* The learner correctly identifies the dominant problem-solution structure, cites the specific signal words supporting this classification, and recognizes the secondary embedded cause-effect relationship within the passage.

**Real-world intuition**

- Recognizing text structure supports more efficient, strategic reading of textbooks and professional documents, since readers can anticipate what kind of information is coming
- Text structure awareness directly informs effective writing instruction (eng.writing), since writers deliberately choose an organizational pattern to match their communicative purpose
- Standardized reading assessments frequently test text-structure identification as a marker of sophisticated reading comprehension beyond literal fact-finding

### Genre Recognition

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.genre-recognition` · Difficulty: proficient · Bloom level: understand · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 2h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to identify a text's genre and explain the conventions typically associated with that genre.

Identifying a text's genre (fiction, nonfiction, poetry, drama, and their subtypes) and the conventions associated with it.

GENRE RECOGNITION is identifying a text's genre (fiction, nonfiction, poetry, drama, and their subtypes) and the conventions associated with it — extending text-structure's organizational-pattern awareness to the broader, genre-level conventions that shape an entire work. FICTION includes subtypes like realistic fiction, fantasy, mystery, and science fiction, each carrying distinct conventions (a mystery typically withholds information for the reader to piece together; fantasy typically includes invented worlds or magic). NONFICTION includes subtypes like biography, informational text, and persuasive essay, generally organized using the structural patterns from text-structure. POETRY and DRAMA carry their own distinct formal conventions (line breaks and figurative language for poetry; dialogue and stage directions for drama). Recognizing a text's genre helps a reader form appropriate expectations and apply the right comprehension strategies for that genre's conventions.

**Key ideas**

- Genre recognition identifies whether a text is fiction, nonfiction, poetry, or drama, and further classifies it into more specific subtypes within each broad category
- Fiction subtypes (realistic fiction, fantasy, mystery, science fiction, historical fiction) each carry distinct conventions that shape reader expectations — a mystery withholds key information for gradual discovery, fantasy typically includes invented worlds or magical elements
- Nonfiction subtypes (biography, informational text, persuasive essay, memoir) typically organize their content using the structural patterns established at text-structure
- Poetry carries distinctive formal conventions including line breaks, stanza structure, rhythm, and concentrated figurative language, differing sharply from prose's paragraph-based organization
- Drama carries its own distinctive conventions — dialogue-driven text, stage directions, and act/scene structure — designed for performance rather than silent individual reading, differing from both prose fiction and poetry

**Vocabulary**

- **genre**
- **fiction**
- **nonfiction**
- **poetry**
- **drama**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Genre is simply a label with no practical effect on how a text should be read or understood.
  *Correction:* Recognizing genre genuinely shapes appropriate reading strategy and expectations — a reader who knows they're reading a mystery should expect withheld information requiring inference (connecting to inference-in-reading), while a reader of an informational text should expect one of the structural patterns from text-structure; genre recognition is a practical comprehension tool, not merely a classification label.
- *Misconception:* Fiction and nonfiction are the only two genre categories that matter, with poetry and drama as minor exceptions.
  *Correction:* Poetry and drama are major, fully distinct genre categories with their own substantial formal conventions (line breaks and figurative concentration for poetry; dialogue and stage directions for drama) — reducing genre awareness to only a fiction/nonfiction binary misses conventions essential for correctly reading and interpreting these two other major genres.
- *Misconception:* A text's genre can always be determined from its subject matter alone.
  *Correction:* Genre is determined by FORMAL conventions and structural choices (how the content is presented), not subject matter alone — a story about a real historical event could be presented as nonfiction (biography, informational text) or as historical fiction (a fictionalized narrative), with the same subject matter handled through genuinely different genre conventions depending on the author's formal choices.
- *Misconception:* All fiction subtypes (mystery, fantasy, science fiction, realistic fiction) share identical conventions, differing only in setting or subject matter.
  *Correction:* Fiction subtypes carry genuinely distinct structural and comprehension-relevant conventions beyond just differing settings — a mystery's convention of deliberately withholding information for the reader to infer differs functionally from fantasy's convention of establishing invented-world rules, meaning different subtypes call for somewhat different reading strategies and expectations.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Determining genre from subject matter alone rather than formal conventions
- Reducing genre awareness to just fiction versus nonfiction, overlooking poetry and drama
- Assuming all fiction subtypes share identical reading conventions

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A four-branch genre tree (fiction/nonfiction/poetry/drama) with common subtypes listed under each branch
- A genre-convention matching exercise pairing genre labels with their distinctive formal features (line breaks, stage directions, withheld information, chronological structure)
- A same-subject-different-genre comparison showing how one historical event could be presented as biography, historical fiction, or a narrative poem

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Identify the genre and subtype of a text excerpt containing dialogue formatted with character names followed by colons, stage direction notes in italics describing character movement, and organized into 'Act 1, Scene 2,' and explain which conventions support this classification.

1. Identify the formal features: character-name-plus-colon dialogue formatting, italicized stage directions describing movement, and Act/Scene organizational labels.
2. Match these features against genre conventions: this dialogue-and-stage-direction formatting, combined with Act/Scene structure, is a defining convention specifically of DRAMA, not prose fiction or poetry.
3. Classify the genre: drama, further identifiable as a script intended for stage performance based on the stage-direction convention.
4. Explain the supporting evidence: prose fiction would present dialogue within paragraph-based narration (with quotation marks and dialogue tags like 'she said'), while poetry would use line breaks and stanzas — neither matches the observed Act/Scene-plus-stage-direction structure, confirming drama as the correct classification.

*Outcome:* The learner correctly identifies the excerpt as drama, citing the specific formal conventions (dialogue formatting, stage directions, Act/Scene structure) that distinguish it from prose fiction or poetry.

**Real-world intuition**

- Genre recognition helps readers select appropriate books and texts matching their interests and reading goals across libraries, bookstores, and digital platforms
- Understanding genre conventions is foundational for literary analysis and study, directly connecting to and preparing for the Literature Fundamentals domain
- Writers deliberately choose and adapt genre conventions for their intended effect, making genre awareness essential background for eng.writing and eng.composition

**Practice progression**

Item types: Identify the genre of a text excerpt based on its formal conventions; Match a genre or subtype to its distinctive conventions; Explain how recognizing a text's genre shapes appropriate reading expectations and strategy; Distinguish two texts on the same subject matter presented in different genres. Suggested item count: 6.

Begin with texts featuring clear, unambiguous genre-defining formal features, progress to matching subtypes to their specific conventions, then to same-subject-different-genre comparisons requiring attention to form rather than content alone.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Classification task, Matching task, Short answer. Bloom alignment: Understand — learners must comprehend the distinguishing formal conventions of each major genre and subtype, correctly applying that understanding to classify unfamiliar text excerpts.

Mastery signal: The learner can correctly identify a text's genre and subtype based on its formal conventions and explain how genre recognition shapes reading strategy, at 75% accuracy or better.

*Note:* Sample item shapes: Classification: identify the genre of an excerpt with clear stanza and line-break structure. | Matching: match 'withheld information requiring inference' to its associated fiction subtype. | Short answer: explain why the same historical event could be presented as either biography or historical fiction.

**Teacher notes**

The same-subject-different-genre comparison is the strongest tool for breaking the misconception that genre is determined by subject matter rather than form — use a concrete example (like one historical event told three different ways) to make this vivid. This concept directly previews and connects to the Literature Fundamentals domain, so flag that forward connection for students.

**Student notes**

Genre isn't just about WHAT a text is about — it's about HOW it's built. Fiction, nonfiction, poetry, and drama each have their own typical conventions (stage directions for drama, line breaks for poetry, chronological or structural organization for nonfiction), and recognizing which genre you're reading helps you know what to expect and how to read it well.

### Author's Purpose and Tone

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.authors-purpose-and-tone` · Difficulty: proficient · Bloom level: analyze · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 2h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to identify an author's purpose (to inform, persuade, or entertain) and describe the tone conveyed through word choice.

Identifying why an author wrote a text (to inform, persuade, entertain) and the attitude conveyed through word choice.

AUTHOR'S PURPOSE AND TONE involves identifying why an author wrote a text (to inform, persuade, or entertain) and the attitude conveyed through word choice — applying inference-in-reading's evidence-based reasoning specifically to the author's underlying intentions and emotional stance, rather than to plot or factual content. AUTHOR'S PURPOSE is most commonly categorized as to INFORM (present factual information neutrally), to PERSUADE (convince the reader of a viewpoint, often using connotation-loaded language from eng.vocab.connotation-denotation), or to ENTERTAIN (engage or amuse through story, humor, or vivid narrative). TONE is the author's attitude toward the subject, conveyed primarily through word choice (connotation), sentence structure, and selective detail — distinct from MOOD, which is the feeling created in the reader, though the two are often related.

**Key ideas**

- Author's purpose is most commonly categorized as to inform (present facts neutrally), to persuade (convince the reader of a viewpoint), or to entertain (engage through story or humor) — some texts blend purposes, but one is often dominant
- Tone is the author's attitude toward the subject, conveyed primarily through word choice (directly connecting to eng.vocab.connotation-denotation's positive/negative/neutral connotation spectrum), sentence structure, and selective detail
- Persuasive texts often reveal their purpose through consistently loaded, connotation-heavy language favoring one side of an issue, distinguishing them from informational texts' more neutral, connotation-balanced word choice
- Tone (the author's attitude) is distinct from mood (the feeling created in the reader) — though a somber tone often produces a somber mood, an author's tone and a reader's resulting mood are not strictly identical concepts
- Identifying purpose and tone requires the same evidence-citing discipline as inference-in-reading: specific word choices, structural patterns, or selective details must be cited as evidence, not simply an unsupported overall impression

**Vocabulary**

- **author's purpose**
- **tone**
- **mood**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Every text has exactly one single, pure purpose (only to inform, only to persuade, or only to entertain), with no blending.
  *Correction:* While one purpose is often dominant, many texts genuinely blend purposes — a persuasive essay might use entertaining anecdotes to engage readers, or an informational text might be written with an engaging, story-like tone — identifying the DOMINANT purpose while acknowledging blending is more accurate than forcing every text into one pure category.
- *Misconception:* Tone and mood are simply two different names for the same thing.
  *Correction:* Tone is the AUTHOR's attitude toward the subject (conveyed through their word choices), while mood is the FEELING created in the reader — these are related but distinct: an author's consistently somber tone typically produces a somber mood in the reader, but the terms describe two different sides of the same communicative relationship, not one identical concept.
- *Misconception:* Identifying tone is a matter of personal, subjective impression with no objective textual evidence involved.
  *Correction:* Identifying tone, like drawing any inference, requires citing specific textual evidence — particular word choices (connotation-loaded language), sentence structures, or selectively included/excluded details — as support; an unsupported claim about tone ('it just felt sad to me') without citing specific evidence fails the same evidence-based standard established at inference-in-reading.
- *Misconception:* Persuasive purpose is always obvious and easy to identify, since persuasive texts openly state their argument.
  *Correction:* Persuasive purpose is not always announced directly — some persuasive texts adopt a seemingly neutral, informational tone while still using subtly loaded connotation and selective detail to favor one viewpoint, making careful attention to word choice (not just explicit argument statements) essential for correctly identifying a text's true persuasive purpose, directly connecting to the more advanced evaluative skills developed at critical-reading.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Forcing every text into one pure purpose category, ignoring legitimate blending
- Confusing tone (author's attitude) with mood (reader's feeling)
- Identifying tone without citing specific supporting word-choice evidence

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A three-purpose comparison chart (inform/persuade/entertain) with a sample opening line for each demonstrating the purpose
- A tone-word bank organized by category (somber, optimistic, sarcastic, urgent, nostalgic) with connecting connotation-loaded example phrases
- A tone-versus-mood diagram showing an arrow from 'author's word choices (tone)' to 'reader's felt response (mood)'

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Identify the purpose and tone of the passage 'Our city's crumbling infrastructure isn't just an inconvenience — it's a slow-motion crisis that threatens every family's safety. While officials squabble over budgets, our children cross dangerously deteriorating bridges every single day,' citing specific evidence.

1. Identify the purpose: the passage aims to convince readers that infrastructure is a serious, urgent problem requiring action — this is PERSUASIVE purpose, not neutral informing or entertaining.
2. Cite evidence for the persuasive purpose: loaded phrases like 'slow-motion crisis' and 'squabble' (rather than neutral 'disagree') reveal a deliberate rhetorical stance, not neutral fact reporting.
3. Identify the tone: urgent and alarmed, verging on critical of officials.
4. Cite evidence for the tone: word choices like 'crumbling,' 'crisis,' 'threatens,' and 'dangerously deteriorating' are all strongly negative-connotation words (connecting to eng.vocab.connotation-denotation) chosen to convey urgency and alarm rather than neutral description.
5. Confirm the connection between purpose and tone: the urgent, alarmed tone directly serves the persuasive purpose, making the infrastructure problem feel more pressing to the reader.

*Outcome:* The learner correctly identifies persuasive purpose and an urgent/alarmed tone, citing specific connotation-loaded word choices as evidence for both, rather than offering an unsupported general impression.

**Real-world intuition**

- Identifying author's purpose and tone is essential for media literacy, helping readers recognize persuasive intent in news, advertising, and political writing
- Understanding tone through word-choice analysis directly supports literary analysis and character/narrator interpretation in fiction and poetry
- Professional writing (marketing, technical documentation, journalism) is deliberately crafted with a specific purpose and tone in mind, making this analytical skill valuable for both reading and writing contexts

**Practice progression**

Item types: Identify a text's dominant purpose (inform/persuade/entertain) with supporting evidence; Identify a text's tone using specific word-choice evidence; Distinguish tone from mood in a given passage; Identify subtly persuasive language in a text that presents itself as purely informational. Suggested item count: 8.

Begin with clearly single-purpose, strongly toned passages, progress to distinguishing tone from mood, then to identifying subtle, less obviously announced persuasive purpose within seemingly neutral text.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Identification task, Evidence-citation task, Short answer. Bloom alignment: Analyze — learners must analyze word choice, structure, and selective detail as evidence for an author's purpose and tone, going beyond an unsupported general impression.

Mastery signal: The learner can correctly identify a text's dominant purpose and tone, citing specific supporting textual evidence, at 75% accuracy or better.

*Note:* Sample item shapes: Identification: identify the purpose of a passage that uses loaded language to argue for a policy change. | Evidence citation: cite three word choices supporting a nostalgic tone. | Short answer: explain the difference between the author's tone and the mood the passage creates in you.

**Teacher notes**

The tone-versus-mood distinction is subtle and frequently conflated even by capable readers; the arrow diagram (author's choices → reader's feeling) is the clearest way to separate them.

**Student notes**

Tone is the author's ATTITUDE toward the subject, shown through word choice — not the same as mood, which is how the text makes YOU feel. Always back up your answer with specific word choices as evidence, just like with inference.

### Comparing and Contrasting Texts

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.compare-and-contrast-texts` · Difficulty: advanced · Bloom level: analyze · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 2h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to analyze similarities and differences between two or more texts on a shared topic, theme, or form.

Analyzing similarities and differences between two or more texts on a shared topic, theme, or form.

COMPARING AND CONTRASTING TEXTS is analyzing similarities and differences between two or more texts on a shared topic, theme, or form — synthesizing text-structure's compare-contrast organizational pattern and summarizing's condensing skill into a genuinely comparative, multi-text analytical task. Unlike comparing ideas WITHIN one text, this concept requires holding two or more separately summarized and understood texts in mind simultaneously, then systematically analyzing where they align and diverge — in content, in perspective or tone (from authors-purpose-and-tone), in structural approach (from text-structure), or in genre (from genre-recognition). This multi-text synthesis is a genuinely more demanding task than any single-text comprehension skill developed earlier in the domain, since it requires accurate, independent comprehension of each text before any valid comparison can begin.

**Key ideas**

- Comparing and contrasting texts requires first independently and accurately comprehending each text (applying literal comprehension, main idea, and summarizing skills to each one separately) before any valid cross-text comparison can begin
- Texts can be compared and contrasted along multiple dimensions: content (what facts or claims each presents), perspective or tone (from authors-purpose-and-tone), structural approach (from text-structure), or genre (from genre-recognition)
- A systematic comparison typically uses a graphic organizer (such as a Venn diagram or comparison table) to track similarities and differences dimension by dimension, rather than relying on a vague overall impression
- Two texts on the same topic can present significantly different or even contradictory information, perspectives, or emphases — recognizing and analyzing this divergence is often the most valuable and interesting outcome of the comparison, directly previewing critical-reading's evaluative stance
- This is a genuinely more demanding synthesis task than any single-text skill developed earlier, since it requires holding multiple independently comprehended texts in mind simultaneously while systematically tracking their relationships

**Vocabulary**

- **compare and contrast (multi-text)**
- **perspective**
- **graphic organizer**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Comparing two texts just means noting that they're both 'about the same thing.'
  *Correction:* A meaningful comparison analyzes similarities and differences across multiple specific dimensions — content, perspective/tone, structure, and genre — not simply noting a shared general topic; two texts 'about the same thing' can differ dramatically in every one of these dimensions, and identifying exactly where and how is the actual analytical work of this skill.
- *Misconception:* Comparing texts is simply comparing two separately memorized summaries side by side with no additional analytical step.
  *Correction:* While accurate independent comprehension (including summarizing) of each text is a necessary first step, genuine comparison requires an additional analytical step: systematically identifying specific points of alignment and divergence across dimensions like content, tone, and structure — simply placing two summaries next to each other without this systematic cross-analysis misses the actual comparative work.
- *Misconception:* When two texts on the same topic present different information or perspectives, one of them must simply be wrong.
  *Correction:* Two texts can present genuinely different but each internally valid information, emphases, or perspectives on the same topic — due to different purposes (from authors-purpose-and-tone), different evidence considered, or different authorial viewpoints — recognizing and analyzing this divergence thoughtfully, rather than assuming one text is simply incorrect, is often the most valuable outcome of a text comparison and previews the more advanced evaluative skills developed at critical-reading.
- *Misconception:* A graphic organizer like a Venn diagram is optional, decorative scaffolding not essential to genuine comparative analysis.
  *Correction:* Systematic use of a graphic organizer (Venn diagram, comparison table) is a genuinely valuable analytical tool, not mere decoration — it structures the comparison dimension by dimension, helping ensure the analysis is systematic and comprehensive rather than a vague, unsupported overall impression of similarity or difference.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Comparing texts only at the level of 'same topic' without analyzing specific dimensions
- Assuming disagreement between two texts means one is simply incorrect
- Skipping accurate independent comprehension of each text before attempting comparison

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A Venn diagram template with 'Text A only,' 'Both texts,' and 'Text B only' regions, pre-labeled with dimension categories (content, tone, structure, genre)
- A side-by-side comparison table with rows for each analytical dimension and columns for each text being compared
- A 'same topic, different perspective' example showing two brief excerpts on an identical topic with contrasting tones highlighted

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Compare and contrast two short texts on the same topic (a new city bike-lane policy): Text A, a city government press release stating 'The new bike lanes will improve safety and reduce traffic congestion for all residents,' and Text B, a local business owner's op-ed stating 'These bike lanes have devastated foot traffic to my store and were pushed through with no community input.'

1. Independently comprehend each text: Text A is an informational/persuasive government announcement emphasizing safety and congestion benefits; Text B is a persuasive personal op-ed emphasizing economic harm and lack of process.
2. Compare content: both texts address the same bike-lane policy, but Text A emphasizes safety/traffic benefits while Text B emphasizes economic harm and procedural concerns — genuinely different emphases, not necessarily contradictory facts.
3. Compare purpose and tone (from authors-purpose-and-tone): Text A's tone is optimistic and promotional; Text B's tone is frustrated and critical — both are persuasive in purpose, but arguing opposite conclusions.
4. Compare perspective: Text A represents an institutional, city-wide view; Text B represents an individual, directly affected business owner's view — different vantage points on the same policy.
5. Conclude: rather than assuming one text is simply 'wrong,' the comparison reveals that the same policy genuinely affects different stakeholders differently, and both texts' claims may be individually valid from their respective perspectives while still conflicting.

*Outcome:* The learner systematically compares both texts across content, tone, and perspective dimensions, and correctly avoids assuming one text is simply incorrect, instead recognizing the divergence as reflecting genuinely different stakeholder perspectives.

**Real-world intuition**

- Comparing multiple news sources on the same event is an essential media literacy skill for developing a well-rounded, critically informed understanding of current events
- Academic research and literature reviews require systematically comparing multiple sources' findings, methods, and perspectives on a shared topic
- Comparative analysis across texts is a core skill in literary study, comparing multiple works' treatment of a shared theme, character type, or historical period

**Practice progression**

Item types: Compare two texts on a shared topic using a Venn diagram or comparison table across multiple dimensions; Identify specific similarities and differences in content, tone, structure, or genre between two texts; Explain why two texts on the same topic present differing information or perspectives without assuming one is simply wrong; Synthesize a comparison analysis into a short written comparative statement. Suggested item count: 6.

**Assessment objectives**

Mastery signal: The learner can systematically compare two texts across content, tone, structure, and perspective, and explain the significance of identified similarities and differences, at 75% accuracy or better.

*Note:* Sample item shapes: Comparison task: complete a Venn diagram comparing two short texts on the same event. | Graphic organizer: fill in a comparison table across four dimensions for two given texts. | Short answer: explain why two news articles about the same event might emphasize different facts.

### Skimming and Scanning

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.skimming-and-scanning` · Difficulty: developing · Bloom level: apply · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 1h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to distinguish skimming from scanning and apply each strategy appropriately based on a specific reading purpose.

Rapidly surveying a text for its general idea (skimming) or locating specific information (scanning).

SKIMMING AND SCANNING is rapidly surveying a text for its general idea (skimming) or locating specific information (scanning) — two distinct, deliberately non-exhaustive reading strategies that trade the deep comprehension developed across literal-comprehension through main-idea-and-details for speed, applied when full careful reading is unnecessary or impractical. SKIMMING involves quickly surveying headings, topic sentences, and visual elements to gain a general sense of a text's content and main idea WITHOUT reading every word — useful for previewing a text before deciding whether to read it fully, or for quickly recalling the general content of a text already read. SCANNING involves rapidly moving one's eyes across text specifically searching for a particular piece of information (a date, a name, a specific fact) WITHOUT reading surrounding content for general comprehension — useful when a reader knows exactly what specific information they need to locate.

**Key ideas**

- Skimming means quickly surveying a text (headings, topic sentences, visual elements) to gain a general sense of its content and main idea, without reading every word closely
- Scanning means rapidly searching text for one specific piece of information (a date, name, or fact), moving past all other content without reading it for general comprehension
- Both strategies deliberately trade the deep, careful comprehension developed across earlier concepts in the domain for speed, and are appropriate specifically when full comprehension of every detail is not the current reading goal
- Skimming is typically used to preview a text before deciding to read it fully, to review a text already read, or to quickly grasp an unfamiliar text's general topic and organization
- Scanning is typically used when a reader has a specific information need (looking up a phone number, a statistic, a definition) and knows precisely what they are searching for, making full sequential reading unnecessary and inefficient

**Vocabulary**

- **skimming**
- **scanning**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Skimming and scanning are the same strategy, just described with two different words.
  *Correction:* Skimming and scanning serve genuinely different purposes and use different eye-movement patterns — skimming surveys broadly for a GENERAL sense of content (main idea, overall topic), while scanning searches narrowly and specifically for ONE particular piece of information, ignoring everything else; confusing them leads to using the wrong strategy for a given reading goal.
- *Misconception:* Skimming and scanning are inferior, lazy substitutes for 'real' careful reading and should be avoided.
  *Correction:* Skimming and scanning are legitimate, purposeful reading strategies appropriate for specific goals (previewing, reviewing, or locating specific information) where full careful reading would be inefficient or unnecessary — using the right strategy for the right purpose is a mark of sophisticated, efficient reading, not a lesser substitute for careful reading, which remains the appropriate strategy for different goals (like close-reading, developed next).
- *Misconception:* Skimming means reading the entire text but very quickly.
  *Correction:* Skimming specifically means surveying SELECTED elements (headings, topic sentences, visual elements, first and last sentences of paragraphs) rather than reading the entire text quickly — genuinely reading every word, even at a fast pace, is not skimming but simply fast full reading, a different strategy with different tradeoffs.
- *Misconception:* Scanning requires understanding the general meaning of the surrounding text while searching for specific information.
  *Correction:* Effective scanning specifically does NOT require comprehending surrounding content — a skilled scanner's eyes move past irrelevant text without processing it for meaning, focused entirely on pattern-matching for the specific target information (a name, date, or keyword), which is precisely what makes scanning fast compared to strategies requiring full comprehension.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Confusing skimming and scanning as the same strategy
- Reading every word quickly and calling it 'skimming'
- Trying to comprehend surrounding content while scanning, slowing the process down unnecessarily

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A skimming eye-movement diagram showing a zigzag path across headings and topic sentences, versus a scanning diagram showing a narrow, targeted search path
- A purpose-matching decision chart: 'need general idea quickly?' → skim; 'need one specific fact?' → scan; 'need full understanding?' → careful/close reading
- A side-by-side demonstration showing the same page skimmed (headings/topic sentences highlighted) versus scanned (one target date circled, rest ignored)

**Worked example**

*Setup:* For each reading goal, determine whether skimming, scanning, or full careful reading is the most appropriate strategy, and explain why: (a) deciding whether a long article is worth reading in full before a deadline, (b) finding a specific statistic mentioned somewhere in a report, (c) preparing to write a detailed literary analysis of a short story.

1. (a) The goal is a quick general sense of the article's content and relevance, not full comprehension yet — SKIMMING is appropriate, surveying headings and topic sentences to decide whether full reading is worthwhile.
2. (b) The goal is locating one specific piece of information (a statistic) without needing to understand the rest of the report — SCANNING is appropriate, searching specifically for numerical data or related keywords.
3. (c) The goal is deep, thorough understanding of every detail, structure, and language choice — full careful reading (eventually close-reading) is appropriate, since neither skimming nor scanning would provide the depth of comprehension this goal requires.
4. Confirm each strategy choice matches its purpose: skimming for a general preview decision, scanning for one specific fact, and full careful reading for deep analytical understanding.

*Outcome:* The learner correctly matches each described reading goal to the most appropriate strategy (skimming, scanning, or full careful reading), with correct reasoning for each choice.

**Real-world intuition**

- Skimming and scanning are essential efficiency skills for navigating large volumes of information in academic research, professional documents, and digital content
- Job seekers and professionals routinely skim job postings, emails, and reports to quickly assess relevance before deciding whether to read in depth
- Test-taking strategies for standardized reading assessments often rely on scanning passages for specific answer-supporting information under time pressure

**Practice progression**

Item types: Determine the most appropriate strategy (skim/scan/full reading) for a given reading goal; Practice skimming a text to identify its general topic and main idea within a time limit; Practice scanning a text to locate one specific piece of information within a time limit; Explain why a given strategy would be inappropriate for a specified reading goal. Suggested item count: 6.

Begin with clearly distinct skim-versus-scan-versus-full-reading goal scenarios, progress to timed skimming and scanning practice on real short texts, then to explaining why a mismatched strategy would fail a given specific reading goal.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Strategy-selection task, Timed skimming/scanning exercise, Short answer. Bloom alignment: Apply — learners must apply the appropriate reading strategy (skimming, scanning, or full reading) to a specific, given reading purpose.

Mastery signal: The learner can correctly select and apply skimming or scanning based on a specific reading goal, and correctly identify when full careful reading is instead required, at 75% accuracy or better.

*Note:* Sample item shapes: Strategy selection: which strategy for finding a phone number in a long document? | Timed exercise: skim a one-page article in 30 seconds and state its general topic. | Short answer: explain why scanning would be an inappropriate strategy for writing a literary analysis essay.

**Teacher notes**

The purpose-matching decision chart is the single most valuable teaching tool, since students often use these strategies inappropriately (scanning when they need general understanding, or skimming when they need one specific fact) — practice with timed, purpose-driven exercises rather than teaching the definitions in isolation. Reassure students explicitly that these are legitimate, purposeful strategies, not lazy shortcuts, to counter a common instructional-culture misconception.

**Student notes**

Skimming = quickly surveying headings and topic sentences to get the GENERAL idea, without reading every word. Scanning = searching fast for ONE specific piece of information, ignoring everything else. Match your strategy to your goal: general idea fast → skim; one specific fact → scan; full deep understanding → read carefully instead.

### Close Reading

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.close-reading` · Difficulty: advanced · Bloom level: analyze · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to conduct a close reading of a short passage, analyzing precise language, structure, and meaning through multiple careful readings.

Careful, deliberate analysis of a short passage's precise language, structure, and meaning.

CLOSE READING is careful, deliberate analysis of a short passage's precise language, structure, and meaning — synthesizing inference-in-reading and authors-purpose-and-tone into their most intensive, exhaustive application, and standing in deliberate contrast to skimming-and-scanning's speed-prioritizing strategies. Close reading typically involves multiple passes through a short, carefully selected passage: a first reading for general understanding (literal comprehension), a second reading attending to specific word choices, structural features, and literary devices, and often a third reading focused on a specific analytical question or interpretive angle. Because close reading is intensive and time-consuming, it is deliberately applied to SHORT passages selected for their particular richness or significance, not to entire lengthy texts — the opposite tradeoff from skimming and scanning's speed-for-depth exchange.

**Key ideas**

- Close reading is careful, deliberate, multi-pass analysis of a SHORT passage's precise language, structure, and meaning — deliberately intensive rather than fast, the opposite tradeoff from skimming and scanning
- A typical close-reading process involves multiple purposeful readings: first for general/literal understanding, then for specific word-choice and structural analysis, and often a third pass focused on a specific interpretive question
- Close reading synthesizes inference-in-reading (drawing evidence-based conclusions) and authors-purpose-and-tone (analyzing word choice for tone) into their most intensive, thorough application on a small, carefully selected excerpt
- Because close reading is time-intensive, it is deliberately applied to short passages chosen for their particular richness, ambiguity, or significance, not attempted across entire lengthy texts
- Close reading requires annotating (marking up) the text directly — underlining, circling, and margin notes — as an active thinking tool, not merely a record-keeping habit, helping the reader track patterns and questions across multiple readings

**Vocabulary**

- **close reading**
- **annotation**
- **multi-pass reading**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Close reading simply means reading slowly, with no additional analytical technique involved.
  *Correction:* Close reading involves specific, purposeful techniques beyond just reading slowly — multiple deliberate passes each with a different analytical focus (general understanding, then word-choice/structure, then a specific interpretive question), plus active annotation — reading slowly without these purposeful techniques is not the same as genuine close reading.
- *Misconception:* Close reading should be applied to entire books or long texts to gain the deepest possible understanding.
  *Correction:* Close reading is deliberately and appropriately applied to SHORT, carefully selected passages precisely because of its intensive, time-consuming nature — attempting to close-read an entire lengthy text is impractical and misapplies the strategy; skimming, scanning, and ordinary careful reading remain appropriate for longer texts, with close reading reserved for especially rich or significant short excerpts within them.
- *Misconception:* Annotating a text while reading is an optional organizational habit, not connected to the actual thinking process of close reading.
  *Correction:* Annotation (underlining, circling, margin notes) functions as an active THINKING tool during close reading, not merely passive record-keeping — physically marking patterns, questions, and reactions helps the reader notice and track connections across multiple close readings that might otherwise be missed, making it a genuine component of the analytical process itself.
- *Misconception:* A single careful reading is sufficient for close reading, since the reader is already reading carefully.
  *Correction:* Genuine close reading specifically involves MULTIPLE purposeful passes through the same short passage, each with a different analytical focus — a single reading, however careful, typically cannot simultaneously achieve general comprehension, detailed word-choice analysis, and a specific interpretive question's exploration, which is why the multi-pass structure is a defining, not optional, feature of close reading.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Attempting to close-read an entire long text instead of a short, carefully selected passage
- Treating a single careful reading as sufficient rather than conducting multiple purposeful passes
- Treating annotation as passive note-taking rather than an active thinking tool

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A three-pass close-reading process diagram (Pass 1: general understanding → Pass 2: word choice/structure → Pass 3: interpretive question) applied to one sample short passage
- An annotated-text example showing underlined phrases, circled words, and margin notes on a short excerpt
- A close-reading-versus-skimming contrast chart showing the opposite speed/depth tradeoffs of the two strategies

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Conduct a close reading of the short passage 'The old house stood at the end of the lane, its windows dark and hollow like empty eyes, watching' across three purposeful passes, and identify one significant finding from each pass.

1. Pass 1 (general understanding): the passage describes an old house with dark windows at the end of a lane, creating an initial impression of a spooky or abandoned setting.
2. Pass 2 (word choice and structure): notice the personification in 'watching' and the simile 'like empty eyes' — these specific word choices transform a simple physical description into something unsettling and almost sentient, revealing a deliberate authorial choice to create menace.
3. Pass 3 (interpretive question — 'why personify the house?'): the personification may suggest the house itself will function as an active, almost character-like presence in the story, foreshadowing its significance beyond a simple setting.
4. Confirm each pass yielded a different level of insight: general content (Pass 1), specific craft/technique (Pass 2), and a genuine interpretive hypothesis about the text's larger meaning (Pass 3) — none of these three insights would likely emerge from a single reading alone.

*Outcome:* The learner conducts three distinct, purposeful passes through the short passage, correctly identifying a different type of insight from each pass, demonstrating the value of close reading's multi-pass structure over a single careful reading.

**Real-world intuition**

- Close reading is the foundational analytical method for literary criticism and academic textual analysis at the secondary and university level
- Legal and contractual document review depends on close-reading-style careful, multi-pass attention to precise language and structure
- Poetry and short-form literary analysis (song lyrics, speeches, key passages) rely heavily on close reading's intensive, evidence-based analytical approach

**Practice progression**

Item types: Conduct a three-pass close reading of a short passage, recording findings from each pass; Annotate a short passage identifying significant word choices, structural features, or literary devices; Formulate a specific interpretive question to guide a third close-reading pass; Explain why close reading is appropriately applied to a short excerpt rather than an entire long text. Suggested item count: 6.

Begin with guided, structured three-pass close readings of very short passages, progress to independently formulating interpretive questions for the third pass, then to close reading passages with more subtle or ambiguous literary devices requiring careful, attentive analysis.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Multi-pass analysis task, Annotation task, Short answer. Bloom alignment: Analyze — learners must analyze a short passage's precise language and structure through multiple purposeful readings, synthesizing insights across passes into a coherent interpretation.

Mastery signal: The learner can conduct a structured, multi-pass close reading of a short passage, identifying distinct insights from general comprehension, word-choice/structural analysis, and interpretive questioning, at 75% accuracy or better.

*Note:* Sample item shapes: Multi-pass analysis: complete a three-pass close reading of a given short poem excerpt. | Annotation: annotate a short passage marking significant word choices and structural features. | Short answer: explain why close reading focuses on short passages rather than entire chapters.

**Teacher notes**

The deliberate short-passage scope is worth stating explicitly and early, since students sometimes assume 'closer reading' scales up naturally to longer texts — it doesn't, by design. Model the three-pass process visibly on one shared short passage before asking students to attempt it independently, since the structured, purposeful nature of each pass is the concept's most important, least intuitive feature.

**Student notes**

Close reading means reading a SHORT passage very carefully, multiple times, each time with a different focus: first for general understanding, then for specific word choices and structure, then to explore a specific question about deeper meaning. Annotate as you go — marking up the text actively helps you think, not just record what you noticed.

### Critical Reading

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.critical-reading` · Difficulty: advanced · Bloom level: evaluate · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to evaluate a text's credibility, evidence, assumptions, and potential bias rather than accepting its claims at face value.

Evaluating a text's credibility, evidence, assumptions, and bias rather than accepting its claims at face value.

CRITICAL READING is evaluating a text's credibility, evidence, assumptions, and bias rather than accepting its claims at face value — the domain's shift from ANALYZING what a text says and how (literal comprehension through close-reading) to EVALUATING whether its claims should be believed, extending authors-purpose-and-tone's purpose-identification into genuine, deliberate judgment. Critical reading asks: what EVIDENCE does the author provide for their claims, and is it sufficient and credible? What ASSUMPTIONS underlie the argument, stated or unstated? Is there detectable BIAS (a slant favoring one perspective, connecting to the loaded-language analysis from authors-purpose-and-tone)? This concept marks a deliberate contrast with summarizing's strict objectivity: while a summary reports only what a text says, critical reading requires the reader to actively judge and evaluate those claims.

**Key ideas**

- Critical reading evaluates a text's CREDIBILITY (is the source trustworthy?), EVIDENCE (is sufficient, credible support provided for claims?), ASSUMPTIONS (what unstated beliefs underlie the argument?), and BIAS (does the text favor one perspective unfairly?)
- Critical reading marks a deliberate shift from summarizing's strict objectivity (reporting only what a text says) to active evaluation and judgment of whether a text's claims should be believed
- Detecting bias often builds directly on authors-purpose-and-tone's loaded-language analysis — consistently one-sided connotation, selective evidence, or an unacknowledged conflict of interest are all bias indicators
- An author's unstated ASSUMPTIONS (beliefs taken for granted without being explicitly argued) can be just as important to identify as their stated claims, since a flawed assumption can undermine an otherwise logically structured argument
- Critical reading does not mean automatic distrust or cynicism toward every text — it means applying a consistent, disciplined evaluative process to determine which claims are well-supported and credible, and which are not, rather than uncritically accepting or uncritically rejecting a text's claims

**Vocabulary**

- **critical reading**
- **credibility**
- **bias**
- **unstated assumption**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Critical reading means being critical (negative) about everything you read, assuming most texts are untrustworthy.
  *Correction:* Critical reading means applying a disciplined EVALUATIVE process — assessing evidence, assumptions, and potential bias — not adopting automatic distrust or cynicism; a critically read text can be judged credible and well-supported just as validly as it can be judged weak or biased, since the goal is accurate evaluation, not default rejection.
- *Misconception:* Critical reading and summarizing require the same objective, non-evaluative stance toward a text.
  *Correction:* Critical reading deliberately and directly contrasts with summarizing's strict objectivity — a summary reports only what a text states without judgment, while critical reading requires the reader to actively evaluate whether those stated claims are well-supported, credible, and free of significant bias, a genuinely different and additional analytical stance.
- *Misconception:* Identifying an author's bias is the same as saying their entire argument is wrong or worthless.
  *Correction:* Detecting bias (a slant favoring one perspective) does not automatically invalidate an entire argument — a biased source might still present some genuinely credible evidence alongside its slant, and critical reading requires evaluating specific claims and evidence individually, rather than wholesale dismissing everything a biased source states.
- *Misconception:* An author's unstated assumptions are unimportant if their stated argument seems logically well-constructed.
  *Correction:* An author's unstated assumptions can undermine an otherwise logically well-constructed argument if the assumption itself is flawed or unsupported — critical reading specifically requires surfacing and evaluating these underlying, often unstated assumptions, not just checking whether the stated logical steps follow from each other.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Treating critical reading as automatic distrust of everything
- Confusing critical reading's evaluative stance with summarizing's objective stance
- Dismissing an entire argument wholesale after identifying one instance of bias, rather than evaluating specific claims individually

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A four-question critical-reading checklist (credibility? evidence? assumptions? bias?) applied as an evaluative lens over a sample text
- A summarizing-versus-critical-reading contrast diagram showing 'objective reporting' on one side and 'active evaluation' on the other
- A bias-detection example highlighting one-sided loaded language and selectively omitted counter-evidence in a sample persuasive text

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Critically read the claim: 'A recent survey of 50 of our own loyal customers found that 98% prefer our product over competitors, proving our product is the best on the market,' evaluating credibility, evidence, assumptions, and bias.

1. Evaluate credibility: the survey is conducted BY the company about its OWN product — this is a significant credibility concern, since the source has an obvious vested interest in a favorable result.
2. Evaluate evidence sufficiency: a sample of only 50 people, and specifically 'loyal customers' (people already predisposed to prefer the product), is a small, non-representative, self-selected sample — insufficient to support the broad claim 'best on the market.'
3. Identify the unstated assumption: the argument assumes that existing loyal customers' preferences accurately represent the broader market's preferences, including people who have never tried the product or who prefer competitors — an unsupported and likely flawed assumption.
4. Identify the bias: the company has a direct financial interest in this claim being believed, and the survey design (surveying only already-loyal customers) reflects a methodology likely to produce a favorable result regardless of the product's actual broader market standing.
5. Conclude: this claim should be read with significant skepticism, not because the underlying data is necessarily fabricated, but because the credibility, evidence sufficiency, assumptions, and bias all raise serious concerns about whether 'proving our product is the best on the market' is actually supported by this evidence.

*Outcome:* The learner systematically evaluates the claim across all four critical-reading dimensions (credibility, evidence, assumptions, bias) and reaches an appropriately skeptical, well-reasoned conclusion rather than either uncritically accepting or dismissively rejecting the claim.

**Real-world intuition**

- Critical reading is an essential media literacy and civic skill for evaluating news sources, political claims, and social media content in an information-saturated environment
- Academic research requires critically evaluating sources' credibility, methodology, and potential bias before relying on their claims as evidence
- Consumer decision-making (evaluating advertising claims, product reviews, and marketing content) depends directly on critical reading skills to avoid being misled by biased or insufficient evidence

**Practice progression**

Item types: Evaluate a text's credibility based on its source and potential conflicts of interest; Assess whether a text's evidence is sufficient and credible to support its claims; Identify an unstated assumption underlying an argument; Identify bias indicators (loaded language, selective evidence, conflict of interest) in a persuasive text. Suggested item count: 8.

Begin with clearly flawed evidence or obvious bias examples, progress to identifying unstated assumptions in more subtly constructed arguments, then to fully systematic four-dimension critical evaluation of realistic, mixed-quality texts.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Evaluation task, Bias-identification task, Short answer. Bloom alignment: Evaluate — learners must make and justify judgments about a text's credibility, evidence sufficiency, underlying assumptions, and potential bias, going beyond analysis alone into genuine evaluative judgment.

Mastery signal: The learner can systematically evaluate a text's credibility, evidence, assumptions, and bias, reaching a well-reasoned, appropriately calibrated judgment (neither uncritical acceptance nor blanket dismissal), at 75% accuracy or better.

*Note:* Sample item shapes: Evaluation: evaluate the credibility and evidence sufficiency of a company's self-reported product claim. | Bias identification: identify loaded language and selective evidence in a persuasive passage. | Short answer: explain an unstated assumption underlying a given argument.

**Teacher notes**

Address the 'critical means negative' misconception immediately and explicitly, since it's a common and consequential misunderstanding of this concept's actual meaning. The four-question checklist (credibility/evidence/assumptions/bias) is the most reliable scaffolding tool, and the self-interested-survey worked example is an unusually clear, realistic demonstration of all four dimensions working together.

**Student notes**

Critical reading means actively evaluating whether a text's claims deserve your trust — checking its credibility, whether it gives enough good evidence, what it's assuming without saying so, and whether it's biased toward one side. This is different from summarizing, where you just report what a text says without judging it. Critical reading isn't about assuming everything is wrong — it's about figuring out what actually deserves your trust.

**Teaching hints — review triggers**

- eng.reading.close-reading

**Spaced repetition / revision guidance**

Revisit whenever a learner either uncritically accepts a weakly supported claim or dismisses an entire source after spotting one flaw; a quick four-dimension evaluation drill on a fresh persuasive passage is an efficient spaced-practice refresher.

### Evaluating Sources

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.evaluating-sources` · Difficulty: advanced · Bloom level: evaluate · Mastery threshold: 0.75 · Estimated study time: 2h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to assess a written source's reliability using the criteria of authority, currency, and relevance.

Assessing the reliability, authority, currency, and relevance of a written source.

EVALUATING SOURCES is assessing the reliability, authority, currency, and relevance of a written source — applying critical-reading's evaluative framework specifically and systematically to the question of whether a SOURCE itself (not just a single claim within it) is trustworthy enough to rely upon. AUTHORITY asks whether the author or publisher has relevant expertise or credentials on the topic; CURRENCY asks whether the information is sufficiently up to date for the topic at hand (crucial for rapidly changing fields, less critical for historical or timeless topics); and RELEVANCE asks whether the source actually addresses the specific question or topic at hand, rather than being merely tangentially related. This systematic, criteria-based approach extends critical-reading's four evaluative questions (credibility, evidence, assumptions, bias) into a practical, repeatable source-vetting procedure essential for research.

**Key ideas**

- Authority: does the author or publisher have relevant expertise, credentials, or institutional standing on this specific topic? (A physicist's credentials carry authority on physics but not necessarily on economics.)
- Currency: is the information sufficiently up to date for the topic? Currency requirements vary significantly by field — a scientific source on a fast-evolving medical topic needs recent currency, while a source on a historical event from centuries ago may remain reliable regardless of publication date
- Relevance: does the source actually address the specific question or topic being researched, rather than being only tangentially or superficially related to it?
- Evaluating sources systematically applies critical-reading's evaluative framework (credibility, evidence, assumptions, bias) specifically to the question of a SOURCE's overall trustworthiness, rather than to one isolated claim
- A source can be authoritative on one topic but not another, current for one purpose but outdated for another, and relevant to one specific question but not a related one — evaluation must always be applied to a source in the context of its SPECIFIC intended use, not as a fixed, universal property of the source itself

**Vocabulary**

- **authority**
- **currency**
- **relevance**
- **source evaluation**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* A source's reliability is a fixed, universal property, true or false regardless of what it is being used for.
  *Correction:* Source evaluation must always consider the SPECIFIC intended use — a highly authoritative historical source might be entirely irrelevant to a question about current events, and a current source might lack sufficient authority for a highly technical scientific claim; the same source can be reliable for one purpose and unreliable for another, depending on the specific question being researched.
- *Misconception:* Currency (how recent a source is) is equally important for every research topic.
  *Correction:* Currency requirements vary significantly by field and topic — a source on a rapidly evolving medical or technological topic needs to be quite recent to remain reliable, while a well-researched historical source about ancient events can remain authoritative and relevant regardless of its publication date, since the underlying historical facts don't change the way scientific consensus can.
- *Misconception:* An author's general fame, popularity, or credentials in any field automatically confer authority on any topic they write about.
  *Correction:* Authority is topic-specific, not a general property of a person's overall credentials or fame — a renowned physicist's expertise confers strong authority on physics topics, but does not automatically confer equivalent authority on unrelated fields like economics or nutrition, even though their general credibility might create an illusion of broader expertise.
- *Misconception:* Evaluating sources is only necessary for obviously questionable or informal sources, not for professionally published or academic-looking sources.
  *Correction:* All sources, including professionally published or academic-looking ones, should be evaluated using the authority/currency/relevance framework — professional appearance or publication venue alone does not guarantee a source is authoritative, current, or relevant for a SPECIFIC research question, making systematic evaluation a universal practice, not one reserved only for obviously suspect sources.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Assuming a source's reliability is fixed and universal rather than purpose-specific
- Applying the same currency standard to every topic regardless of how quickly the field changes
- Assuming general fame or credentials confer topic-specific authority

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A three-criteria evaluation checklist (authority/currency/relevance) with a rating scale for each, applied to a sample source
- A topic-specific-authority diagram showing the same author rated high-authority on their expert field and low-authority on an unrelated field
- A currency-requirement comparison showing a fast-moving field (technology) needing very recent sources beside a stable field (ancient history) tolerating much older sources

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Evaluate the reliability of a source — a blog post from 2015 written by a celebrity chef, discussing the latest advances in cancer immunotherapy research — for the specific research question 'What are the current standard treatments for a specific type of cancer?' using authority, currency, and relevance.

1. Evaluate authority: the author is a celebrity chef, with expertise in culinary arts, not medicine or oncology — this source has LOW authority for a medical research question, regardless of the author's general fame or credibility in their own field.
2. Evaluate currency: the source is from 2015, and cancer immunotherapy is a rapidly evolving medical field — a source from several years ago is likely significantly outdated for current standard-of-care information, making currency a serious concern for this fast-moving topic.
3. Evaluate relevance: while the source's TOPIC (cancer immunotherapy) is relevant to the research question, its specific framing as general commentary rather than clinical or research-based content may limit its direct usefulness for a question about current standard treatments.
4. Conclude: this source scores poorly on authority and currency for this specific research question, making it an unreliable source for this purpose — a reader should instead seek a source authored by a medical/oncology expert, published recently, and specifically addressing current standard treatments.

*Outcome:* The learner systematically evaluates the source across all three criteria (authority, currency, relevance) for this specific research question, correctly concluding that the source is unreliable for this particular purpose despite covering a topically related subject.

**Real-world intuition**

- The authority/currency/relevance framework is a standard, widely taught research skill used in academic writing, library instruction, and information literacy education
- Evaluating source reliability is essential for combating misinformation in an era of easily accessible but variably reliable online information
- Professional research, journalism, and evidence-based decision-making all depend on systematic source evaluation before relying on information as credible support

**Practice progression**

Item types: Evaluate a source's authority for a given research question; Evaluate a source's currency requirements based on the topic's rate of change; Evaluate a source's relevance to a specific research question; Compare two sources on the same topic and determine which is more reliable for a specific purpose. Suggested item count: 8.

Begin with clearly authoritative-versus-non-authoritative source comparisons, progress to currency evaluation across different topic types, then to full three-criteria evaluation and comparing multiple sources for a specific research purpose.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Evaluation task, Comparison task, Short answer. Bloom alignment: Evaluate — learners must make and justify judgments about a source's reliability using the authority/currency/relevance framework, tailored to a specific intended use or research question.

Mastery signal: The learner can systematically evaluate a source's authority, currency, and relevance for a specific research question, at 80% accuracy or better.

*Note:* Sample item shapes: Evaluation: evaluate a 10-year-old technology article's currency for a current research question. | Comparison: which of two sources is more authoritative for a medical research question? | Short answer: explain why a historical source's age doesn't necessarily undermine its reliability.

**Teacher notes**

The topic-specific-authority point (a source can be authoritative on one topic and not another) is this concept's single most important, most transferable insight — the celebrity-chef-writing-about-oncology worked example makes this vivid and memorable. Emphasize that evaluation is always relative to a SPECIFIC research question, not an abstract, fixed property of the source.

**Student notes**

To evaluate a source's reliability, check three things: Authority (does the author have real expertise in THIS specific topic?), Currency (is the information recent enough for THIS topic?), and Relevance (does the source actually address YOUR specific question?). A source can be reliable for one purpose and unreliable for another — always evaluate it against your specific research question.

### Reading Across Genres

*Concept ID: `eng.reading.reading-across-genres` · Difficulty: expert · Bloom level: evaluate · Mastery threshold: 0.7 · Estimated study time: 3h*

**Learning objective.** Learners will be able to synthesize understanding across multiple texts and genres on a related topic or theme into a coherent, well-supported overall understanding.

Synthesizing understanding across multiple texts and genres on a related topic or theme.

READING ACROSS GENRES is synthesizing understanding across multiple texts and genres on a related topic or theme — the domain's capstone concept, drawing together compare-and-contrast-texts' multi-text analysis and evaluating-sources' reliability assessment into a single, comprehensive synthesis skill. This concept extends beyond comparing just two texts (compare-and-contrast-texts) to integrating understanding across MULTIPLE texts, often spanning different GENRES (a news article, a historical account, a poem, and a personal memoir on the same topic, drawing on genre-recognition) and varying reliability (requiring evaluating-sources' vetting). The goal is not simply cataloging what each source says, but constructing a genuinely synthesized, well-supported overall understanding that accounts for each source's genre-specific conventions, purpose, reliability, and unique contribution to the topic.

**Key ideas**

- Reading across genres synthesizes understanding across MULTIPLE texts (more than the two-text comparison developed at compare-and-contrast-texts), often spanning different genres (from genre-recognition) on a shared topic or theme
- Genuine synthesis is more than cataloging what each source individually says — it constructs a new, integrated understanding that accounts for how each source's genre, purpose, and reliability shape its particular contribution to the topic
- Different genres offer genuinely different, complementary types of insight into the same topic — a historical account might provide verified facts, a poem might convey emotional or thematic resonance, and a memoir might provide authentic personal perspective, each valuable in different, non-competing ways
- Synthesis requires applying evaluating-sources' reliability assessment across all texts being integrated, weighing each source's contribution according to its authority, currency, and relevance rather than treating all sources as equally authoritative
- As the domain's capstone, this concept requires and directly exercises nearly every prior reading skill simultaneously: literal comprehension and inference within each text, main idea and summarizing for each source, text structure and genre recognition to understand each source's form, critical reading and source evaluation to assess reliability, and comparison to identify relationships across all the sources together

**Vocabulary**

- **synthesis**
- **reading across genres**

**Common misconceptions**

- *Misconception:* Reading across genres just means reading several different types of texts on the same topic, with no additional synthesizing step required.
  *Correction:* Genuine synthesis requires an additional, active integrative step beyond simply reading multiple genre-diverse texts — it means constructing a new, coherent overall understanding that accounts for how each source's genre, purpose, and reliability shape its specific contribution, not merely cataloging separate summaries of each individual source side by side.
- *Misconception:* All genres provide the same type of insight into a topic, so their contributions can simply be added together equally.
  *Correction:* Different genres offer genuinely different, complementary types of insight — a historical account's verified facts, a poem's emotional/thematic resonance, and a memoir's authentic personal perspective are not interchangeable or equally weighted contributions, but distinct, complementary lenses that a skilled synthesizer weighs and integrates according to their different natures and purposes, not simply summed as equivalent evidence.
- *Misconception:* Every source used in a synthesis should be treated as equally reliable and authoritative.
  *Correction:* Genuine synthesis requires applying evaluating-sources' authority/currency/relevance framework across all sources being integrated, weighing each source's contribution according to its assessed reliability for the specific topic — treating a highly authoritative source and a weakly supported source as equally weighted inputs would produce a poorly calibrated, unreliable synthesis.
- *Misconception:* Synthesis across genres is a single, isolated skill unrelated to the rest of the reading skills developed throughout the domain.
  *Correction:* As the domain's capstone concept, reading across genres directly requires and exercises nearly every prior reading skill simultaneously — literal comprehension and inference within each text, main idea identification and summarizing for each source, text structure and genre recognition to understand each source's form, and critical reading and source evaluation to assess reliability — making it a genuine synthesis of the entire domain's skill set, not an isolated new technique.

**Common mistakes in practice**

- Producing a side-by-side catalog of source summaries instead of a genuine integrated synthesis
- Treating all sources as equally reliable regardless of their evaluated authority and currency
- Assuming different genres compete rather than complement each other in building understanding

**Visual teaching opportunities**

- A hub-and-spoke diagram with a central topic surrounded by different genre sources (news article, poem, historical account, memoir), each labeled with its distinct type of contribution
- A synthesis-versus-cataloging comparison showing a simple list of separate source summaries beside a genuinely integrated synthesis paragraph on the same topic
- A skill-integration diagram showing this capstone concept drawing arrows from nearly every prior eng.reading concept converging into one synthesis skill

**Worked example**

*Setup:* Synthesize an understanding of a historical famine event using three genre-diverse sources: a peer-reviewed historical analysis (nonfiction, high authority) presenting statistical mortality data, a survivor's memoir (nonfiction/personal narrative) describing personal experience and emotional impact, and a period poem written during the famine expressing collective grief. Explain how each source contributes differently to the overall understanding.

1. Evaluate each source's reliability and genre-specific contribution using evaluating-sources and genre-recognition: the historical analysis offers high-authority, verified statistical evidence about the famine's scale and causes; the memoir offers authentic, if necessarily subjective, personal experiential detail; the poem offers emotional and cultural resonance reflecting how the event was collectively felt and remembered.
2. Recognize that these three contributions are complementary, not competing or redundant — the statistical data alone would not convey the famine's human impact, while the memoir and poem alone would not establish its verified scale and causes.
3. Construct a synthesized understanding: the famine was a large-scale event (established by the historical analysis) that produced severe individual suffering (illustrated by the memoir) and left a lasting collective emotional and cultural imprint (expressed by the poem) — no single source alone would provide this full picture.
4. Confirm this synthesis is genuinely integrated (not simply three separate summaries listed side by side), explicitly connecting each source's specific contribution to a coherent, unified understanding of the event.

*Outcome:* The learner constructs a genuinely integrated synthesis explaining how three genre-diverse sources' distinct types of contribution (statistical, personal, cultural/emotional) combine into a more complete understanding than any single source could provide alone.

**Real-world intuition**

- Academic research papers and literature reviews require synthesizing multiple genre-diverse sources into a coherent, well-supported argument or understanding
- Journalism and documentary work often integrate statistical reports, personal interviews, and historical records to construct a full, multi-dimensional account of an event or issue
- Informed civic and professional decision-making increasingly requires synthesizing information across diverse source types (data reports, personal accounts, expert analysis, cultural commentary) rather than relying on any single source type alone

**Practice progression**

Item types: Synthesize an integrated understanding of a topic from three or more genre-diverse sources; Explain the distinct, complementary contribution each genre-diverse source makes to a shared topic; Evaluate and weigh sources of varying reliability within a synthesis; Distinguish a genuine synthesis from a simple side-by-side cataloging of separate source summaries. Suggested item count: 8.

Begin with two clearly complementary genre-diverse sources on a familiar topic, progress to three or more sources requiring reliability weighing, then to constructing a full written synthesis integrating multiple sources of varying genre and reliability into one coherent understanding.

**Assessment objectives**

Formats: Synthesis task, Evaluation task, Extended response. Bloom alignment: Evaluate — learners must evaluate, weigh, and integrate multiple genre-diverse sources of varying reliability into a coherent, well-supported synthesized understanding, representing the domain's most demanding cognitive task.

Mastery signal: The learner can construct a genuine synthesis integrating multiple genre-diverse sources, correctly explaining each source's distinct contribution and appropriately weighing reliability, at 70% accuracy or better given this concept's advanced difficulty and 0.70 mastery threshold.

*Note:* Sample item shapes: Synthesis: write a short synthesis paragraph integrating a news article, a scientific report, and a personal essay on the same topic. | Evaluation: explain how you would weigh a highly authoritative but older source against a less authoritative but very current one. | Extended response: explain what a poem contributes to understanding a historical event that a statistical report cannot.
