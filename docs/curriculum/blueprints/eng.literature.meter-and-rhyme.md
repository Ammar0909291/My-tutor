**Concept:** Meter and Rhyme — the patterns of stressed and unstressed syllables (meter) and
end-sound repetition (rhyme scheme) in verse, two independent poetic dimensions that are often
present together but do not determine or imply one another.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Poetic Forms (prerequisite) taught recognizing named
poem structures (sonnet, haiku, free verse) and their conventions. Meter and Rhyme extends this by
teaching the two specific sound-pattern dimensions that many of those forms depend on: the rhythm of
stressed and unstressed syllables within lines (meter), and the repetition of end sounds across
lines (rhyme scheme) — analyzed as genuinely separate systems, not one implying the other. This
concept has no further downstream unlocks in the current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) analyze meter and rhyme scheme as two independent
poetic dimensions, recognizing that a poem's rhyming does not imply it has a regular meter (and vice
versa), and (b) scan a line's meter by identifying its natural stressed/unstressed syllable pattern,
recognizing that minor metrical substitutions are a normal, accepted variation within an overall
consistent pattern rather than proof the poem has no meter at all.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-A-POEM-THAT-RHYMES-MUST-ALSO-HAVE-A-REGULAR-CONSISTENT-METER

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes that identifying a poem's rhyme scheme is the same task as
identifying its meter, or that a poem with a clear, regular rhyme scheme must also have a regular,
consistent meter, failing to recognize that rhyme (end-sound repetition) and meter (stress pattern
within lines) are independent dimensions that can vary separately from each other.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a poem with a clear, consistent AABB rhyme scheme but
lines of noticeably varying length and stress pattern (irregular meter), next to a poem with the same
consistent rhyme scheme and a genuinely regular, consistent meter throughout. Ask: "Do both poems
have the same rhyme scheme? Do both poems have the same kind of rhythm when you read them aloud? What
does this tell you about whether rhyme and meter always go together?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming rhyme implies regular meter is like assuming a song with a
consistent chorus melody must also have a steady, unchanging beat throughout every verse — the two
are actually separate musical elements that can vary independently. Rhyme is about sound repetition
at line-ends; meter is about the rhythm within lines. A poem can have one without the other, or both,
or neither."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Analyze rhyme and meter as two separate questions. First ask: what sound
pattern repeats at the ends of lines (rhyme scheme)? Then, separately, ask: what stress pattern
repeats within the lines (meter)? Don't assume finding one tells you the answer to the other."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A poem with a consistent AABB rhyme scheme but irregular, varying line rhythm (rhyme present,
   meter irregular) vs. a poem with the same AABB rhyme scheme and a genuinely regular, consistent
   meter (both rhyme and meter present and regular).
2. A student who identifies a poem's rhyme scheme and assumes that settles its meter too
   (misconception) vs. a student who separately scans the poem's stress pattern after identifying its
   rhyme scheme, treating them as two distinct analyses (correct approach).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to conflate rhyme and meter after the bridge, give them three
poems — one with rhyme but irregular meter, one with meter but no rhyme (blank verse), and one with
both — and have them analyze rhyme scheme and meter separately for each, confirming the two do not
determine each other.

### MC-B-ANY-DEVIATION-FROM-THE-DOMINANT-STRESS-PATTERN-MEANS-THE-POEM-HAS-NO-METER

**Trigger signal:** Student treats any single line or syllable that deviates from a poem's otherwise
dominant stress pattern as proof the poem "has no meter" or "doesn't really follow" its metrical
scheme, failing to recognize that poets commonly use accepted metrical substitutions and variations
within an overall consistent pattern — a poem can be genuinely written in a meter while still
containing occasional, deliberate departures from the strict pattern.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a poem written predominantly in a consistent stressed/
unstressed pattern across most lines, with one line containing a deliberate substitution (a reversed
stress at the line's start, common for emphasis). Ask: "Does the rest of the poem clearly follow one
consistent pattern? Does one line with a variation mean the whole poem has no meter, or could this be
a deliberate, accepted variation within an overall pattern?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Treating any single deviation as proof of 'no meter' is like insisting a
song isn't really in 4/4 time just because one measure has a brief syncopated variation — a single
deliberate variation within an overall consistent pattern doesn't erase the pattern; it's a normal,
often intentional part of how the pattern is used. Meter describes the DOMINANT, overall pattern, not
an unbroken, rigid rule with zero exceptions."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't conclude a poem has 'no meter' just because you find one line that
varies from the dominant pattern. Look at the overall, dominant stress pattern across most of the
poem first — occasional substitutions are a normal, often deliberate variation within that pattern,
not proof the pattern doesn't exist."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A poem with one line containing a deliberate stress-reversal for emphasis, set within an otherwise
   consistent metrical pattern across the rest of the poem (a genuine meter with an accepted
   variation) vs. a poem with stress patterns that vary unpredictably line after line with no
   dominant pattern at all (genuinely irregular, no consistent meter).
2. A student who declares a poem "has no meter" after finding one varying line (misconception,
   over-rigid expectation) vs. a student who identifies the dominant pattern across most lines first,
   then notes the single variation as a deliberate substitution (correct analysis).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to over-rigidly reject meter at the first deviation after the
bridge, give them a poem with two deliberate substitutions within an otherwise consistent metrical
pattern, and have them first identify the dominant pattern across the majority of lines, then
separately note each substitution and its likely purpose (emphasis, meaning).

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.literature.poetic-forms`):** "Identify the form of this poem (e.g., sonnet,
haiku, free verse) and one convention associated with it." A student who cannot recognize basic
poetic forms lacks the foundation for analyzing the meter and rhyme conventions specific to those
forms — route to `eng.literature.poetic-forms` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Song-Chorus-vs-Steady-Beat and Syncopated-Measure-in-4/4-Time Anchor.** A song with a
consistent chorus melody that doesn't require a steady, unchanging beat throughout — directly seeding
the fix for MC-A (rhyme and meter are separate dimensions that vary independently). Second, a song
still counted as 4/4 time despite one measure containing a brief syncopated variation — directly
seeding the fix for MC-B (meter describes a dominant overall pattern; occasional deliberate
substitutions don't erase it).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Rhyme Scheme as an Independent System

Teach identifying a poem's rhyme scheme (labeling end sounds AABB, ABAB, and so on) as a standalone
analysis. Student practice: given a poem, label its rhyme scheme.

### TA-2 — Rhyme and Meter Are Independent Dimensions (MC-A)

Using the song-chorus-vs-steady-beat anchor, teach that identifying rhyme does not determine meter.
Student practice: given a poem with clear rhyme but irregular meter, confirm the rhyme scheme and
separately assess whether the meter is regular.

### TA-3 — Scanning a Line's Dominant Stress Pattern

Teach marking stressed and unstressed syllables across several lines to identify the dominant
metrical pattern. Student practice: given four lines from a poem, mark stress patterns and state the
dominant pattern.

### TA-4 — Recognizing Accepted Metrical Substitution (MC-B)

Using the syncopated-measure anchor, teach that a line deviating from the dominant pattern is a
normal substitution, not proof of no meter. Student practice: given a poem with one substituted line
within an otherwise consistent pattern, identify the dominant pattern and explain the substitution's
likely purpose.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A short poem, fully worked: rhyme scheme labeled (AABB); meter separately
scanned across all lines, revealing a consistent dominant iambic pattern with one line containing a
deliberate stress-reversal at its start; the rhyme-meter independence confirmed (both happen to be
regular here, but were assessed as separate questions), and the single substitution explained as
deliberate emphasis rather than "no meter." Annotation confirms both the rhyme-meter independence
(fixing MC-A) and the accepted-substitution recognition (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new poem with a different rhyme scheme and one metrical substitution. Student is
prompted with guiding questions ("What is the rhyme scheme? Separately, what is the dominant stress
pattern across most lines? Does one varying line mean there's no meter, or is it a substitution within
an overall pattern?") to complete the analysis.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new poem, unaided. Student labels rhyme scheme, scans dominant meter
separately, and correctly identifies any substitutions as variations within the pattern.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Labeling a Rhyme Scheme**
Given a poem, label its rhyme scheme (e.g., AABB, ABAB).

**MP-2 [P36] — Confirming Rhyme-Meter Independence**
Given a poem with clear rhyme but irregular meter, confirm the rhyme scheme and separately assess
whether the meter is regular.

**MP-3 [P36] — Scanning Dominant Stress Pattern**
Given four lines from a poem, mark stress patterns and state the dominant metrical pattern.

**MP-4 [P36] — Identifying an Accepted Metrical Substitution**
Given a poem with one substituted line within an otherwise consistent pattern, identify the dominant
pattern and explain the substitution's likely purpose.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Meter and Rhyme Analysis**
Given a new poem, label its rhyme scheme, scan its dominant meter separately, and correctly identify
any substitutions.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite.
During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1/TA-2 (conflating
rhyme and meter) and MC-B during TA-3/TA-4 (over-rigid rejection of meter at any deviation) — these
are largely independent and often need separate, explicit correction. If either fires, branch to the
relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1/MP-2
failures indicate residual MC-A; MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1
through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-poem example before
re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly labels rhyme schemes on provided poems (MP-1) but assumes regular meter
  follows automatically on a new poem (MP-5), provide a mandatory "assess rhyme and meter as two
  separate questions" checklist step before concluding either.
- If the student's stress-pattern scanning is strong (MP-3) but they reject meter entirely at the
  first substitution on a new poem (MP-4), require identifying the dominant pattern across the
  majority of lines FIRST, before evaluating any single varying line.
- If the student struggles to mark stress patterns at all, provide a fully-marked example line as a
  reference model before free scanning.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.literature.poetic-forms`'s convention-recognition work
  should transfer readily to meter/rhyme analysis, since both require recognizing an underlying
  pattern a specific poem follows or varies from — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
