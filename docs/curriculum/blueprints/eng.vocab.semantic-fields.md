**Concept:** Semantic Fields — groups of words related in meaning that structure a shared
conceptual domain (such as vocabulary for emotions or weather), distinct from simple synonym sets or
words that merely tend to appear together.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Thesaurus and Dictionary Skills (prerequisite) taught
looking up precise word meanings and finding synonyms. Etymology (prerequisite) taught tracing word
origins. Semantic Fields extends both by teaching a broader organizing structure: a conceptual
domain (like "emotion" or "weather") contains many distinct, non-synonymous words that all relate to
that shared domain, and recognizing this structure is different from either finding synonyms or
noticing words that simply co-occur in the same sentences. This concept has no further downstream
unlocks in the current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) distinguish a semantic field (a set of distinct,
often non-synonymous words all connected to one shared conceptual domain) from a synonym set (words
that mean nearly the same thing), and (b) distinguish genuine semantic-field membership from mere
collocation (words that frequently appear near each other without sharing the same conceptual
domain).

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-A-SEMANTIC-FIELD-IS-JUST-A-LIST-OF-SYNONYMS-FOR-ONE-WORD

**Trigger signal:** Student treats a semantic field as equivalent to a synonym list, assuming all
words within a semantic field must mean nearly the same thing, rather than recognizing that a
semantic field contains many distinct, often quite different words that are connected not by shared
meaning but by belonging to the same broader conceptual domain (e.g., "emotion" includes joy, anger,
fear, and sadness — none of which are synonyms of each other).

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student the words "joy," "anger," "fear," and "sadness." Ask:
"Do 'joy' and 'anger' mean the same thing, or anything close to the same thing? Do they belong to the
same broader category — emotion?" Contrast this with an actual synonym set: "happy," "joyful,"
"elated," "delighted" — words that DO mean nearly the same thing.

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming a semantic field is a synonym list is like assuming everyone in a
single family must look identical — a family shares a connection (they're related), but the members
can look completely different from each other. A semantic field's words share a conceptual domain
(emotion, weather, cooking) without needing to mean the same thing at all — in fact, they usually
don't."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't expect words in a semantic field to mean the same thing. Ask
instead: do these words all belong to the same broader conceptual domain, even if their individual
meanings are quite different from each other? That shared domain — not shared meaning — is what
defines a semantic field."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Joy," "anger," "fear," "sadness" (a genuine semantic field — the emotion domain — despite having
   very different individual meanings) vs. "happy," "joyful," "elated," "delighted" (a synonym set —
   all meaning nearly the same thing).
2. A student who expects every word in the "weather" semantic field to mean roughly "rain"
   (misconception, treating the field as a synonym set) vs. a student who recognizes "rain," "wind,"
   "sunshine," and "storm" all belong to the weather domain despite meaning very different things
   (correct semantic-field recognition).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to conflate semantic fields with synonym sets after the
bridge, give them a semantic field (cooking: bake, boil, fry, roast, simmer) and have them explain,
for each pair of words, why they are NOT synonyms even though they share the same conceptual domain.

### MC-B-ANY-WORDS-THAT-OFTEN-APPEAR-TOGETHER-IN-THE-SAME-CONTEXT-BELONG-TO-THE-SAME-SEMANTIC-FIELD

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes any two words that frequently co-occur in the same sentences or
contexts (collocations) must belong to the same semantic field, failing to distinguish genuine
shared-domain membership from mere frequent co-occurrence — for example, treating "umbrella" as part
of the weather semantic field because it often appears alongside "rain," when "umbrella" actually
belongs to the objects/tools domain.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student the sentence "I grabbed my umbrella because of the
rain." Ask: "Does 'umbrella' describe a weather phenomenon, the way 'rain,' 'wind,' and 'storm' do?
Or is 'umbrella' actually an object — a tool people use in response to weather? Just because two
words often appear together, does that mean they belong to the same conceptual domain?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming words that often appear together must share a semantic field is
like assuming 'lifeguard' and 'ocean' belong to the same category just because they're always
mentioned together — a lifeguard is a person, the ocean is a body of water; they're related in
context, not in conceptual domain. Frequent co-occurrence (collocation) is a different relationship
than genuine shared-domain membership (semantic field)."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't assume two words share a semantic field just because they often
appear near each other. Ask: do these words describe the same KIND of thing — both weather
phenomena, both emotions, both cooking actions? If one word is a tool or object used in response to
the other, they're related by context, not by shared conceptual domain."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Umbrella" and "rain" (frequently co-occur, but "umbrella" is an object/tool, not a weather
   phenomenon — different semantic fields) vs. "rain" and "storm" (both genuine weather phenomena —
   same semantic field).
2. A student who groups "umbrella" into the weather semantic field because of frequent co-occurrence
   (misconception) vs. a student who recognizes "umbrella" belongs to the objects/tools field despite
   its strong contextual association with weather words (correct distinction).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to conflate collocation with semantic-field membership after
the bridge, give them five word pairs that frequently co-occur (doctor/hospital, teacher/classroom,
rain/umbrella) and have them determine, for each pair, whether both words share the same conceptual
domain or whether one is merely contextually associated with the other.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.vocab.thesaurus-and-dictionary-skills`):** "Find two synonyms for this word using
a thesaurus, and explain any nuance of meaning that differs between them." A student who cannot use
reference tools to find precise synonyms lacks the foundation to distinguish synonym sets from
semantic fields — route to `eng.vocab.thesaurus-and-dictionary-skills` review first.

**PD-2 (checks `eng.vocab.etymology`):** "Trace this word's origin and explain how it relates to the
word's current meaning." A student who cannot trace word origins lacks part of the foundation for
recognizing how related words within a domain can have distinct histories and meanings — route to
`eng.vocab.etymology` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Family-Resemblance and Lifeguard-vs-Ocean Anchor.** A family whose members share a connection
(they're related) without looking identical — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (a semantic field's
words share a conceptual domain, not a common meaning). Second, "lifeguard" and "ocean" — always
mentioned together in context, but one is a person and one is a body of water, not the same kind of
thing — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (frequent co-occurrence/collocation is not the same as
genuine shared semantic-domain membership).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Semantic Fields vs. Synonym Sets (MC-A)

Using the family-resemblance anchor, teach that a semantic field's words share a conceptual domain,
not a meaning. Student practice: given a semantic field (emotion: joy, anger, fear, sadness), explain
why the words are not synonyms despite sharing a domain.

### TA-2 — Semantic Fields vs. Collocation (MC-B)

Using the lifeguard-vs-ocean anchor, teach distinguishing shared conceptual domain from mere
frequent co-occurrence. Student practice: given word pairs that frequently co-occur, determine
whether both words share a semantic field or are merely contextually associated.

### TA-3 — Mapping a Semantic Field

Extend TA-1/TA-2: given a conceptual domain (e.g., cooking), generate a list of words that genuinely
belong to that semantic field, excluding words that are merely associated with cooking contexts
(e.g., "kitchen," a location, not a cooking action).

### TA-4 — Full Application: Analyzing an Unfamiliar Semantic Field

Give the student a new conceptual domain (e.g., movement: walk, run, sprint, amble, trudge). They
must confirm the words are not synonyms but share the domain (TA-1), exclude any merely-associated
words that don't belong (TA-2), and produce a mapped semantic field (TA-3) — the full synthesis
task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** The "weather" conceptual domain, fully worked: "rain," "wind," "sunshine,"
"storm" confirmed as a genuine semantic field (not synonyms, but sharing the domain); "umbrella" and
"raincoat" explicitly excluded as objects/tools merely associated with weather through collocation,
not weather phenomena themselves. Annotation confirms both the field-vs-synonym distinction (fixing
MC-A) and the field-vs-collocation distinction (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new conceptual domain (emotion). Student is prompted with guiding questions
("Do these words mean the same thing, or do they just share a broader category? Is this word
actually part of the domain, or is it just something that tends to appear alongside it?") to complete
the mapping.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new conceptual domain, unaided. Student maps the genuine semantic field,
excluding both false synonym assumptions and merely-associated collocations.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Distinguishing a Semantic Field from a Synonym Set**
Given a semantic field's word list, explain why the words are not synonyms despite sharing a
conceptual domain.

**MP-2 [P36] — Distinguishing Semantic-Field Membership from Collocation**
Given a word pair that frequently co-occurs, determine whether both words share a semantic field or
are merely contextually associated.

**MP-3 [P36] — Mapping a Semantic Field**
Given a conceptual domain, generate a list of words that genuinely belong to that semantic field.

**MP-4 [P36] — Excluding a False Member**
Given a candidate word list for a semantic field that includes one merely-associated collocation,
identify and exclude the word that doesn't actually belong.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Analysis of an Unfamiliar Semantic Field**
Given a new conceptual domain, confirm the words share the domain without being synonyms, exclude
any merely-associated words, and produce a mapped semantic field.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a gap in either routes back to the
relevant prerequisite. During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during
TA-1 (expecting synonymy) and MC-B during TA-2 (accepting collocations as field members) — these are
largely independent and often need separate, explicit correction. If either fires, branch to the
relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1
failure indicates residual MC-A; MP-2/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-3/MP-5 failure with
MP-1/MP-2 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-domain mapping example
before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly distinguishes fields from synonym sets on provided examples (MP-1) but
  reverts to synonym-style thinking when mapping a new domain (MP-5), provide a mandatory
  "do these words mean the same thing, or just share a category?" check for every candidate word.
- If the student's field-vs-synonym distinction is strong (MP-1) but they still admit collocations
  into their mapped fields (MP-3, MP-4), require an explicit "is this the SAME KIND of thing, or just
  something associated with it?" check before adding any word to a field.
- If the student struggles to generate field members at all, provide a partial starter list for the
  conceptual domain as scaffolding before free generation.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.vocab.etymology`'s word-history tracing should transfer
  readily to recognizing that a domain's words can have entirely separate origins yet still share a
  conceptual field — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
