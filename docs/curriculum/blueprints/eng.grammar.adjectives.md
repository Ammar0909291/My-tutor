## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-ADJECTIVES-MUST-COME-BEFORE-THE-NOUN
- **Trigger signal:** Student assumes adjectives always sit directly before the noun they modify (attributive position: "the red ball"), failing to recognize predicate adjectives that follow a linking verb and describe the subject from a distance (e.g., "The ball IS red" — "red" still modifies "ball" but sits after the verb "is," not directly before the noun).
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "In 'The ball is red,' does 'red' come right before 'ball,' the way it would in 'the red ball'? Is 'red' still describing 'ball,' just from a different position in the sentence?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Adjectives can appear directly before a noun (attributive position: 'the red ball') OR after a linking verb, describing the subject from a distance (predicate position: 'The ball IS red'). Both are genuine adjective uses — the position changes, but the describing job stays the same."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Check whether a word describes a noun, whether it sits directly before that noun OR appears after a linking verb (is, seems, looks, feels) describing the subject."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "the red ball" (attributive — before the noun) vs. "The ball is red" (predicate — after the linking verb "is").
  - "a happy child" (attributive) vs. "The child seems happy" (predicate, after "seems").
- **s6_path:** "It's a very natural first pattern to notice adjectives right before nouns, since that's the most common and visually obvious position — but predicate adjectives (after linking verbs) are just as valid and common, especially in descriptions and statements about how something IS."

### MC-ADJECTIVE-ORDER-DOESNT-MATTER
- **Trigger signal:** Student places multiple adjectives before a noun in an arbitrary order, unaware that English has a strong (though usually unstated/unconscious for native speakers) conventional order — opinion, size, age, shape, color, origin, material, purpose (e.g., "a lovely small old round red French wooden dining table") — producing sentences that sound distinctly unnatural when the order is scrambled (e.g., "a wooden red table" sounds fine, but "a red wooden nice small table" sounds off).
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "Say 'a wooden old table.' Now say 'an old wooden table.' Which one sounds more natural to a fluent English speaker?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "When stacking multiple adjectives before a noun, English speakers follow a conventional order (roughly: opinion → size → age → shape → color → origin → material → purpose) even without consciously knowing the rule — 'a lovely old wooden table' sounds natural; 'a wooden old lovely table' sounds off, even though every individual word is correct."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "When using multiple adjectives together, check them against the conventional order (opinion, size, age, shape, color, origin, material, purpose) rather than placing them arbitrarily."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "a beautiful small antique round French wooden table" (follows conventional order — opinion, size, age, shape, origin, material) vs. a scrambled version like "a wooden French round antique small beautiful table" (same words, unnatural order).
  - "big red ball" (size before color — natural) vs. "red big ball" (unnatural order).
- **s6_path:** "Most native speakers never consciously learn this rule — they just develop a 'feel' for it through exposure — but having the explicit order as a checklist gives you a genuine shortcut to sounding natural without needing years of exposure."

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The describing-word detective**

> Present an object (a picture or physical item, e.g., a ball). Ask the student to generate as many describing words as possible (color, size, texture, shape). Arrange these words in front of the noun ("the big red bouncy ball") and behind a linking verb ("The ball is big. The ball is red."), showing both positions describe the same object. This anchors adjectives as descriptive words with two valid sentence positions.

---

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Attributive Adjectives (Before the Noun) [C]**

Present sentences with single adjectives directly before nouns; student identifies the adjective and the noun it describes.

**TA-2 — Predicate Adjectives (After Linking Verbs) [C]**

Present sentences with adjectives after linking verbs (is, seems, looks, feels, becomes); student identifies these as adjectives describing the subject, directly targeting MC-ADJECTIVES-MUST-COME-BEFORE-THE-NOUN.

**TA-3 — Comparative and Superlative Forms [C→P]**

Present simple sentences using comparative (-er, more) and superlative (-est, most) adjective forms; student identifies the base adjective and its comparison form.

**TA-4 — Adjective Order with Multiple Adjectives [P]**

Present sets of adjectives describing one noun; student arranges them in the conventional order (opinion, size, age, shape, color, origin, material, purpose), directly targeting MC-ADJECTIVE-ORDER-DOESNT-MATTER.

**TA-5 — Identifying Adjectives in Extended Text [P]**

Student reads a short passage and identifies all adjectives, noting both attributive and predicate positions, integrating both patterns into a realistic reading context.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — attributive adjective)**

> "In 'The blue car sped away,' what's the adjective, and what does it describe?"

Target response: "blue" — describes "car."

**WE-2 (Foundational — predicate adjective)**

> "In 'The car is fast,' what's the adjective, and where does it sit relative to the noun?"

Target response: "fast" — describes "car," but sits after the linking verb "is," not directly before "car."

**WE-3 (Intermediate — adjective order)**

> "Put these in natural order: 'wooden,' 'small,' 'round' — with the noun 'table.'"

Target response: "a small round wooden table" (size → shape → material).

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Attributive adjective, novel sentence**
"In 'The tiny puppy slept,' what's the adjective?"
*Target:* "tiny" — describes "puppy."

**MP-2 [P36] — Predicate adjective, novel sentence**
"In 'The soup tastes delicious,' what's the adjective, and where does it sit?"
*Target:* "delicious" — describes "soup," sits after the linking verb "tastes."

**MP-3 [P36] — Comparative/superlative, novel example**
"In 'This is the tallest building in the city,' what's the base adjective and its form?"
*Target:* base = "tall"; form = superlative ("tallest").

**MP-4 [P36] — Adjective order, novel set**
"Put these in natural order: 'French,' 'old,' 'beautiful' — with the noun 'painting.'"
*Target:* "a beautiful old French painting" (opinion → age → origin).

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain adjective positions and order**
"In your own words: can adjectives come after a verb, not just before a noun? And does the order matter when you use several together?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that adjectives can appear in predicate position (after linking verbs) as well as attributive position (before nouns), and that multiple adjectives follow a conventional order (opinion, size, age, shape, color, origin, material, purpose) for natural-sounding English.

**Protocol routing:** S0 (secure on nouns, new to adjectives): dwell on TA-1's attributive position before introducing predicate adjectives. S1 (already uses adjectives fluently in speech but lacks explicit order-awareness): focus on TA-4's adjective-order checklist, since native-like intuition often lacks explicit, teachable structure. S6 (frustrated by the seemingly arbitrary adjective-order rule): validate that this rule is genuinely unconscious even for native speakers, and that having it explicit is a real advantage, not remedial work. S9 (L1 has a different or no fixed adjective order, or places adjectives after nouns by default, e.g. French, Spanish for many descriptive adjectives): expect both TA-1's basic word-order pattern and TA-4's multi-adjective order to require deliberate, explicit practice, since English's pre-noun default and its specific stacking order are significant, well-documented transfer challenges.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Predicate adjectives deserve equal early attention**: explicitly teach the after-linking-verb position alongside the more visually obvious before-noun position, directly countering the natural single-position assumption.
- **Adjective order is real, learnable, and usually unconscious even for native speakers**: presenting it as an explicit checklist (opinion-size-age-shape-color-origin-material-purpose) gives learners a genuine, high-value shortcut most native speakers never had access to.
- **Comparative/superlative forms connect naturally to base adjectives**: teach these as adjective FORMS rather than a separate category, reinforcing that they're still doing the describing job.
- **Adjective-position transfer for L1s with post-noun defaults**: for S9 learners from L1s that place adjectives after nouns by default, budget dedicated, explicit practice on English's pre-noun default position, treating this as a significant, well-documented transfer challenge rather than a minor detail.
