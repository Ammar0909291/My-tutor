**Concept:** Rhetorical Devices — deliberate stylistic and structural techniques, such as
repetition, parallelism, and antithesis, used to enhance persuasive or expressive effect.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Rhetorical Appeals (prerequisite) teaches the three
broad LEVERS of persuasion — ethos, pathos, logos. Rhetorical Devices teaches the specific language
TOOLS a writer uses to execute those appeals more powerfully — a well-placed repetition can intensify
pathos, a balanced antithesis can sharpen a logical contrast for logos. This concept unlocks
`eng.composition.figurative-language-in-composition` (devices are the structural cousin of
figurative language, which works through imagery and comparison rather than pattern and structure)
and `eng.composition.rhetorical-analysis` (analyzing a real speech or text for its persuasive
strategy requires first being able to name the specific devices at work).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) identify at least three rhetorical devices
(repetition, parallelism, antithesis) in a given passage, citing the exact words that create the
pattern, (b) explain the specific persuasive or expressive EFFECT each identified device produces
(not just naming it), and (c) deliberately use at least one device in their own short piece of
persuasive or expressive writing.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-NAMING-THE-DEVICE-IS-THE-SAME-AS-EXPLAINING-ITS-EFFECT

**Trigger signal:** Student correctly labels a rhetorical device (e.g., "this is repetition") but
stops there, without explaining WHAT EFFECT the device produces in that specific passage — treating
identification as the entire task rather than the first half of it.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a passage using repetition (e.g., a speech repeating
"we will..." at the start of several sentences) and two student responses: one that says only "this
uses repetition" and one that says "this uses repetition of 'we will' at the start of each sentence,
which builds a driving, escalating sense of momentum and collective determination as the list
grows." Ask: "Which response actually tells us anything about why the writer chose this technique?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Naming a device is like correctly identifying a tool as 'a hammer' — true,
but it doesn't tell anyone what the hammer actually DID in this specific situation. Did it build
something, break something, drive in a specific nail? A rhetorical device analysis needs both parts:
what tool was used, AND what job it did in this exact passage."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "After naming a device, always add: 'which creates the effect of ___' or
'which makes the reader feel/think ___.' If your analysis stops at the device's name, you've done
half the job."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "This is an example of parallelism." (naming only, no effect explained) vs. "This uses
   parallelism — 'to fight, to build, to hope' — which places these three actions on equal footing,
   suggesting they are all equally urgent and connected." (naming plus specific effect).
2. A student analysis that lists five devices by name with zero explanation of effect (surface-level
   labeling) vs. an analysis that identifies two devices, each with a specific, textually-grounded
   explanation of its persuasive or expressive effect (deeper, effect-focused analysis).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to stop at naming after the bridge, require them to complete
the sentence stem "This [device name] creates the effect of ___ because ___" for every device they
identify, making the effect-explanation step structurally unavoidable.

### MC-B-ANY-REPEATED-WORD-OR-BALANCED-PHRASE-IS-AUTOMATICALLY-A-DELIBERATE-DEVICE

**Trigger signal:** Student labels any incidental repetition or coincidentally balanced phrasing in a
text as a deliberate rhetorical device, without considering whether the repetition/balance is
purposeful and meaningful or simply an artifact of ordinary sentence construction — e.g., calling the
repetition of the word "the" throughout a paragraph "repetition for effect."

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a passage with two kinds of repetition: the ordinary,
functional repetition of small grammatical words ("the," "and," "a") that appear many times simply
because language requires them, and a deliberate repetition of a meaningful phrase at structurally
significant points (e.g., the same clause opening three consecutive sentences, each building toward
a climax). Ask: "Both repeat words. Which repetition feels like it was chosen on purpose for an
effect, and which is just how sentences normally work?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Not every repeated word is a rhetorical choice — that's like calling every
single brick in a building 'architecture' just because it repeats. Real rhetorical repetition is
noticeable, structurally placed (often at the start or end of parallel clauses), and tied to
meaning — it stands out from the ordinary background repetition that all language naturally has."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before calling something a deliberate device, ask: is this repetition or
pattern noticeable and structurally placed, or is it just ordinary words like 'the' or 'and'
repeating because language requires them? Devices are chosen; background repetition is just how
sentences work."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. The word "the" appearing eight times in a paragraph (ordinary, unavoidable repetition, not a
   device) vs. the phrase "I have a dream" opening four consecutive sentences (deliberate,
   structurally placed repetition — a genuine device).
2. A coincidentally similar sentence length appearing twice in unrelated parts of a text (not
   meaningfully connected, not a deliberate device) vs. two clauses of matching grammatical
   structure placed side by side specifically to highlight a contrast ("ask not what your country can
   do for you — ask what you can do for your country" — deliberate antithesis).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to over-identify ordinary repetition as deliberate devices
after the bridge, have them apply a "would removing this change the meaning or impact?" test — genuine
devices, when removed or flattened into plain restatement, visibly weaken the passage's effect;
ordinary grammatical repetition does not.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.composition.rhetorical-appeals`):** "Identify which appeal — ethos, pathos, or
logos — this short passage primarily uses." A student who cannot identify basic appeals lacks the
persuasive-purpose foundation rhetorical devices are meant to serve — route to
`eng.composition.rhetorical-appeals` review first.

**PD-2 (checks basic pattern-noticing, informal pre-check):** "Read this short passage aloud. Does
anything about its structure or word choice feel deliberately patterned or repeated?" A student who
notices nothing at all needs a brief primer on close reading for pattern before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Hammer-and-Job Anchor.** Show a hammer alongside three photos: a nail driven cleanly into
wood, a broken window, and a picture frame being assembled. Ask: "Naming the tool ('hammer') doesn't
tell us which of these three jobs it did. What additional information do we need?" This directly
seeds the fix for MC-A — device-naming alone is like naming the tool without saying what job it
did. Extend the anchor by asking: "If I tap something gently with the hammer just because my hand
happened to bump it, is that the same as deliberately using the hammer to build something?" —
seeding the fix for MC-B, since accidental contact with a tool isn't the same as a deliberate,
purposeful use of it.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Recognizing Repetition, Parallelism, and Antithesis

Introduce the three named devices with clear definitions and canonical examples (repetition of a
word or phrase for emphasis; parallelism as matching grammatical structure across a series;
antithesis as a balanced contrast of opposing ideas in parallel structure). Student practice: given
six short passages, identify which device (if any) each uses.

### TA-2 — Distinguishing Deliberate Devices from Ordinary Repetition

Directly address MC-B using the "would removing this change the impact?" test. Student practice:
given eight examples of repeated or patterned language, sort into "deliberate device" and "ordinary,
unavoidable repetition."

### TA-3 — Explaining Effect, Not Just Naming (MC-A)

Using the sentence-stem requirement ("This [device] creates the effect of ___ because ___"), teach
students to move from naming to explaining. Student practice: given five correctly-identified
devices with no effect explanation, write the missing effect explanation for each.

### TA-4 — Using a Device Deliberately in Original Writing

Give the student a short persuasive or expressive writing prompt and have them deliberately
incorporate at least one rhetorical device, then explain in their own words what effect they intended
it to produce — practicing production, not just analysis.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Passage: a short excerpt using antithesis ("Ask not what your country can do
for you — ask what you can do for your country"). Fully worked: device identified (antithesis),
textual evidence cited (the parallel "what X can do for Y" structure with the two halves reversed),
and effect explained (the reversal reframes the listener's expected relationship to their country,
shifting emphasis from receiving to contributing). Annotation confirms both the naming-plus-effect
requirement (fixing MC-A) and that this is clearly a deliberate, structurally placed device, not
ordinary repetition (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New passage using repetition (a short excerpt repeating a phrase at the start of
several sentences). Student is prompted with guiding questions ("What phrase repeats, and where is
it placed structurally? Does removing the repetition change the passage's impact — if so, how? What
specific effect does the repetition create here — urgency, building momentum, emphasis?") to complete
the identification, distinction, and effect explanation.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new passage using parallelism, unaided. Student identifies the device,
cites textual evidence, distinguishes it from ordinary repetition, and explains its specific effect —
then writes one original sentence using the same device for a self-chosen purpose.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Device Identification with Evidence**
Given three passages, identify which rhetorical device (repetition, parallelism, or antithesis) each
uses, citing the exact words that create the pattern.

**MP-2 [P36] — Deliberate Device vs. Ordinary Repetition**
Given four examples of repeated or patterned language, sort into "deliberate device" and "ordinary,
unavoidable repetition," applying the "would removing this change the impact?" test.

**MP-3 [P36] — Explaining Effect**
Given a correctly-identified device with no effect explanation, write a specific, textually-grounded
explanation of the effect it produces.

**MP-4 [P36] — Combined Identification and Effect Analysis**
Given a new passage, identify the device used, cite evidence, and explain its effect in one
combined response, without being prompted to do each step separately.

**MP-5 [P36] — Deliberate Original Use**
Write two original sentences using a specified rhetorical device (assigned: parallelism or
antithesis) for a self-chosen persuasive purpose, and explain the intended effect.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; an appeals gap routes back to that
prerequisite, a pattern-noticing gap gets a brief primer. During Component 4 (S1, active instruction),
MC-B typically surfaces during TA-1/TA-2 (over-identification during initial recognition practice)
and MC-A during TA-3 (stopping at naming) — MC-B tends to appear first chronologically since students
start by over-eagerly spotting patterns before learning to explain effect. If either fires, branch to
the relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1
failure indicates basic device recognition (TA-1) needs review; MP-2 failure alone indicates residual
MC-B; MP-3 failure alone indicates residual MC-A; MP-4 failure with MP-1 through MP-3 passing
indicates an integration gap between the separately-practiced skills; MP-5 failure alone indicates a
production/transfer gap — provide one more guided production example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student can identify and explain devices in others' writing (MP-1 through MP-4) but
  struggles to deploy them naturally in original writing (MP-5), treat as a production gap and
  provide an additional guided production example emphasizing that devices should serve the writer's
  actual intended effect, not be forced in artificially.
- If the student's own attempts at using devices (WE-3, MP-5) feel forced or ornamental rather than
  purposeful, connect back to the rhetorical-appeals concept: which appeal is this device meant to
  strengthen, and does it actually do so here?
- If the student conflates rhetorical devices with the literary devices from
  `eng.literature.literary-devices-overview` (assuming identical terminology and purpose), clarify
  the distinction: rhetorical devices here serve persuasive/expressive composition purposes, while
  the cross-linked literary devices concept covers a broader analytical toolkit for reading fiction
  and poetry — related, not identical, vocabularies.
- Students with strong pattern-recognition instincts but weak explanatory writing may benefit from
  oral rehearsal of the effect-explanation ("say the effect out loud before writing it down") before
  attempting MP-3/MP-4 in written form.
