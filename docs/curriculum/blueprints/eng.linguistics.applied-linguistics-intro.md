**Concept:** Introduction to Applied Linguistics — a survey of PRACTICAL applications of linguistic
theory to real-world problems, including language teaching methodology, language testing/assessment
design, and language policy, all grounded in the descriptive linguistic findings from prior concepts
rather than personal intuition or tradition alone.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Sociolinguistics (prerequisite) and Language Acquisition
(prerequisite) together establish how language varies socially and how it's actually learned. Applied
Linguistics teaches how these DESCRIPTIVE findings get applied to solve practical problems — designing
better language-teaching methods (informed by acquisition research), designing fairer language tests
(informed by sociolinguistic awareness of dialect variation), and shaping language policy (informed by
both). This concept unlocks `eng.linguistics.bilingualism-and-multilingualism` (understanding
bilingual individuals and communities draws on applied linguistic principles about teaching, testing,
and policy).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) explain that effective language-teaching methods
should be grounded in acquisition research findings (e.g., the value of meaningful input and
communicative practice) rather than tradition or intuition alone, (b) identify at least one way a
language test can be unfair if it fails to account for legitimate dialect variation, distinguishing
testing a skill from penalizing a dialect, and (c) explain that language policy decisions (e.g.,
which language(s) to use in schools or government) involve real social trade-offs and are not purely
neutral, technical decisions.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-ANY-LANGUAGE-TEACHING-METHOD-THAT-FEELS-TRADITIONAL-OR-FAMILIAR-MUST-BE-EFFECTIVE

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes that a language-teaching method is effective simply because it
feels traditional, familiar, or was the way they themselves were taught, without evaluating it
against actual acquisition research findings — e.g., assuming rote memorization of isolated
vocabulary lists or endless grammar-rule drilling with no communicative practice must be effective
teaching simply because it is common practice.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two teaching approaches for the same grammar point: one
that relies purely on memorizing rule statements and completing isolated drill exercises with no
communicative context, and one that embeds the same grammar point in meaningful, communicative
practice (using the structure to actually accomplish a real communicative task). Ask: "Does language
acquisition research (from the prerequisite concept) suggest which approach better matches how people
actually internalize language — rule memorization alone, or meaningful, communicative use?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming a traditional teaching method must be effective is like assuming a
centuries-old medical remedy must work simply because it's been used for a long time — but medicine
progresses by testing traditional practices against actual evidence, keeping what works and
discarding what doesn't. Applied linguistics does the same for language teaching: methods should be
evaluated against real acquisition research, not assumed effective just because they're familiar or
long-standing."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before assuming a teaching method is effective, ask: does it align with
what acquisition research actually shows about how people internalize language (meaningful input,
communicative practice, systematic exposure), or does it rely purely on tradition and unverified
intuition?"

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Rote memorization of isolated vocabulary lists with no communicative context (traditional but not
   well-supported by acquisition research) vs. vocabulary embedded in meaningful, communicative
   practice (supported by the research on how people actually acquire and retain new vocabulary).
2. A student assuming a teaching method must work because "that's how everyone learns it"
   (misconception) vs. a student asking whether a specific method actually aligns with known
   acquisition principles before endorsing it (correct, evidence-based reasoning).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to assume traditional methods are effective by default after
the bridge, have them evaluate two more teaching approaches against a specific acquisition-research
finding studied earlier (e.g., overregularization evidence for active rule-construction), determining
whether each approach's assumptions about HOW learning happens are actually consistent with that
evidence.

### MC-B-A-STANDARDIZED-LANGUAGE-TEST-THAT-USES-ONE-DIALECT-IS-A-NEUTRAL-FAIR-MEASURE-OF-LANGUAGE-SKILL

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes a standardized language test using only one prestige dialect's
norms is a neutral, purely technical measure of a student's underlying language ability, failing to
recognize that such a test can unfairly penalize speakers of other, equally legitimate and systematic
dialects for features that reflect dialect difference rather than any genuine deficiency in the
underlying skill being tested.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a hypothetical test item that marks a systematic,
legitimate dialect feature (studied in the Accents and Dialects/Sociolinguistics concepts) as
"incorrect," alongside the same underlying skill (e.g., reading comprehension) tested through content
that doesn't penalize dialect variation. Ask: "Is the first test measuring the actual skill it claims
to measure (reading comprehension), or is it partly measuring something else (conformity to one
specific dialect) disguised as a skill measurement?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming a one-dialect standardized test is neutral is like assuming a
height-measuring device calibrated only in one unit system is 'unbiased' just because it produces a
number — but if it systematically produces lower readings for people using an equally valid
measurement convention, it's not actually measuring height fairly across everyone. A test that
penalizes systematic, legitimate dialect features isn't neutrally measuring the underlying skill — it's
partly measuring dialect conformity, which isn't the same thing."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When evaluating whether a test is fair, ask: does it accurately measure
the intended skill for speakers of ALL relevant dialects, or does it inadvertently penalize systematic,
legitimate dialect features that have nothing to do with the actual skill being tested? A test that
conflates dialect conformity with skill isn't neutral."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A reading comprehension test that marks a legitimate dialect grammatical feature as an "error" in
   a student's written response, when the underlying comprehension is genuinely demonstrated
   (conflating dialect with skill, unfair) vs. a test designed to assess comprehension without
   penalizing dialect-specific grammatical features unrelated to comprehension itself (fair, skill-
   focused).
2. A student assuming any standardized test using one dialect's norms must be objective and fair
   (misconception) vs. a student recognizing that such a test can systematically disadvantage
   dialect-speakers for reasons unrelated to the actual skill being measured (correct, critical
   understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to view one-dialect tests as automatically neutral after the
bridge, have them identify a specific test item that could disadvantage a dialect speaker and propose
a way to test the same underlying skill without penalizing the dialect feature.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.linguistics.sociolinguistics-intro`):** "Is code-switching evidence of confusion
or of skilled bilingual competence?" A student who cannot answer correctly lacks the foundational,
non-judgmental framing this concept applies to testing and policy — route to
`eng.linguistics.sociolinguistics-intro` review first.

**PD-2 (checks `eng.linguistics.language-acquisition-intro`):** "Is a child's overregularization error
('goed') evidence of failed imitation or active rule-construction?" A student who cannot reason about
this correctly lacks the acquisition-research foundation this concept applies to teaching methodology
— route to `eng.linguistics.language-acquisition-intro` review before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Traditional-Medicine-Testing and Miscalibrated-Height-Device Anchor.** Combine two images:
medicine testing traditional remedies against real evidence, keeping what works and discarding what
doesn't, rather than assuming tradition equals effectiveness — directly seeding the fix for MC-A
(evaluate teaching methods against acquisition research, not just familiarity). Second, a height-
measuring device calibrated only to one unit system, producing systematically distorted results for
anyone using a different, equally valid convention — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (a one-dialect
test can conflate dialect conformity with the actual skill being measured).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Evaluating Teaching Methods Against Acquisition Research (MC-A)

Using the traditional-medicine anchor, teach students to evaluate teaching methods against actual
acquisition findings rather than assuming tradition equals effectiveness. Student practice: given
three teaching approaches, evaluate each against a specific acquisition-research principle studied
earlier.

### TA-2 — Testing and Assessment: Skill vs. Dialect Conflation (MC-B)

Using the miscalibrated-height-device anchor, teach students to identify when a test conflates
dialect conformity with the underlying skill being measured. Student practice: given three test items,
identify which ones risk penalizing legitimate dialect features rather than genuinely measuring the
intended skill.

### TA-3 — Language Policy Trade-offs

Introduce language policy as involving genuine social trade-offs (e.g., which language(s) to use in
schools) rather than purely neutral technical decisions. Student practice: given a hypothetical
language policy scenario, identify at least one genuine trade-off or competing value at stake.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Evaluating a Real-World Applied Linguistics Scenario

Give the student a new scenario involving teaching, testing, or policy. They must evaluate it against
acquisition research (TA-1) or dialect-fairness principles (TA-2) as appropriate, and identify any
policy trade-offs involved (TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Scenario: a school considering whether to adopt a new vocabulary-teaching
method (isolated flashcard memorization) versus a communicative-practice-based method. Fully worked:
evaluated against the acquisition-research principle that meaningful, communicative use supports
stronger retention than isolated memorization; a testing scenario is also examined (a spelling test
item that penalizes a dialect-consistent pronunciation-based spelling), identified as conflating
dialect with the actual spelling-knowledge skill; a policy trade-off noted (adopting a new method
requires teacher retraining resources, a genuine practical trade-off). Annotation confirms the
evidence-based teaching evaluation (fixing MC-A) and the dialect/skill distinction (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New scenario: a test item that could penalize a dialect feature in a math word
problem's language, and a proposed new bilingual education policy. Student is prompted with guiding
questions ("Does this test item risk conflating a dialect feature with the actual math skill being
tested? What genuine trade-off does the bilingual education policy involve — what's gained, and what
resource or political challenge does it raise?") to complete the analysis.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new applied linguistics scenario, unaided. Student evaluates it against
acquisition research and/or dialect-fairness principles as appropriate, and identifies a genuine
policy trade-off if relevant.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Evaluating a Teaching Method**
Given a teaching method description, evaluate it against a specific acquisition-research principle
and determine whether it aligns with or contradicts that principle.

**MP-2 [P36] — Identifying Dialect-Conflation in Testing**
Given three test items, identify which one(s) risk penalizing legitimate dialect features rather than
genuinely measuring the intended skill.

**MP-3 [P36] — Proposing a Fairer Test Alternative**
Given a test item that conflates dialect with skill, propose a way to test the same underlying skill
without penalizing the dialect feature.

**MP-4 [P36] — Identifying a Policy Trade-off**
Given a language policy scenario, identify at least one genuine trade-off or competing value at
stake.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Applied Linguistics Evaluation**
Given a new scenario involving teaching, testing, or policy, evaluate it against the appropriate
principle (acquisition research or dialect fairness) and identify any policy trade-offs involved.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a gap in either prerequisite routes
back before Component 3. During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during
TA-1 (defaulting to tradition) and MC-B during TA-2 (assuming one-dialect tests are neutral) — these
are largely independent and often need separate, explicit correction. If either fires, branch to the
relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1
failure indicates residual MC-A; MP-2/MP-3 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-4 failure alone
indicates the policy-trade-off extension (TA-3) needs review, distinct from either misconception;
MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided
full-scenario example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters teaching-method evaluation (MP-1) but struggles specifically with test-
  fairness analysis (MP-2, MP-3), revisit the underlying dialect/skill distinction from
  `eng.phonetics.accents-and-dialects` and `eng.linguistics.sociolinguistics-intro` before continuing,
  since this concept assumes that distinction is already solid.
- If the student proposes fairer test alternatives (MP-3) that are technically dialect-neutral but
  impractically complex to implement at scale, acknowledge the practical trade-off explicitly — this
  connects naturally to TA-3's policy trade-offs theme.
- If the student's policy trade-off identification (MP-4) remains one-sided (only naming benefits or
  only naming costs of a policy), push for identifying genuine trade-offs on BOTH sides, since real
  policy decisions rarely have costless "right answers."
- Students with personal experience of being tested in a dialect or language variety different from
  their own home variety may have strong, relevant intuitions for MC-B — invite this experience
  explicitly and validatingly if the student is comfortable sharing it, since it can ground the
  abstract fairness principle powerfully.
