## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-REVISING-MEANS-FIXING-SURFACE-ERRORS-LIKE-SPELLING-AND-GRAMMAR
- **Trigger signal:** Student, when asked to "revise" a draft, focuses on correcting spelling, grammar, and punctuation errors — activities that actually belong to editing — rather than reconsidering the big-picture content questions revision is specifically about: is the argument clear, is the organization logical, is evidence sufficient, does each section actually serve the piece's purpose.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present a draft with perfect spelling/grammar but a confusing, poorly-organized argument that jumps between unrelated points, alongside the same draft's content-revised version (reorganized, with a clearer argument, but still containing a few surface typos). Ask which version is actually the stronger, more improved piece of writing.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Revising for content means reconsidering the big-picture questions — is the argument clear and well-supported, is the organization logical, does each part serve the piece's purpose — not correcting spelling or grammar. A draft with flawless spelling but confusing organization is a bigger problem than a well-organized draft with a few typos; content revision addresses the bigger problem first."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "When revising for content, don't get distracted by spelling or grammar — focus entirely on the big-picture questions: is my argument clear? Is my evidence sufficient and well-placed? Does my organization make logical sense? Save the surface-level correction for the later editing stage."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Revision-as-surface-correction (revising means fixing spelling, grammar, and punctuation errors) vs. (b) Revision-as-content-reconsideration (revising means reconsidering big-picture content — argument clarity, organization, evidence sufficiency — deferring surface correction to editing).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1 re-check, then re-run TA-1's content-vs-surface sort before continuing.

### MC-A-DRAFT-WITH-ENOUGH-WORDS-OR-PARAGRAPHS-DOESNT-NEED-CONTENT-REVISION
- **Trigger signal:** Student judges a draft's completeness by its length or paragraph count alone (assuming a draft that "looks long enough" must already be adequately developed), rather than actually evaluating whether the content genuinely supports the argument, has sufficient evidence, and organizes logically — potentially missing that a long draft can still have significant content gaps, weak evidence, or disorganization that genuinely needs revision regardless of length.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present a long draft (many paragraphs, substantial word count) that actually repeats the same point multiple times with minimal new evidence, alongside a shorter, more tightly-organized draft that develops fewer points but with genuinely sufficient evidence for each. Ask which one actually needs more content revision, regardless of which is longer.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Length and paragraph count don't measure content quality — a long draft can still have real content gaps, insufficient evidence, or repetitive, undeveloped points, while a shorter draft can be genuinely well-developed. Judge content adequacy by evaluating the argument, evidence, and organization directly, not by how much text there is."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't assume a draft is content-complete just because it's long. Evaluate directly: does each point have sufficient evidence? Is there repetition without new development? Is the organization logical? A draft can genuinely need content revision regardless of its length."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Length-as-completeness (a draft with sufficient word count/paragraph count is adequately developed and doesn't need content revision) vs. (b) Direct-content-evaluation (content adequacy must be judged by evaluating argument clarity, evidence sufficiency, and organization directly, independent of length).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1, then re-present the Concrete Anchor's empty-calories framing before continuing to TA-3.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

### PD-1: Drafting Readiness
- **Tests:** Can the student already produce a complete rough draft, marking uncertain spots without stopping the drafting process — the raw material content revision reworks?
- **Probe:** Ask the student to describe their most recent drafting experience.
- **Pass condition:** Describes producing a complete (even if rough) draft.
- **Fail routing:** If the student has no complete-draft experience, route to `eng.writing.drafting` review — content revision assumes a complete draft already exists to rework.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**"Empty Calories vs. Nutritious Content."** Present two "meal plates": one visually large and full-looking but consisting mostly of empty-calorie filler (repeated identical items, little actual nutritional variety), and one smaller but genuinely nutritionally complete (a balanced, sufficient meal with real substance). Demonstrate that the large plate LOOKS more complete but doesn't actually nourish — directly seeding MC-A-DRAFT-WITH-ENOUGH-WORDS-OR-PARAGRAPHS-DOESNT-NEED-CONTENT-REVISION's fix (length isn't content quality). Separately, present a "surface polish" spray bottle (for shining/cleaning the plate's surface — analogous to editing) next to a "recipe redesign" card (analogous to content revision — reworking what's actually IN the meal) and confirm spraying a badly-composed meal's plate doesn't fix its actual content problem, seeding MC-REVISING-MEANS-FIXING-SURFACE-ERRORS-LIKE-SPELLING-AND-GRAMMAR's fix.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1: Content vs. Surface Concern Sorting
Present a checklist of potential draft issues (a confusing paragraph, a spelling error, insufficient evidence for a claim, a missing transition, a comma splice, an underdeveloped point) and have the student sort each as content (revision) or surface (editing). Directly counters MC-REVISING-MEANS-FIXING-SURFACE-ERRORS-LIKE-SPELLING-AND-GRAMMAR.

### TA-2: Evaluating Evidence Sufficiency
Given a draft paragraph making a claim, student evaluates whether the provided evidence genuinely supports the claim adequately or whether more/better evidence is needed, regardless of the paragraph's length.

### TA-3: Direct Content Evaluation, Not Length-Based Judgment
Using the discrimination pairs from MC-A-DRAFT-WITH-ENOUGH-WORDS-OR-PARAGRAPHS-DOESNT-NEED-CONTENT-REVISION, present a long, repetitive draft and a shorter, well-developed draft and have the student identify which genuinely needs more content revision, based on direct evaluation rather than length.

### TA-4: Reorganizing for Logical Flow
Given a draft with disorganized paragraph order (a logical argument scrambled), student reorders the content into a more logical sequence.

### TA-5: Full Content Revision of an Original Draft
Student revises their own or a provided draft for content: strengthening evidence, reorganizing for logic, and cutting or developing underdeveloped sections, without touching surface-level errors — the generative capstone.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

### WE-1: Content vs. Surface Sorting (Scaffolded)
**Issues list:** "Paragraph 3 doesn't have enough evidence for its claim" (content) / "There's a comma splice in sentence 5" (surface) / "The essay jumps from point A to point C without explaining the connection" (content) / "'recieve' should be 'receive'" (surface).
**Walkthrough:** Sort correctly, confirming content issues require rethinking/rewriting while surface issues are simple corrections deferred to editing.

### WE-2: Evidence Sufficiency Evaluation (Guided)
**Claim with evidence:** "Exercise improves mental health. Many people feel better after exercising."
**Walkthrough:** Evaluate: the evidence ("many people feel better") is vague and unsupported by specifics. Identify the need for more concrete evidence (a specific study, statistic, or detailed example) regardless of how long the paragraph already is.

### WE-3: Full Content Revision (Independent)
**Draft:** A 5-paragraph essay that is lengthy but repeats its main point three times with minimal new development, has one paragraph out of logical order, and lacks evidence for its central claim.
**Walkthrough:** Student revises: cuts the redundant repetition, reorders the misplaced paragraph, and adds/strengthens evidence for the central claim — producing a shorter but more genuinely developed piece.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Sort content vs. surface issues**
Given a list of draft issues, student correctly sorts each as content (revision) or surface (editing). *(Directly targets MC-REVISING-MEANS-FIXING-SURFACE-ERRORS-LIKE-SPELLING-AND-GRAMMAR.)*

**MP-2 [P36] — Identify content gaps regardless of length**
Given a long but repetitive/underdeveloped draft and a shorter, well-developed one, student correctly identifies which genuinely needs more content revision, independent of length. *(Directly targets MC-A-DRAFT-WITH-ENOUGH-WORDS-OR-PARAGRAPHS-DOESNT-NEED-CONTENT-REVISION.)*

**MP-3 [P36] — Evaluate evidence sufficiency**
Given a claim and its supporting evidence, student evaluates whether the evidence is genuinely sufficient or needs strengthening.

**MP-4 [P36] — Reorganize for logical flow**
Given a draft with scrambled paragraph order, student reorders it into a logical sequence.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full content revision of an original draft**
Student revises a draft for content — strengthening evidence, reorganizing, and cutting/developing sections — without addressing surface-level errors.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** At session open (S0), confirm PD-1 passes — content revision reworks an already-complete rough draft, so a student without complete-draft experience has nothing concrete to revise. During instruction (S1), watch for both misconception patterns: conflating revision with surface correction (MC-REVISING-MEANS-FIXING-SURFACE-ERRORS-LIKE-SPELLING-AND-GRAMMAR) and judging completeness by length (MC-A-DRAFT-WITH-ENOUGH-WORDS-OR-PARAGRAPHS-DOESNT-NEED-CONTENT-REVISION) both substitute a shallow, surface-level heuristic for genuine content evaluation — redirect both to the Empty Calories anchor. If the student focuses on spelling/grammar or judges a draft "done" by its length during TA-1/TA-3 (S6), drop back to the Concrete Anchor's demonstrations before returning to independent revision. At mastery-probe stage (S9), do not certify mastery unless MP-1 and MP-2 both pass — a student who evaluates evidence and reorganizes well (MP-3/MP-4) but still conflates revision with editing or judges by length has not learned the core content-evaluation judgment this concept requires.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student focuses on spelling/grammar instead of content, weight remediation toward MC-REVISING-MEANS-FIXING-SURFACE-ERRORS-LIKE-SPELLING-AND-GRAMMAR's bridge text and re-run TA-1's content-vs-surface sort before returning to independent revision.
- If the student judges a draft's completeness by its length, weight remediation toward MC-A-DRAFT-WITH-ENOUGH-WORDS-OR-PARAGRAPHS-DOESNT-NEED-CONTENT-REVISION's bridge text and TA-3's direct-content-evaluation practice specifically.
- This concept feeds one direct downstream unlock (`eng.writing.editing-and-proofreading`) — do not certify mastery without TA-5's full content revision, since editing/proofreading assumes the content is already solid and ready for surface-level polish.
- Reuse the "empty calories vs. nutritious content" framing explicitly when introducing `eng.writing.editing-and-proofreading` — that concept is where the surface-level "spray and polish" work finally happens, appropriately, after content is genuinely solid.
