## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-THE-SAME-REGISTER-AND-STYLE-WORKS-ACROSS-ALL-DIGITAL-PLATFORMS
- **Trigger signal:** Student applies one uniform digital communication style (either always casual or always formal) across every platform — texting, email, professional messaging apps, social media — without recognizing that different digital platforms carry different register expectations, just as spoken contexts do, and that a message appropriate on one platform can be a mismatch on another.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present the same casual message ("hey can u send the report? thx") sent to a close friend via text (appropriate) versus sent to a supervisor via professional email (a register mismatch, likely to seem unprofessional). Ask whether the SAME message can be equally appropriate across every digital platform.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Different digital platforms carry different register expectations, just like spoken contexts do — a text to a friend, a professional email, and a public social media post each call for a different register. A message that fits perfectly on one platform can be a real mismatch on another, even though both are 'digital communication.'"
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Before sending a digital message, consider the specific platform and audience: does this call for the same casual register as a text to a friend, or does it need the more formal, professional register appropriate for email to a supervisor or colleague? Match your register to the specific platform and relationship, not a single uniform digital style."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Uniform-digital-register (one register/style works appropriately across all digital platforms) vs. (b) Platform-specific register (different digital platforms carry different register expectations requiring the writer to match style to the specific platform and relationship).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1 re-check, then re-run TA-1's platform-register matching before continuing.

### MC-TONE-IS-EASY-TO-CONVEY-IN-TEXT-BASED-DIGITAL-COMMUNICATION
- **Trigger signal:** Student assumes their intended tone (sarcasm, humor, gentle criticism) will be clearly understood in text-based digital communication the same way it would in face-to-face speech, underestimating how easily tone is misread in text without vocal inflection, facial expression, or immediate clarifying feedback — leading to messages that are frequently misinterpreted as harsher, colder, or different in meaning than intended.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present a message intended as light sarcasm ("Oh great, ANOTHER meeting, can't wait.") sent via text with no additional context, and ask how a reader who doesn't know the sender well might interpret it — genuinely annoyed versus playfully sarcastic — noting that both readings are plausible without vocal tone or facial expression to disambiguate.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Text-based digital communication strips away vocal tone, facial expression, and the immediate back-and-forth clarification that spoken communication has — this makes tone (especially sarcasm, subtle humor, or gentle criticism) genuinely easy to misread. What feels obviously playful to the sender can land as cold, confusing, or even hostile to the reader."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Assume tone can be easily misread in text-based digital communication, especially with people who don't know you well. Consider adding explicit tone markers (a clarifying phrase, an emoji if appropriate to the context, or simply rephrasing to be more direct) when there's real risk of misinterpretation, particularly for sarcasm or criticism."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Tone-transfers-easily assumption (intended tone will be clearly understood in text the same way it would in speech) vs. (b) Tone-is-fragile-in-text recognition (text strips away vocal/facial cues, making tone genuinely easy to misread, especially for sarcasm or subtle emotional content).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1, then re-present the Concrete Anchor's muted-instrument framing before continuing to TA-3.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

### PD-1: Register-and-Formality Readiness
- **Tests:** Can the student already judge register appropriateness for a specific audience/context — the foundational judgment digital-communication skill applies specifically to different online/messaging platforms?
- **Probe:** Ask the student to identify whether a casual message would be appropriate in a formal context.
- **Pass condition:** Correctly judges register-context fit.
- **Fail routing:** If register judgment is weak, route to `eng.vocab.register-and-formality` review — digital communication applies this same judgment specifically to platform-specific contexts.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**"The Platform Dial and the Muted Instrument."** Present a dial with settings for different digital platforms (Text to a Friend, Professional Email, Social Media Post, Work Messaging App) and have the student turn the dial to select the appropriate register for a given message purpose, confirming the same content needs different phrasing across settings — directly seeding MC-THE-SAME-REGISTER-AND-STYLE-WORKS-ACROSS-ALL-DIGITAL-PLATFORMS's fix. Separately, present a musical instrument playing through a thick mute (muffling tone/expression) to represent text-based communication — versus the same instrument playing openly (full tonal expression) to represent face-to-face speech — demonstrating that subtlety and tone genuinely get lost/muffled in the text "instrument," seeding MC-TONE-IS-EASY-TO-CONVEY-IN-TEXT-BASED-DIGITAL-COMMUNICATION's fix.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1: Platform-Register Matching
Present 4 messages with the same basic content but requiring different registers for different platforms (text to friend, professional email, work chat, public social post) and have the student match/adjust register appropriately for each. Directly counters MC-THE-SAME-REGISTER-AND-STYLE-WORKS-ACROSS-ALL-DIGITAL-PLATFORMS.

### TA-2: Tone-Risk Assessment
Present messages with tone-ambiguous content (sarcasm, brief criticism, dry humor) and have the student assess the risk of misinterpretation for each, identifying which need tone-clarifying adjustments.

### TA-3: Adding Tone Clarity
Using the discrimination pairs from MC-TONE-IS-EASY-TO-CONVEY-IN-TEXT-BASED-DIGITAL-COMMUNICATION, present a tone-risky message and have the student revise it to reduce misinterpretation risk (rephrasing, adding a clarifying phrase, or an appropriate marker for the context).

### TA-4: Platform-Specific Conventions
Teach basic conventions for common platforms (email: subject lines, greetings/sign-offs; professional chat: brevity but still respectful; social media: public/permanent nature of posts) and have the student identify appropriate conventions for 3 platform scenarios.

### TA-5: Composing Platform-Appropriate Messages for a Novel Scenario
Given one message purpose, student composes 3 versions appropriately adapted for 3 different platforms/audiences, applying both register-matching and tone-clarity principles — the generative capstone.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

### WE-1: Platform-Register Matching (Scaffolded)
**Content:** Asking a colleague to review a document. **Text to a close work friend:** "hey, can u take a quick look at this when u get a sec?" **Professional email to a supervisor:** "Hi [Name], when you have a moment, could you please review the attached document? Thank you for your time."
**Walkthrough:** Confirm both convey the same request but with appropriately different registers for their respective platforms/relationships.

### WE-2: Tone-Risk Revision (Guided)
**Risky message:** "Sure, THAT'S going to work out great." (sarcastic, easily misread as genuine or hostile in text)
**Walkthrough:** Revise for clarity: "I have some concerns about this approach — can we discuss alternatives?" (directly conveys the underlying concern without relying on tone that could be misread).

### WE-3: Multi-Platform Composition (Independent)
**Purpose:** Letting people know about a schedule change.
**Walkthrough:** Student composes a casual group-chat version ("hey team, meeting moved to 3pm!"), a professional email version ("Please note: today's meeting has been rescheduled to 3:00 PM. Thank you for your flexibility."), and a public social media announcement version, each appropriately adapted.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Match register to platform**
Given the same content and 2 different platforms, student produces appropriately different register versions for each. *(Directly targets MC-THE-SAME-REGISTER-AND-STYLE-WORKS-ACROSS-ALL-DIGITAL-PLATFORMS.)*

**MP-2 [P36] — Revise a tone-risky message for clarity**
Given a message with tone-misinterpretation risk, student revises it to reduce that risk. *(Directly targets MC-TONE-IS-EASY-TO-CONVEY-IN-TEXT-BASED-DIGITAL-COMMUNICATION.)*

**MP-3 [P36] — Assess tone risk**
Given 3 messages, student identifies which carry the highest risk of tone misinterpretation and explains why.

**MP-4 [P36] — Apply platform-specific conventions**
Given a platform scenario (email, chat, social media), student applies the appropriate conventions.

**MP-5 [P36] — Compose multi-platform messages**
Given one message purpose, student composes appropriately adapted versions for 3 different digital platforms.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** At session open (S0), confirm PD-1 passes — digital communication applies general register judgment to specific digital platforms, so weak register judgment undermines the platform-specific application. During instruction (S1), watch for both misconception patterns: uniform digital register (MC-THE-SAME-REGISTER-AND-STYLE-WORKS-ACROSS-ALL-DIGITAL-PLATFORMS) and assuming tone transfers easily to text (MC-TONE-IS-EASY-TO-CONVEY-IN-TEXT-BASED-DIGITAL-COMMUNICATION) both underestimate how much digital communication varies by context and loses information relative to speech — redirect both to the Platform Dial/Muted Instrument anchor. If the student applies one register everywhere or ignores tone-misread risk during TA-1/TA-3 (S6), drop back to the Concrete Anchor's demonstrations before returning to independent composition. At mastery-probe stage (S9), do not certify mastery unless MP-1 and MP-2 both pass — a student who applies platform conventions well (MP-4) but still uses a uniform register or ignores tone risk has not learned the core adaptive judgment this concept requires.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student applies the same register across all digital platforms, weight remediation toward MC-THE-SAME-REGISTER-AND-STYLE-WORKS-ACROSS-ALL-DIGITAL-PLATFORMS's bridge text and re-run TA-1's platform-matching practice before returning to independent composition.
- If the student assumes tone will be clearly understood in text, weight remediation toward MC-TONE-IS-EASY-TO-CONVEY-IN-TEXT-BASED-DIGITAL-COMMUNICATION's bridge text and TA-3's tone-clarity revision practice specifically.
- This concept feeds one direct downstream unlock (`eng.communication.cross-cultural-communication`) — do not certify mastery without TA-5's multi-platform composition, since cross-cultural communication assumes the student can already adapt register and tone-clarity practices flexibly across different contexts.
- Reuse the "muted instrument" framing explicitly when introducing `eng.communication.cross-cultural-communication` — that concept extends the same tone-fragility awareness to cross-cultural contexts where misinterpretation risk is compounded by differing cultural norms.
