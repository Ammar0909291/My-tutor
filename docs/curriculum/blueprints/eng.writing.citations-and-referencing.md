**Concept:** Citations and Referencing — formally acknowledging the sources used in a piece of
writing, following a consistent citation style, both to give credit and to let a reader verify or
follow up on the evidence used.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Thesis Statements (prerequisite) teaches the student to
make a claim that will be supported by evidence. Citations and Referencing teaches the mechanical and
ethical layer that comes next: once a claim is being supported with sourced evidence (facts,
quotations, data, ideas from other writers), that evidence must be traceable back to where it came
from. This concept unlocks `eng.composition.research-paper-writing` (a full research paper cannot be
attempted until source acknowledgment is reliable, since research writing is built entirely from
synthesized outside sources).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) identify which specific pieces of information in a
piece of writing require a citation and which do not (common knowledge vs. specific/borrowed
information), (b) correctly format both an in-text citation and its corresponding reference-list
entry for a given source, and (c) explain, in their own words, why omitting a citation for
non-common-knowledge information constitutes a specific problem (misrepresenting borrowed ideas as
one's own) rather than merely a formatting oversight.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-ONLY-DIRECT-QUOTES-NEED-CITATION

**Trigger signal:** Student cites sources only when using a source's exact wording in quotation
marks, but fails to cite when paraphrasing or summarizing a source's ideas, facts, or data in their
own words — believing that changing the wording alone removes the need for attribution.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a source sentence ("Renewable energy costs have
fallen 70% in the last decade, according to the International Energy Agency") next to two student
versions: one in quotation marks with a citation, and one paraphrased into different words with NO
citation ("Solar and wind power have become much cheaper over the past ten years"). Ask: "Where did
this second student learn this fact? Did they discover it themselves, or did it come from the same
place as the quote?" The student must recognize the idea/fact originated from the source either way
— only the wording changed, not the ownership of the underlying information.

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Citation isn't about whether you used someone's exact WORDS — it's about
whether you used someone's IDEAS, FACTS, or DATA that you didn't discover or verify yourself. Think
of it like borrowing a friend's car: whether you drive it exactly as they left it or repaint it a
different color first, it's still their car, and you still need to say you borrowed it. Changing the
wording is repainting the car — it's still borrowed."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Ask: could I have written this specific fact, statistic, or idea
without having read or heard it somewhere? If no — if it came from a specific source, whether you
quote it directly or put it in your own words — it needs a citation. Only genuinely common knowledge
(facts widely known and not attributable to one specific source) is exempt."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A directly quoted sentence with a citation (obviously cited) vs. the same fact paraphrased into
   different words with no citation (still requires one, since the FACT, not just the wording, came
   from the source).
2. "Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius at sea level." (common knowledge, widely known, no single
   attributable source, no citation needed) vs. "This specific school's water fountain testing found
   contamination levels 40% above the state limit." (a specific, non-common-knowledge finding that
   requires citing wherever that specific data came from).

**s6_path:** If the student still omits citations for paraphrased material after the bridge, have
them trace each fact or idea in their draft back to its origin by literally writing "I learned this
from ___" in the margin next to every non-common-knowledge sentence — any sentence where that blank
cannot be filled with "my own direct observation/experience" needs a citation, regardless of wording.

### MC-B-CITING-A-SOURCE-MEANS-THE-INFORMATION-IS-AUTOMATICALLY-TRUE-OR-GOOD-EVIDENCE

**Trigger signal:** Student treats the mere presence of a citation as proof that the cited claim is
reliable or that using the citation absolves them of evaluating the source's credibility — e.g.,
citing an unreliable or biased source and treating the citation itself as sufficient justification
("it's true because I cited it").

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two properly formatted citations for the same claim
(that a new food product is either healthy or unhealthy) — one from a peer-reviewed nutrition study,
one from a promotional blog post funded by a company that sells a competing product. Ask: "Both are
cited correctly, in the same format. Does correct citation formatting tell us anything about whether
either source is trustworthy?" The student must recognize that citation format and source
credibility are two entirely separate questions — a citation properly points to WHERE information
came from, but says nothing about whether that place is reliable.

**Bridge text [P30]:** "A citation is like an address on an envelope — it tells you exactly where a
piece of mail came from, but it doesn't tell you whether what's inside the envelope is true or
trustworthy. You still have to open the envelope and evaluate what's inside. Citing a source
correctly is a separate step from judging whether that source deserves to be trusted."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "A citation answers 'where did this come from?' — it does NOT answer
'is this reliable?' Before using a source, separately ask: who wrote this, do they have relevant
expertise, do they have a bias or financial interest in the claim, and is this information confirmed
elsewhere? Citation and credibility are two different jobs."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A properly formatted citation to a peer-reviewed scientific journal (format correct, source
   generally credible) vs. an equally properly formatted citation to an anonymous blog with no stated
   expertise or evidence (format correct, source credibility highly questionable) — same formatting
   quality, very different reliability.
2. "It must be true, I cited it." (treating citation as proof of truth) vs. "I cited it so you can
   verify where this claim came from and judge its credibility yourself." (treating citation as
   transparency, not proof).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to conflate correct citation with source credibility after the
bridge, give them two differently reliable sources on the same topic and have them write a
one-sentence credibility evaluation for EACH separately from writing the citation itself — making the
two tasks physically distinct steps in their process.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.writing.thesis-statements`):** "Write a one-sentence thesis for this short
writing prompt." A student who cannot produce a basic thesis lacks the argumentative structure that
citations are meant to support — route to `eng.writing.thesis-statements` review first.

**PD-2 (checks basic source-awareness, informal pre-check):** "Where did you learn the fact you just
used in your last sentence — a textbook, a website, something you observed yourself, or common
knowledge?" A student who cannot distinguish sourced information from personal knowledge at all
needs a brief primer before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Borrowed-Car Anchor.** Physically (or with a drawn diagram) show a car with a "borrowed from:
[owner's name]" tag on it, whether the car is driven exactly as-is or has been repainted a different
color. Ask the student: "Does repainting the car change whose car it originally was?" This directly
seeds fixes for both misconceptions: it shows that changing the "wording" (the paint job) doesn't
change where the underlying "vehicle" (the idea, fact, or data) came from, so it still needs the
"borrowed from" tag regardless of paraphrase (fixing MC-A) — and separately, it shows that the tag
itself only tells you WHO the car came from, not whether that person's car is reliable or
well-maintained (fixing MC-B, extended with a second question: "does the tag tell you if the car is
safe to drive?").

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — What Requires a Citation (Common Knowledge vs. Specific Information)

Teach the common-knowledge test: information widely known and not attributable to one specific
source (e.g., "the sun rises in the east") needs no citation; specific facts, data, direct quotes,
paraphrased ideas, and unique arguments from an identifiable source always do. Student practice:
sort ten example sentences into "needs citation" and "common knowledge, no citation needed."

### TA-2 — Paraphrasing Still Requires Citation

Directly address MC-A using the "I learned this from ___" margin test. Student practice: given five
paraphrased sentences (with their original source sentences shown alongside), identify which still
need citations despite the reworded language.

### TA-3 — In-Text Citation and Reference List Mechanics

Teach the mechanical format: a brief in-text citation (author/year or equivalent, per the assigned
style) paired with a full reference-list entry containing complete source information. Student
practice: given source information (author, title, date, publisher) and a sentence using that
source, produce both the in-text citation and the reference-list entry correctly formatted.

### TA-4 — Citation Format vs. Source Credibility

Directly address MC-B using the borrowed-car anchor's second question. Teach a basic four-question
credibility check (who wrote this, what's their expertise, do they have a bias or financial
interest, is it confirmed elsewhere). Student practice: given two correctly-cited sources on the
same claim, evaluate and compare their credibility separately from their citation formatting.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Prompt: "Support the claim that regular exercise improves focus, using a
given source passage." Fully worked: source fact identified, correctly paraphrased into the
student's own words, in-text citation added, reference-list entry constructed, and a one-sentence
credibility check of the source performed separately. Annotation labels each step and confirms the
citation appears despite the paraphrase (addressing MC-A) and that the credibility check is a
distinct step from the citation itself (addressing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New source and claim supplied (a source about screen time and sleep). Student is
given the source passage and prompted with guiding questions ("Is this specific fact common
knowledge or does it need a citation? Have you paraphrased it, and does that remove the need for a
citation? What is this source's apparent expertise or possible bias?") to complete the paraphrase,
citation, reference entry, and credibility check.

**WE-3 (Independent):** New source and claim, unaided. Student completes the full paraphrase,
citation, reference-list entry, and credibility evaluation without guiding questions.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Common Knowledge vs. Citable Information**
Given six sentences, sort them into "needs citation" and "common knowledge, no citation needed,"
justifying each classification in one phrase.

**MP-2 [P36] — Paraphrase Still Needs Citation**
Given an original source sentence and a paraphrased version of it with no citation, identify that
the paraphrase still requires a citation and explain why in one sentence using the "borrowed car"
reasoning.

**MP-3 [P36] — Citation and Reference-List Mechanics**
Given source information (author, title, date, publisher) and a sentence using that source, produce
a correctly formatted in-text citation and its matching reference-list entry.

**MP-4 [P36] — Credibility Evaluation Separate from Citation**
Given two correctly-cited sources making conflicting claims about the same topic, evaluate each
source's credibility separately using the four-question check, without treating either citation's
correctness as proof of reliability.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Application**
Given a new claim and a new source passage, produce a properly paraphrased and cited sentence, its
reference-list entry, and a one-sentence credibility evaluation of the source, all correctly
distinguished as separate steps.

MP-4 failure alone indicates residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an
integration gap — provide one more fully-guided combined example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters the mechanical formatting (MP-3) but repeatedly forgets citations on
  paraphrased material in their own independent writing (WE-3, MP-5), treat this as a
  habit-formation gap rather than a knowledge gap — require an explicit "source check" pass as a
  mandatory step in their writing process for several subsequent assignments.
- If the student becomes overly suspicious of all sources after learning the credibility check
  (rejecting even well-established, reliable sources), recalibrate by showing that most textbooks,
  peer-reviewed journals, and established reference works reliably pass the four-question check —
  the goal is discernment, not blanket distrust.
- If the student conflates "properly cited" with "good writing" broadly (assuming a heavily-cited
  paper is automatically a strong paper regardless of the writer's own reasoning), clarify that
  citation supports but does not replace the writer's own argument and reasoning — flag for
  connection to `eng.composition.claim-evidence-reasoning`.
- Students working in a context with an unfamiliar citation style (if the assigned style differs
  from examples used in instruction) should be given one additional worked example in the specific
  assigned style before independent practice, since the underlying logic transfers but the exact
  format does not automatically.
