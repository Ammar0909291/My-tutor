**Concept:** Business Writing — writing clear, professional correspondence and documents for
workplace contexts, including memos, reports, and emails, prioritizing the reader's time and the
message's actionable clarity above all else.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Advanced Academic Writing (prerequisite) teaches
sophisticated, extended scholarly argumentation. Business Writing teaches a genuinely different set of
priorities: workplace readers are typically busy, scanning rather than closely reading, and need to
know quickly what's being asked of them or what decision is needed — this requires front-loading the
main point, using clear formatting, and being concise rather than building toward a conclusion the way
an academic essay does. This concept unlocks `eng.communication.professional-communication` (broader
workplace communication competencies build on this written foundation).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) write a business email or memo that states its main
point or request in the first sentence or two, rather than building up to it, (b) use clear formatting
(short paragraphs, bullet points, subject lines) to make a document scannable rather than requiring a
close, linear read, and (c) explicitly identify what action, if any, the reader is expected to take,
rather than leaving the purpose of the communication implicit or ambiguous.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-BUSINESS-WRITING-SHOULD-BUILD-UP-TO-THE-MAIN-POINT-LIKE-AN-ACADEMIC-ESSAY

**Trigger signal:** Student, applying the academic essay convention of building context before
stating a thesis, writes business emails or memos that bury the actual request or main point several
sentences or paragraphs in, requiring the busy reader to read through substantial preamble before
understanding what's actually being asked or communicated.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two emails with the same underlying request (asking a
manager to approve a schedule change): one that opens with several sentences of context and
background before finally stating the request in the final paragraph, and one that states the request
in the first sentence, with supporting context following. Ask: "If you were a busy manager with fifty
unread emails, which one would you understand and be able to act on fastest?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Building up to your point like an academic essay works when a reader has
committed to reading the whole thing carefully — but a business reader is more like someone glancing
at a series of road signs while driving past at speed. If the most important information (which exit
to take) is buried at the bottom of a sign packed with small print, they'll miss it entirely. Business
writing needs to put the destination — the main point or request — right at the top, in large,
unmissable letters, with supporting details after."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "In business writing, state your main point, request, or conclusion in
the FIRST sentence or two — don't build up to it. Supporting context and explanation should come
AFTER the main point is already clear, not before it."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "I wanted to reach out regarding some scheduling matters that have come up recently, and after
   considering several options, I was hoping we might discuss the possibility of adjusting..."
   (buries the actual request several sentences in) vs. "I'd like to request a schedule change for next
   week — could we move my Thursday shift to Friday? Here's the context:..." (states the request
   immediately, then explains).
2. A student applying essay-building instincts to a workplace email (misapplied register) vs. a
   student recognizing that business readers need the main point immediately, with context
   subordinated (correctly calibrated register).

**s6_path:** If the student continues burying the main point after the bridge, have them draft their
email, then literally delete everything before the sentence that states the actual request or main
point, and rewrite starting FROM there, adding back only the context genuinely necessary to
understand or act on that opening statement.

### MC-B-A-BUSINESS-DOCUMENT-JUST-NEEDS-TO-CONTAIN-THE-RIGHT-INFORMATION-FORMATTING-DOESNT-MATTER

**Trigger signal:** Student writes a business document with technically all the necessary
information present, but formatted as one or more dense, unbroken paragraphs with no visual structure
(short paragraphs, bullet points, headers, clear subject lines) — believing that as long as the
content is accurate and complete, HOW it's visually organized on the page doesn't matter.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student the same business content (a list of three action items
following a meeting) presented two ways: as one dense paragraph containing all three items woven
together in prose, and as a short intro sentence followed by three clearly bulleted action items. Ask:
"Both contain the same information. Which one could a reader actually scan and extract the three
action items from in five seconds, and which would require them to read carefully all the way
through?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Believing formatting doesn't matter as long as the content is accurate is
like believing a grocery list scribbled as one run-on sentence works just as well as a list with each
item on its own line — technically, both contain the same information, but one can be scanned at a
glance in the store aisle while the other requires stopping to carefully parse a paragraph. Business
readers scan; formatting (short paragraphs, bullet points, clear subject lines) is what makes scanning
actually work."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "In business writing, actively use visual structure — short paragraphs
(2-3 sentences), bullet points for lists or action items, and a clear, specific subject line — to make
your document scannable. Don't rely on the reader to carefully parse dense prose to extract key
information."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Following our meeting, I wanted to note that we agreed I would draft the report by Friday, Sarah
   would contact the vendor, and Tom would update the budget spreadsheet before next week's
   check-in." (accurate content, but requires careful reading to extract the three distinct action
   items) vs. "Following our meeting, here are our action items: • Draft the report (me) — due Friday
   • Contact the vendor (Sarah) • Update the budget spreadsheet (Tom) — before next week's check-in"
   (same content, scannable in seconds).
2. A student who believes visual formatting is a cosmetic afterthought irrelevant to communication
   quality (misconception) vs. a student who recognizes formatting as a functional tool that
   determines whether busy readers can actually extract and act on the information (correct
   understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to under-format after the bridge, have them take their own
dense paragraph and identify every distinct action item, deadline, or decision point buried within
it, then reformat those specific pieces of information as a bulleted list, comparing scannability
before and after.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.communication.academic-writing-advanced`):** "Identify the thesis-building
structure of this short academic paragraph." A student who cannot recognize academic essay
conventions lacks the contrast point this concept builds against — route to
`eng.communication.academic-writing-advanced` review first.

**PD-2 (checks basic audience-awareness, informal pre-check):** "Would a busy manager reading fifty
emails prefer a long, detailed email or a short, clear one with the main point up front?" A student
who cannot reason about this basic audience consideration needs a brief primer before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Road-Sign-at-Speed and Grocery-List Anchor.** Combine two images: a road sign with the critical
information (which exit to take) buried in small print at the bottom, versus a sign with it prominent
at the top — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (front-load the main point). Second, a grocery list
written as one run-on sentence versus one item per line — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (visual
formatting determines scannability, which determines whether the information is actually usable).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Stating the Main Point First (MC-A)

Using the road-sign anchor, teach the front-loading principle: state the request, decision, or main
point in the first sentence or two. Student practice: given three emails that bury their main point,
identify the actual main point and rewrite the opening to state it immediately.

### TA-2 — Subordinating Context to Support, Not Precede, the Main Point

Teach that context and explanation should follow the main point, providing just enough support to be
useful without restoring the "build-up" structure. Student practice: given a front-loaded opening
sentence and a paragraph of context, arrange the context in logical support order after the opening.

### TA-3 — Formatting for Scannability (MC-B)

Using the grocery-list anchor, teach short paragraphs, bullet points for lists/action items, and
clear subject lines. Student practice: given a dense paragraph containing multiple distinct action
items, reformat it using short paragraphs and bullets.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Writing a Complete Business Communication

Give the student a workplace scenario (e.g., requesting a meeting reschedule, summarizing a project
update with action items). They must write a complete email or memo that states the main point
immediately (TA-1), provides appropriately subordinated context (TA-2), and uses scannable formatting
(TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Scenario: requesting approval for a schedule change. Fully worked: opening
sentence states the request directly ("I'd like to request approval to move my Thursday shift to
Friday next week"); context follows briefly ("due to a scheduling conflict with a prior commitment");
formatted as a short email with a clear subject line ("Schedule Change Request — Next Week"). Annotation
confirms both the front-loaded main point (fixing MC-A) and the clear, scannable structure (fixing
MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New scenario (summarizing a team meeting with three action items) with a dense,
unformatted draft provided. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("What's the actual main point
or purpose of this email — is it stated first? What are the distinct action items buried in this
paragraph — could they be pulled out as a bulleted list?") to revise into a front-loaded, scannable
document.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new workplace scenario, unaided. Student writes a complete business
communication with a front-loaded main point, appropriately subordinated context, and scannable
formatting.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying a Buried Main Point**
Given an email that buries its request or main point, identify what the actual main point is and
where in the email it currently appears.

**MP-2 [P36] — Front-Loading a Main Point**
Given the buried-main-point email from MP-1, rewrite the opening to state the main point in the
first sentence or two.

**MP-3 [P36] — Identifying Formatting Opportunities**
Given a dense paragraph containing multiple distinct action items or pieces of information, identify
which parts could be reformatted as a bulleted list for scannability.

**MP-4 [P36] — Reformatting for Scannability**
Given the dense paragraph from MP-3, reformat it using short paragraphs and bullet points while
preserving all the original information.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Business Communication**
Given a new workplace scenario, write a complete email or memo with a front-loaded main point,
appropriately subordinated context, and scannable formatting.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; an academic-writing gap routes back
to that prerequisite, an audience-awareness gap gets a brief primer. During Component 4 (S1, active
instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1/TA-2 (essay-building instincts transferred
incorrectly) and MC-B during TA-3 (under-formatting) — these are largely independent register-
transfer errors and often need separate, explicit correction, since a student can master front-
loading while still under-formatting, or vice versa. If either fires, branch to the relevant s6_path
drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1/MP-2 failures indicate
residual MC-A; MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing
indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-document example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters front-loading and formatting in isolation (MP-2, MP-4) but reverts to essay-
  style build-up or dense paragraphs under the combined demand of MP-5, provide a structured checklist
  (state the point first, then support; format for scanning) as scaffolding before removing it.
- If the student has just completed `eng.communication.academic-writing-advanced` and shows strong
  transfer resistance (actively defending the build-up structure as "more thorough" or
  "more professional"), explicitly frame this as a genuinely different register with different goals
  — not a lowering of standards, but a different, equally legitimate skill suited to a different
  reading context.
- If the student over-corrects into excessive bullet-pointing (fragmenting genuinely connected prose
  into disconnected bullet fragments that lose necessary logical connection), clarify that bullets
  suit discrete items (action items, lists) while connected reasoning may still need brief prose —
  formatting should match the content's actual structure.
- Students with real workplace or internship experience may already have intuitive business-writing
  habits — leverage this directly by having them bring in or recall a real example email and analyze
  it against this concept's principles.
