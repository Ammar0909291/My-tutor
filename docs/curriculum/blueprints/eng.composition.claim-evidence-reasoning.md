**Concept:** Claim, Evidence, and Reasoning (CER) — the structure of a sound argument: a claim
(the position being argued), evidence (facts or data supporting the claim), and reasoning (the
explicit logical connection tying the evidence to the claim).

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Audience and Purpose (prerequisite) teaches the student
to identify who they're writing for and why, including persuasive purpose. Claim, Evidence, and
Reasoning teaches the actual internal architecture of a persuasive or analytical argument once
purpose is set — not just "have a claim" and "have evidence," but the reasoning STEP that most
student writing skips, leaving evidence sitting next to a claim with no explained connection. This
concept unlocks `eng.composition.argumentation-basics` (building complete, multi-claim arguments
depends on being able to construct one sound claim-evidence-reasoning unit reliably first).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) identify the claim, evidence, and reasoning
components in a given argumentative passage, (b) recognize when reasoning is MISSING even though a
claim and evidence are both present, and (c) construct a complete claim-evidence-reasoning unit of
their own, including an explicit reasoning sentence that states WHY the evidence supports the claim
rather than simply placing the two next to each other.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-EVIDENCE-NEXT-TO-A-CLAIM-IS-ENOUGH

**Trigger signal:** Student writes a claim followed immediately by a piece of evidence with no
explicit sentence connecting the two — e.g., "Students should have longer lunch periods. A survey
found 60% of students said they feel rushed at lunch." — leaving the logical connection implicit and
assuming the reader will supply it.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student this exact claim-then-evidence pair with no reasoning,
and ask: "Does this survey result necessarily mean lunch periods should be LONGER? Could it instead
mean lines move too slowly, or that students are distracted, or that the survey question was
worded in a way that doesn't tell us the actual cause?" Guide the student to see that without an
explicit reasoning sentence, several different, even contradictory, conclusions could be drawn from
the same evidence — the evidence alone doesn't do the connecting work.

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Evidence sitting next to a claim is like two ends of a broken bridge facing
each other across a gap — a reader can SEE both sides, but nothing carries them across unless you
build the middle section yourself. Reasoning is that middle section: the sentence that explicitly
explains why this specific evidence leads to this specific claim, so the reader doesn't have to
guess or build the bridge themselves."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "After stating your evidence, always add a reasoning sentence that
starts with something like 'This shows that...' or 'This means that...' or 'Because of this...' — and
finish it by explicitly connecting the evidence back to your claim. If you can remove that sentence
and the argument still makes exactly the same sense, you haven't actually written reasoning yet."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Students should have longer lunch periods. A survey found 60% of students feel rushed." (claim +
   evidence, no reasoning — the connection is left to the reader) vs. "Students should have longer
   lunch periods. A survey found 60% of students feel rushed, which shows that current lunch periods
   don't leave enough time to eat and socialize without stress — the exact problem a longer period
   would directly solve." (claim + evidence + explicit reasoning).
2. A paragraph where every piece of evidence is followed by an unstated assumption that the reader
   will connect it to the claim (evidence-claim gap) vs. a paragraph where every piece of evidence is
   followed by a sentence explicitly stating the logical link (reasoning present).

**s6_path:** If the student still leaves the gap unexplained after the bridge, have them literally
insert the sentence stem "This shows that ___ because ___" directly after every piece of evidence in
their draft and force themselves to complete both blanks before moving on.

### MC-B-REASONING-MEANS-JUST-RESTATING-THE-CLAIM-AGAIN

**Trigger signal:** Student attempts to write a reasoning sentence but simply restates the original
claim in slightly different words rather than actually explaining the logical connection between the
specific evidence and the claim — e.g., "This shows that lunch periods should be longer" as the
entire reasoning sentence, which adds no new explanatory content beyond the claim itself.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a claim, evidence, and a "reasoning" sentence that
merely restates the claim ("This shows lunch should be longer.") alongside a genuine reasoning
sentence that explains the mechanism connecting evidence to claim ("This shows that current lunch
periods don't leave enough time to eat and socialize without stress, which is the specific problem a
longer period would solve.") Ask: "Does the first sentence tell us anything we didn't already know
from the claim alone? Does the second?" The student should notice the first sentence is circular —
it just repeats the conclusion — while the second adds genuinely new explanatory content.

**Bridge text [P30]:** "A restated claim is like an echo — it sounds like an answer, but it's really
just your own voice bouncing back at you, with nothing new added. Real reasoning has to add
something the claim alone didn't already say: HOW or WHY the evidence specifically leads there. If
your reasoning sentence could be deleted and swapped for the claim itself with no real loss of
information, it's an echo, not reasoning."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Test your reasoning sentence: does it explain a MECHANISM or LOGICAL
STEP that connects the specific evidence to the claim — something new, not already stated in the
claim itself? If your reasoning sentence and your claim sentence are saying almost the same thing in
different words, rewrite the reasoning to explain the 'why' or 'how' instead."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "This shows lunch should be longer." (restates the claim — an echo, not reasoning) vs. "This shows
   that the current time doesn't allow students to both eat and socialize without stress, and a
   longer period directly targets that specific gap." (explains the actual mechanism connecting the
   evidence to the claim).
2. A student paragraph where the reasoning sentence is a near-verbatim repeat of the topic sentence
   (circular, no new information) vs. one where the reasoning sentence introduces a new explanatory
   idea (the specific cause-effect mechanism) not present in the claim itself.

**s6_path:** If the student continues to write circular reasoning after the bridge, have them cover
up the claim sentence entirely and ask: "If someone only read your evidence and your reasoning
sentence — without ever seeing the claim — could they figure out what the claim probably is?" If the
reasoning sentence is too generic or circular to allow that, it needs a more specific mechanism
added.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.composition.audience-and-purpose`):** "For this argumentative prompt, name the
audience and the purpose." A student who cannot commit to an audience and persuasive/analytical
purpose needs `eng.composition.audience-and-purpose` reviewed first, since CER is applied in service
of a specific argument for a specific reader.

**PD-2 (checks basic claim vs. fact distinction, informal pre-check):** "Which of these two sentences
is an arguable claim, and which is a plain fact: 'Recess is held at noon' or 'Recess should be
extended to 30 minutes'?" A student who cannot make this distinction needs a brief claim-recognition
primer before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Broken-Bridge Anchor.** Draw two riverbanks with a gap between them — one bank labeled
"Evidence," the other labeled "Claim" — and no bridge connecting them. Ask the student: "How does
something get from one side to the other?" Then draw in a bridge deck labeled "Reasoning" connecting
the two banks explicitly. This directly seeds fixes for both misconceptions: it shows that evidence
and claim sitting on opposite banks with nothing connecting them leaves a real gap the reader must
somehow cross alone (fixing MC-A) — and, extending the anchor, a "bridge" built out of the SAME
material as one of the two banks (i.e., a reasoning sentence that's really just a restated claim)
doesn't actually span the gap, it just extends one bank a little further without ever reaching the
other side (fixing MC-B).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Identifying Claim, Evidence, and Reasoning

Introduce the three components with clear definitions and examples. Student practice: given five
complete argumentative paragraphs, underline and label the claim, evidence, and reasoning in each.

### TA-2 — Detecting the Missing Bridge (MC-A)

Using the broken-bridge anchor, teach students to detect when a claim and evidence are present but
reasoning is missing. Student practice: given five claim-evidence pairs with no reasoning, identify
the gap and explain what specific reasoning sentence would need to be added.

### TA-3 — Writing Genuine Reasoning (Not an Echo) (MC-B)

Using the "cover up the claim" test, teach students to write reasoning sentences that add real
explanatory content rather than restating the claim. Student practice: given five reasoning
sentences (some circular, some genuine), sort them and revise the circular ones into genuine
reasoning.

### TA-4 — Constructing a Full CER Unit from Scratch

Give the student a claim and raw evidence (not yet reasoned) and have them construct the full
three-part unit, writing their own reasoning sentence that passes both the "This shows that... because
..." test and the "cover up the claim" test.

### TA-5 — Evaluating and Strengthening Weak Arguments

Give the student five flawed CER units (mixing missing-reasoning and circular-reasoning problems,
plus at least one where the evidence itself doesn't actually support the stated claim at all). For
each, diagnose the specific flaw and revise. This is the synthesis task — diagnosis plus repair
across multiple flaw types, including a flaw type (evidence-claim mismatch) not directly covered by
either misconception but requiring the same close-reading skill.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Prompt: "Argue whether the school should add a study hall period." Fully
worked: Claim — "The school should add a dedicated study hall period." Evidence — "A pilot program at
a nearby school found students who had a study hall period turned in 20% more completed homework
assignments." Reasoning — "This shows that having dedicated, supervised time built into the school
day directly increases the chance that students complete their work, rather than relying on
unpredictable time after school — which is exactly the gap a study hall period would close here."
Annotation confirms the reasoning passes both the "This shows that... because..." test (fixing MC-A)
and the "cover up the claim" test (fixing MC-B), since it introduces the specific mechanism
(dedicated, supervised time reduces reliance on unpredictable after-school time) not stated in the
claim itself.

**WE-2 (Guided):** Same prompt type, new topic ("Argue whether the school should allow phones during
lunch"). Student is given the claim and a piece of evidence (a stated reduction in lunchtime
conflicts at a school that piloted phone use) and prompted with guiding questions ("What specific
mechanism connects fewer conflicts to allowing phones — is it distraction, is it something else? If
you cover up the claim, does your reasoning sentence alone let a reader guess what the claim
probably is?") to complete a genuine, non-circular reasoning sentence.

**WE-3 (Independent):** New prompt ("Argue whether students should choose their own reading books in
English class"). Student constructs the full claim-evidence-reasoning unit unaided, self-checking
against both misconception tests before submitting.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Component Identification**
Given a complete argumentative paragraph, underline and label the claim, the evidence, and the
reasoning.

**MP-2 [P36] — Detecting Missing Reasoning**
Given a claim-evidence pair with no reasoning sentence, identify that reasoning is missing and write
the specific reasoning sentence needed to bridge the gap.

**MP-3 [P36] — Detecting Circular Reasoning**
Given a claim, evidence, and a reasoning sentence that merely restates the claim, identify the
circularity using the "cover up the claim" test and revise it into genuine reasoning.

**MP-4 [P36] — Constructing a Full CER Unit**
Given a claim and raw, unreasoned evidence, write a complete reasoning sentence that passes both the
"This shows that... because..." test and the "cover up the claim" test.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Diagnosis and Repair**
Given a flawed argumentative paragraph with an unnamed defect (missing reasoning, circular
reasoning, or evidence that doesn't actually support the claim), identify the specific flaw and
revise the paragraph into a sound CER unit.


**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; an audience/purpose gap routes back
to that prerequisite, a claim-recognition gap gets a brief primer. During Component 4 (S1, active
instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-2 and MC-B during TA-3 — these often occur in
sequence for the same student, since a student who first learns to ADD a reasoning sentence (fixing
MC-A) frequently defaults to a circular one at first (triggering MC-B) before the distinction fully
lands. Watch for this natural progression rather than treating a TA-3 trigger as a regression. At
the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure indicates basic component identification
(TA-1) needs review; MP-2 failure alone indicates residual MC-A; MP-3 failure alone indicates
residual MC-B; MP-4 failure with MP-1 through MP-3 passing indicates a production gap distinct from
recognition; MP-5 failure alone (with all others passing) indicates a diagnosis-under-ambiguity gap,
particularly if the failure involves the evidence-claim mismatch flaw not directly taught in TA-2/
TA-3 — provide one more mixed-flaw worked example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student passes MP-1 through MP-4 but fails MP-5 specifically on the evidence-claim mismatch
  variant (evidence that doesn't actually support the stated claim at all, as opposed to missing or
  circular reasoning), this is a distinct skill from the two taught misconceptions — provide one
  additional worked example focused specifically on evaluating whether evidence is RELEVANT to a
  claim, not just whether reasoning connects them.
- If the student's reasoning sentences pass both tests but remain overly simplistic (correct but
  thin), this indicates the mechanics are secure and readiness for the more sophisticated reasoning
  chains in `eng.composition.argumentation-basics` — flag as a strength, not a gap.
- If the student overcorrects into excessively long reasoning sentences that restate the evidence
  multiple times rather than explaining it once clearly, redirect using the "cover up the claim" test
  to check for genuine new content rather than volume.
- Students who struggle with abstract claim-evidence-reasoning work may benefit from starting with
  concrete, physical claims (e.g., "This ball is bouncy" with evidence of bounce height) before
  moving to the abstract, opinion-based prompts used in TA-4/TA-5 and the Worked Examples.
