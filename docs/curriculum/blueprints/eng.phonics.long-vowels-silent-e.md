## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-SILENT-E-DOES-NOTHING
- **Trigger signal:** Student ignores the final "e" entirely when decoding, reading "cake" as if it were "cak," or fails to add a final "e" when spelling a long-vowel word (spells "make" as "mak"), treating the letter as truly inert rather than as a signal that changes the preceding vowel.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "Read 'cap.' Now read 'cape.' Do they have the same vowel sound? What's the only difference in spelling between them?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "The final 'e' itself is not pronounced — that part is true — but it has an important JOB: it signals that the vowel earlier in the word should say its long sound (its alphabet name) instead of its short sound. It's silent, but not meaningless."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Whenever you see a word ending in a single consonant + silent e, expect the earlier vowel to say its long (alphabet-name) sound, and check for that pattern before decoding."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "cap" (no final e, short /æ/) vs. "cape" (final e, long /eɪ/).
  - "kit" (short /ɪ/) vs. "kite" (long /aɪ/).
- **s6_path:** "It's a reasonable first guess that a silent letter does nothing at all — but this particular silent letter is doing real work from a distance, changing how an earlier letter sounds, which is a genuinely unusual and important pattern in English spelling."

### MC-ANY-E-AT-THE-END-MEANS-LONG-VOWEL
- **Trigger signal:** Student over-applies the silent-e rule to ALL words ending in "e," including those where it doesn't lengthen the vowel (e.g., "have," "give," "love," "come" — words that end in silent e but keep a short vowel sound due to spelling conventions, often because English words rarely end in "v" or "u"), or where the "e" is part of a different pattern entirely.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "The silent-e rule says the earlier vowel should be long. Read 'have' — does the 'a' actually say its long sound ('hay-v'), or does it stay short?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "The silent-e-makes-the-vowel-long pattern is the MOST common reason a word ends in silent e, but it's not the only one — a small set of common words (have, give, live, love, come, some, done) keep a short vowel despite the final e, often because English spelling avoids ending words in certain letters (v, u) even when the sound doesn't call for an e. These are learned as a separate, smaller exception list."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Apply the silent-e-long-vowel rule as the default expectation, but check known high-frequency exception words separately — don't force the rule onto every single word ending in e."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "cake" (rule applies: long /eɪ/) vs. "have" (exception: short /æ/, despite ending in e).
  - "hope" (rule applies: long /oʊ/) vs. "love" (exception: short /ʌ/).
- **s6_path:** "A handful of very common words break this rule, which can feel frustrating right after learning it — but they're a short, learnable list of exceptions, not a sign that the rule itself is unreliable."

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The magic-e wand**

> Write "cap" on a whiteboard/card. Have the student read it aloud (short /æ/). Then, with a flourish, add an "e" at the end (physically drawing an arrow or a small "magic wand" symbol from the e back to the vowel) to make "cape." Read it aloud together, exaggerating the vowel change. Repeat with 2-3 more pairs (kit/kite, hop/hope), each time physically adding the "e" and narrating: "the e reaches back and changes the vowel's sound." This anchors silent-e as an active, distance-acting signal, not an inert letter.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Minimal Pair Contrast (CVC vs. CVCe) [C]**

Present pairs differing only by final silent e (cap/cape, kit/kite, hop/hope, cut/cute, pet/Pete); student reads both and identifies the vowel-sound change.

**TA-2 — Predicting the Vowel Sound from Spelling [C]**

Present new CVCe words (not yet read); student predicts the vowel will be long BEFORE reading, based on spotting the silent-e pattern, then confirms by reading.

**TA-3 — Spelling with Silent E [C→P]**

Given a spoken long-vowel word (e.g., "say 'bike'"), student spells it correctly, including the final silent e, rather than spelling it as if it were a short-vowel CVC word.

**TA-4 — High-Frequency Exception Recognition [P]**

Introduce the small exception list (have, give, live, love, come, some, done, are) as sight-recognized words that do NOT follow the silent-e-long-vowel rule; student identifies these by rote recognition rather than by rule application, directly targeting MC-ANY-E-AT-THE-END-MEANS-LONG-VOWEL.

**TA-5 — Reading Novel CVCe Words in Context [P]**

Present unseen CVCe words within short sentences; student reads fluently, applying the rule by default and flagging any word that looks like it might be an exception before decoding.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — minimal pair)**

> "Read 'pin.' Now read 'pine.' What changed?"

Target response: "pin" has short /ɪ/; "pine" has long /aɪ/ — the added silent e changed the vowel sound.

**WE-2 (Foundational — prediction)**

> "Before you read it, look at 'note' — do you expect a long or short vowel? Why?"

Target response: long — because it ends in a single consonant + silent e.

**WE-3 (Intermediate — exception recognition)**

> "Read 'give.' Does the 'i' say its long sound, like the silent-e rule predicts?"

Target response: no — "give" is an exception; the "i" stays short (/ɪ/) despite the final e.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Minimal pair, novel words**
"Read 'rob.' Now read 'robe.' What's different about the vowel sound?"
*Target:* "rob" short /ɒ/; "robe" long /oʊ/.

**MP-2 [P36] — Predict before reading**
"Look at 'plane' — predict the vowel sound before reading it aloud."
*Target:* predicts long /eɪ/ based on the CVCe pattern, then confirms correctly.

**MP-3 [P36] — Spelling application**
"Spell the word 'slide.'"
*Target:* s-l-i-d-e, correctly including the final silent e to signal the long /aɪ/ sound.

**MP-4 [P36] — Exception identification**
"Read 'love.' Does this follow the silent-e rule, or is it an exception? How do you know?"
*Target:* identifies it as an exception — the vowel stays short (/ʌ/) despite ending in silent e, recognized as a memorized high-frequency exception rather than forced through the rule.

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain the rule and its exceptions**
"In your own words: what does a silent e at the end of a word usually do, and what should you watch out for?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that silent e usually signals the earlier vowel should be long, while also naming that a small set of common words are exceptions to check separately.

**Protocol routing:** S0 (secure on short vowels, new to silent e): dwell heavily on TA-1's minimal pairs with the magic-e wand demonstration repeated multiple times before moving to prediction. S1 (already notices the pattern intuitively but can't yet apply it to spelling): compress TA-1/TA-2, focus on TA-3's spelling application. S6 (frustrated by exception words after just learning "the rule"): explicitly frame the exceptions as a short, separate, memorizable list (Component 1 s6_path) rather than evidence the rule is unreliable — introduce exceptions only after the core rule (TA-1-3) is secure, never simultaneously. S9 (L1 has no equivalent silent-letter-affecting-a-distant-vowel convention, e.g. most phonetic-orthography languages): treat this as a genuinely novel orthographic concept requiring extra concrete demonstration (the wand technique) rather than assuming any transfer from L1 spelling intuitions.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Silent-e is active, not inert**: the core conceptual hurdle is recognizing that a silent letter can still have a functional effect on a DIFFERENT letter's sound — the magic-e wand anchor (Component 3) should be revisited explicitly whenever MC-SILENT-E-DOES-NOTHING resurfaces.
- **Exceptions taught AFTER the rule is secure**: never introduce the have/give/love exception list before the core CVC/CVCe contrast (TA-1) is independently reliable — introducing both simultaneously risks undermining confidence in the rule itself.
- **Prediction-before-reading as a standing habit**: TA-2's "predict, then confirm" routine should become a standing decoding habit reinforced throughout later phonics work, not a one-time exercise.
- **Novel orthographic convention for some L1 backgrounds**: for S9 learners from consistently phonetic L1 orthographies, budget extra time for the core insight (a silent letter changing another letter's sound) since it may have no direct parallel in their prior literacy experience.
