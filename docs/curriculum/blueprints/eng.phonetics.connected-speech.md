## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-CONNECTED-SPEECH-CHANGES-ARE-SLOPPY-SPEECH
- **Trigger signal:** Student treats elision, assimilation, and linking as careless or "incorrect" pronunciation to be avoided, attempting to always pronounce words in their careful, isolated, citation-form pronunciation even in natural connected speech (e.g., insists on fully pronouncing "want to" as two crisp, separate words rather than the natural "wanna," even when the context calls for natural conversational register).
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "Listen to a native/fluent speaker say 'What are you going to do?' naturally in conversation. Do they pronounce every single word fully and separately, or do some sounds blend, drop, or link together?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Connected speech changes (elision — dropping sounds; assimilation — sounds changing to match neighbors; linking — sounds joining across word boundaries) are NOT sloppy mistakes — they are regular, rule-governed features of natural, fluent spoken English used by careful and casual speakers alike. Recognizing and understanding them is essential for listening comprehension of real speech, and appropriate use supports natural-sounding production."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Treat connected-speech features as a normal, describable part of natural spoken English — expected and systematic, not an error to eliminate — while still recognizing that very formal or deliberately clear speech may use fuller, separated forms."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "want to" → "wanna" in casual natural speech (a genuine, describable connected-speech pattern) vs. a fully separated "want... to" in slow, formal, or emphatic speech — both are legitimate, context-appropriate registers, not one "correct" and one "wrong."
  - Genuine mumbling/unclear speech (actually hard to understand, a communication problem) vs. systematic connected-speech reduction that remains fully intelligible to fluent listeners (not a problem at all).
- **s6_path:** "It's a very reasonable instinct to think 'careful and separate' must be more correct — but connected speech is how real English actually sounds in natural use, and recognizing it is a genuinely necessary skill for understanding real conversations, not a corruption of 'proper' English."

### MC-CONNECTED-SPEECH-IS-RANDOM
- **Trigger signal:** Student perceives connected-speech changes as unpredictable noise rather than systematic, rule-governed phenomena — treats each instance of elision/assimilation/linking as an isolated surprise rather than recognizing the underlying pattern (e.g., doesn't generalize that a /t/ or /d/ commonly assimilates before certain following consonants, or that a consonant-then-vowel word boundary commonly links).
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You were confused by 'gonna' and separately confused by 'gimme' — these look like two random one-off things, but let's check: does 'going to' → 'gonna' and 'give me' → 'gimme' both involve reducing/merging the middle sounds in a similar predictable way?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Connected speech changes follow genuine, systematic patterns, not random variation — there are recognizable categories (elision commonly drops certain consonant clusters or unstressed vowels; assimilation commonly changes a sound to match a following sound's place of articulation; linking commonly joins a final consonant to a following vowel-initial word) that apply across many different word combinations, not just isolated memorized examples."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "When you notice a connected-speech change, check which general pattern (elision, assimilation, or linking) it belongs to, rather than treating it as an isolated exception to memorize word-by-word."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "going to" → "gonna" and "want to" → "wanna" (same general elision/reduction pattern applied to different word pairs) vs. treating each as an unrelated memorized chunk.
  - "did you" → "didja" (assimilation, /d/+/j/ → /dʒ/) generalizing to "would you" → "wouldja" (the same assimilation pattern).
- **s6_path:** "Connected speech can feel like an endless list of special cases to memorize one by one — but recognizing the small number of underlying pattern TYPES (elision, assimilation, linking) turns it into a much more manageable, generalizable system."

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The word-blender**

> Say two words separately and clearly: "want" ... "to." Then physically bring two hands together (representing the words) while saying them together naturally and quickly, producing "wanna" — showing the two words physically and audibly "blending." Repeat with "going" + "to" → "gonna," and "give" + "me" → "gimme." This anchors connected speech as a physical, visualizable merging process rather than abstract or careless.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Elision Recognition (Sound Dropping) [C]**

Present common elision examples (want to→wanna, going to→gonna, next day→"nex' day," probably→"prob'ly"); student identifies which sounds are dropped/reduced and recognizes the pattern generalizes across similar word combinations, directly targeting MC-CONNECTED-SPEECH-IS-RANDOM.

**TA-2 — Assimilation Recognition (Sound Change) [C]**

Present common assimilation examples (did you→"didja," would you→"wouldja," ten bikes→"tem bikes" [n→m before b]); student identifies how a sound changes to become more similar to a neighboring sound.

**TA-3 — Linking Recognition (Sounds Joining Across Words) [C→P]**

Present examples of consonant-to-vowel linking across word boundaries (turn it off → "tur-ni-toff," an apple → "a-napple" [linking r/consonant]); student identifies where word boundaries "disappear" in natural pronunciation.

**TA-4 — Register Appropriateness Judgment [P]**

Present a range of contexts (casual conversation with friends vs. a formal presentation vs. reading a legal document aloud); student judges whether connected-speech reduction is appropriate/expected or whether fuller, separated pronunciation is more suitable, directly targeting MC-CONNECTED-SPEECH-CHANGES-ARE-SLOPPY-SPEECH by establishing that BOTH registers are legitimate in their appropriate contexts.

**TA-5 — Listening Comprehension of Connected Speech [P]**

Present audio (or modeled) natural connected speech containing multiple elision/assimilation/linking instances; student correctly identifies the underlying full-form words despite the reductions, demonstrating that this concept's primary practical value is LISTENING comprehension of real speech.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — elision)**

> "In casual speech, 'going to' often becomes what?"

Target response: "gonna" — the middle sounds reduce/merge.

**WE-2 (Foundational — assimilation)**

> "In casual speech, 'did you' often becomes what?"

Target response: "didja" — the /d/ and /j/ sounds merge into /dʒ/.

**WE-3 (Intermediate — register judgment)**

> "Would you say 'gonna' when reading a formal speech aloud, or when chatting with a friend? Why?"

Target response: with a friend — casual register; formal reading typically uses the fuller "going to," correctly reasoning about register appropriateness rather than declaring one form universally correct.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Elision, novel example**
"What does 'kind of' often reduce to in casual speech?"
*Target:* "kinda."

**MP-2 [P36] — Assimilation, novel example**
"What does 'don't you' often reduce to in casual speech?"
*Target:* "doncha" (or similar) — correctly identifies the sound-merging pattern.

**MP-3 [P36] — Linking, novel example**
"How would 'run away' sound linked together in fast natural speech?"
*Target:* correctly identifies the consonant-to-vowel linking across the word boundary (the 'n' linking into 'away').

**MP-4 [P36] — Register judgment, novel context**
"You're giving a formal presentation to executives. Would you use 'wanna' or 'want to'?"
*Target:* "want to" — correctly reasons that the formal register calls for the fuller form.

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain connected speech**
"In your own words: why does spoken English sound different from how words look written down, and is that a problem?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that connected speech (elision, assimilation, linking) is a systematic, expected feature of natural fluent speech, not an error, and that understanding it is essential for listening comprehension of real conversation.

**Protocol routing:** S0 (secure on intonation, new to connected speech specifically): dwell on TA-1's elision examples with the word-blender anchor before introducing assimilation/linking. S1 (already produces connected speech naturally in casual conversation but lacks the analytical vocabulary): use this concept to build metalinguistic awareness of an already-present production skill, focusing on TA-1-3's pattern-naming. S6 (anxious that using "wanna"/"gonna" is "bad English" from prior formal-correctness instruction): explicitly validate via TA-4 that both registers are legitimate and context-dependent — this may require gently revising a prior belief. S9 (L2 learner whose primary exposure has been formal/written English, or conversely whose informal spoken exposure has outpaced formal instruction): for the former, prioritize TA-5's listening-comprehension focus since connected speech is often the biggest real-world listening barrier for classroom-only learners; for the latter, use this concept to formalize/name patterns already intuitively used.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **This is fundamentally a listening-comprehension skill first**: while production awareness matters (TA-4), the primary real-world value of this concept is enabling comprehension of natural fast speech — TA-5 should receive proportionate emphasis, especially for classroom-taught L2 learners.
- **Connected speech is systematic, not exception-by-exception**: always frame new examples in terms of the three named pattern types (elision, assimilation, linking) rather than as isolated memorized forms, directly countering MC-CONNECTED-SPEECH-IS-RANDOM.
- **Register-appropriateness is the correct frame, not correctness**: never present connected-speech forms as simply "wrong" — the framework is casual-register-appropriate vs. formal-register-appropriate, both legitimate.
- **Listening-heavy L2 exposure vs. formal-only L2 exposure need different emphasis**: for S9 learners, diagnose which type of prior exposure they've had and weight TA-4 (register awareness) vs. TA-5 (listening comprehension) accordingly.
