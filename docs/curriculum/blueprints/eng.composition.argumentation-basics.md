**Concept:** Argumentation Basics — constructing a logically structured argument that states and
defends a position, built from multiple linked claim-evidence-reasoning units organized into a
coherent whole.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Claim, Evidence, and Reasoning (prerequisite) teaches
the student to build ONE sound CER unit. Argumentation Basics teaches how to assemble MULTIPLE CER
units into a complete, organized argument that defends a single overarching position — deciding how
many supporting points to use, how to order them, and how to make the whole argument feel unified
rather than a pile of disconnected reasons. This concept unlocks
`eng.composition.counterargument-and-rebuttal` (acknowledging and answering opposing views requires
a solid argument structure to defend first) and `eng.composition.logical-fallacies` (recognizing
BROKEN argument structures requires first knowing what a sound one looks like).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can construct a multi-point argument that (a) states one
clear central position, (b) supports it with two or three genuinely distinct CER units (not
repetitions of the same point in different words), and (c) organizes those points in a deliberate
order (e.g., strongest point first or last, logical progression) rather than a random sequence, while
explaining the reasoning behind that ordering choice.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-MORE-REASONS-IS-ALWAYS-A-STRONGER-ARGUMENT

**Trigger signal:** Student piles up as many supporting points as possible, believing sheer quantity
of reasons makes an argument stronger, even when several of the "reasons" are really the same point
restated in different words or are individually weak — e.g., listing five reasons for a position
where three are near-duplicates and the other two are barely developed.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two argument outlines for the same position (school
should start later): one with five thin, overlapping points (tired students, tired teachers, tired
parents, tired bus drivers, tired everyone) and one with two well-developed, genuinely distinct
points (sleep science research on adolescent circadian rhythms; a specific pilot program's measured
attendance improvement). Ask: "Which argument would actually change a skeptical school board
member's mind — five thin, overlapping points, or two well-developed different ones?" Guide the
student to notice the five-point version is actually one point repeated, while the two-point version
brings genuinely different kinds of evidence to bear.

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Piling up overlapping reasons is like trying to build a strong table by
adding more legs of the exact same short length instead of making the legs actually reach the
ground — it doesn't add stability, it just adds clutter. A strong argument is built from a FEW
genuinely different, well-developed points, not the maximum possible number of points."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before adding another point, ask: is this genuinely a NEW kind of
reason, or is it the same underlying point wearing different words? Two or three fully developed,
truly distinct points almost always beat five overlapping ones."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Five points about school start times that are really all restating "everyone is tired" (repetitive,
   not genuinely distinct) vs. two points — one about adolescent sleep science, one about a specific
   pilot program's attendance data — that bring genuinely different evidence types to the argument.
2. A student adding a sixth reason to an already-strong argument just to "have more" (quantity
   over quality) vs. a student stopping at three well-developed reasons because a fourth would
   overlap with the first (recognizing diminishing, not additive, returns).

**s6_path:** If the student still piles up overlapping points after the bridge, have them write each
point's core idea in exactly five words and physically compare the five-word summaries side by side
— overlapping summaries reveal the repetition immediately.

### MC-B-ARGUMENT-ORGANIZATION-DOESNT-MATTER-AS-LONG-AS-THE-POINTS-ARE-GOOD

**Trigger signal:** Student assembles genuinely strong, distinct points but arranges them in a random
or arbitrary order, believing organization is a cosmetic afterthought rather than something that
affects how convincing the argument feels — e.g., burying the single strongest point in the middle
of the argument with no strategic reasoning behind the sequence.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student the SAME three strong points about a position,
arranged two different ways: once in a random, arbitrary order, and once with a deliberate structure
(e.g., building from a broadly relatable point to the single strongest, most specific point placed
last, where it has maximum impact right before the conclusion). Ask: "These have the identical three
points. Does the order change how convincing it feels to read?" Guide the student to notice that
placement affects emphasis — the last point read often lands with the most weight, and a strong point
buried in the middle can feel like an afterthought.

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Organizing your points is like planning where to put the courses in a meal —
serving your best dish first might set high expectations that make the rest feel like a letdown;
saving it for last can build anticipation and end on your strongest note. The exact same three dishes
served in a different order create a different overall experience. Your argument's points work the
same way — the SAME points in a different order can feel more or less convincing."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "After choosing your points, deliberately decide an order and be able to
explain WHY: are you building toward your strongest point for a memorable ending, or leading with
your strongest point to hook a skeptical reader immediately? Either can work — but the order should
be a deliberate choice, not left to chance."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. The same three-point argument arranged randomly (no explainable reason for the sequence) vs.
   arranged with the strongest, most specific point saved for last, explicitly building toward it
   (a deliberate, explainable structural choice).
2. A student who can explain "I put this point last because it's my strongest and most memorable"
   (deliberate ordering) vs. a student who says "I just wrote them in the order I thought of them"
   (no ordering rationale at all).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to order points arbitrarily after the bridge, have them rank
their own points from weakest to strongest first, as a separate step, before deciding where each
point goes in the final argument — forcing the ranking to happen before the placement decision.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.composition.claim-evidence-reasoning`):** "Write one complete claim, evidence,
and reasoning unit for this simple prompt." A student who cannot construct one sound CER unit lacks
the building block this concept assembles — route to `eng.composition.claim-evidence-reasoning`
review first.

**PD-2 (checks basic point-counting/overview skill, informal pre-check):** "How many separate
supporting points do you see in this short sample argument?" A student who cannot distinguish
separate points from restated ones needs a brief primer before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Table-Legs-and-Meal-Courses Anchor.** Combine two physical demonstrations: first, show a small
table propped up by four identically short legs that still don't reach the ground (illustrating
that MORE legs of the same inadequate length doesn't create stability — directly seeding the fix for
MC-A, since it shows quantity without genuine distinctness/development doesn't add support). Second,
lay out three index cards representing "courses" of a meal and physically rearrange their order
twice, asking each time how the experience changes (illustrating that identical content in different
sequence changes the overall effect — directly seeding the fix for MC-B).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Distinguishing Genuinely Different Points from Restatements

Using the five-word-summary test, teach students to identify when supporting points are actually
distinct versus restatements of the same underlying idea. Student practice: given a five-point
outline, identify which points overlap and which are genuinely distinct.

### TA-2 — Selecting Two or Three Strong, Distinct Points

Teach the "quality over quantity" principle directly addressing MC-A. Student practice: given five
candidate points for one position, select the two or three strongest, most distinct ones and justify
the selection.

### TA-3 — Ranking Points by Strength

Teach a ranking step (weakest to strongest) as a distinct, separate task from placement. Student
practice: given three selected points, rank them from weakest to strongest with a one-sentence
justification for each ranking.

### TA-4 — Deliberate Ordering Strategies

Directly address MC-B: teach two common ordering strategies (strongest-first for immediate impact
with skeptical audiences; strongest-last for a memorable, building conclusion) and connect the
choice to audience (recalling the Audience and Purpose concept). Student practice: given three ranked
points and two different audience profiles, choose and justify a different order for each.

### TA-5 — Constructing a Full Multi-Point Argument

Give the student a position and raw material (five candidate points). They must select two or three
genuinely distinct points, develop each into a full CER unit, rank them, and arrange them in a
deliberate, justified order — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Prompt: "Argue whether students should be allowed to retake tests." Fully
worked: five candidate points are shown, three identified as overlapping ("less pressure," "less
anxiety," "less stress" — all restating the same underlying idea) and two as genuinely distinct
(measurable long-term retention improvement from a study; a specific school's pilot program
completion-rate data). The two distinct points are each developed into a full CER unit, ranked, and
arranged with the stronger point (the pilot data, more concrete and local) placed last for impact.
Annotation confirms both the selection process (fixing MC-A) and the deliberate ordering rationale
(fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New position ("Argue whether the school should offer a coding elective"). Student
is given five candidate points (with some overlap built in) and prompted with guiding questions
("Which of these points are really saying the same thing in different words? Once you've picked your
two or three distinct points, which is strongest, and where should it go for the most impact given
this audience?") to complete the selection, ranking, and ordering.

**WE-3 (Independent):** New position ("Argue whether the school should extend the library's open
hours"). Student generates their own candidate points, selects and develops two or three distinct
ones into full CER units, ranks them, and arranges them in a deliberately justified order, unaided.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Distinct vs. Overlapping Points**
Given a five-point outline, identify which points are genuinely distinct and which are restatements,
using the five-word-summary test.

**MP-2 [P36] — Selecting Strong, Distinct Points**
Given five candidate points for a position, select the two or three strongest and most distinct,
justifying the selection.

**MP-3 [P36] — Ranking by Strength**
Given three selected points, rank them from weakest to strongest with a one-sentence justification
for each.

**MP-4 [P36] — Deliberate Ordering for a Given Audience**
Given three ranked points and a specified audience (skeptical vs. sympathetic), choose and justify an
order suited to that audience.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Multi-Point Argument Construction**
Given a new position and five candidate points, construct a complete multi-point argument: select
two or three distinct points, develop each into a CER unit, rank them, and justify a deliberate order.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a CER gap routes back to that
prerequisite, a point-counting gap gets a brief primer. During Component 4 (S1, active instruction),
MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1/TA-2 (point selection) and MC-B during TA-4 (ordering) — these
are somewhat sequential, since ordering only becomes a live question once genuinely distinct points
exist to order. If either fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery
probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1/MP-2 failures indicate residual MC-A; MP-3 failure alone
indicates the ranking step itself (TA-3) needs review, distinct from either misconception; MP-4
failure alone indicates residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an
integration gap — provide one more fully-guided example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student can select and rank points correctly (MP-1 through MP-3) but defaults to a single
  ordering strategy regardless of audience (MP-4), revisit the audience-sensitivity connection to
  `eng.composition.audience-and-purpose` alongside TA-4.
- If the student produces genuinely strong individual CER units but struggles to see them as part of
  ONE unified argument (treating each point as its own isolated mini-essay), flag this as a
  cohesion gap for reinforcement alongside `eng.writing.transitions-and-cohesion`.
- If the student over-applies the "fewer is better" lesson from MC-A and cuts a genuinely distinct,
  valuable point just to minimize count, clarify that two or three is a guideline for typical
  assignment lengths, not an absolute ceiling — the test is genuine distinctness, not a hard number.
- Students who excel at generating candidate points but struggle with the selection/ranking
  discipline may benefit from a "brainstorm freely, then ruthlessly cut" two-phase process made
  explicit, rather than trying to select while still generating.
