## Component 1 — Learning Objectives

- LO1: Define a **field extension** $K/F$ as a field $K$ containing a subfield $F$, and define the **degree** $[K:F] = \dim_F(K)$ (the dimension of $K$ as a vector space OVER $F$) — computing this degree for simple, familiar extensions.
- LO2: State and apply the **Tower Law**: for a chain of fields $F\subseteq E\subseteq K$, $[K:F]=[K:E][E:F]$ — the degree of the "outermost" extension equals the PRODUCT of the degrees of each step in the chain.
- LO3: Distinguish **simple** extensions ($K=F(\alpha)$, generated by adjoining a single element $\alpha$) from general extensions, and distinguish **algebraic** extensions (every element of $K$ satisfies some polynomial with coefficients in $F$) from **transcendental** extensions (some element of $K$ satisfies NO such polynomial) — correctly classifying familiar examples.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Check

Assumes mastery of `math.abst.field` (the field axioms — a commutative ring where every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse; examples $\mathbb{Q},\mathbb{R},\mathbb{C}$).

## Component 3 — Core Explanation

$K$ is a **field extension** of $F$ (written $K/F$) if $K$ is itself a field CONTAINING $F$ as a subfield — every element of $F$ is also an element of $K$, and $F$'s field operations are just $K$'s operations restricted to $F$.

**The degree** $[K:F]$ is defined as $\dim_F(K)$ — the dimension of $K$, viewed as a VECTOR SPACE over $F$ (scalars from $F$, vectors are elements of $K$ itself). E.g. $\mathbb{C}$ over $\mathbb{R}$: every complex number $a+bi$ is uniquely a linear combination $a(1)+b(i)$ of the basis $\{1,i\}$ with REAL coefficients $a,b$ — so $[\mathbb{C}:\mathbb{R}]=2$.

**The Tower Law**: for a chain of fields $F\subseteq E\subseteq K$ (each an extension of the previous),
$$[K:F] = [K:E]\cdot[E:F]$$
— the degree of the full extension is the PRODUCT (not the sum) of the degrees of each individual step. This mirrors how a basis for $K$ over $F$ can be constructed by combining a basis for $K$ over $E$ with a basis for $E$ over $F$ (multiplying corresponding basis elements together, giving $[K:E]\times[E:F]$ total combinations).

**Simple, algebraic, and transcendental extensions**: a **simple** extension $K=F(\alpha)$ is generated by adjoining a SINGLE element $\alpha$ to $F$ (the smallest field containing both $F$ and $\alpha$). An extension is **algebraic** if EVERY element of $K$ satisfies some polynomial equation with coefficients in $F$ (e.g. $i$ satisfies $x^2+1=0$ over $\mathbb{R}$). An extension is **transcendental** if SOME element of $K$ satisfies NO such polynomial (e.g. $\pi$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ — a famous, hard-won result that $\pi$ is not a root of ANY polynomial with rational coefficients).

## Component 4 — Worked Examples

**Example 1 (LO1 — computing the degree directly)**: $[\mathbb{C}:\mathbb{R}]=2$ (basis $\{1,i\}$, every complex number $a+bi$ uniquely expressed with real $a,b$). $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2):\mathbb{Q}]=2$ (every element of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2)$ uniquely expressed as $a+b\sqrt2$ with rational $a,b$, so basis $\{1,\sqrt2\}$).

**Example 2 (LO2 — the Tower Law applied concretely)**: Consider $\mathbb{Q}\subseteq\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2)\subseteq\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,\sqrt3)$. Given $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2):\mathbb{Q}]=2$ and $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,\sqrt3):\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2)]=2$ (adjoining $\sqrt3$ to $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2)$ is itself a degree-2 step, since $\sqrt3\notin\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2)$ and $\sqrt3$ satisfies $x^2-3=0$): by the Tower Law, $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,\sqrt3):\mathbb{Q}] = [\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,\sqrt3):\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2)]\times[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2):\mathbb{Q}] = 2\times2=4$.

**Example 3 (LO3 — algebraic vs. transcendental, breaking MC-1)**: $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2)/\mathbb{Q}$ is ALGEBRAIC — every element $a+b\sqrt2$ satisfies a polynomial over $\mathbb{Q}$ (e.g. $\sqrt2$ itself satisfies $x^2-2=0$; more generally, every element of this finite-degree extension satisfies some polynomial, a general fact about FINITE extensions). Contrast: $\mathbb{Q}(\pi)/\mathbb{Q}$ is TRANSCENDENTAL — $\pi$ satisfies NO polynomial with rational coefficients (a deep, genuinely hard theorem, first proven by Lindemann in 1882 — not something to re-derive here, but essential to know as a fact). A student who assumes "$\pi$ is just some real number, so of course it satisfies SOME polynomial like every other number" would be directly contradicting this specific, celebrated, and non-obvious mathematical fact.

## Component 5 — Teaching Actions

### Teaching Action A01 — Extensions and Degree, via the Already-Known $\mathbb{Q}\subset\mathbb{R}\subset\mathbb{C}$ Chain (Primitive P11: Representation Shift)

Recall the already-familiar chain $\mathbb{Q}\subset\mathbb{R}\subset\mathbb{C}$, each a field containing the previous. State: "$K/F$ just formalizes 'a bigger field built on top of a smaller one' — and the DEGREE measures how much bigger, in a precise, dimension-counting sense: how many independent $F$-coefficients you need to express every element of $K$." Work Example 1's direct degree computations.

Shift representation to the Tower Law: work Example 2's chained extension, showing the degree multiplies (not adds) across steps, and connecting this to the basis-combination intuition (Component 3).

- **MC-1 hook**: ask "since $\pi$ is a perfectly ordinary real number, does it satisfy SOME polynomial equation with rational coefficients, the same way $\sqrt2$ does?" — a "yes" answer reveals MC-1 (assuming every real number must be algebraic over $\mathbb{Q}$, not recognizing transcendental numbers like $\pi$ as a genuine, proven exception).

### Teaching Action A02 — Algebraic vs. Transcendental, and the Tower Law's Multiplicative Structure (Primitive P06: Contrast Pair)

**Contrast 1 (targets MC-1)**: Place $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2)/\mathbb{Q}$ (algebraic, $\sqrt2$ satisfies $x^2-2=0$) directly beside $\mathbb{Q}(\pi)/\mathbb{Q}$ (transcendental, $\pi$ satisfies NO polynomial over $\mathbb{Q}$ — a proven, celebrated fact, not a gap in current knowledge). State clearly: "algebraic numbers are a genuinely SPECIAL, restricted class — most familiar 'nice' irrational numbers (like $\sqrt2,\sqrt[3]5$) happen to be algebraic, but $\pi$ and $e$ are proven transcendental, and in fact 'almost all' real numbers (in a precise measure-theoretic sense) are transcendental, even though algebraic numbers are the ones we most often encounter by name."

**Contrast 2**: Revisit the Tower Law's multiplicative (not additive) structure explicitly — ask "if you extend by degree 2, then extend again by degree 3, is the total degree 5 or 6?" — work through the basis-combination argument (2 basis elements from the first step, each combined with 3 basis elements from the second step, giving $2\times3=6$ total independent combinations) to confirm multiplication, not addition, governs the Tower Law.

### Teaching Action A03 — Mastery Gate (Primitive P91)

P91 = P77 → P55 → P76 → P55 → P75 → P55 → P74 → P55 → P78.

- **P77 (4-problem set)**:
  1. Compute $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt5):\mathbb{Q}]$, stating a basis.
  2. Given $\mathbb{Q}\subseteq\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2)\subseteq\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,i)$ with $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2):\mathbb{Q}]=2$ and $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,i):\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2)]=2$, use the Tower Law to find $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,i):\mathbb{Q}]$.
  3. Explain why $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2)/\mathbb{Q}$ is a SIMPLE extension, identifying the generating element $\alpha$.
  4. A student claims every extension of $\mathbb{Q}$ must be algebraic, since "extensions are built by adjoining specific numbers, and specific numbers always satisfy some equation." Using $\mathbb{Q}(\pi)$, explain why this claim is false.
- **P76 (Transfer Probe, mode = independence)**: "A classic result in the theory of compass-and-straightedge constructions states that a length is constructible (using only compass and straightedge from a unit length) if and only if it lies in a field extension of $\mathbb{Q}$ whose degree is a power of 2 (i.e., $[K:\mathbb{Q}]=2^n$ for some $n$). (a) Using the Tower Law, explain why building up a constructible length through a SEQUENCE of degree-2 extension steps (each step adjoining a square root, as compass-and-straightedge operations naturally do) automatically produces a total degree that is a power of 2. (b) The classical problem 'doubling the cube' requires constructing a length equal to $\sqrt[3]{2}$, which generates a degree-3 extension of $\mathbb{Q}$ (not a power of 2) — using this lesson's degree/Tower-Law framework, explain why this fact alone proves the cube cannot be doubled with compass and straightedge, without needing to check any specific attempted construction."

## Component 6 — Misconception Registry

| ID | Label | Description | Severity |
|---|---|---|---|
| MC-1 | EVERY-REAL-NUMBER-ASSUMED-ALGEBRAIC | Believing every real number (including $\pi$, $e$) must satisfy some polynomial equation with rational coefficients, not recognizing transcendental numbers as a genuine, proven exception | Foundational |
| MC-2 | TOWER-LAW-DEGREES-ADDED-RATHER-THAN-MULTIPLIED | Believing the Tower Law's degree for a chain of extensions is the SUM of the individual step degrees, rather than their PRODUCT | Foundational |
| MC-3 | SIMPLE-EXTENSION-CONFUSED-WITH-SMALL-DEGREE-EXTENSION | Believing "simple" extension means "small degree" or "easy to understand," rather than the precise technical meaning: generated by adjoining a SINGLE element $\alpha$ (which can still have arbitrarily large or even infinite degree) | Moderate |

### Protocol B — Repair Actions

- **B01 (targets MC-1)**: P27 (name it: "Universal Algebraicity Assumption") → P41 (detect: ask whether $\pi$ satisfies some polynomial equation with rational coefficients) → P64 (conceptual shift: re-anchor on Lindemann's theorem as a genuine, historically hard-won mathematical fact — $\pi$'s transcendence is proven, not merely unconfirmed).
- **B02 (targets MC-2)**: P27 (name it: "Tower Law Additive Misapplication") → P41 (detect: ask for the total degree of a two-step chain with individual degrees 2 and 3, checking for an answer of 5 instead of 6) → P64 (conceptual shift: re-derive via the basis-combination argument explicitly, showing multiplication, not addition, correctly counts the combined basis).
- **B03 (targets MC-3)**: P27 (name it: "Simple-Extension Misnomer Confusion") → P41 (detect: ask whether a "simple" extension must have small or easily-computed degree) → P64 (conceptual shift: re-anchor on the precise definition — "simple" refers to the GENERATOR COUNT (a single element $\alpha$), completely independent of how large the resulting degree $[F(\alpha):F]$ turns out to be).

## Component 8 — Teaching Notes

- estimated_hours = 6 with an expert/understand tag places this at the "2 main TAs + gate" tier — A01 (extensions and degree, grounded in the familiar $\mathbb{Q}\subset\mathbb{R}\subset\mathbb{C}$ chain) and A02 (algebraic/transcendental contrast plus the Tower Law's multiplicative structure) jointly cover all three LOs.
- MC-1 (every real number assumed algebraic) was made this blueprint's central focus because algebraic numbers genuinely dominate everyday mathematical experience (nearly every specific number a student names by formula — roots, ratios, familiar constants built from arithmetic — turns out to be algebraic), making the EXISTENCE of transcendental numbers feel almost exotic or exceptional rather than the norm (in fact the reverse is true measure-theoretically) — Example 3's direct algebraic/transcendental pairing was chosen to make this correction concrete and historically grounded (citing Lindemann's actual 1882 result) rather than asserting it as an unexplained curiosity.
- The compass-and-straightedge transfer probe was chosen because the degree/Tower-Law framework's most celebrated classical application — proving ancient construction problems (doubling the cube, trisecting an angle) impossible — gives this otherwise abstract algebraic machinery a a strikingly concrete, historically famous payoff, directly exercising the Tower Law's multiplicative structure in service of a genuine impossibility proof.
