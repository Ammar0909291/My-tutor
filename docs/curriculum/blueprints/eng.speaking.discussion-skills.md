## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-DISAGREEING-MEANS-BEING-RUDE-OR-CONFRONTATIONAL
- **Trigger signal:** Student avoids voicing genuine disagreement in a group discussion, either staying silent when they actually disagree or vaguely half-agreeing to avoid conflict, treating any expressed disagreement as inherently rude or damaging to the group dynamic.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present two ways of disagreeing with a group member's suggestion: silently going along with an idea the student thinks is flawed (avoiding "rudeness" but letting a weak idea stand unchallenged) versus "I see why you'd think that, but I actually think the data points the other way, because..." (respectful, substantive disagreement that improves the discussion). Ask which response actually serves the group's goal of reaching the best conclusion.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Respectful disagreement isn't rude — it's one of the most valuable contributions you can make to a discussion. Groups that never disagree often miss flaws in their thinking. The key isn't whether you disagree, it's HOW: acknowledge the other person's point first, then explain your different view with reasons, not just contradiction."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "When you disagree, say so — but frame it respectfully: acknowledge what the other person said, then explain your reasoning for a different view. Silence or fake agreement doesn't help the group; substantive, respectful disagreement does."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Disagreement-as-rudeness (voicing a different view is inherently confrontational and should be avoided) vs. (b) Disagreement-as-contribution (respectfully voiced disagreement, with acknowledgment and reasoning, is a valuable and expected part of productive discussion).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1 re-check, then re-run TA-2's acknowledge-then-disagree frame before continuing.

### MC-A-GOOD-DISCUSSION-CONTRIBUTION-MEANS-INTRODUCING-A-NEW-IDEA
- **Trigger signal:** Student believes the only way to contribute meaningfully to a discussion is to introduce an entirely new point, ignoring or ranking as less valuable the equally important skill of building on, extending, or synthesizing what previous speakers have already said — sometimes repeatedly changing the subject rather than deepening the current thread.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present a discussion transcript where Speaker A makes a point, and two possible next turns: Speaker B introduces a completely unrelated new topic (technically "new," but abandons A's point without engagement) versus Speaker C says "Building on what you said, I think that also means..." and extends A's idea further. Ask which contribution actually moves the discussion's shared understanding forward.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Introducing a new idea is only one way to contribute — and often not the most valuable one. Building on someone else's point, adding a supporting example, or connecting two earlier points together are equally valuable discussion moves. A great discussion contributor is often someone who deepens what's already been said, not just someone with the most new ideas."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Before jumping to a new point, consider whether building on, extending, or connecting what was just said would serve the discussion better. Phrases like 'building on that...' or 'that connects to what was said earlier because...' are just as valuable a contribution as a brand-new idea."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) New-idea-only-value (only introducing a fresh point counts as a real contribution) vs. (b) Multi-mode-contribution (building on, extending, connecting, and introducing new ideas are all equally valid, valuable discussion moves).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1, then re-present the Concrete Anchor's building-blocks framing before continuing to TA-3.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

### PD-1: Question-and-Answer Readiness
- **Tests:** Can the student already formulate clear questions and give bullseye-accurate answers in a one-on-one exchange — the foundational precision this concept extends into a multi-participant group setting?
- **Probe:** Engage the student in a short question-answer exchange and assess whether their answers directly address what was asked.
- **Pass condition:** Demonstrates reasonably precise, on-target responses.
- **Fail routing:** If answer precision is weak, route to `eng.speaking.asking-and-answering-questions` review — group discussion multiplies the importance of staying precisely on-topic across more participants.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**"The Building-Blocks Tower."** Present a set of building blocks representing discussion contributions. Have the student physically build a tower where each new block must either: (a) sit directly ON TOP of the previous block (building on/extending a prior point), (b) connect to a block elsewhere in the tower with a small bridge piece (connecting two earlier points), or (c) start a new stack next to the existing one (introducing a genuinely new idea) — all three are legitimate tower-building moves. Contrast this with a scattered pile of blocks that never connect (representing a discussion where everyone introduces disconnected new ideas with no building or connecting, producing no coherent shared structure) — seeding MC-A-GOOD-DISCUSSION-CONTRIBUTION-MEANS-INTRODUCING-A-NEW-IDEA's fix. Separately, demonstrate a "respectful challenge" block that can be placed to gently redirect the tower's direction (representing disagreement) without knocking any existing blocks down — seeding MC-DISAGREEING-MEANS-BEING-RUDE-OR-CONFRONTATIONAL's fix, showing disagreement can reshape the discussion without destroying it.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1: Recognizing Discussion Contribution Types
Present a discussion transcript and have the student label each turn as "new idea," "building on," "connecting," or "respectful disagreement," recognizing all four as legitimate contribution types.

### TA-2: The Acknowledge-Then-Disagree Frame
Using the discrimination pairs from MC-DISAGREEING-MEANS-BEING-RUDE-OR-CONFRONTATIONAL, teach the explicit frame: acknowledge the prior speaker's point first ("I see your point about..."), then state the differing view with reasoning ("but I think... because..."). Student practices this frame on 3 scenarios with a provided disagreement point.

### TA-3: Building On and Connecting Practice
Using the discrimination pairs from MC-A-GOOD-DISCUSSION-CONTRIBUTION-MEANS-INTRODUCING-A-NEW-IDEA, present a discussion in progress and have the student practice generating a "building on" contribution and a "connecting" contribution (linking two earlier points) rather than defaulting to a new topic.

### TA-4: Balancing Contribution Types in a Live Discussion
Student participates in or narrates a short group discussion (4-5 turns) and self-tracks which contribution type each of their turns represents, aiming for a mix rather than all-new-ideas or all-agreement.

### TA-5: Full Discussion Simulation with Respectful Disagreement
Student engages in a longer simulated discussion (6-8 turns) on an assigned topic, including at least one instance of respectful disagreement using the acknowledge-then-disagree frame and at least one building-on/connecting contribution — the generative capstone integrating both misconception fixes.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

### WE-1: Contribution-Type Labeling (Scaffolded)
**Transcript excerpt:** A: "I think the school should add more sports programs." B: "Building on that, I'd add that it could also help with student stress levels." C: "That connects to what we discussed earlier about mental health resources." D: "I actually disagree — I think funding is too limited, and academics should come first."
**Walkthrough:** Label B as "building on," C as "connecting," D as "respectful disagreement" (note D acknowledges implicitly by engaging with the funding reality, though could be strengthened with explicit acknowledgment).

### WE-2: Acknowledge-Then-Disagree Frame (Guided)
**Scenario:** A group member says, "I think we should skip the introduction and get straight to the main content." Student disagrees.
**Walkthrough:** Apply the frame: "I see why you'd want to save time, but I think a short introduction actually helps the audience follow the main content better, because it sets up context they'll need." Confirm this acknowledges the other person's reasoning before offering a substantive alternative.

### WE-3: Full Discussion with Mixed Contributions (Independent)
**Task:** Student participates in a 6-turn discussion about whether homework should be assigned on weekends, tracking their own contribution types.
**Walkthrough:** Student produces a mix: one new idea (introducing a new consideration like family time), one building-on contribution, one respectful disagreement using the frame, confirming a balanced discussion profile rather than all one type.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Voice a respectful disagreement**
Given a discussion scenario with a point the student is meant to disagree with, student produces a response using the acknowledge-then-disagree frame rather than staying silent or vaguely agreeing. *(Directly targets MC-DISAGREEING-MEANS-BEING-RUDE-OR-CONFRONTATIONAL.)*

**MP-2 [P36] — Generate a building-on or connecting contribution**
Given a discussion in progress, student generates a contribution that builds on or connects to a prior point rather than defaulting to a new, disconnected topic. *(Directly targets MC-A-GOOD-DISCUSSION-CONTRIBUTION-MEANS-INTRODUCING-A-NEW-IDEA.)*

**MP-3 [P36] — Contribution-type labeling**
Given a discussion transcript, student correctly labels each turn's contribution type.

**MP-4 [P36] — Balanced discussion participation**
Student participates in a short discussion, producing at least 2 different contribution types across their turns.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full discussion simulation**
Student conducts or narrates a 6-8 turn discussion demonstrating balanced contribution types, including one respectful disagreement and one building-on/connecting contribution.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** At session open (S0), confirm PD-1 passes — discussion skills extend one-on-one question-answer precision into a multi-participant setting, so weakness in basic answer precision compounds across more speakers. During instruction (S1), watch for both misconception patterns: avoiding disagreement (MC-DISAGREEING-MEANS-BEING-RUDE-OR-CONFRONTATIONAL) and over-valuing new ideas over building/connecting (MC-A-GOOD-DISCUSSION-CONTRIBUTION-MEANS-INTRODUCING-A-NEW-IDEA) both stem from an incomplete model of what makes a discussion contribution valuable — redirect both to the Building-Blocks Tower anchor. If the student stays silent during a disagreement opportunity or keeps introducing disconnected topics during TA-2/TA-3 (S6), drop back to the Concrete Anchor's respectful-challenge-block and connecting-bridge demonstrations before returning to live discussion practice. At mastery-probe stage (S9), do not certify mastery unless MP-1 and MP-2 both pass — a student who labels contribution types correctly (MP-3) but cannot actually produce a respectful disagreement or a building-on contribution in live practice has not yet internalized the participatory skill this concept requires.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student avoids voicing disagreement, weight remediation toward MC-DISAGREEING-MEANS-BEING-RUDE-OR-CONFRONTATIONAL's bridge text and re-run TA-2's acknowledge-then-disagree frame practice before returning to live discussion.
- If the student defaults to introducing new, disconnected topics, weight remediation toward MC-A-GOOD-DISCUSSION-CONTRIBUTION-MEANS-INTRODUCING-A-NEW-IDEA's bridge text and TA-3's building-on/connecting practice specifically.
- This concept feeds two direct downstream unlocks (`eng.speaking.public-speaking-basics`, `eng.speaking.debate-skills`) — do not certify mastery without TA-5's full discussion simulation, since debate skills in particular require comfortable, structured disagreement as a core mechanic, not an avoided one.
- Reuse the "building-blocks tower" framing explicitly when introducing `eng.speaking.debate-skills` — that concept formalizes structured disagreement (the respectful-challenge block) into a more rigorous argumentative format.
