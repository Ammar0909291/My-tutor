## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-MULTIPLE-MEANING-WORDS-ARE-JUST-HOMONYMS
- **Trigger signal:** Student treats multiple-meaning (polysemous) words as identical to homonyms, missing a subtle but real distinction: homonyms are typically UNRELATED meanings that happen to share spelling/sound by coincidence (a "bat" the animal vs. a "bat" for baseball — no meaningful connection), while POLYSEMY involves meanings that are RELATED, having evolved from a shared core sense (e.g., "bright" meaning "shining with light" and "bright" meaning "intelligent" — both connect to an underlying idea of "clarity/illumination"), even though the everyday distinction between the two is sometimes fuzzy and dictionaries don't always agree.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said 'bright' (shining) and 'bright' (smart) are exactly like 'bat' (animal) and 'bat' (sports equipment) — totally unrelated meanings that just happen to share a word. Is there any conceptual connection between 'shining' and 'intelligent,' the way there's clearly NO connection between a flying mammal and sports equipment?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Homonyms (like 'bat'/'bat') typically have historically UNRELATED meanings that coincidentally share the same spelling/sound. POLYSEMOUS multiple-meaning words (like 'bright' meaning both 'shining' and 'intelligent') have meanings that are RELATED, connected by an underlying shared concept (both relate to a kind of 'clarity' or vividness) — even though the distinction can be fuzzy at the edges and isn't always agreed upon by dictionaries."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Check whether a word's multiple meanings are conceptually RELATED (polysemy) or historically UNRELATED/coincidental (homonymy) — recognizing this distinction, even though borderline cases exist."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "bright" (shining) / "bright" (intelligent) — related meanings, both connected to clarity/vividness (polysemy) vs. "bat" (animal) / "bat" (sports equipment) — unrelated, coincidental (homonymy).
  - "head" (body part) / "head" (leader, as in "head of the company") — related through a shared "topmost/most important part" concept (polysemy) vs. a genuinely coincidental homonym pair.
- **s6_path:** "Since both categories involve one word-form with multiple meanings, it's very reasonable to treat them the same — but noticing whether the meanings are conceptually connected or purely coincidental is a real, if sometimes subtle, distinction worth developing a feel for."

### MC-DICTIONARY-LISTS-MEANINGS-IN-IMPORTANCE-ORDER
- **Trigger signal:** Student assumes a dictionary always lists a word's meanings in order of importance or frequency of use (with meaning #1 always being the "main" or most common sense), rather than recognizing that dictionaries vary in their ordering convention — some list by historical/etymological order (oldest meaning first), others by frequency — meaning the FIRST listed sense is not automatically the most relevant one for a given context, and readers must use CONTEXT, not dictionary position, to determine which sense applies.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You picked the first meaning listed in the dictionary for 'bank' just because it was first. Does the CONTEXT of the sentence — 'We sat on the river bank' — actually support that first-listed meaning, or does context point to a different one?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Dictionaries order meanings according to varying conventions (historical/etymological order in some, frequency order in others) — the FIRST listed meaning is not automatically the most common or most relevant one for your specific context. Always use the surrounding CONTEXT to determine which of a word's multiple meanings actually applies, rather than defaulting to whichever sense the dictionary happens to list first."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Use context, not dictionary listing order, to determine which meaning of a multiple-meaning word applies in a given sentence."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "We sat on the river bank" (context clearly indicates the landform meaning, regardless of which sense a dictionary lists first) vs. blindly picking the dictionary's first-listed sense.
  - A word whose dictionary's first entry is a rare/historical sense, while the sentence context calls for a more common modern sense — context should still win.
- **s6_path:** "It's a very reasonable assumption that a dictionary lists its 'main' meaning first, since that's how many reference lists work — but dictionary ordering conventions vary, and only context reliably tells you which meaning is actually intended in a specific sentence."

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The meaning-web vs. coincidence-pair sort**

> Present a word with multiple meanings (e.g., "head": body part, leader, top of a page, foam on beer). Draw a "meaning web" connecting each sense back to a shared core concept ("topmost/most important part"). Then present a true homonym pair (e.g., "bat"/"bat") and show there's NO connecting web possible — they're simply two coincidental meanings. This anchors the relatedness test visually.

---

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Identifying Related (Polysemous) Multiple Meanings [C]**

Present polysemous words (head, bright, run, foot); student maps the related meanings back to a shared core concept.

**TA-2 — Contrasting Polysemy with Homonymy [C]**

Present a mixed set including both polysemous words and true homonyms; student sorts them using the relatedness test, directly targeting MC-MULTIPLE-MEANING-WORDS-ARE-JUST-HOMONYMS.

**TA-3 — Using Context to Select the Correct Meaning [C→P]**

Present sentences using a multiple-meaning word in different contexts; student selects the contextually correct meaning, directly targeting MC-DICTIONARY-LISTS-MEANINGS-IN-IMPORTANCE-ORDER by explicitly NOT defaulting to a "first" or "main" meaning.

**TA-4 — Checking Dictionary Entries Without Assuming Order = Importance [P]**

Given a dictionary entry with multiple numbered senses, student determines which sense fits a specific sentence's context, regardless of the sense's numerical position in the entry.

**TA-5 — Multiple-Meaning Words in Extended Text [P]**

Student reads a passage containing several multiple-meaning words used in specific contexts and correctly identifies the intended sense of each.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — related meanings)**

> "How are 'foot' (body part) and 'foot' (base of a mountain) related in meaning?"

Target response: both relate to the lowest/bottom part of something — a shared conceptual connection (polysemy).

**WE-2 (Foundational — polysemy vs. homonymy)**

> "Are 'foot' (body part/base of mountain) and 'bat' (animal/sports equipment) the same kind of multiple-meaning word?"

Target response: no — "foot" is polysemous (related meanings); "bat" is a true homonym (unrelated, coincidental meanings).

**WE-3 (Intermediate — context over dictionary order)**

> "The word 'run' has many dictionary meanings. In 'She has a run in her stockings,' which meaning applies, regardless of dictionary listing order?"

Target response: a tear/snag in fabric — determined by context, not by whichever sense the dictionary lists first.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Related meanings, novel word**
"How are 'bright' (shining) and 'bright' (intelligent) related in meaning?"
*Target:* both connect to an idea of clarity/vividness — a shared conceptual link.

**MP-2 [P36] — Polysemy vs. homonymy, novel pair**
"Is 'bank' (financial institution) and 'bank' (river edge) polysemy or homonymy?"
*Target:* debatable/borderline in some analyses, but commonly treated as homonymy (historically unrelated origins) — correctly reasoning through the relatedness test rather than assuming automatically.

**MP-3 [P36] — Context over dictionary order, novel example**
"In 'The company will table this proposal,' which meaning of 'table' applies — furniture, or to postpone/set aside?"
*Target:* to postpone/set aside — determined by context (verb usage in a business sentence), not by whichever sense might be listed first in a dictionary.

**MP-4 [P36] — Selecting sense from a dictionary entry**
"A dictionary lists five senses for 'run.' Given the sentence 'He hit a home run,' which sense applies?"
*Target:* correctly identifies the baseball-scoring sense, regardless of its position among the five listed senses.

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain multiple-meaning words**
"In your own words: are multiple-meaning words the same as homonyms, and does a dictionary's first-listed meaning tell you which sense to use?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that polysemous multiple-meaning words have related senses (unlike often-unrelated homonyms), and that context — not dictionary listing order — determines which sense applies in a given sentence.

**Protocol routing:** S0 (secure on homonyms-homophones, new to polysemy specifically): dwell on TA-1's clear meaning-web examples before introducing the sometimes-fuzzy polysemy/homonymy contrast. ## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Relatedness test distinguishes polysemy from homonymy, with acknowledged fuzzy edges**: teach the conceptual-connection test explicitly while being honest that some cases are genuinely debatable even among linguists — this builds appropriate epistemic humility alongside the skill.
- **Context always overrides dictionary listing position**: this is a crucial, practical skill especially for dictionary-dependent learners — never let "it's listed first" substitute for genuine context-based sense selection.
- **Meaning-web visualization aids polysemy recognition**: reuse this technique whenever a new polysemous word is introduced, to make the underlying conceptual connection concrete and memorable.
- **Special priority for dictionary-reliant EL learners**: for S9 learners who frequently consult dictionaries, explicitly and repeatedly reinforce the context-over-order principle, since this population is especially likely to encounter and be misled by dictionary entry-ordering conventions.
