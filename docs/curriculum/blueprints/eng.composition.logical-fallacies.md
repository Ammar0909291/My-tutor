**Concept:** Logical Fallacies — common errors in reasoning that undermine an argument's validity,
such as ad hominem (attacking the person instead of the argument), straw man (misrepresenting an
opposing argument to make it easier to attack), and false dilemma (presenting only two options when
more exist).

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Argumentation Basics (prerequisite) taught constructing a
sound, multi-point argument. Logical Fallacies teaches the inverse skill: recognizing when an
argument's REASONING is broken, even if it sounds persuasive or the conclusion happens to be one the
listener agrees with — a genuinely distinct skill from evaluating whether a conclusion is likeable or
true. This concept has no further downstream unlocks in the current curriculum but completes the
argumentation sequence by teaching students to spot flawed reasoning, both in others' arguments and in
their own.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) identify at least three common logical fallacies (ad
hominem, straw man, false dilemma) in a given argument, naming the specific fallacy and explaining
what reasoning error it represents, (b) explain that a fallacious argument can still have a TRUE
conclusion — the fallacy is about broken reasoning, not necessarily a false result — and (c) avoid
committing these same fallacies in their own argumentative writing.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-IF-AN-ARGUMENTS-CONCLUSION-IS-TRUE-OR-AGREEABLE-THE-ARGUMENT-CANT-BE-FALLACIOUS

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes that if an argument's final conclusion happens to be true, or is
one the student personally agrees with, the argument's REASONING must also be sound, failing to
recognize that a fallacy is specifically about a broken logical connection between premises and
conclusion — a conclusion can be true while the argument offered for it is still fallacious (a
"broken bridge that happens to lead somewhere real, but not because the bridge actually holds").

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student an argument with a true, agreeable conclusion (e.g.,
"Exercise is good for you") supported by a clearly fallacious reasoning path (e.g., an ad hominem
attack on someone who questions a specific exercise claim: "You can't trust anything they say about
exercise, they're out of shape themselves"). Ask: "Is the CONCLUSION (exercise is good) true? Is the
REASONING used to support it (attacking the person's fitness rather than addressing their actual
point) still a broken, fallacious argument, even though the conclusion happens to be true?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming a true conclusion means the argument for it must be sound is like
assuming a lottery ticket that happened to win must have been a smart financial decision — the OUTCOME
being good doesn't mean the REASONING that led to a choice was valid. A fallacious argument can
'accidentally' arrive at a true conclusion through broken reasoning, the same way someone can
accidentally end up in the right place after a wrong turn — the destination being correct doesn't
retroactively make the wrong turn a good navigation decision."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Evaluate an argument's REASONING (does each step actually follow
logically from the last?) as a SEPARATE question from whether you agree with or believe the
conclusion. A true or agreeable conclusion doesn't automatically mean the argument supporting it was
valid — check the reasoning independently."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Exercise is good for you because that person who disagrees is out of shape." (true conclusion,
   but the reasoning is a fallacious ad hominem attack, not actual evidence about exercise) vs.
   "Exercise is good for you because multiple studies show it reduces cardiovascular disease risk."
   (true conclusion supported by valid reasoning).
2. A student who defends a fallacious argument because they happen to agree with its conclusion
   (misconception) vs. a student who can identify the fallacy in an argument while still agreeing
   that its conclusion happens to be true (correct, reasoning-focused evaluation).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to excuse fallacies in arguments with agreeable conclusions
after the bridge, give them a fallacious argument for a conclusion they personally hold, and have them
identify the specific fallacy present, demonstrating that fallacy-detection is independent of
agreement with the conclusion.

### MC-B-EVERY-STRONG-DISAGREEMENT-OR-CRITICISM-OF-A-PERSON-IS-AN-AD-HOMINEM-FALLACY

**Trigger signal:** Student over-applies the ad hominem label to any criticism of a person made
during an argument, even when that criticism is directly and legitimately RELEVANT to evaluating their
claim (e.g., pointing out that a source lacks relevant expertise or has an undisclosed financial
conflict of interest on the specific topic) — failing to distinguish a genuine ad hominem (attacking
irrelevant personal characteristics instead of addressing the argument) from a legitimate credibility
challenge (raising a relevant concern about the source's reliability on this specific claim).

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two criticisms of a person making a claim about a new
medical treatment's safety: one that attacks an irrelevant personal characteristic ("Don't trust them,
they're not even friendly") and one that raises a relevant, legitimate concern ("This person receives
funding directly from the company selling the treatment, which is a relevant conflict of interest for
evaluating their safety claims"). Ask: "Are both of these attacking the person instead of the
argument in the same way? Is the second one actually irrelevant to evaluating the claim, or is it a
legitimate reason for caution?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Not every mention of a person's characteristics while evaluating their claim
is a fallacy — it's like the difference between dismissing a witness's testimony because 'they wear
ugly shoes' (genuinely irrelevant to their reliability) versus noting that 'this witness has a
financial stake in the case's outcome' (a legitimate, RELEVANT reason to weigh their testimony more
cautiously). A true ad hominem attacks something irrelevant to the argument's actual merit; a
legitimate credibility challenge raises something genuinely relevant to trustworthiness on this
specific claim."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before labeling a personal criticism as ad hominem, ask: is this
characteristic actually IRRELEVANT to evaluating the claim (a true ad hominem), or is it a genuinely
relevant concern about expertise, bias, or conflict of interest on THIS SPECIFIC topic (a legitimate
credibility challenge, not a fallacy)?"

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Don't trust them, they're not even friendly." (irrelevant personal characteristic, genuine ad
   hominem) vs. "This person has an undisclosed financial conflict of interest relevant to this
   specific claim." (relevant credibility concern, not a fallacy).
2. A student who labels any mention of a source's potential bias as automatically "ad hominem"
   (over-application) vs. a student who distinguishes a relevant, legitimate bias concern from an
   irrelevant personal attack (correct, nuanced application).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to over-apply the ad hominem label after the bridge, give them
five example criticisms of people making claims and have them sort each into "relevant credibility
concern" and "irrelevant personal attack (genuine ad hominem)," justifying each classification.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.composition.argumentation-basics`):** "Identify the claim, and at least one
supporting point, in this short argument." A student who cannot identify basic argument structure
lacks the foundation this concept builds against — route to `eng.composition.argumentation-basics`
review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Lottery-Ticket-and-Relevant-Witness Anchor.** Combine two images: a winning lottery ticket
(a good outcome that doesn't retroactively make buying it a smart decision) — directly seeding the fix
for MC-A (a true conclusion doesn't validate broken reasoning). Second, two witness criticisms side by
side — one about irrelevant shoes, one about a genuine financial conflict of interest — directly
seeding the fix for MC-B (not every personal mention is an ad hominem; relevance is the deciding
factor).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Ad Hominem: Genuine vs. Legitimate Credibility Concerns (MC-B)

Using the relevant-witness anchor, teach the distinction between genuine ad hominem (irrelevant
personal attack) and legitimate credibility challenges (relevant bias/expertise concerns). Student
practice: given six example criticisms, sort into "genuine ad hominem" and "legitimate credibility
concern."

### TA-2 — Straw Man: Misrepresenting the Opposition

Introduce straw man as a distinct fallacy: misrepresenting an opponent's argument as weaker or more
extreme than it actually is, to make it easier to attack. Student practice: given three pairs of
original arguments and their misrepresented "straw man" versions, identify what specifically was
distorted in each straw man.

### TA-3 — False Dilemma: Artificially Limited Options

Introduce false dilemma as presenting only two options when more genuinely exist. Student practice:
given three false-dilemma statements, identify at least one additional option the argument ignores.

### TA-4 — Reasoning vs. Conclusion: A Fallacy Doesn't Depend on Whether You Agree (MC-A)

Using the lottery-ticket anchor, teach that fallacy identification is independent of agreement with
the conclusion. Student practice: given a fallacious argument for a conclusion the student is likely
to personally hold, identify the specific fallacy present regardless of agreement.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Argument: "We shouldn't listen to the new city council proposal — the person
who wrote it failed math in high school, so of course their plan for the budget is bad. Besides, we
either accept the whole budget as written or the city goes bankrupt — those are the only options."
Fully worked: two fallacies identified — ad hominem (the high school math grade is irrelevant to the
current, professionally-reviewed proposal, unlike a genuine relevant credibility concern) and false
dilemma (presenting only two extreme options when partial amendments, alternative funding sources, or
phased implementation are also genuinely possible). Annotation confirms both fallacies are correctly
distinguished from legitimate criticism (fixing MC-B for the ad hominem) and identified independent of
whether the reader might agree the proposal has real problems (fixing MC-A).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New argument containing a straw man and a genuine, relevant credibility concern
(not a fallacy). Student is prompted with guiding questions ("Does this response accurately represent
the original argument, or does it exaggerate or distort it into an easier target? Is this
mentioned personal characteristic actually irrelevant to the claim, or does it raise a genuinely
relevant concern about bias or expertise?") to correctly identify the straw man and correctly NOT
flag the legitimate concern as a fallacy.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new argument containing at least two fallacies (from ad hominem, straw man,
false dilemma) and at least one legitimate (non-fallacious) criticism, unaided. Student correctly
identifies the fallacies and correctly distinguishes the legitimate criticism from a fallacy.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Distinguishing Ad Hominem from Legitimate Credibility Concerns**
Given four example criticisms of a person making a claim, sort into "genuine ad hominem" and
"legitimate credibility concern," justifying each classification.

**MP-2 [P36] — Identifying Straw Man**
Given an original argument and a distorted "straw man" restatement of it, identify specifically what
was misrepresented.

**MP-3 [P36] — Identifying False Dilemma**
Given a false-dilemma statement, identify at least one additional, genuinely available option the
argument ignores.

**MP-4 [P36] — Fallacy Identification Independent of Agreement**
Given a fallacious argument supporting a conclusion the student is likely to personally agree with,
identify the specific fallacy present.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Fallacy Identification**
Given a new argument containing at least two fallacies and one legitimate (non-fallacious) point,
identify each fallacy by name and correctly distinguish the legitimate point from a fallacy.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite.
During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-B typically surfaces during TA-1 (over-applying the ad

relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1
failure indicates residual MC-B; MP-2/MP-3 failures indicate the straw-man/false-dilemma mechanics

MC-A; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more
guided full-identification example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters fallacy identification on neutral topics (MP-1 through MP-4) but struggles
  specifically when the fallacious argument supports a position the student holds strongly (MP-5),
  this is expected and mirrors the affective challenge seen in `eng.composition.rhetorical-analysis`
  and `eng.composition.counterargument-and-rebuttal` — provide additional practice specifically with
  emotionally invested topics.
- If the student begins over-applying fallacy labels to ANY criticism or disagreement in everyday
  discourse (a common overcorrection once these concepts feel powerful and new), recalibrate by
  returning to the "is this actually irrelevant, or genuinely relevant?" test for ad hominem
  specifically, and by confirming a straw man genuinely distorts the original rather than just
  disagreeing with it.
- If the student can identify fallacies in others' arguments but continues to commit them in their
  own argumentative writing (a transfer gap from analysis to production), have them review a recent
  piece of their own argumentative writing specifically checking for these three fallacy types.
- Students who enjoy this concept's "gotcha" appeal may become overly combative in applying it
  socially — gently reinforce that the goal is more honest, rigorous reasoning (including in one's
  own arguments), not simply winning debates by finding fault in others.
