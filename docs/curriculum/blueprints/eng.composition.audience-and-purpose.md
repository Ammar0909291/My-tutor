**Concept:** Audience and Purpose — analyzing the intended reader and the communicative goal of a
piece of writing, then using that analysis to drive concrete choices (word choice, tone, evidence
type, structure, level of formality) in the writing itself.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Essay Structure (prerequisite) teaches the container —
introduction, body, conclusion. Audience and Purpose teaches the steering wheel: the same structural
container can be filled with radically different content depending on WHO is reading and WHY the
writer is writing. Without this concept, a student can build a technically correct five-paragraph
essay that is completely mismatched to its situation — a persuasive letter to a city council written
like a diary entry, or a lab report written like a pep talk. This concept unlocks
`eng.composition.claim-evidence-reasoning` (what counts as convincing evidence depends on who you're
convincing) and `eng.composition.rhetorical-appeals` (ethos/pathos/logos only make sense once a
student can name a specific audience to appeal to).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can, given a writing prompt or an existing piece of writing,
correctly identify (a) the intended audience — their assumed knowledge, values, and relationship to
the writer, (b) the primary purpose — to inform, persuade, entertain, or express — and (c) name at
least three concrete choices in the text (vocabulary, tone, evidence, structure, or formality) that
were shaped by that audience/purpose analysis, or that should be revised because they mismatch it.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-PURPOSE-IS-THE-TOPIC

**Trigger signal:** Student says the "purpose" of an essay is its topic — e.g., "the purpose of my
essay is dogs" or "my purpose is climate change" — conflating subject matter with communicative
intent.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two essays with the identical topic (school uniforms)
but opposite purposes: one argues FOR a uniform policy (persuade), one explains HOW the policy works
(inform). Ask: "These have the same topic. Are they doing the same job?" The student must notice the
job — what the writer wants to happen in the reader's mind — is different even though the subject is
identical.

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Topic is WHAT you're writing about. Purpose is WHAT YOU WANT THE READING TO
DO — change the reader's mind, teach them something, make them feel something, or share your own
feelings. Two essays can share a topic and have completely different purposes, the way two chefs can
use the same ingredient — chicken — to make soup or to make a sandwich. Same ingredient, different
job."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Topic answers 'what is this about?' Purpose answers 'what is this
FOR?' — to inform, to persuade, to entertain, or to express. Always name both, separately, before
writing."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "The topic is recycling." vs. "The purpose is to persuade the reader to start recycling." — first
   names subject matter, second names the intended effect on the reader.
2. Two essays on the same topic (video games) with different purposes (one informs about game
   design careers, one persuades parents to allow more game time) — same topic, different purpose,
   proven by asking what each essay wants the reader to DO after reading.

**s6_path:** If the student cannot separate topic from purpose after the bridge, use the "same
ingredient, different dish" analogy concretely: hand them a single fact ("video games are popular
among teenagers") and have them write one sentence that informs and one sentence that persuades
using that exact fact, then compare the two sentences side by side.

### MC-B-AUDIENCE-MEANS-WHOEVER-READS-IT

**Trigger signal:** Student treats "audience" as a passive fact ("anyone could read this") rather
than an active design constraint, producing writing with no adjustment for reader knowledge or
values — e.g., using technical jargon in a letter meant for young children, or explaining basic
vocabulary to an expert reader.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present the same explanation of "photosynthesis" written for a
7-year-old and written for a biology professor. Ask the student to identify which is which and WHY —
what specific words or explanations gave it away. The student discovers audience isn't just "who
happens to read it" but a set of assumptions the writer must actively build in — the professor
version assumes chemical formulas are already known; the child version cannot assume that.

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Audience isn't just 'whoever picks this up' — it's a set of choices YOU make
about what your reader already knows, cares about, and needs from you. Think of it like packing a
suitcase for someone else's trip: you pack differently for a toddler's trip to the beach than for a
businessperson's trip to a conference, even if it's the same suitcase. The reader is a person with
specific needs, not a blank slate."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before writing, ask three questions about your audience: What do they
already know about this topic? What do they care about? What is their relationship to me (stranger,
peer, authority figure)? Your answers should visibly change your word choice and what you explain
versus assume."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A note to a close friend ("ngl this movie was mid") vs. a note to a teacher ("I found the film's
   pacing to be somewhat uneven") on the identical opinion — same content, different audience
   relationship driving different register.
2. An instruction manual passage assuming no prior knowledge ("First, locate the power button on the
   right side of the device") vs. one assuming expert knowledge ("Power on and initialize per
   standard boot sequence") — the second fails a beginner audience even though both are
   grammatically fine.

**s6_path:** If the student still writes "one-size-fits-all" prose after the bridge, have them
write the SAME two-sentence explanation of a simple process (e.g., "how to make a paper airplane")
twice — once for a kindergartner, once for an adult who has never seen paper — and then read both
aloud, asking which words changed and why.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.writing.essay-structure`):** "Here is a jumbled short piece of writing — put
its sentences into a working introduction, body, and conclusion order." A student who cannot
reconstruct basic essay structure lacks the container this concept steers — route to
`eng.writing.essay-structure` review before continuing.

**PD-2 (checks basic purpose vocabulary):** "Match each of these four short texts (a diary entry, a
persuasive ad, a recipe, a joke) to its likely purpose: inform, persuade, entertain, or express." A
student who cannot make any correct matches needs the four-purpose vocabulary explicitly pre-taught
(a 5-minute vocabulary primer, not a full prerequisite concept) before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Letter-Rewrite Anchor.** Give the student a single short paragraph of true information (e.g.,
"Our school is getting a new library wing next year, funded by a grant, with more study rooms and a
better computer lab.") Ask them to write this same information as: (1) a text message to a friend,
(2) a formal announcement for the school newsletter, (3) a persuasive flyer trying to get students
excited enough to volunteer for the opening event. Same facts, three audiences, three purposes,
three outputs — laid side by side, the differences in word choice, tone, and length become
physically visible on the page. This concrete, hands-on rewriting exercise directly seeds fixes for
both misconceptions: it forces the student to notice that facts (topic) stay constant while the
*purpose* (inform vs. persuade) and the *audience* (friend vs. newsletter reader vs. potential
volunteer) actively reshape the writing (fixing MC-B), and it makes visible that "what you want the
reader to do" is a separate design decision from "what you're writing about" (fixing MC-A) — because
the underlying facts never change across all three versions even though the purpose clearly does.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Separating Topic from Purpose

Introduce the four communicative purposes (inform, persuade, entertain, express) as answers to "what
do I want this reading to DO to my reader?" — distinct from topic, which answers "what is this
about?" Use the school-uniform same-topic/different-purpose pair from MC-A's conflict evidence.
Student practice: given five short texts on one shared topic, label each with its purpose.

### TA-2 — Profiling an Audience

Teach the three-question audience profile: what do they know, what do they care about, what's our
relationship? Apply it to profile three audiences (a younger sibling, a school principal, a
stranger online) for the same message. Student practice: write a one-sentence audience profile for
each of three given writing prompts.

### TA-3 — How Purpose Shapes Evidence and Tone

Show how the SAME claim needs different supporting evidence depending on purpose: to inform, use
verifiable facts and neutral tone; to persuade, use facts PLUS emotional or ethical appeals and a
more urgent tone; to entertain, use vivid or surprising details and a playful tone; to express, use
personal detail and an intimate tone. Student practice: given one claim, draft one supporting
sentence for each of the four purposes.

### TA-4 — How Audience Shapes Word Choice and Formality

Teach the formality spectrum (casual → neutral → formal) and connect it to audience relationship —
close/informal audiences allow slang and contractions; distant/formal audiences require full
sentences and precise vocabulary. Student practice: rewrite one sentence at three formality levels
for three named audiences.

### TA-5 — Full Audience-Purpose Analysis and Application

Give the student a full writing prompt with no audience/purpose stated. They must (a) choose a
specific audience and purpose, (b) justify the choice in one sentence, and (c) write one paragraph
that visibly reflects at least three concrete choices (vocabulary, tone, evidence type, or
formality) driven by that choice. This is the synthesis task that proves transfer beyond the
scaffolded practice of TA-1 through TA-4.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Prompt: "Write about a school field trip." Fully worked: Audience = parents
who weren't there; Purpose = inform. Model paragraph: "On Tuesday, our class visited the science
museum. Students explored the space exhibit and completed a worksheet on planetary distances. The
trip supported our current unit on the solar system and gave students hands-on experience with
scale models they had only seen in textbooks." Annotation calls out: neutral tone (informing, not
persuading), no slang (audience is adults, not peers), concrete factual details (what a parent needs
to know, not what would entertain a friend).

**WE-2 (Guided):** Same prompt, new audience/purpose pair supplied to the student: Audience =
a close friend; Purpose = entertain. Student is given the opening line ("You will NOT believe what
happened at the science museum...") and prompted with guiding questions ("What tone does 'you will
NOT believe' set? What would a friend want to hear that a parent wouldn't care about?") to complete
the paragraph, with feedback on whether the tone and content stayed consistent with the chosen
audience/purpose throughout.

**WE-3 (Independent):** Same prompt, no audience/purpose supplied. Student must choose their own
audience and purpose, state the choice explicitly, and write the paragraph unaided. Evaluated
against the Component 9 checklist for internal consistency between the stated choice and the
executed writing.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Topic vs. Purpose Identification**
Given three short texts on the identical topic (recess time at school) — one argues for longer
recess, one explains the school's recess schedule, one is a funny anecdote about a recess mishap —
identify the shared topic and the three DIFFERENT purposes.

**MP-2 [P36] — Audience Profiling**
Given a single writing prompt ("Write about why bicycles are useful"), produce a two-sentence
audience profile for a specified reader (a 6-year-old) versus a different specified reader (a city
council member deciding on bike lane funding), naming at least one concrete difference in what each
reader already knows or cares about.

**MP-3 [P36] — Evidence and Tone Matching**
Given the claim "Our school should start 30 minutes later," write one supporting sentence suited to
an informing purpose (neutral, factual) and one supporting sentence suited to a persuading purpose
(uses an emotional or values-based appeal), and label which is which.

**MP-4 [P36] — Formality Rewrite**
Rewrite the sentence "I think we should not have this rule anymore" three times: casually (for a
close friend), neutrally (for a classmate you don't know well), and formally (for a school
principal), and name what changed in each version.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Analysis and Application**
Given an unstated writing prompt ("Write about a change you'd like to see in your community"),
choose and state a specific audience and purpose, then write one paragraph and underline three
places where the writing was visibly shaped by that choice (a word, a piece of evidence, or a
tone shift), explaining each underline in one phrase.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a structural gap routes back to
Essay Structure, a vocabulary gap gets a brief primer rather than a full prerequisite detour. During
Component 4 (S1, active instruction), watch for both misconception trigger signals simultaneously
since they often co-occur — a student conflating topic/purpose (MC-A) frequently ALSO treats
audience passively (MC-B), because both stem from underspecifying "what is this writing supposed to
DO." If either signal fires mid-sequence, branch to that misconception's s6_path drill before
resuming the TA sequence; do not let both remain unaddressed simultaneously going into Component 5.
If a student fails the mastery probe set (S9 evaluation), route based on WHICH probes failed: MP-1/
MP-2 failures indicate the analysis stage (Component 4 TA-1/TA-2) needs review; MP-3/MP-4 failures
indicate the application stage (TA-3/TA-4) needs review; MP-5 failure alone, with MP-1 through MP-4
passing, indicates a transfer gap — route to one additional independent-application example before
re-probing, not a full re-teach.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student is a strong essay-structure performer but freezes when no audience/purpose is
  given (as in WE-3 or MP-5), this indicates transfer difficulty rather than conceptual gap — provide
  one additional worked model at the independent level before re-attempting, not a full concept
  re-teach.
- If the student defaults to the same formal, neutral tone regardless of audience (flattening every
  assignment into "school essay voice"), suspect MC-B is only partially resolved — the audience
  profile step (TA-2) may need to be re-anchored concretely (e.g., "read this paragraph out loud as
  if you were talking to your grandmother, then as if you were texting your best friend").
- If the student over-corrects into inappropriate informality for formal prompts (e.g., using slang
  in a persuasive letter to an official), treat this as evidence the formality spectrum (TA-4) needs
  a second, more explicit anchor rather than assuming audience awareness itself has failed.
- English language learners or students with limited exposure to varied registers may need the
  formality spectrum (TA-4) modeled with more explicit side-by-side examples before attempting
  independent rewrites — extend WE-2 with an additional guided formality-rewrite example before
  moving to WE-3.
