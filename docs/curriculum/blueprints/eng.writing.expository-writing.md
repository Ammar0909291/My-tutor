## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-EXPOSITORY-WRITING-MEANS-STATING-YOUR-OPINION-ABOUT-THE-TOPIC
- **Trigger signal:** Student writes expository content as if it were persuasive/opinion writing, inserting personal opinions, value judgments, or "I think" framing, rather than explaining, informing, or clarifying a topic through objective facts, definitions, and examples — confusing "writing about a topic" with "arguing a position on a topic."
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present "I think photosynthesis is one of the most amazing processes in nature and everyone should appreciate it more." (opinion/persuasive framing) alongside "Photosynthesis is the process by which plants convert sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide into glucose and oxygen." (expository — explains the process objectively using facts and definitions). Ask which one is explaining the topic versus expressing a personal viewpoint about it.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Expository writing's job is to explain, inform, or clarify — not to argue a position or share a personal opinion. Save your opinions and value judgments for persuasive writing. In expository writing, focus on facts, definitions, processes, and examples that help a reader UNDERSTAND the topic, not FEEL a certain way about it."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Before writing a sentence, check: am I explaining/defining/clarifying something factual, or am I expressing an opinion or value judgment? Expository writing should stick to the first — objective explanation — and save opinion for persuasive writing instead."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Expository-as-opinion-sharing (expository writing includes personal opinions and value judgments about the topic) vs. (b) Expository-as-objective-explanation (expository writing explains, informs, or clarifies using facts, definitions, and examples, without personal opinion or persuasive framing).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1 re-check, then re-run TA-1's opinion-vs-fact sort before continuing.

### MC-A-DEFINITION-OR-FACT-ALONE-IS-ENOUGH-TO-EXPLAIN-A-COMPLEX-TOPIC
- **Trigger signal:** Student provides only a bare definition or single fact when explaining a complex topic, without using examples, analogies, or elaboration to actually help the reader understand HOW or WHY something works — mistaking a technically-correct but under-explained statement for genuine clarification.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present "Osmosis is the movement of water across a semi-permeable membrane." (bare definition, technically correct but unclear to someone unfamiliar with the concept) alongside the same definition followed by a concrete example ("For instance, when you soak a wilted celery stalk in water, water moves into the celery's cells through osmosis, making it firm again.") Ask which explanation actually helps a reader who's never heard of osmosis understand it.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "A bare, technically-correct definition often isn't enough to actually help a reader understand a complex idea — it just restates the concept in formal language without connecting it to anything the reader can picture or relate to. Good expository writing follows a definition with a concrete example, analogy, or further elaboration that makes the abstract idea tangible."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "After stating a definition or fact, ask: would someone unfamiliar with this topic actually understand it from this definition alone, or do they need a concrete example, analogy, or further explanation to make it click? Add that elaboration whenever the bare definition would leave a reader confused."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Definition-sufficiency (a correct, formal definition or fact alone adequately explains a complex topic) vs. (b) Definition-plus-elaboration requirement (a definition needs a concrete example, analogy, or further elaboration to genuinely clarify a complex topic for an unfamiliar reader).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1, then re-present the Concrete Anchor's translator-lens framing before continuing to TA-3.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

### PD-1: Transitions-and-Cohesion Readiness
- **Tests:** Can the student already connect explanatory ideas smoothly, especially cause-effect and example-signaling transitions — the connective devices expository writing relies on heavily?
- **Probe:** Ask the student to connect two explanatory sentences with an appropriate transition (e.g., "for example," "as a result").
- **Pass condition:** Selects a reasonably appropriate transition.
- **Fail routing:** If transition selection is weak, route to `eng.writing.transitions-and-cohesion` review — expository writing depends heavily on cause-effect and example transitions specifically.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**"The Expert Translator."** Present the metaphor of a translator whose job is to take complex, technical information and translate it into clear, accessible language for someone unfamiliar with the topic — NOT to add their own opinion about whether the information is good or important (seeding MC-EXPOSITORY-WRITING-MEANS-STATING-YOUR-OPINION-ABOUT-THE-TOPIC's fix: the translator's job is faithful explanation, not persuasion). Give the student a bare technical definition card and have them act as translator: add a concrete example or analogy that would make the concept click for someone hearing it for the first time (seeding MC-A-DEFINITION-OR-FACT-ALONE-IS-ENOUGH-TO-EXPLAIN-A-COMPLEX-TOPIC's fix: bare definitions alone don't count as successful translation). Contrast a "translator" who inserts opinions ("this is such an important concept!") with one who stays purely explanatory but adds a clarifying example.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1: Opinion vs. Fact Sorting
Present 6 sentences, half opinion/value-judgment framing and half objective factual/explanatory content, and have the student sort each, identifying what makes the opinion sentences inappropriate for expository writing. Directly counters MC-EXPOSITORY-WRITING-MEANS-STATING-YOUR-OPINION-ABOUT-THE-TOPIC.

### TA-2: Definitions That Actually Clarify
Using the discrimination pairs from MC-A-DEFINITION-OR-FACT-ALONE-IS-ENOUGH-TO-EXPLAIN-A-COMPLEX-TOPIC, present bare definitions of complex topics and have the student add a concrete example, analogy, or elaboration that makes the definition genuinely clarifying for an unfamiliar reader.

### TA-3: Organizational Patterns for Explanation
Teach common expository organizational patterns (definition, process/sequence, cause-effect, compare-contrast, problem-solution — echoing text structure) and have the student identify the most appropriate pattern for explaining 3 different sample topics.

### TA-4: Using Evidence and Examples Objectively
Student practices supporting an explanatory claim with a specific fact, statistic, or example, keeping the framing objective/explanatory rather than persuasive.

### TA-5: Composing a Full Original Expository Piece
Student writes a complete original expository piece explaining an assigned or chosen topic, using an appropriate organizational pattern, objective explanatory framing, and definitions elaborated with examples — the generative capstone.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

### WE-1: Opinion vs. Fact Identification (Scaffolded)
**Sentences:** "Recycling is honestly one of the best things humans have ever done for the planet." (opinion) / "Recycling reduces the amount of waste sent to landfills by processing materials for reuse." (fact/explanation).
**Walkthrough:** Identify the first as opinion-laden (value judgment "one of the best things") and the second as objective explanation, confirming only the second belongs in pure expository writing.

### WE-2: Definition Plus Elaboration (Guided)
**Bare definition:** "Inflation is a general increase in prices and fall in the purchasing value of money."
**Walkthrough:** Add elaboration: "For example, if a loaf of bread cost $2 last year and costs $2.20 this year with no change in quality, that 10% increase reflects inflation — the same amount of money now buys less."

### WE-3: Full Expository Composition (Independent)
**Topic:** Explain how vaccines work.
**Walkthrough:** Student selects a process/sequence organizational pattern, defines key terms (antigen, immune response) with elaborating examples, and maintains objective, explanatory framing throughout without inserting opinions about vaccination policy.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Reject opinion-laden expository writing**
Given a passage mixing opinion and fact, student identifies the opinion-laden sentences and revises them into objective explanatory statements. *(Directly targets MC-EXPOSITORY-WRITING-MEANS-STATING-YOUR-OPINION-ABOUT-THE-TOPIC.)*

**MP-2 [P36] — Elaborate a bare definition**
Given a bare technical definition, student adds a concrete example or analogy that would clarify it for an unfamiliar reader. *(Directly targets MC-A-DEFINITION-OR-FACT-ALONE-IS-ENOUGH-TO-EXPLAIN-A-COMPLEX-TOPIC.)*

**MP-3 [P36] — Select an appropriate organizational pattern**
Given a topic, student selects and justifies an appropriate expository organizational pattern.

**MP-4 [P36] — Support a claim with objective evidence**
Given an explanatory claim, student supports it with a specific fact or example while maintaining objective framing.

**MP-5 [P36] — Compose a full original expository piece**
Student writes a complete original expository piece on an assigned topic, using appropriate organization, objective framing, and elaborated definitions.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** At session open (S0), confirm PD-1 passes — expository writing relies heavily on cause-effect and example transitions to connect explanatory ideas, so weak transition selection undermines clarity. During instruction (S1), watch for both misconception patterns: inserting opinions (MC-EXPOSITORY-WRITING-MEANS-STATING-YOUR-OPINION-ABOUT-THE-TOPIC) and leaving definitions under-elaborated (MC-A-DEFINITION-OR-FACT-ALONE-IS-ENOUGH-TO-EXPLAIN-A-COMPLEX-TOPIC) both fail to serve expository writing's core explanatory purpose — redirect both to the Expert Translator anchor. If the student inserts opinion framing or leaves a definition bare during TA-1/TA-2 (S6), drop back to the Concrete Anchor's translator demonstration before returning to composition. At mastery-probe stage (S9), do not certify mastery unless MP-1 and MP-2 both pass — a student who selects organizational patterns well (MP-3) but still inserts opinions or leaves definitions bare has not learned the core explanatory discipline this concept requires.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student inserts opinions or value judgments, weight remediation toward MC-EXPOSITORY-WRITING-MEANS-STATING-YOUR-OPINION-ABOUT-THE-TOPIC's bridge text and re-run TA-1's opinion-vs-fact sort before returning to composition.
- If the student leaves definitions bare and under-elaborated, weight remediation toward MC-A-DEFINITION-OR-FACT-ALONE-IS-ENOUGH-TO-EXPLAIN-A-COMPLEX-TOPIC's bridge text and TA-2's elaboration practice specifically.
- This concept feeds two direct downstream unlocks (`eng.writing.the-writing-process`, `eng.writing.essay-structure`) — do not certify mastery without TA-5's full original composition, since essay structure assumes the student can already produce sustained, well-organized objective explanatory content.
- Reuse the "expert translator" framing explicitly when introducing `eng.communication.technical-writing` — that concept extends the same faithful-explanation-without-opinion discipline to specialized technical/professional contexts.
