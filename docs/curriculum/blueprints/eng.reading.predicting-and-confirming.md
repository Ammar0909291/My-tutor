**Concept:** Predicting and Confirming — forming reasonable, text-grounded predictions about
upcoming content and actively revising them as new information is read, rather than treating a
first guess as fixed.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Inference in Reading (prerequisite) taught drawing
conclusions from clues already present in a text. Predicting and Confirming extends this forward in
time: using the same clue-based reasoning to anticipate what comes NEXT, then genuinely updating
that anticipation — rather than defending it — once new text confirms or contradicts it. This
concept has no further downstream unlocks in the current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) form a prediction about upcoming text content that
is grounded in specific clues already present (title, headings, opening details) rather than an
ungrounded guess, and (b) genuinely revise a prediction when new text contradicts it, rather than
reading selectively to preserve the original guess.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-A-PREDICTION-IS-JUST-A-GUESS-AND-DOESNT-NEED-TO-BE-BASED-ON-TEXT-CLUES

**Trigger signal:** Student produces predictions with no grounding in the text's actual available
clues (title, headings, opening sentences, illustrations), treating "predicting" as equivalent to
"making up any guess," rather than reasoning from specific evidence already present to what is likely
to come next.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a text titled "The Drought That Changed the Village"
with an opening paragraph describing cracked, dry farmland. Ask the student for two predictions: one
ungrounded ("the story is about a dragon") and one grounded in the title and opening details ("the
story is probably about how the village deals with water shortage"). Ask: "Which prediction actually
uses clues already in front of you? Which one could be true of almost any story?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Making an ungrounded guess and calling it a 'prediction' is like a weather
forecaster predicting rain by picking a card at random, instead of looking at the clouds and
barometric pressure already visible. A real prediction uses the evidence already available — the
title, the headings, the opening details — to reason toward what's likely to come next, not a guess
disconnected from any of it."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before predicting, look for the actual clues in front of you: the
title, headings, opening sentences, images. Base your prediction specifically on those clues — be
ready to explain which clue led you to your prediction."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "The story is about a dragon." (given a drought-themed title and opening — an ungrounded guess
   disconnected from any available clue) vs. "The story is probably about how the village deals with
   water shortage." (grounded specifically in the title and opening details).
2. A student who predicts without being able to point to any clue that led there (misconception) vs.
   a student who can name the specific clue that grounds their prediction (correct, evidence-based
   prediction).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to guess ungrounded after the bridge, give them a new text's
title and opening paragraph and have them state their prediction alongside the specific clue (a
word, phrase, or detail) that led to it, for three separate predictions in a row.

### MC-B-ONCE-A-PREDICTION-IS-MADE-IT-SHOULD-BE-DEFENDED-RATHER-THAN-REVISED

**Trigger signal:** Student continues to defend or read selectively in favor of an initial
prediction even after new text information directly contradicts it, rather than genuinely revising
the prediction — treating the first guess as something to protect rather than a working hypothesis
to be updated as evidence accumulates.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a prediction made after the first paragraph ("this
character will succeed easily") followed by a later paragraph clearly showing the character failing
repeatedly. Ask: "Does the new information support the original prediction, or contradict it? What
should happen to the original prediction now?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Defending an original prediction against contradicting evidence is like a
detective who, having named one suspect early on, ignores every new clue that points elsewhere just
to avoid admitting the first guess was wrong. A prediction is a working hypothesis, not a commitment
— when new text clearly contradicts it, the strong reader revises the prediction rather than
defending it."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Treat your prediction as a working guess, not a fixed answer. As you
read on, check: does the new information support my prediction, or contradict it? If it contradicts
it, revise your prediction to match what the text is now actually showing you."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A reader who, after evidence of repeated failure, still insists "the character will succeed
   easily" (defending the original guess against contradicting evidence) vs. a reader who revises to
   "the character seems to be struggling more than I first thought — maybe this story is really about
   overcoming failure" (genuine revision based on new evidence).
2. A student who reads selectively, noticing only details that support their first guess
   (misconception) vs. a student who actively checks new information against their prediction and
   updates it when needed (correct confirming discipline).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to defend contradicted predictions after the bridge, give them
a short text where a mid-text detail clearly contradicts their stated prediction, and have them
explicitly state the contradiction and write a revised prediction before continuing to read.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.reading.inference-in-reading`):** "What can you infer from this sentence, and
what specific clue led you to that inference?" A student who cannot draw basic clue-based inferences
lacks the foundation for grounding a prediction in text evidence — route to
`eng.reading.inference-in-reading` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Random-Card Weather-Forecaster and Stubborn-Detective Anchor.** A weather forecaster predicting
rain by picking a random card instead of reading the clouds and pressure already visible — directly
seeding the fix for MC-A (a real prediction is grounded in available clues, not an ungrounded guess).
Second, a detective who ignores every new clue that contradicts their first-named suspect just to
avoid admitting they were wrong — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (a prediction is a working
hypothesis to be revised, not defended against contradicting evidence).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Grounding a Prediction in Available Clues (MC-A)

Using the random-card-forecaster anchor, teach identifying specific text clues (title, headings,
opening details) and reasoning from them to a prediction. Student practice: given a title and
opening paragraph, state a prediction and name the specific clue behind it.

### TA-2 — Treating a Prediction as a Working Hypothesis (MC-B)

Using the stubborn-detective anchor, teach checking new text information against an existing
prediction rather than defending it. Student practice: given a prediction and a contradicting later
detail, state explicitly whether the new information supports or contradicts the prediction.

### TA-3 — Revising a Prediction When Contradicted

Extend TA-2: when contradiction is found, write a genuinely revised prediction reflecting the new
evidence rather than only noting the contradiction. Student practice: given a contradicted
prediction, write a specific revised prediction.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Predict, Read, Confirm or Revise

Give the student a new short text in three parts. After part one, they predict (TA-1); after part
two, they check the prediction against new evidence and revise if needed (TA-2/TA-3); after part
three, they confirm or make a final revision — the full synthesis task across a complete predict-
read-confirm cycle.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A short text about a character preparing for a competition, fully worked: an
initial prediction grounded in the title and opening ("the character will need to overcome a
setback"), a mid-text detail introduced (the character loses an early round), the prediction checked
against this detail and explicitly revised ("the setback is bigger than expected — the story is
probably about recovering from a real failure, not just a minor obstacle"). Annotation confirms both
the clue-grounded prediction (fixing MC-A) and the genuine revision (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new short text in stages. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("What
specific clue is this prediction based on? Does the next part of the text support or contradict your
prediction? If it contradicts it, what should your revised prediction say?") to complete each stage.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new short text in stages, unaided. Student predicts, checks against new
evidence at each stage, and revises when contradicted.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Grounding a Prediction in a Specific Clue**
Given a title and opening paragraph, state a prediction and name the specific clue it is based on.

**MP-2 [P36] — Distinguishing a Supporting Detail from a Contradicting One**
Given a prediction and a new text detail, state whether the detail supports or contradicts the
prediction.

**MP-3 [P36] — Writing a Genuine Revision**
Given a prediction clearly contradicted by new text evidence, write a specific revised prediction
reflecting that evidence.

**MP-4 [P36] — Detecting Selective Reading in Favor of a Guess**
Given a reader's account that ignores a contradicting detail to preserve their original prediction,
identify what the reader is doing wrong and state the correct revision.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Predict-Read-Confirm Cycle**
Given a new short text in stages, predict after the first stage, check the prediction against
subsequent stages, and revise or confirm it as appropriate.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite.
During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1 (ungrounded
guessing) and MC-B during TA-2/TA-3 (defending a contradicted prediction) — these are largely
independent and often need separate, explicit correction. If either fires, branch to the relevant
s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure
indicates residual MC-A; MP-2/MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1
through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-cycle example
before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly names clues on provided titles (MP-1) but cannot generate a grounded
  prediction unaided on a new text (MP-5), provide a clue checklist (title, headings, opening
  details, images) as a scaffolding prompt before free prediction.
- If the student correctly identifies contradictions (MP-2) but their revisions (MP-3) merely
  restate the contradiction without producing a new, updated prediction, model one fully-worded
  revision sentence structure ("I originally thought ___, but now I think ___ because ___") as a
  template.
- If the student struggles to notice contradictions at all, flag the specific contradicting detail
  explicitly before asking them to state whether it supports or contradicts their prediction.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.reading.inference-in-reading`'s clue-based reasoning
  should transfer readily to grounded prediction, since both require reasoning from specific text
  evidence rather than general assumption — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
