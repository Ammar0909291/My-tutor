**Concept:** Writing Research Methodology — describing and justifying the methods used in an original
research project in written form, with enough precision that another researcher could understand
exactly what was done and evaluate whether the methods actually suit the research question.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Advanced Academic Writing (prerequisite) teaches
sophisticated scholarly prose generally. Writing Research Methodology teaches a specific, high-stakes
application: describing HOW a study was conducted with enough precision for replication and
evaluation, and explicitly JUSTIFYING why those specific methods suit the research question — not
merely listing steps taken. This concept unlocks `eng.communication.editing-for-publication`
(preparing research writing for publication builds on first mastering its core methodological
component).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) describe a research method with enough specific
detail (what, how, how many, over what timeframe) that another researcher could understand exactly
what was done, (b) explicitly justify why the chosen method suits the specific research question,
rather than simply describing the method's steps with no rationale, and (c) identify and
transparently acknowledge at least one limitation of the chosen method, rather than presenting the
method as if it were flawless.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-DESCRIBING-A-METHOD-MEANS-JUST-LISTING-THE-STEPS-TAKEN-WITH-NO-JUSTIFICATION

**Trigger signal:** Student writes a methods description as a bare list of steps performed ("We
surveyed 50 students. We analyzed the results using a chi-square test.") with no explanation of WHY
these specific choices (this sample size, this particular statistical test, this particular method
rather than an alternative) suit the research question being investigated.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two methods descriptions for the same study: one that
lists only the steps taken with no rationale ("We surveyed 50 students about their study habits."),
and one that also justifies the choices ("We surveyed 50 students, a sample size sufficient to detect
meaningful differences given the study's exploratory scope, about their study habits, since
self-report is the most direct way to capture the subjective time-allocation decisions this
research question is centrally concerned with."). Ask: "Which version helps a reader understand not
just WHAT was done, but WHY these particular choices were the right ones for this specific question?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "A bare list of method steps with no justification is like a recipe that lists
ingredients and steps but never explains why a particular technique was chosen (why fold, not stir;
why bake at this temperature) — a fellow cook can follow the steps, but has no way to judge whether
the technique actually suits the dish, or to adapt it intelligently if something changes. A methods
section needs to explain not just what was done, but why those specific choices suit this specific
research question."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "For every significant methodological choice you describe (sample size,
method of data collection, type of analysis), add a brief justification: why does THIS choice suit
THIS specific research question, compared to plausible alternatives? A methods section should read as
reasoned choices, not just a procedural checklist."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "We surveyed 50 students about their study habits." (bare procedural description, no
   justification) vs. "We surveyed 50 students about their study habits, using self-report because
   the research question concerns subjective time-allocation decisions best captured directly from
   participants." (procedural description plus explicit justification).
2. A student who lists a statistical test used with no explanation of why it fits the data type or
   research question (unjustified procedure) vs. a student who explains why that specific test is
   appropriate given the specific type of data collected (justified, reasoned method choice).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to write bare procedural lists after the bridge, have them go
through their own draft and add, after every methodological choice, an explicit "...because ___"
clause justifying it relative to the specific research question.

### MC-B-A-GOOD-METHODOLOGY-SECTION-SHOULD-PRESENT-THE-METHOD-AS-FLAWLESS-WITH-NO-ACKNOWLEDGED-LIMITATIONS

**Trigger signal:** Student writes a methodology section that presents the chosen method as if it
were flawless and beyond question, omitting any acknowledgment of the method's real limitations (e.g.,
a small sample size, a self-report method's potential for inaccurate recall, a specific population
that limits how broadly the findings generalize) — believing that acknowledging limitations would
weaken the research's credibility.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two methodology sections for the same study: one that
presents the method with no acknowledged limitations at all, and one that transparently acknowledges a
specific limitation (e.g., "self-reported study time may not perfectly reflect actual time spent, a
limitation inherent to this data collection method") while still defending the method's overall
appropriateness for the research question. Ask: "Which version actually seems more credible and
rigorous to a careful reader — the one that claims perfection, or the one that's honest about a
specific, real limitation while still defending its overall choices?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Presenting a method as flawless is like a mechanic claiming a car repair has
literally zero possible failure points — a careful customer trusts the mechanic MORE, not less, when
they honestly note 'this part should hold for years, but it's the one component we can't fully test
under all conditions,' because it signals genuine expertise and honesty rather than salesmanship.
Acknowledging a real, specific limitation while still defending the method's overall suitability
signals genuine methodological rigor, not weakness."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "After justifying your method, identify at least one genuine limitation
(sample size, self-report accuracy, generalizability to other populations) and state it directly,
rather than presenting the method as flawless. Acknowledging a real limitation, while still defending
the method's overall appropriateness, is a sign of rigor, not weakness."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A methodology section presenting the chosen sample as if it perfectly represents the entire
   population with no caveat (implausible, undermines credibility to a careful reader) vs. one that
   acknowledges the sample's specific limitations (e.g., drawn from one school, not necessarily
   representative of all students) while still defending its adequacy for the study's specific,
   narrower purpose (honest, credible).
2. A student avoiding any mention of limitations out of fear of "weakening" the research
   (misconception) vs. a student transparently naming a real limitation as evidence of careful,
   honest methodological thinking (correct understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to omit limitations after the bridge, have them list every
methodological choice made in their own study and, for each, identify at least one genuine trade-off
or limitation it involves — forcing explicit consideration of limitations as a required step, not an
optional afterthought.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.communication.academic-writing-advanced`):** "Revise this padded paragraph and
add a sentence situating its claim relative to an existing scholarly position." A student who cannot
perform this combined revision-and-situating task lacks the sophistication this concept builds on —
route to `eng.communication.academic-writing-advanced` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Recipe-Technique-and-Honest-Mechanic Anchor.** Combine two images: a recipe that explains WHY a
specific technique (folding, not stirring) is used, not just listing the step — directly seeding the
fix for MC-A (justify method choices, don't just list them). Second, an honest mechanic who notes a
specific, real limitation of a repair while still defending its overall soundness, which builds MORE
trust than a claim of flawless perfection — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (acknowledging genuine
limitations signals rigor, not weakness).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Describing Methods with Precise, Replicable Detail

Teach the level of specificity needed for a method description to be genuinely replicable (exact
numbers, timeframes, procedures — not vague generalities). Student practice: given a vague method
description, revise it to include specific, replicable detail.

### TA-2 — Justifying Method Choices Relative to the Research Question (MC-A)

Using the recipe-technique anchor, teach the "...because..." justification habit for every
significant methodological choice. Student practice: given three bare method descriptions, add an
explicit justification connecting each choice to the specific research question.

### TA-3 — Identifying Genuine Limitations

Teach students to identify real, specific limitations of a given method (sample size, self-report
accuracy, generalizability) rather than vague, generic caveats. Student practice: given three method
descriptions, identify at least one genuine, specific limitation for each.

### TA-4 — Acknowledging Limitations While Defending Overall Appropriateness (MC-B)

Using the honest-mechanic anchor, teach students to state a limitation directly while still defending
the method's overall suitability for the research question — not retracting the method, just being
transparent about its bounds. Student practice: given a method and an identified limitation from
TA-3, write a sentence that acknowledges the limitation while defending the method's continued
appropriateness.

### TA-5 — Full Application: Writing a Complete Methodology Section

Give the student a research question and a chosen method with rough procedural notes. They must
write a full methodology description with precise, replicable detail (TA-1), explicit justification
for the choice (TA-2), and an honest, specific acknowledgment of at least one limitation while
defending the method's overall appropriateness (TA-3, TA-4) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Research question: "Does listening to music while studying affect retention of
new vocabulary?" Fully worked: method described with specific detail (30 participants, randomly
assigned to a music or silent condition, studying 20 new vocabulary words for 15 minutes, tested
immediately after); justification added (random assignment chosen specifically to control for
individual differences in baseline vocabulary retention ability); limitation acknowledged (immediate
testing may not reflect longer-term retention, a genuine limitation of this design, though appropriate
given the study's specific focus on immediate encoding effects). Annotation confirms both the
justification (fixing MC-A) and the honest limitation acknowledgment (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New research question and rough method notes. Student is prompted with guiding
questions ("What specific numbers, timeframes, and procedures need to be included for this to be
replicable? Why is this specific method choice suited to this specific research question, compared to
an alternative? What's one genuine, specific limitation of this method — not a vague generic one?")
to complete a full methodology section.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new research question and method, unaided. Student writes a complete
methodology section with precise detail, explicit justification, and an honest limitation
acknowledgment.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Precise, Replicable Description**
Given a vague method description, revise it to include specific, replicable detail (numbers,
timeframes, exact procedures).

**MP-2 [P36] — Justifying a Method Choice**
Given a bare method description and its research question, write an explicit justification
connecting the method choice to the specific question.

**MP-3 [P36] — Identifying a Genuine Limitation**
Given a method description, identify at least one genuine, specific limitation (not a vague, generic
caveat).

**MP-4 [P36] — Acknowledging a Limitation While Defending the Method**
Given a method and an identified limitation, write a sentence that acknowledges the limitation
directly while still defending the method's overall appropriateness for the research question.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Methodology Section**
Given a new research question and rough method notes, write a complete methodology section with
precise detail, explicit justification, and an honest limitation acknowledgment.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite.
During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1/TA-2 (bare procedural
listing) and MC-B during TA-3/TA-4 (avoiding limitations) — MC-A generally needs resolving first since
a student who hasn't learned to justify choices at all often hasn't developed the analytical habit
needed to identify genuine limitations either. If either fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill
before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure indicates the
precision/replicability step (TA-1) needs review, distinct from either misconception; MP-2 failure
alone indicates residual MC-A; MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1
through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-section example
before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters isolated justification and limitation-identification (MP-2, MP-3) but
  reverts to bare, unjustified, limitation-free writing under the combined demand of MP-5, provide a
  structured checklist (describe precisely, justify with "because," name one real limitation, defend
  overall choice) as scaffolding before removing it.
- If the student's identified "limitations" (MP-3) are actually vague, generic hedges ("no method is
  perfect") rather than specific, real trade-offs of THIS method, push for concrete specificity tied
  to the actual study design.
- If the student overcorrects after MC-B into undermining their own method entirely (treating the
  limitation as disqualifying rather than a bounded, acknowledged trade-off), clarify that the goal is
  honest transparency plus continued defense, not self-sabotage.
- Given this concept's expert-level difficulty, students without prior exposure to any real research
  design (even informally, e.g., a science fair project) may benefit from a concrete, hands-on
  example of an actual small study before attempting the abstract methodology-writing task.
