## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-TOPIC-SENTENCE-JUST-ANNOUNCES-THE-TOPIC-RATHER-THAN-MAKING-A-CLAIM
- **Trigger signal:** Student writes a "topic sentence" that merely announces the general subject ("This paragraph is about dogs." or "I am going to talk about my summer vacation.") rather than making an actual specific claim or controlling idea that the following sentences will develop and support.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present "This paragraph is about dogs." (announces a subject, makes no claim) alongside "Dogs make excellent family pets because of their loyalty and trainability." (states a specific, developable claim). Ask which one tells the reader what the paragraph will actually argue or show, versus which one just names a subject with nothing to prove.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Announcing a topic ('this is about X') doesn't give the reader anything to expect beyond a general subject area — it makes no claim the following sentences can support or develop. A real topic sentence states a specific, arguable or developable idea about that topic, giving the paragraph something to actually prove or show, not just a label."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Instead of naming your subject ('this paragraph is about...'), state a specific claim or idea ABOUT that subject that your supporting sentences will then develop or prove. Ask: does this sentence give the reader something specific to expect, or just a general subject label?"
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Subject-announcement (a topic sentence merely names the general subject area) vs. (b) Specific-claim statement (a topic sentence states a specific, developable idea about the subject that the paragraph's supporting sentences will prove or develop).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1 re-check, then re-run TA-1's claim-vs-announcement sort before continuing.

### MC-A-TOPIC-SENTENCE-MUST-BE-VERY-GENERAL-AND-VAGUE-TO-COVER-EVERYTHING
- **Trigger signal:** Student, trying to ensure the topic sentence "covers" every detail that might appear in the paragraph, writes an overly vague, broad statement that could apply to almost any paragraph on the subject, rather than a specific, focused claim that genuinely previews the particular content of THIS paragraph.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present "Dogs are interesting animals with many qualities." (vague, could introduce nearly any paragraph about dogs, gives no specific preview) alongside "Golden retrievers are especially well-suited to families with young children." (specific, focused, genuinely previews a particular claim the paragraph will support). Ask which sentence actually helps a reader predict what specific content is coming.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "A vague, broad topic sentence doesn't need to cover every possible detail about the general subject — it needs to precisely preview what THIS particular paragraph will say. Being specific and focused is a strength, not a limitation; a focused topic sentence actually helps the reader follow your paragraph better than a vague one that could apply to anything."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't try to make your topic sentence broad enough to cover every possible detail about the subject. Make it specific and focused on exactly what THIS paragraph will develop — a precise, narrow claim previews your paragraph's actual content far better than a vague, generic one."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Maximum-breadth topic sentences (a topic sentence should be vague/general enough to cover any possible content) vs. (b) Precise-focus topic sentences (a topic sentence should be specific and focused, accurately previewing the particular claim this paragraph develops, not hedging broadly).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1, then re-present the Concrete Anchor's sharp-photo framing before continuing to TA-3.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

### PD-1: Paragraph-Structure Readiness
- **Tests:** Can the student already identify a paragraph's controlling idea and recognize that every sentence should support it — the direct foundation topic-sentence writing crafts into a single, well-formed sentence?
- **Probe:** Give a paragraph and ask the student to state its controlling idea in their own words.
- **Pass condition:** Correctly identifies the controlling idea.
- **Fail routing:** If controlling-idea identification fails, route to `eng.writing.paragraph-structure` review — topic-sentence writing is the craft of stating that controlling idea precisely in one sentence.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**"The Camera Focus."** Present two photographs of the same subject (a dog): one blurry and zoomed way out (representing a vague, overly broad topic sentence — "technically" shows the subject but tells the viewer nothing specific) and one sharp, focused close-up showing a specific detail (e.g., the dog mid-catch of a ball — representing a specific, focused claim). Explain: a topic sentence isn't just naming what's in the photo ("this is a picture of a dog" — MC-A-TOPIC-SENTENCE-JUST-ANNOUNCES-THE-TOPIC-RATHER-THAN-MAKING-A-CLAIM's fix, an announcement) — it's also not helped by zooming out to blur everything into vague generality (MC-A-TOPIC-SENTENCE-MUST-BE-VERY-GENERAL-AND-VAGUE-TO-COVER-EVERYTHING's fix) — the best topic sentence is like the sharp, focused photo: specific enough that the viewer immediately knows what particular moment/claim they're looking at.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1: Claim vs. Announcement Sort
Present 6 candidate "topic sentences," half mere subject announcements and half genuine specific claims, and have the student sort each, explaining what's missing from the announcements. Directly counters MC-A-TOPIC-SENTENCE-JUST-ANNOUNCES-THE-TOPIC-RATHER-THAN-MAKING-A-CLAIM.

### TA-2: Specific vs. Vague Claim Sort
Present pairs of topic sentences on the same subject, one vague/broad and one specific/focused, and have the student identify which one actually previews particular content the paragraph will develop. Directly counters MC-A-TOPIC-SENTENCE-MUST-BE-VERY-GENERAL-AND-VAGUE-TO-COVER-EVERYTHING.

### TA-3: Revising a Weak Topic Sentence
Given a weak topic sentence (either an announcement or an overly vague claim), student revises it into a specific, developable claim, then checks it against a set of provided supporting details to confirm the revised sentence genuinely previews them.

### TA-4: Writing an Original Topic Sentence
Given a paragraph's worth of supporting details (without a topic sentence), student writes an original topic sentence that specifically and accurately previews those details — the generative capstone.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

### WE-1: Claim vs. Announcement (Scaffolded)
**Candidates:** "This paragraph will discuss recycling." (announcement) / "Recycling programs significantly reduce a community's landfill waste." (claim).
**Walkthrough:** Confirm the first only names the subject with no specific claim; the second states something specific the paragraph can go on to prove with evidence.

### WE-2: Specific vs. Vague Claim (Guided)
**Candidates:** "Exercise is good for you in many ways." (vague, could introduce almost any exercise paragraph) / "Regular morning exercise improves focus during the school day." (specific, focused).
**Walkthrough:** Confirm the second precisely previews a specific claim (morning exercise → improved focus) while the first is too broad to predict what the paragraph will actually discuss.

### WE-3: Writing an Original Topic Sentence (Independent)
**Supporting details provided:** A paragraph's details describe how community gardens provide fresh produce to low-income families, teach children about where food comes from, and bring neighbors together.
**Walkthrough:** Student writes a specific topic sentence like "Community gardens benefit neighborhoods in several practical ways," then confirms it's specific enough to preview these particular details (not just "gardens are nice") and not a vague announcement ("this paragraph is about community gardens").

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Reject a mere announcement**
Given a "topic sentence" that only announces the subject, student identifies why it fails as a topic sentence and revises it into a genuine claim. *(Directly targets MC-A-TOPIC-SENTENCE-JUST-ANNOUNCES-THE-TOPIC-RATHER-THAN-MAKING-A-CLAIM.)*

**MP-2 [P36] — Reject an overly vague claim**
Given an overly broad, vague topic sentence, student identifies why it fails to preview specific content and revises it into a focused claim. *(Directly targets MC-A-TOPIC-SENTENCE-MUST-BE-VERY-GENERAL-AND-VAGUE-TO-COVER-EVERYTHING.)*

**MP-3 [P36] — Claim-vs-announcement sort**
Given 4 candidate sentences, student correctly sorts each as announcement or genuine claim.

**MP-4 [P36] — Original topic sentence from supporting details**
Given a set of supporting details without a topic sentence, student writes an original, specific topic sentence that accurately previews them.

**MP-5 [P36] — Revise a weak topic sentence with verification**
Given a weak topic sentence and its paragraph's supporting details, student revises the sentence into a specific claim and verifies it against the actual details provided.

```

```

**Protocol routing paragraph:** At session open (S0), confirm PD-1 passes — topic-sentence writing is the craft of stating a paragraph's controlling idea precisely, so a student who cannot identify a controlling idea when reading will struggle to write one when composing. During instruction (S1), watch for both misconception patterns: writing mere subject announcements (MC-A-TOPIC-SENTENCE-JUST-ANNOUNCES-THE-TOPIC-RATHER-THAN-MAKING-A-CLAIM) and writing overly vague, broad claims (MC-A-TOPIC-SENTENCE-MUST-BE-VERY-GENERAL-AND-VAGUE-TO-COVER-EVERYTHING) — both fail to give the reader a specific, developable preview, and both should be redirected to the Camera Focus anchor. If the student produces an announcement or a vague claim during TA-3/TA-4 (S6), drop back to the Concrete Anchor's sharp-vs-blurry photo demonstration before returning to composition. At mastery-probe stage (S9), do not certify mastery unless MP-1 and MP-2 both pass — a student who sorts examples correctly (MP-3) but still writes announcements or vague claims in original composition has not yet internalized the precision this concept requires.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student writes mere subject announcements, weight remediation toward MC-A-TOPIC-SENTENCE-JUST-ANNOUNCES-THE-TOPIC-RATHER-THAN-MAKING-A-CLAIM's bridge text and re-run TA-1's claim-vs-announcement sort before returning to composition.
- If the student writes overly vague, broad claims, weight remediation toward MC-A-TOPIC-SENTENCE-MUST-BE-VERY-GENERAL-AND-VAGUE-TO-COVER-EVERYTHING's bridge text and TA-2's specific-vs-vague sort specifically.
- This concept feeds one direct downstream unlock (`eng.writing.supporting-details`) — do not certify mastery without TA-4's original composition, since supporting-details work assumes the student can already write a specific topic sentence that supporting details genuinely need to develop.
- Reuse the "camera focus" framing explicitly when introducing `eng.writing.supporting-details` — that concept teaches selecting details specific enough to genuinely support the sharp, focused claim established here, rather than vague supporting statements.
