## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-THE-ONLY-DIFFERENCE-BETWEEN-METAPHOR-AND-SIMILE-IS-THE-WORD-LIKE-OR-AS
- **Trigger signal:** Student correctly memorizes the surface rule (simile uses "like"/"as," metaphor doesn't) but treats this as a purely mechanical word-spotting task, missing that the presence/absence of "like"/"as" reflects a genuine difference in how directly the comparison asserts identity — a metaphor claims something IS another thing, while a simile explicitly signals it's a comparison, not identity — which shapes how strongly/directly each device asserts its comparison.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present "Her voice was music." (metaphor — directly asserts identity, no hedge) alongside "Her voice was like music." (simile — explicitly signals a comparison, not identity). Ask the student whether these two sentences make claims of equal directness/strength, or whether the metaphor's lack of "like" actually changes something about how forcefully the comparison lands.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "The word difference isn't just a spelling rule to spot — it reflects a real difference in directness. A metaphor says something IS another thing, asserting the comparison boldly and directly. A simile explicitly signals 'this is LIKE that,' clearly marking it as a comparison rather than identity. That difference in directness genuinely changes the effect, not just the wording."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "When you spot 'like' or 'as,' don't just label it a simile and move on — notice that a simile explicitly marks the comparison as a comparison, which often feels gentler or more explicit than a metaphor's direct, bold claim of identity. The word isn't decoration; it's doing real rhetorical work."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Surface-word-spotting (metaphor/simile classification is just checking for the presence of "like"/"as") vs. (b) Directness-as-meaningful-difference (the presence/absence of "like"/"as" reflects a genuine difference in how directly/boldly the comparison is asserted, affecting the device's rhetorical effect).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1 re-check, then re-run TA-1's directness-comparison practice before continuing.

### MC-ANY-COMPARISON-USING-LIKE-OR-AS-IS-A-SIMILE
- **Trigger signal:** Student flags any sentence containing "like" or "as" as a simile, without checking whether the word is actually functioning to make a figurative comparison between two essentially unlike things, versus being used in its ordinary literal sense (e.g., "as" meaning "because," or "like" meaning "similar to" in a literal, non-figurative comparison of genuinely similar things, such as "her dress was like her sister's dress" — comparing two dresses, not a figurative leap).
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present "She ran like the wind." (simile — figurative comparison between running speed and wind, two genuinely unlike things) alongside "As it was raining, we stayed inside." ("as" meaning "because" — not a comparison at all) and "Her coat was similar to mine, like a matching set." (literal similarity between two actual coats, not a figurative leap). Ask the student to identify which uses of "like"/"as" are actually similes.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "'Like' and 'as' have other everyday jobs beyond signaling similes — 'as' often just means 'because,' and 'like' can describe genuine, literal similarity between similar things rather than a figurative comparison between two essentially DIFFERENT kinds of things. A simile specifically makes a figurative leap between unlike things; not every 'like' or 'as' is doing that job."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Before calling something a simile, check: is 'like' or 'as' actually comparing two ESSENTIALLY DIFFERENT things in a figurative way, or is it doing an ordinary job (meaning 'because,' or describing two genuinely similar/identical things literally)? Only the figurative, unlike-things comparison counts as a simile."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Word-presence-equals-simile (any sentence containing "like" or "as" is a simile) vs. (b) Figurative-function-check (a simile requires "like"/"as" to be functioning as a figurative comparison between essentially unlike things, not its literal/causal/genuinely-similar everyday uses).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1, then re-present the Concrete Anchor's word-job-sorting framing before continuing to TA-3.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

### PD-1: Literary-Devices-Overview Readiness
- **Tests:** Can the student already push past naming a device to explain its specific effect, and apply the removal test — the two analytical habits this concept applies specifically to metaphor and simile?
- **Probe:** Give a passage with an unnamed figurative device and ask the student to name it and explain its effect.
- **Pass condition:** Produces both a correct name and a reasonable effect explanation.
- **Fail routing:** If either skill is weak, route to `eng.literature.literary-devices-overview` review — this concept applies that general analytical discipline to two specific devices.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**"The Word-Job Sorting Table and the Bold-Claim vs. Soft-Signal Dial."** Present a sorting table with 3 bins labeled "Figurative Comparison" (simile), "Means 'Because'" (causal "as"), and "Literal Similarity" (genuinely similar things, non-figurative) — have the student sort 6 "like"/"as" sentences into their correct bin, directly seeding MC-ANY-COMPARISON-USING-LIKE-OR-AS-IS-A-SIMILE's fix. Separately, present a dial with two settings — "BOLD CLAIM" (metaphor: "her voice WAS music") and "SOFT SIGNAL" (simile: "her voice was LIKE music") — and physically turn the dial between the two versions of the same comparison, having the student describe how the directness/boldness changes, seeding MC-THE-ONLY-DIFFERENCE-BETWEEN-METAPHOR-AND-SIMILE-IS-THE-WORD-LIKE-OR-AS's fix.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1: Directness Comparison Practice
Present pairs of the same comparison as both metaphor and simile (as in the Conflict Evidence example) and have the student describe the difference in directness/boldness each version creates, beyond just correctly labeling them. Directly counters MC-THE-ONLY-DIFFERENCE-BETWEEN-METAPHOR-AND-SIMILE-IS-THE-WORD-LIKE-OR-AS.

### TA-2: The Figurative-Function Check for "Like"/"As"
Using the discrimination pairs from MC-ANY-COMPARISON-USING-LIKE-OR-AS-IS-A-SIMILE, present 6 sentences using "like"/"as" in different functions (simile, causal "as," literal similarity) and have the student sort them using the word-job table, confirming only the figurative-unlike-things comparisons are genuine similes.

### TA-3: Explaining Metaphor/Simile Effect
Given a metaphor or simile in a passage, student explains the specific effect it creates (what quality is being transferred/emphasized from the comparison) rather than just naming the device.

### TA-4: Identifying and Explaining Devices in a Novel Passage
Student analyzes a new passage containing both a metaphor and a simile (or a "like"/"as" false-positive to reject), correctly identifying each, explaining directness differences, and confirming genuine figurative function — the generative capstone.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

### WE-1: Directness Comparison (Scaffolded)
**Metaphor:** "The city was a jungle." **Simile:** "The city was like a jungle."
**Walkthrough:** Both compare the city to a jungle (implying chaos, danger, density), but the metaphor asserts this boldly and directly as if it were literally true, while the simile explicitly signals a comparison, feeling slightly more measured/explicit about the figurative nature.

### WE-2: Figurative-Function Sorting (Guided)
**Sentences:** "He fought like a lion." (simile — figurative) / "As the sun set, they packed up." ("as" = "when/because," not a comparison) / "Her shoes were like her mother's — same brand, same color." (literal similarity, not figurative).
**Walkthrough:** Sort each using the word-job table, confirming only the first is a genuine simile.

### WE-3: Full Passage Analysis (Independent)
**Passage:** "Her thoughts were a swarm of bees, buzzing and impossible to settle. She felt as restless as a child waiting for a storm to pass."
**Walkthrough:** Student identifies "thoughts were a swarm of bees" as a metaphor (bold, direct identity claim emphasizing chaotic, buzzing mental activity) and "restless as a child waiting for a storm" as a simile (explicit comparison emphasizing anticipatory unease), explaining both effects.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Describe directness difference**
Given the same comparison as both metaphor and simile, student describes how directness/boldness differs between the two versions, not just labeling them. *(Directly targets MC-THE-ONLY-DIFFERENCE-BETWEEN-METAPHOR-AND-SIMILE-IS-THE-WORD-LIKE-OR-AS.)*

**MP-2 [P36] — Reject a non-figurative "like"/"as"**
Given a sentence using "like" or "as" in a non-figurative function (causal or literal similarity), student correctly identifies it as NOT a simile. *(Directly targets MC-ANY-COMPARISON-USING-LIKE-OR-AS-IS-A-SIMILE.)*

**MP-3 [P36] — Explain a metaphor's specific effect**
Given a metaphor, student explains the specific quality being emphasized through the comparison.

**MP-4 [P36] — Full passage identification and explanation**
Given a novel passage with a metaphor and simile (and possibly a false-positive "like"/"as"), student correctly identifies each, explains their effects, and rejects any non-figurative uses.

**MP-5 [P36] — Convert between metaphor and simile forms**
Given one figurative comparison stated as a metaphor, student rewrites it as a simile (and vice versa), explaining how the directness/boldness shifts between the two forms.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** At session open (S0), confirm PD-1 passes — this concept applies the general name-then-explain and removal-test disciplines specifically to metaphor and simile, so weak general device analysis undermines the specific application. During instruction (S1), watch for both misconception patterns: treating the word difference as merely mechanical (MC-THE-ONLY-DIFFERENCE-BETWEEN-METAPHOR-AND-SIMILE-IS-THE-WORD-LIKE-OR-AS) and over-flagging any "like"/"as" as a simile (MC-ANY-COMPARISON-USING-LIKE-OR-AS-IS-A-SIMILE) both under-analyze the actual function of the comparison — redirect both to the Word-Job Table/Bold-Claim Dial anchor. ## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student treats metaphor/simile distinction as purely mechanical word-spotting, weight remediation toward MC-THE-ONLY-DIFFERENCE-BETWEEN-METAPHOR-AND-SIMILE-IS-THE-WORD-LIKE-OR-AS's bridge text and re-run TA-1's directness-comparison practice before returning to independent analysis.
- If the student over-flags non-figurative "like"/"as" uses as similes, weight remediation toward MC-ANY-COMPARISON-USING-LIKE-OR-AS-IS-A-SIMILE's bridge text and TA-2's word-job sorting practice specifically.
- This concept feeds two direct downstream unlocks (`eng.literature.symbolism`, `eng.literature.imagery`) — do not certify mastery without TA-4's full passage analysis, since both downstream concepts assume the student can already distinguish genuine figurative comparison from literal or coincidental language use.
- Reuse the "word-job sorting table" framing explicitly when introducing `eng.literature.symbolism` — that concept requires the same figurative-vs-literal function check applied to recurring images/objects rather than single comparisons.
