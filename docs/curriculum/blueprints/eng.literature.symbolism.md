## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-ANY-OBJECT-MENTIONED-REPEATEDLY-IN-A-STORY-IS-AUTOMATICALLY-A-SYMBOL
- **Trigger signal:** Student flags any object that appears multiple times in a story as automatically symbolic, without checking whether the story's context actually invests that object with representative meaning beyond its literal function — confusing mere repetition with genuine symbolic significance, and missing that some repeated objects are just functional/practical (e.g., a character's car mentioned repeatedly just because they drive it often) rather than standing for something larger.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present a story where a character's car is mentioned 5 times purely as a practical means of transportation with no thematic weight, alongside a story where a wilting plant is mentioned repeatedly and its condition consistently tracks a character's declining emotional state. Ask the student whether repetition ALONE makes something symbolic, or whether the wilting plant example does additional work the car example doesn't.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Repetition alone doesn't make something a symbol — a car mentioned often just because a character drives it a lot is still just a car, functionally present. A genuine symbol requires the story to invest that object with meaning BEYOND its literal function, often by having it track, echo, or foreshadow something thematic (like the wilting plant mirroring a character's emotional decline)."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Before calling a repeated object symbolic, ask: does the story actually connect this object's changes or qualities to a larger idea, theme, or character state, or is it just present because it's practically necessary to the plot? Only the former is genuine symbolism."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Repetition-equals-symbolism (any object mentioned multiple times is automatically symbolic) vs. (b) Investment-of-meaning-check (genuine symbolism requires the story to connect the object to a larger idea/theme beyond its literal, practical function).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1 re-check, then re-run TA-1's repetition-vs-investment sort before continuing.

### MC-A-SYMBOL-HAS-ONE-FIXED-UNIVERSAL-MEANING-ACROSS-ALL-STORIES
- **Trigger signal:** Student assumes common symbols (a rose = love, a dove = peace, darkness = evil) carry one fixed, universal meaning that transfers automatically from story to story, rather than checking what a specific object actually represents WITHIN the particular story's own context — missing that the same object can symbolize different things in different works, and a story can even deliberately subvert a symbol's conventional association.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present a story where darkness is associated with comfort and safety (a character finding peace in nighttime solitude) rather than the conventional "darkness = evil/danger" association. Ask the student whether this story is "using the symbol wrong," or whether the story has established its own specific meaning for darkness within its own context.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Common symbolic associations (darkness = danger, light = hope) are cultural defaults, not fixed universal rules every story must follow. A skilled author can use a conventional symbol in its expected way, or deliberately establish a different, even opposite meaning within their own story's specific context. Always check what THIS story actually does with the symbol, rather than assuming the conventional association automatically applies."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't assume a symbol's conventional cultural meaning automatically applies. Trace what the SPECIFIC story does with the object — how it's described, what it's associated with, what changes alongside it — to determine what it represents in THIS particular work, even if that differs from the common cultural association."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Fixed-universal-meaning (common symbols carry one automatic, transferable meaning across all stories) vs. (b) Context-specific-meaning (a symbol's actual meaning must be traced within each story's own specific context, which may follow or deliberately subvert conventional associations).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1, then re-present the Concrete Anchor's costume-context framing before continuing to TA-3.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

### PD-1: Metaphor-and-Simile Readiness
- **Tests:** Can the student already distinguish genuine figurative function from coincidental or literal language use — the exact discrimination symbolism analysis extends to recurring objects/images across an entire work?
- **Probe:** Given a metaphor and a coincidental word-overlap, ask the student to distinguish which is genuinely figurative.
- **Pass condition:** Correctly distinguishes both.
- **Fail routing:** If figurative-function discrimination is weak, route to `eng.literature.metaphor-and-simile` review — symbolism requires this same discrimination applied across a whole work.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**"The Investment Meter and the Costume Change."** Present a physical "investment meter" (a dial from "just present/functional" to "thematically invested") and have the student test a repeated object by asking whether the story connects its changes to a larger idea — if yes, the meter reads high (genuine symbol); if the object is just practically necessary with no thematic tracking, the meter stays low, directly seeding MC-ANY-OBJECT-MENTIONED-REPEATEDLY-IN-A-STORY-IS-AUTOMATICALLY-A-SYMBOL's fix. Separately, present the same prop (e.g., a rose) worn in 3 different "costumes" by 3 different stories — one using it conventionally (love), one using it to represent something else specific to that story's context (a fading memory), and one deliberately subverting it (danger/thorns emphasized over romance) — demonstrating the SAME object can be costumed differently by different stories, seeding MC-A-SYMBOL-HAS-ONE-FIXED-UNIVERSAL-MEANING-ACROSS-ALL-STORIES's fix.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1: Repetition vs. Investment Sorting
Present 2 story excerpts, one where a repeated object is purely functional and one where it's genuinely thematically invested (as in the Conflict Evidence example), and have the student apply the investment-meter test to distinguish them. Directly counters MC-ANY-OBJECT-MENTIONED-REPEATEDLY-IN-A-STORY-IS-AUTOMATICALLY-A-SYMBOL.

### TA-2: Tracing a Symbol's Meaning Within Its Story Context
Given a story with a genuine symbol, student traces its specific meaning by examining how it's described, what it's associated with, and what changes alongside it — building the meaning from the story's own evidence rather than assuming a conventional association.

### TA-3: Conventional vs. Subverted Symbolic Associations
Using the discrimination pairs from MC-A-SYMBOL-HAS-ONE-FIXED-UNIVERSAL-MEANING-ACROSS-ALL-STORIES, present a story using a common symbol in a subverted or non-conventional way and have the student trace the story's OWN specific meaning rather than assuming the cultural default.

### TA-4: Connecting Symbol to Theme
Given a story's already-identified theme, student explains how a specific symbol reinforces or develops that theme through its recurring presence and changes.

### TA-5: Full Symbolism Analysis of a Novel Story
Student analyzes a new story: identifies a genuinely invested symbolic object (rejecting merely-repeated functional objects), traces its story-specific meaning, and connects it to the story's theme — the generative capstone.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

### WE-1: Investment Test Application (Scaffolded)
**Excerpt A:** A character's umbrella is mentioned each time it rains, purely as practical gear. **Excerpt B:** A character's grandmother's clock is mentioned repeatedly, and its slowing, inconsistent ticking mirrors the grandmother's declining health throughout the story.
**Walkthrough:** Apply the investment meter: Excerpt A's umbrella stays low (functional only); Excerpt B's clock reads high (its condition tracks a larger theme of decline), confirming only the clock is a genuine symbol.

### WE-2: Tracing Story-Specific Meaning (Guided)
**Story:** A caged bird appears throughout a story about a character feeling trapped in an unwanted arranged marriage; by the story's end, the cage door is left open but the bird doesn't fly out.
**Walkthrough:** Trace the bird's specific meaning within this story: representing the character's own constrained freedom, and the ending detail (door open, bird stays) suggesting internalized constraint even when external freedom becomes possible — a meaning built from this story's own evidence.

### WE-3: Subverted Symbol Analysis (Independent)
**Story:** A story where "sunshine" — conventionally associated with happiness — is consistently associated with the protagonist's anxiety and exhaustion (harsh, unrelenting, exposing), while "rain" brings relief and comfort.
**Walkthrough:** Student identifies that this story subverts the conventional sunshine=happy/rain=sad associations, tracing the story's own specific meanings from its actual descriptive evidence rather than assuming the cultural default.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Reject a merely-repeated, non-invested object**
Given a repeated but purely functional object, student correctly identifies it as NOT symbolic, using the investment test. *(Directly targets MC-ANY-OBJECT-MENTIONED-REPEATEDLY-IN-A-STORY-IS-AUTOMATICALLY-A-SYMBOL.)*

**MP-2 [P36] — Trace a subverted symbol's story-specific meaning**
Given a story using a common symbol in a subverted way, student traces the story's own specific meaning rather than assuming the conventional cultural association. *(Directly targets MC-A-SYMBOL-HAS-ONE-FIXED-UNIVERSAL-MEANING-ACROSS-ALL-STORIES.)*

**MP-3 [P36] — Apply the investment test**
Given a new story excerpt, student applies the investment test to determine whether a repeated object is genuinely symbolic.

**MP-4 [P36] — Connect a symbol to theme**
Given a story's theme and a genuine symbol, student explains how the symbol reinforces that theme.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full novel-story symbolism analysis**
Given a new story, student identifies a genuine symbol, traces its story-specific meaning, and connects it to the theme.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** At session open (S0), confirm PD-1 passes — symbolism analysis extends figurative-function discrimination across an entire work, so weak figurative-vs-literal discrimination undermines the whole-work application. During instruction (S1), watch for both misconception patterns: treating repetition as sufficient for symbolism (MC-ANY-OBJECT-MENTIONED-REPEATEDLY-IN-A-STORY-IS-AUTOMATICALLY-A-SYMBOL) and assuming fixed universal symbol meanings (MC-A-SYMBOL-HAS-ONE-FIXED-UNIVERSAL-MEANING-ACROSS-ALL-STORIES) both bypass the genuine analytical work of tracing a symbol's specific, story-grounded significance — redirect both to the Investment Meter/Costume Change anchor. If the student flags a merely-functional object as symbolic or assumes a conventional meaning during TA-1/TA-3 (S6), drop back to the Concrete Anchor's demonstrations before returning to independent analysis. At mastery-probe stage (S9), do not certify mastery unless MP-1 and MP-2 both pass — a student who applies the investment test in straightforward cases (MP-3) but still assumes universal meanings for subverted symbols has not learned the core context-tracing judgment this concept requires.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student flags merely-repeated functional objects as symbolic, weight remediation toward MC-ANY-OBJECT-MENTIONED-REPEATEDLY-IN-A-STORY-IS-AUTOMATICALLY-A-SYMBOL's bridge text and re-run TA-1's investment-test sort before returning to independent analysis.
- If the student assumes conventional/universal symbol meanings without checking story context, weight remediation toward MC-A-SYMBOL-HAS-ONE-FIXED-UNIVERSAL-MEANING-ACROSS-ALL-STORIES's bridge text and TA-3's subverted-symbol practice specifically.
- This concept feeds one direct downstream unlock (`eng.literature.short-story-study`) — do not certify mastery without TA-5's full symbolism analysis, since short-story study assumes the student can already trace symbolic meaning independently within a work's own specific context.
- Reuse the "investment meter, not repetition-counting" framing explicitly when introducing `eng.literature.short-story-study` — that concept applies the same rigorous, story-grounded analytical discipline across a story's full range of literary elements.
