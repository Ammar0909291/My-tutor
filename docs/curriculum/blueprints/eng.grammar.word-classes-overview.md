## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-WORD-CLASS-IS-FIXED-PER-WORD
- **Trigger signal:** Student assumes a word belongs permanently to exactly one word class regardless of how it's used in a sentence (e.g., insists "run" is always a verb, becoming confused when it functions as a noun in "I went for a run"), not recognizing that many English words can shift word class depending on their function in a specific sentence.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said 'run' is always a verb. Look at this sentence: 'I went for a run this morning.' Is 'run' doing a verb's job here (an action) or a noun's job (a thing)?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Word class is determined by how a word FUNCTIONS in a specific sentence, not by some fixed label attached to the word forever. Many English words (run, light, fast, cut, book) can serve as different word classes depending on their role in context — 'run' is a verb in 'I run every day' but a noun in 'I went for a run.'"
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Before labeling a word's class, check what JOB it's doing in THIS specific sentence — don't assume a fixed class based on the word alone."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "run" as a verb ("I run every day" — an action) vs. "run" as a noun ("I went for a run" — a thing/activity being referred to).
  - "light" as an adjective ("a light backpack") vs. "light" as a noun ("turn on the light") vs. "light" as a verb ("light the candle").
- **s6_path:** "It's a very reasonable assumption that each word has one fixed job, since many words genuinely do stay in one class most of the time — but recognizing that some common words shift class by context is an important, learnable pattern in English."

### MC-WORD-CLASS-DETERMINED-BY-MEANING-ALONE
- **Trigger signal:** Student tries to classify a word purely by its meaning/topic (e.g., "it's about an action, so it must be a verb") rather than by its grammatical FUNCTION and position in the sentence, leading to misclassification of words whose meaning suggests one class but whose grammatical role indicates another (e.g., classifying "the arrival" as a verb because "arrive" feels action-like, when "arrival" is functioning as a noun — the subject of the sentence, taking a determiner "the").
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said 'arrival' must be a verb because it's about an action. But look at the sentence: 'The arrival surprised everyone.' Does 'arrival' have 'the' in front of it, the way nouns often do? Is it DOING the surprising, or is it the THING that surprised everyone?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Word class is determined by grammatical FUNCTION and POSITION in a sentence (does it take a determiner like 'the'? does it show action or being? does it modify another word?) — not by whether the underlying concept feels action-like or thing-like. 'Arrival' names an event but functions grammatically as a noun, since it takes 'the' and acts as the sentence's subject."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Classify a word by checking its grammatical signals (does it take 'the/a'? does it show tense? does it describe a noun?) and its position/role in the sentence, not by whether its meaning feels like an action or a thing."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "arrival" (noun — takes "the," acts as subject) vs. "arrive" (verb — shows tense: "arrived," "arrives").
  - "quickly" (adverb — modifies the verb "ran" in "she ran quickly") vs. "quick" (adjective — modifies the noun "runner" in "a quick runner") — same core meaning, different grammatical signal and function.
- **s6_path:** "It's natural to classify words by what they seem to be 'about' — but grammatical signals (determiners, tense markers, position) are the more reliable test, and checking those directly is a skill worth building deliberately."

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 [P41] — Word-recognition readiness**
Prompt: "Read this sentence and tell me the meaning of each word: 'The dog ran quickly.'"
- Pass: correctly recognizes and understands each word's meaning, not just decodes it (per `eng.vocab.word-recognition`).
- Fail → [P52]: brief word-recognition refresher before proceeding. Persistent failure → route to eng.vocab.word-recognition for reteaching.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The word-class sorting stations**

> Set up eight labeled "stations" (noun, verb, adjective, adverb, pronoun, preposition, conjunction, interjection), each with a simple icon/example (noun: a picture of a thing; verb: a picture of an action; etc.). Take one simple sentence, cut it into individual word cards, and physically sort each word card to its matching station, discussing WHY each word belongs there (its job in the sentence). Repeat with a second sentence reusing one word (e.g., "run") in a different class, to introduce the idea that a word's station can change based on its job.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Naming Words vs. Action Words (Nouns and Verbs) [C]**

Present simple sentences; student identifies the noun(s) (naming a person/place/thing/idea) and verb(s) (showing action or being) in each, the two most foundational classes.

**TA-2 — Describing Words (Adjectives and Adverbs) [C]**

Present sentences; student identifies adjectives (describing nouns) and adverbs (describing verbs/adjectives/other adverbs), practicing the function-based distinction between the two.

**TA-3 — Connecting and Substituting Words (Pronouns, Prepositions, Conjunctions) [C→P]**

Present sentences; student identifies pronouns (standing in for nouns), prepositions (showing relationships, often of location/time), and conjunctions (joining words/clauses).

**TA-4 — Context-Dependent Classification (Same Word, Different Class) [P]**

Present sentence pairs using the SAME word in two different word classes (run/run, light/light, book/book); student classifies each instance by its function in that specific sentence, directly targeting MC-WORD-CLASS-IS-FIXED-PER-WORD.

**TA-5 — Grammatical Signal Detection (Function Over Meaning) [P]**

Present tricky cases where meaning might mislead (arrival/arrive, quickly/quick, a noun derived from a verb-feeling concept); student uses grammatical signals (determiners, tense markers, modification targets) rather than surface meaning to classify correctly, directly targeting MC-WORD-CLASS-DETERMINED-BY-MEANING-ALONE.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — noun/verb identification)**

> "In 'The cat jumped,' which word is the noun, and which is the verb?"

Target response: "cat" is the noun (a naming word); "jumped" is the verb (an action word).

**WE-2 (Foundational — adjective/adverb identification)**

> "In 'The happy dog ran quickly,' find the adjective and the adverb."

Target response: "happy" is the adjective (describes "dog"); "quickly" is the adverb (describes "ran").

**WE-3 (Intermediate — context-dependent classification)**

> "In 'I like to read books' and 'I went for a swim,' is 'swim' functioning the same way as 'read'?"

Target response: "read" is a verb (an action, "to read"); "swim" here is a noun (a thing — the activity being referred to, taking "a" before it) — correctly identifying that grammatical function, not just meaning, determines the class.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Noun/verb identification, novel sentence**
"In 'The bird sang,' which word is the noun and which is the verb?"
*Target:* "bird" (noun); "sang" (verb).

**MP-2 [P36] — Adjective/adverb identification, novel sentence**
"In 'The tall man spoke softly,' find the adjective and the adverb."
*Target:* "tall" (adjective, describes "man"); "softly" (adverb, describes "spoke").

**MP-3 [P36] — Pronoun/preposition/conjunction identification**
"In 'She put the book on the table, and he smiled,' find a pronoun, a preposition, and a conjunction."
*Target:* "she"/"he" (pronouns); "on" (preposition); "and" (conjunction).

**MP-4 [P36] — Context-dependent classification, novel example**
"In 'I light the candle' and 'The room needs more light,' is 'light' the same word class both times?"
*Target:* correctly identifies "light" as a verb in the first (an action) and a noun in the second (a thing) — different classes based on function.

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain word classes**
"In your own words: how do you figure out what word class a word belongs to, and can the same word ever belong to different classes?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that word class depends on a word's grammatical FUNCTION in a specific sentence (not fixed meaning alone), and that many words can shift class depending on how they're used.

**Protocol routing:** S0 (secure on word recognition, new to grammatical classification): dwell on TA-1/TA-2's most intuitive classes (noun/verb/adjective/adverb) before introducing the more abstract connecting-word classes (pronoun/preposition/conjunction). S1 (already has informal grammar sense from reading/speaking but lacks formal vocabulary): use this concept to build explicit, transferable labels for already-present intuitions, moving relatively quickly through TA-1-3. S6 (overwhelmed by eight categories introduced at once): explicitly sequence and chunk by function-type groups (naming/action, describing, connecting) rather than presenting all eight simultaneously, per the TA sequence. S9 (L1 grammar system organizes word classes very differently, e.g. no article system affecting noun-signal detection, or a different core parts-of-speech framework): expect this concept to require careful, explicit contrastive teaching rather than assumed transfer — particularly for TA-5's grammatical-signal detection, which often relies on English-specific signals (articles, do-support, etc.) that may not have a direct L1 parallel.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Function over meaning is the central lesson**: every classification task should train the habit of checking grammatical signals and sentence role, actively countering the intuitive but unreliable strategy of classifying by surface meaning/topic.
- **Class-shifting is common and expected, not an exception**: many everyday English words (run, light, book, cut, fast) shift class by context — treat this as a standard feature to expect, not a rare edge case.
- **Chunked introduction by function-group**: introduce the eight word classes in three intuitive groups (naming/action; describing; connecting/substituting) rather than all at once, to manage cognitive load.
- **English-specific grammatical signals may not transfer from L1**: for S9 learners, explicitly teach the specific English signals (determiners like "the/a," tense marking, adverb endings like "-ly") as concrete, learnable tests, rather than assuming intuitive transfer from the learner's L1 grammatical system.
