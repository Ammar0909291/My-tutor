**Concept:** Introduction to Discourse Analysis — the study of how meaning and coherence are
constructed across stretches of connected text or talk BEYOND the single sentence: how conversations,
paragraphs, and extended texts hang together as unified wholes rather than isolated, disconnected
utterances.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Pragmatics (prerequisite) taught how context shapes the
meaning of a SINGLE utterance. Discourse Analysis extends this to MULTIPLE connected utterances or
sentences, studying how coherence is built ACROSS a conversation or text — through cohesive devices
(pronouns referring back to earlier content, conjunctions signaling logical relationships) and through
conversational structure (turn-taking, topic management). This concept unlocks
`eng.linguistics.corpus-linguistics-intro` (analyzing large collections of real language use draws on
discourse-level analysis skills) and `eng.communication.discourse-markers-advanced` (a more
specialized, advanced study of the specific words and phrases that signal discourse structure).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) identify cohesive devices (pronoun reference,
conjunctions, lexical repetition) that link sentences together into a coherent text, (b) explain that
COHERENCE (a text making overall logical sense) is a separate property from mere COHESION (surface-
level linking devices being present) — a text can have cohesive devices and still fail to cohere
logically, or cohere logically with relatively few explicit cohesive devices, and (c) analyze basic
conversational structure, such as how speakers manage turn-taking and topic shifts in a dialogue.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-A-TEXT-WITH-LOTS-OF-COHESIVE-DEVICES-LIKE-PRONOUNS-AND-CONJUNCTIONS-IS-AUTOMATICALLY-COHERENT

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes that a text or conversation full of surface-level cohesive
devices (pronouns, conjunctions, transition words) must automatically make logical sense, failing to
recognize that COHESION (linking devices) and COHERENCE (actual logical sense) are separate
properties — a text can be dense with pronouns and conjunctions while still failing to cohere
logically (e.g., pronouns with unclear or shifting referents, or conjunctions connecting ideas that
don't actually follow from each other).

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a short passage packed with cohesive devices (multiple
pronouns, "therefore," "however," "in addition") that nonetheless fails to make logical sense (the
pronouns' referents are unclear or the logical connections signaled by "therefore" don't actually
hold), alongside a shorter passage with fewer explicit cohesive devices that nonetheless makes clear,
coherent sense. Ask: "Does having more linking words automatically mean the passage makes more sense?
Which passage is actually easier to follow?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming lots of cohesive devices guarantee coherence is like assuming a
bridge with many visible bolts and connectors must automatically be structurally sound — but if those
bolts don't actually connect to anything solid, or connect the wrong pieces together, the bridge can
still collapse despite looking well-fastened. Cohesion (the visible linking devices) is necessary but
not sufficient for coherence (the text actually making sense) — you need to check both separately."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't judge a text's coherence just by counting how many pronouns,
conjunctions, or transition words it uses. Ask separately: do the pronouns clearly point back to
something specific? Do the logical connections signaled by words like 'therefore' or 'however'
actually hold up? Cohesion and coherence are different questions."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A passage dense with pronouns and conjunctions where the pronoun referents are unclear and the
   logical connections don't actually follow (high cohesion, low coherence) vs. a clearer, shorter
   passage with fewer explicit devices but a genuinely logical, easy-to-follow structure (adequate
   cohesion, high coherence).
2. A student who assumes more transition words automatically improve a text's clarity (misconception)
   vs. a student who checks whether the transitions actually signal a genuine, valid logical
   relationship between the connected ideas (correct, coherence-focused understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to conflate cohesion with coherence after the bridge, have them
take a densely cohesive but incoherent passage and trace every pronoun back to its intended referent,
identifying where the reference breaks down or becomes ambiguous — making the cohesion/coherence gap
concrete.

### MC-B-CONVERSATIONAL-TURN-TAKING-JUST-HAPPENS-RANDOMLY-WITH-NO-UNDERLYING-STRUCTURE

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes that conversational turn-taking (who speaks when, how topics
shift) happens haphazardly with no systematic structure, failing to recognize that discourse analysts
have identified real, describable patterns in how speakers signal turn completion, negotiate
overlaps, and manage topic shifts — these patterns are systematic and analyzable, not random social
chaos.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a transcribed conversation and point out specific
signals a speaker uses to indicate they're finishing their turn (a falling intonation pattern, a
specific phrase like "so yeah," a pause at a grammatically complete point) that reliably prompt the
next speaker to begin. Ask: "Does this handoff between speakers happen at completely random,
unpredictable moments, or does it consistently happen at specific, signaled points?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming turn-taking is random chaos is like assuming traffic flows through a
busy intersection with no underlying system just because there's no single person directing every
car — but traffic lights, right-of-way conventions, and driver signals actually create a real,
functioning system beneath the apparent chaos. Conversational turn-taking similarly follows real,
identifiable patterns (completion signals, appropriate pause points) that speakers use and respond to
systematically, even without consciously thinking about it."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When analyzing a conversation, look for the SPECIFIC signals a speaker
uses to indicate they're finishing a turn (falling intonation, a closing phrase, reaching a
grammatically complete point) and how the next speaker responds to those signals — this reveals a
real, systematic structure underlying what might look like unstructured chat."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Assuming two speakers' turn-taking in a conversation is pure luck or chaos (misconception) vs.
   identifying the specific completion signal (a falling pitch, a specific phrase, a grammatical
   completion point) that prompted the turn to pass to the other speaker (correct, structure-aware
   analysis).
2. A student unable to explain why one speaker's interruption "worked" (was accepted) while another
   speaker's interruption at a different point caused friction (missing the structural pattern) vs. a
   student who identifies that the accepted interruption came at a natural turn-completion point while
   the friction-causing one didn't (correct structural analysis).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to view turn-taking as random after the bridge, give them a
transcribed conversation and have them mark every point where a turn changes speakers, then identify
what specific signal (if any) preceded each change, testing whether a systematic pattern emerges
across multiple instances.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.linguistics.pragmatics-intro`):** "Explain the difference between a sentence's
literal meaning and what a speaker actually intends to communicate in context." A student who cannot
reason about context-dependent meaning lacks the foundation this concept builds on — route to
`eng.linguistics.pragmatics-intro` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Bolted-Bridge and Busy-Intersection Anchor.** Combine two images: a bridge covered in visible
bolts and connectors that nonetheless fails structurally because the bolts don't connect to anything
solid — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (surface cohesion devices don't guarantee actual coherence).
Second, a busy intersection with no single traffic director yet functioning through a real, systematic
set of signals and conventions — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (conversational turn-taking follows
real, identifiable patterns, not random chaos).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Identifying Cohesive Devices

Introduce cohesive devices: pronoun reference, conjunctions, lexical repetition, and synonymy used to
link sentences. Student practice: given a short text, identify all cohesive devices used and what
each one links back to.

### TA-2 — Cohesion vs. Coherence (MC-A)

Using the bolted-bridge anchor, teach the distinction between cohesion (surface linking devices) and
coherence (actual logical sense). Student practice: given two passages (one cohesive but incoherent,
one coherent with minimal cohesive devices), evaluate each separately on both dimensions.

### TA-3 — Turn-Taking Structure in Conversation (MC-B)

Using the busy-intersection anchor, teach that conversational turn-taking follows identifiable
completion signals. Student practice: given a transcribed short conversation, mark each turn change
and identify the signal (if any) that preceded it.

### TA-4 — Topic Management Across a Conversation

Extend TA-3 to topic shifts: how speakers signal moving from one topic to another (explicit
transition phrases, questions redirecting the conversation). Student practice: given a longer
transcribed conversation, identify where topic shifts occur and what signaled each shift.

### TA-5 — Full Application: Analyzing an Extended Text or Conversation

Give the student a new short text or transcribed conversation. They must identify cohesive devices
and evaluate overall coherence separately (TA-1, TA-2), and, if analyzing a conversation, identify
turn-taking signals and topic management (TA-3, TA-4) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A short passage with clear pronoun reference and conjunctions used
coherently. Fully worked: cohesive devices identified (each pronoun's referent traced, each
conjunction's logical relationship confirmed as valid); coherence separately confirmed (the passage
makes clear logical sense as a whole). A contrasting incoherent-but-cohesive passage is then shown,
with its pronoun reference breakdown identified specifically. Annotation confirms the cohesion/
coherence distinction (fixing MC-A) is applied to both examples.

**WE-2 (Guided):** A transcribed short conversation with clear turn-taking signals. Student is
prompted with guiding questions ("At each point where the speaker changes, what specific signal —
intonation, a closing phrase, a completed grammatical unit — seems to have prompted the handoff? Does
this look random, or does a consistent pattern emerge across multiple turn changes?") to complete a
turn-taking structure analysis.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new short text or transcribed conversation, unaided. Student identifies
cohesive devices and evaluates coherence separately, and (if a conversation) identifies turn-taking
and topic-management signals.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying Cohesive Devices**
Given a short text, identify at least three cohesive devices (pronoun reference, conjunctions,
lexical repetition) and what each links back to.

**MP-2 [P36] — Separating Cohesion from Coherence**
Given a passage that is cohesive but not fully coherent, identify the specific point where coherence
breaks down despite the presence of linking devices.

**MP-3 [P36] — Identifying Turn-Taking Signals**
Given a transcribed conversation, mark each turn change and identify the specific signal that
preceded it.

**MP-4 [P36] — Identifying Topic Shifts**
Given a longer transcribed conversation, identify where topic shifts occur and what signaled each
shift.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Discourse Analysis**
Given a new text or conversation, identify cohesive devices, evaluate coherence separately, and (if a
conversation) identify turn-taking and topic-management structure.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite. During
Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1/TA-2 (assuming device
density equals coherence) and MC-B during TA-3 (viewing turn-taking as random) — these are largely
independent and often need separate, explicit correction, since one concerns written-text analysis and
the other concerns spoken conversation analysis. If either fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill
before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure indicates the basic
device-identification step (TA-1) needs review, distinct from either misconception; MP-2 failure alone
indicates residual MC-A; MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through
MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-analysis example before
re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters written-text cohesion/coherence analysis (MP-1, MP-2) but struggles
  specifically with spoken conversation analysis (MP-3, MP-4), this may reflect less prior exposure to
  transcribed spoken language — provide additional transcript examples with clear audio or intonation
  notation before independent analysis.
- If the student's coherence judgments (MP-2) are based on personal preference or writing style
  rather than genuine logical connection, redirect to the specific test: does the logical
  relationship signaled by each connector actually hold, checked independently of style preference.
- If the student conflates topic shifts with simple digressions or interruptions, clarify that
  legitimate topic management (TA-4) is typically signaled explicitly and cooperatively, distinct
  from an unsignaled, disruptive change of subject.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.linguistics.pragmatics-intro`'s cooperative-principle work
  should transfer that reasoning readily to TA-3/TA-4's conversational-structure analysis; if this
  transfer doesn't occur, revisit whether the cooperative principle was genuinely internalized as a
  systematic, predictable mechanism rather than a one-off example.
