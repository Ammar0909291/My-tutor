**Concept:** Idioms — fixed expressions whose overall meaning cannot be derived from the literal
meaning of their individual words (e.g., "kick the bucket" doesn't literally involve kicking a
bucket), requiring the whole expression to be learned as a single unit of meaning.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Collocations (prerequisite) taught that certain words
habitually co-occur, but their combined meaning is still roughly derivable from the individual words
("make a decision"). Idioms teach the more extreme case: fixed expressions whose meaning is
COMPLETELY non-literal and cannot be figured out word-by-word at all. This concept has no further
downstream unlocks in the current curriculum but is cross-linked to
`eng.linguistics.pragmatics-intro`, since correctly interpreting an idiom depends on recognizing its
conventional, context-dependent meaning rather than its literal one.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) recognize when an expression is being used
idiomatically (its non-literal meaning) rather than literally, using context to distinguish the two,
(b) explain the overall meaning of at least five common English idioms, and (c) recognize that idioms
are typically FIXED in form — most cannot be freely altered (changing individual words, adding modifiers)
without losing their idiomatic meaning or sounding wrong to a fluent speaker.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-YOU-CAN-FIGURE-OUT-AN-IDIOMS-MEANING-BY-TRANSLATING-EACH-WORD-LITERALLY

**Trigger signal:** Student attempts to interpret an idiom by combining the literal meanings of its
individual words, rather than recognizing the expression must be learned as a whole, fixed unit of
meaning — e.g., interpreting "it's raining cats and dogs" as somehow involving actual animals falling
from the sky, or being confused when the literal, word-by-word meaning doesn't match the context.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student the sentence "It's raining cats and dogs outside, so
bring an umbrella." and ask: "Does this sentence make sense if you imagine actual cats and dogs falling
from the sky? What does the SENTENCE'S CONTEXT (bringing an umbrella) suggest the phrase actually
means?" Guide the student to see that the phrase means "raining heavily," a meaning with no
derivable connection to the literal words "cats" and "dogs."

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Trying to translate an idiom word-by-word is like trying to understand a
country's national flag by analyzing the literal properties of its individual colors (blue means sad,
red means angry) — but a flag's overall meaning as a SYMBOL of that nation has nothing to do with what
each color might mean on its own. An idiom's overall meaning is a fixed, conventional symbol for a
concept, not a sum of its individual words' literal meanings."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When you encounter a phrase that doesn't make literal sense in context,
consider that it might be an idiom — a fixed expression that must be learned as a WHOLE unit of
meaning, not decoded word-by-word. Use the surrounding context to infer the overall, conventional
meaning instead."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "It's raining cats and dogs." interpreted as literally involving falling animals (word-by-word,
   incorrect) vs. correctly understood as meaning "raining heavily" (whole-unit, conventional
   meaning).
2. A student confused by an idiom because the literal words don't add up (misconception) vs. a
   student who recognizes the mismatch as a signal to treat the phrase as a fixed, non-literal
   expression and infer its meaning from context (correct approach).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to attempt word-by-word translation after the bridge, give them
three more idioms in clear context and have them first attempt (and fail) a literal interpretation,
then use the surrounding context to infer the actual conventional meaning, reinforcing that this
"literal attempt fails, context succeeds" pattern is expected for idioms.

### MC-B-YOU-CAN-FREELY-CHANGE-WORDS-WITHIN-AN-IDIOM-AS-LONG-AS-THE-MEANING-STAYS-SIMILAR

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes idioms are flexible expressions where individual words can be
freely substituted with synonyms as long as the general topic stays similar, failing to recognize
that most idioms are FIXED expressions — altering even one word (even to an apparent synonym)
typically breaks the idiom, either making it meaningless or sounding wrong to a fluent speaker.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student the correct idiom "kick the bucket" (meaning to die)
alongside an altered version using an apparent synonym, "kick the pail" (using "pail," a genuine
synonym for "bucket"). Ask: "Does 'kick the pail' still mean 'to die' to a fluent English speaker, or
does changing even this one word to a synonym break the idiom entirely?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming you can freely swap synonyms into an idiom is like assuming you can
freely substitute one ingredient for a very similar one in a chemical formula and get the same
reaction — but a formula's specific combination is fixed and precise; substituting even a very similar
element can produce a completely different (or no) result. Idioms are similarly FIXED expressions —
their exact wording is conventionally locked in, and substituting even a close synonym typically
breaks the expression entirely."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Treat idioms as fixed, memorized units — don't assume you can substitute
individual words with synonyms and preserve the idiomatic meaning. Learn the exact conventional wording
of each idiom, since altering it usually breaks it."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Kick the bucket" (the fixed, correct idiom, meaning to die) vs. "Kick the pail" (a synonym
   substitution that breaks the idiom entirely, no longer understood as meaning "to die" by fluent
   speakers).
2. A student assuming idiom wording is flexible as long as the general meaning is preserved
   (misconception) vs. a student who recognizes idioms as fixed expressions requiring exact,
   memorized wording (correct understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to assume idiom flexibility after the bridge, give them three
more idioms and have them generate a synonym-substituted version of each, then check with a reference
or fluent-speaker judgment whether the substituted version still carries the idiomatic meaning,
confirming the pattern that substitution typically breaks the expression.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.vocab.collocations`):** "Which of these word pairs is a natural collocation in
English: 'make a decision' or 'do a decision'?" A student who cannot identify basic collocations lacks
the foundation this concept extends to fully non-literal expressions — route to
`eng.vocab.collocations` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The National-Flag-and-Chemical-Formula Anchor.** Combine two images: a national flag whose overall
meaning as a symbol of a country has nothing to do with the literal properties of its individual
colors — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (idioms carry a fixed, conventional whole-unit meaning, not
a word-by-word one). Second, a precise chemical formula where substituting one element for a very
similar one produces an entirely different result — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (idioms are
fixed expressions; substituting even a close synonym typically breaks them).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Recognizing When an Expression Is Idiomatic (MC-A)

Using the national-flag anchor, teach students to recognize when a phrase's literal meaning doesn't
fit its context, signaling an idiom. Student practice: given four sentences containing idioms in
clear context, identify each idiom and infer its overall conventional meaning from context.

### TA-2 — Learning Common Idiom Meanings

Introduce five to six common English idioms with their conventional meanings directly. Student
practice: given six sentences using these idioms, match each to its correct meaning.

### TA-3 — Idioms Are Fixed Expressions (MC-B)

Using the chemical-formula anchor, teach that idioms cannot be freely altered with synonyms. Student
practice: given three idioms and a synonym-substituted version of each, determine whether the
substituted version still carries the idiomatic meaning.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Interpreting and Using Idioms Correctly

Give the student a new short text containing two or three idioms in context. They must identify each
as idiomatic (TA-1), state its correct conventional meaning (TA-2), and confirm they can use each
idiom's exact, fixed wording correctly in an original sentence (TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Sentence: "After studying all night, she finally hit the books and aced the
exam." Fully worked: "hit the books" identified as idiomatic (literal meaning of physically striking
books doesn't fit the context of aced exam preparation); correct meaning inferred from context
(studying hard); confirmed as a fixed expression (checking that "hit the textbooks" or "punch the
books" would not carry the same idiomatic meaning to a fluent speaker). Annotation confirms both the
context-based idiomatic recognition (fixing MC-A) and the fixed-wording confirmation (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New sentence containing a different idiom. Student is prompted with guiding
questions ("Does the literal meaning of this phrase fit the sentence's context? What does the context
suggest the phrase actually means? If you swapped one word for a close synonym, would the idiom still
work?") to complete the identification, meaning inference, and fixed-wording check.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new short text containing two or three idioms, unaided. Student identifies
each as idiomatic, states its correct meaning, and confirms fixed wording by testing (and rejecting) a
synonym substitution for at least one.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Recognizing Idiomatic Use in Context**
Given three sentences, identify which contain an idiom (non-literal, fixed expression) based on
context, and infer each one's overall meaning.

**MP-2 [P36] — Matching Idioms to Meanings**
Given five common idioms, match each to its correct conventional meaning.

**MP-3 [P36] — Testing Fixed Wording**
Given an idiom and a synonym-substituted version, determine whether the substituted version still
carries the idiomatic meaning, explaining why or why not.

**MP-4 [P36] — Using an Idiom Correctly**
Given a common idiom's meaning, use the idiom correctly (with its exact, fixed wording) in an
original sentence.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Contextual Idiom Analysis**
Given a new short text containing two or three idioms, identify each, state its correct meaning, and
confirm fixed wording for at least one by testing a synonym substitution.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite. During
Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1 (word-by-word attempts) and
MC-B during TA-3 (assuming flexible substitution) — MC-A generally needs resolving first since
recognizing an expression AS idiomatic is a prerequisite to learning that its wording is fixed. If
either fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9),
route by failure: MP-1 failure indicates residual MC-A; MP-2 failure alone indicates the meaning-
memorization step (TA-2) needs review, distinct from either misconception; MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate
residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one
more guided contextual example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student is a language learner whose first language has different idioms for similar
  concepts, leverage this as a comparative example — most languages have idioms, and comparing across
  languages can make the "fixed, non-literal, conventional expression" concept feel less arbitrary.
- If the student masters recognition and meaning (MP-1, MP-2) but struggles to actively USE idioms
  correctly in their own writing (MP-4), treat this as a production gap — provide more guided practice
  generating original sentences with a given idiom before independent use.
- If the student becomes overly cautious and starts flagging every non-literal or figurative phrase
  as an "idiom" (over-generalizing), clarify that idioms are a specific category of FIXED,
  conventional expressions, distinct from other figurative language like metaphor or simile, which are
  more flexible and original to the writer.
- Students with strong metaphor/simile skills from `eng.composition.figurative-language-in-
  composition` should be shown the distinction explicitly: idioms are conventional and fixed, while
  original metaphors are created and can vary — the two are related but not identical categories of
  non-literal language.
