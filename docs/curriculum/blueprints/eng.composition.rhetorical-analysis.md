**Concept:** Rhetorical Analysis — analyzing how a text's rhetorical choices (appeals, devices,
structure, word choice) work together to achieve its persuasive or communicative effect on a
specific audience.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Rhetorical Devices (prerequisite) teaches the student
to identify individual techniques. Critical Reading (prerequisite) teaches evaluating a text's
reliability and reasoning. Rhetorical Analysis fuses both into a higher-order skill: instead of just
spotting devices or judging credibility, the student must explain HOW a text's specific choices work
TOGETHER, for a specific audience, to build an overall effect — this is genuinely evaluative work,
not just identification. This concept unlocks `eng.composition.comparative-essay-writing` (comparing
two texts' rhetorical strategies requires being able to fully analyze one first) and
`eng.composition.research-paper-writing` (evaluating source texts rhetorically is part of using them
critically in research).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can, given a real persuasive or argumentative text, (a)
identify the specific rhetorical choices at work (appeals used, devices employed, structural
decisions), (b) explain how those choices work together, not in isolation, to build a cumulative
effect for the text's specific intended audience, and (c) evaluate how EFFECTIVE those choices
actually are for that audience — not simply whether the writer agrees with the text's position.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-RHETORICAL-ANALYSIS-MEANS-LISTING-EVERY-DEVICE-FOUND

**Trigger signal:** Student treats rhetorical analysis as an exhaustive scavenger hunt — listing
every appeal and device found in a text with no discussion of how they work together or what
cumulative effect they produce — e.g., "This uses ethos, then pathos, then a metaphor, then
repetition, then logos," with no synthesis connecting these choices into a coherent account of the
text's overall persuasive strategy.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two analyses of the same short speech: one that lists
five devices found with a sentence about each in isolation, and one that groups those same five
devices into a coherent account of how they build toward the speech's climax together (e.g., "The
early credibility-building [ethos] earns trust, which the escalating repetition [device] later
exploits to build emotional momentum [pathos] toward the closing call to action"). Ask: "Which
analysis actually explains how the SPEECH works as a whole, rather than just what's in it?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "A list of devices found in a text is like listing every ingredient in a
meal without ever describing how they combine into a dish — 'this has salt, this has garlic, this
has butter' tells you what's present, but not what it tastes like together or why the chef combined
them that way. Rhetorical analysis needs to explain the COMBINED effect, not just the ingredient
list."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "After identifying the devices and appeals present, always add a
synthesis step: how do these choices work TOGETHER to build one cumulative effect on the audience?
Which choice sets up which other choice? If your analysis is just a list with no connecting
explanation, it's an inventory, not an analysis."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "This speech uses ethos, pathos, and repetition." (an inventory, no connection explained) vs.
   "This speech first establishes ethos to earn the audience's trust, then leverages that trust with
   an escalating repetition that builds emotional momentum (pathos) toward its closing appeal — each
   choice sets up the next." (a synthesized account of combined effect).
2. A student essay organized device-by-device with no connecting thread (list structure) vs. a
   student essay organized around the text's overall persuasive ARC, citing devices as evidence
   along the way (synthesis structure).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to produce inventories after the bridge, have them draw
literal arrows connecting each identified device to the one before and after it in the text, forcing
them to articulate what each arrow represents (what does device 1 set up for device 2 to use?)
before writing any prose.

### MC-B-EVALUATING-EFFECTIVENESS-MEANS-STATING-WHETHER-YOU-AGREE-WITH-THE-ARGUMENT

**Trigger signal:** Student conflates evaluating a text's RHETORICAL effectiveness (how well its
persuasive strategy works ON ITS INTENDED AUDIENCE) with stating personal agreement or disagreement
with the text's position — e.g., "This argument is not effective because I don't think school
uniforms should be required," regardless of whether the text's rhetorical strategy is actually
well-suited to persuading its stated audience.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Give the student a persuasive text whose position they personally
disagree with, but whose rhetorical strategy is genuinely well-matched to its stated audience (strong
ethos-building for a skeptical audience, well-connected pathos, relevant logos). Ask: "Do you agree
with this argument's conclusion? Separately — regardless of whether you agree — is its rhetorical
strategy well-suited to persuading the specific audience it's aimed at?" Guide the student to see
these are two entirely separate questions with potentially different answers — a text can be
rhetorically skillful while arguing for a position the analyst personally rejects.

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Evaluating rhetorical effectiveness is like judging a chess move's quality
regardless of which side you're rooting for — a brilliant move is brilliant whether you want that
player to win or not. Rhetorical analysis asks 'does this strategy work well for its intended
audience?' — a completely separate question from 'do I personally agree with the conclusion?'"

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before writing an evaluation, ask two SEPARATE questions: (1) Do I
personally agree with this argument's position? (2) Regardless of my answer to (1), is the
rhetorical strategy well-matched to the stated audience and likely to persuade them? Answer both,
and never let (1) silently substitute for (2)."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "This argument fails because I disagree with school uniforms." (agreement substituted for
   rhetorical evaluation) vs. "Regardless of my own view on uniforms, this argument's heavy reliance
   on emotional appeals without any supporting data is unlikely to persuade the school board audience
   it's aimed at, who has stated a preference for data-driven proposals." (genuine rhetorical
   evaluation, independent of personal agreement).
2. A student rating an argument they agree with as automatically "effective" without examining its
   actual strategy (agreement-as-proxy) vs. a student rating an argument they agree with as
   rhetorically WEAK because it fails to address its stated audience's specific concerns (genuine,
   agreement-independent evaluation).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to substitute agreement for evaluation after the bridge, have
them analyze a text arguing for a position they personally hold, deliberately looking for
rhetorical WEAKNESSES in it — if they can identify weaknesses in an argument they agree with, this
proves they can separate the two judgments.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.composition.rhetorical-devices`):** "Identify one rhetorical device in this
short passage." A student who cannot identify basic devices lacks the vocabulary this concept builds
on — route to `eng.composition.rhetorical-devices` review first.

**PD-2 (checks `eng.reading.critical-reading`):** "Evaluate whether this short passage's supporting
evidence is reliable." A student who cannot perform basic source/evidence evaluation needs
`eng.reading.critical-reading` reviewed before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Recipe-versus-Ingredient-List Anchor.** Show a written ingredient list ("salt, garlic, butter,
onion") alongside a recipe describing how those same ingredients are combined, in what order, and
why ("saute the onion and garlic in butter first to build a flavor base, then add salt to draw out
the moisture..."). Ask: "Which one tells you what the dish will actually taste like?" This directly
seeds the fix for MC-A — identifying devices without explaining their combination is like listing
ingredients without the recipe. Extend the anchor with a chess framing for MC-B: "A judge scoring a
chess match evaluates the QUALITY of the moves, not which side they happen to be rooting for" —
rhetorical effectiveness is judged the same way, independent of the analyst's personal agreement.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Cataloguing Rhetorical Choices (the Ingredient List Step)

Teach students to first identify the individual appeals, devices, and structural choices present in
a text — this is a necessary but incomplete first step. Student practice: given a short speech,
catalogue every appeal and device present.

### TA-2 — Synthesizing How Choices Work Together (MC-A)

Using the arrow-drawing test, teach students to explain how catalogued choices build on each other
toward a cumulative effect. Student practice: given the catalogue from TA-1, draw connecting arrows
and write a synthesis paragraph explaining the combined strategy.

### TA-3 — Separating Personal Agreement from Rhetorical Evaluation (MC-B)

Using the chess-judge framing, teach the two-separate-questions method. Student practice: given three
texts (one the student likely agrees with, one they likely disagree with, one neutral), answer both
questions separately for each.

### TA-4 — Evaluating Audience Fit

Teach that genuine rhetorical evaluation centers on whether the strategy suits the STATED audience,
not a generic "is this well-written" judgment. Student practice: given a text and its stated
audience, evaluate specifically whether the chosen appeals and devices are well-matched to that
audience's likely values and objections.

### TA-5 — Full Rhetorical Analysis

Give the student a complete, real persuasive text with a stated audience. They must catalogue its
choices (TA-1), synthesize how those choices work together (TA-2), and evaluate its effectiveness
for its specific audience independent of personal agreement (TA-3, TA-4) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A short excerpt from a persuasive speech aimed at a specific stated audience
(city council members skeptical of a proposed budget item). Fully worked: catalogue (ethos via
citing relevant experience, a specific statistic for logos, one instance of parallelism); synthesis
(the speaker builds credibility first, which primes the council to trust the subsequent statistic,
and the parallel structure at the close makes the ask memorable); evaluation (the strategy is
well-matched to a skeptical, data-oriented audience because it leads with credibility and data before
any emotional appeal, independent of whether the reader personally supports the proposal). Annotation
confirms the synthesis step (fixing MC-A) and the agreement-independent evaluation (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New excerpt, new audience (a persuasive letter aimed at parents worried about a
new policy). Student is prompted with guiding questions ("What choices are present? How does each
choice set up the next? Setting aside whether you personally support this policy, does this
strategy address what a worried parent audience would actually care about?") to complete the full
analysis.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new, complete short text with a stated audience, unaided. Student
catalogues, synthesizes, and evaluates for audience fit without guiding questions.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Cataloguing Choices**
Given a short persuasive passage, catalogue the specific appeals and devices present.

**MP-2 [P36] — Synthesizing Combined Effect**
Given a catalogue of choices from a passage, write a synthesis explaining how at least two of them
work together to build a cumulative effect, not just listing them.

**MP-3 [P36] — Separating Agreement from Evaluation**
Given a text arguing for a position the student is likely to personally hold, identify at least one
genuine rhetorical weakness in its strategy, demonstrating the two judgments are separable.

**MP-4 [P36] — Audience-Fit Evaluation**
Given a text and its stated audience, evaluate whether the specific rhetorical choices are
well-matched to that audience's likely values and concerns.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Rhetorical Analysis**
Given a new complete persuasive text with a stated audience, produce a full analysis: catalogue the
choices, synthesize how they work together, and evaluate effectiveness for the specific audience,
independent of personal agreement.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a gap in either prerequisite routes
back before Component 3. During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during
TA-1/TA-2 (inventory-only responses) and MC-B during TA-3 (agreement substituting for evaluation) —
watch each TA for its matching trigger. Because MC-B can surface strong personal reactions (a student
may resist evaluating a text they feel strongly about), maintain a neutral, curious tone rather than
challenging the student's personal position directly — the target is the SEPARATION of judgments, not
a change in the student's opinion. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure
indicates basic cataloguing (TA-1) needs review; MP-2 failure alone indicates residual MC-A; MP-3
failure alone indicates residual MC-B; MP-4 failure alone indicates the audience-fit extension (TA-4)
specifically; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one
more fully-guided full-analysis example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student can catalogue and synthesize (MP-1, MP-2) but struggles specifically with texts
  whose position they hold strongly (MP-3, MP-5), this is expected and not a conceptual failure —
  provide additional practice specifically with emotionally-charged topics the student cares about,
  since the skill is hardest to apply exactly where it matters most.
- If the student produces technically separated evaluations but their "rhetorical weakness" claims in
  MP-3 are themselves weak or contrived (grasping for a flaw just to prove separation), clarify that
  genuine critical distance sometimes means concluding a text they agree with IS rhetorically strong
  — the goal is honest evaluation, not forced criticism.
- If the student excels at analyzing texts on emotionally neutral topics but their skills don't
  transfer to charged topics, treat this as evidence the separation skill (TA-3) needs more
  scaffolded practice specifically under affective load, not that the underlying analytical skill is
  absent.
- Students moving quickly through cataloguing but producing thin synthesis paragraphs may benefit
  from the arrow-drawing physical step (TA-2) as a mandatory intermediate artifact, not an optional
  aid, until the synthesis habit is automatic.
