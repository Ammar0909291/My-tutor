**Concept:** Quotation Marks — punctuation conventions for marking direct quotations (someone's exact
words), titles of short works (poems, articles, short stories), and dialogue in narrative writing.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Direct and Indirect Speech (prerequisite) taught the
grammatical difference between reporting exact words versus paraphrasing. Comma Usage (prerequisite)
taught precise comma placement. Quotation Marks teaches the specific punctuation conventions that
apply ONLY to direct speech (never indirect/reported speech) and extends to a second, unrelated use:
marking titles of short works. This concept has no further downstream unlocks in the current
curriculum but is a foundational mechanical skill for narrative and academic writing.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) correctly place quotation marks around a direct
quotation (someone's exact words) while NEVER placing them around indirect/reported speech, (b)
correctly punctuate the comma and end punctuation relative to quotation marks in dialogue, and (c)
correctly use quotation marks to mark the title of a short work (poem, article, short story), distinct
from the italics/underlining convention used for longer, standalone works.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-QUOTATION-MARKS-SHOULD-BE-USED-AROUND-INDIRECT-REPORTED-SPEECH-TOO

**Trigger signal:** Student places quotation marks around indirect/reported speech (a paraphrase of
what someone said, not their exact words) — e.g., writing "She said that she was tired." with
quotation marks around "that she was tired," when indirect speech should NEVER be quoted, since it is
by definition a paraphrase, not the speaker's exact words.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two sentences: "She said, 'I am tired.'" (direct speech,
her exact words, correctly quoted) and "She said that she was tired." (indirect speech, a paraphrase —
notice the pronoun and verb tense have changed from what she would have actually said). Ask: "Are the
exact words 'she was tired' something she literally said out loud, or is this a paraphrased report of
what she said? Should paraphrased, reported content ever be placed in quotation marks?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Placing quotation marks around indirect speech is like putting a museum's
'authentic artifact' label on a modern replica — quotation marks are a specific promise to the reader
that these are someone's EXACT, verbatim words, not a paraphrase. Indirect speech is, by definition, a
paraphrase (with pronouns and tenses shifted), so it can never honestly carry that 'exact words'
promise."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Only ever place quotation marks around a speaker's EXACT, word-for-word
statement (direct speech). If the sentence is reporting what someone said in your own paraphrased
words (indirect speech, often signaled by 'that' and shifted pronouns/tenses), never use quotation
marks."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "She said, 'I am tired.'" (direct speech, exact words, correctly quoted) vs. "She said that she was
   tired." (indirect speech, a paraphrase, correctly NOT quoted).
2. A student who writes "He said 'that he would be late.'" (incorrectly quoting a paraphrase) vs. a
   student who correctly writes "He said, 'I will be late.'" (quoting only the genuine direct
   quotation) or "He said that he would be late." (correctly unquoted indirect speech).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to quote indirect speech after the bridge, have them convert
five direct-speech sentences into indirect speech (adjusting pronouns and tenses as needed) and
explicitly remove the quotation marks each time, reinforcing that the shift to paraphrase removes the
quoting requirement.

### MC-B-COMMAS-AND-END-PUNCTUATION-GO-OUTSIDE-THE-CLOSING-QUOTATION-MARK-IN-AMERICAN-ENGLISH-DIALOGUE

**Trigger signal:** Student places commas and periods OUTSIDE the closing quotation mark in
American English conventions (e.g., writing "I am tired", she said. instead of "I am tired," she
said.), applying a punctuation-outside convention that may be common in some other English varieties
or simply guessed incorrectly, rather than the standard American convention of placing commas and
periods INSIDE the closing quotation mark.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two versions: "I am tired", she said. (comma placed
outside the closing quotation mark) and "I am tired," she said. (comma placed inside the closing
quotation mark, the standard American convention). Ask: "In standard American English writing
convention, which placement is correct for the comma relative to the closing quotation mark?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Punctuation placement relative to quotation marks is a fixed convention, like
which side of the road to drive on — there's no universal 'correct by nature' answer, but within one
system (standard American English), the convention is consistently applied: commas and periods go
INSIDE the closing quotation mark, every time, regardless of whether the punctuation is logically 'part
of' the quotation."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "In standard American English convention, always place a comma or period
directly before the closing quotation mark (inside it), even when the punctuation isn't logically part
of the quoted words themselves. Treat this as a fixed, memorized rule for this specific writing
convention."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "I am tired", she said. (comma outside the closing quotation mark — not standard American
   convention) vs. "I am tired," she said. (comma inside the closing quotation mark — correct standard
   American convention).
2. A student who places the period outside the closing quotation mark at the end of a quoted
   sentence (incorrect for standard American convention) vs. a student who places it inside (correct).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to place commas/periods outside the closing quotation mark
after the bridge, have them punctuate five dialogue sentences, checking each one specifically for
correct comma/period placement relative to the closing quotation mark before checking anything else.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.grammar.direct-and-indirect-speech`):** "Is this sentence direct or indirect
speech: 'She told me that she liked the movie'?" A student who cannot distinguish direct from indirect
speech lacks the foundation this concept builds on — route to
`eng.grammar.direct-and-indirect-speech` review first.

**PD-2 (checks `eng.grammar.comma-usage`):** "Correctly punctuate this sentence containing an
introductory phrase followed by a comma." A student who cannot apply basic comma rules lacks the
punctuation-precision foundation this concept builds on — route to `eng.grammar.comma-usage` review
before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Museum-Authenticity-Label and Drive-on-One-Side Anchor.** Combine two images: a museum's
"authentic artifact" label, which makes a specific promise (this is the genuine original, not a
replica) that a modern replica cannot honestly carry — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (quotation
marks promise exact words, which indirect speech by definition isn't). Second, a fixed traffic
convention (which side of the road to drive on) — no universal "correct by nature" answer, but
consistently applied within one system — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (comma/period placement
relative to quotation marks is a fixed, memorized convention for standard American English).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Never Quoting Indirect Speech (MC-A)

Using the museum-authenticity anchor, teach that quotation marks are reserved exclusively for direct,
exact speech. Student practice: given six sentences (mixing direct and indirect speech), identify
which should and should not be quoted.

### TA-2 — Comma and End Punctuation Placement in Dialogue (MC-B)

Using the drive-on-one-side anchor, teach the standard American convention of placing commas and
periods inside the closing quotation mark. Student practice: given four dialogue sentences with
missing or misplaced punctuation, correctly punctuate each.

### TA-3 — Quoting Titles of Short Works

Introduce the second, distinct use of quotation marks: marking titles of short works (poems, articles,
short stories), as distinct from longer, standalone works (which use italics). Student practice: given
six titles of varying work types, correctly apply quotation marks or note that italics would be used
instead.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Punctuating Dialogue and Titles Correctly

Give the student a short passage containing dialogue (mixing direct and indirect speech) and a
reference to a short work's title. They must correctly quote only direct speech (TA-1), correctly
place commas/periods relative to quotation marks (TA-2), and correctly quote the short work's title
(TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Passage: "My favorite poem is 'The Road Not Taken.' 'I love how it makes me
think,' she said. She told me that it was her favorite too." Fully worked: the poem title correctly
quoted (a short work); the direct quotation correctly quoted with the comma placed inside the closing
quotation mark; the indirect speech ("she told me that it was her favorite too") correctly left
unquoted. Annotation confirms all three rules (fixing MC-A for the indirect speech, fixing MC-B for
the comma placement, and applying the title rule correctly).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New passage with a mix of direct speech, indirect speech, and a short story title,
containing intentional errors. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("Is this sentence reporting
someone's exact words, or a paraphrase — should it be quoted? Where does the comma go relative to the
closing quotation mark here? Is this title a short work needing quotation marks, or a longer work
needing italics?") to correct each error.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new short passage containing direct speech, indirect speech, and a short
work title, unaided. Student correctly applies all quotation mark conventions.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Distinguishing Direct from Indirect Speech for Quoting**
Given four sentences, identify which are direct speech (requiring quotation marks) and which are
indirect speech (never quoted).

**MP-2 [P36] — Correct Comma/Period Placement**
Given three dialogue sentences with missing punctuation, correctly place commas and periods relative
to the closing quotation mark following standard American convention.

**MP-3 [P36] — Quoting Titles of Short Works**
Given four titles of varying work types (poem, novel, short story, article), correctly identify which
require quotation marks and which would use italics instead.

**MP-4 [P36] — Correcting Quotation Errors**
Given three sentences with quotation mark errors (indirect speech incorrectly quoted, or misplaced
punctuation), identify and correct each error.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Passage Punctuation**
Given a new short passage containing direct speech, indirect speech, and a short work title, correctly
apply all quotation mark conventions throughout.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a gap in either prerequisite routes
back before Component 3. During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during
TA-1 (over-quoting indirect speech) and MC-B during TA-2 (punctuation placement) — these are largely
independent conventions and often need separate, explicit correction. If either fires, branch to the
relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure
indicates residual MC-A; MP-2 failure alone indicates residual MC-B; MP-3 failure alone indicates the
title-quoting extension (TA-3) needs review, distinct from either misconception; MP-4/MP-5 failures
with MP-1 through MP-3 passing indicate an integration gap — provide one more guided full-passage
example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters each rule in isolation (MP-1 through MP-3) but reverts to errors when all
  three are mixed together (MP-5), provide a structured checklist (is this direct speech? where does
  the comma go? is this a short-work title?) as scaffolding during mixed practice before removing it.
- If the student has been exposed to British/other English conventions (where punctuation placement
  relative to quotation marks can differ), acknowledge this explicitly as a genuine, valid alternative
  convention in a different variety of English, while confirming which specific convention (standard
  American) this concept and their assigned writing context requires.
- If the student confuses italics-worthy longer works (novels, plays, albums) with quotation-mark-
  worthy short works (poems, articles, short stories, songs) beyond the practiced examples, provide a
  quick-reference list of common work types sorted into each category.
- Students with strong narrative-writing interests should be encouraged to apply these rules
  immediately in their own dialogue-heavy creative writing, since sustained authentic practice
  reinforces the mechanical rules more durably than isolated drills alone.
