**Concept:** Dialectology — the systematic study of regional and social dialects as a specialized
branch of sociolinguistics, including how linguists MAP dialect boundaries (isoglosses) and document
gradual, continuous variation rather than sharp, uniform dialect lines.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Sociolinguistics (prerequisite) established language
variation as tied to social factors broadly. Dialectology narrows the focus to the specialized
methodology for systematically documenting and mapping REGIONAL variation specifically — using tools
like the isogloss (a mapped boundary line for a single specific linguistic feature) to show that
dialect regions are not simple, uniform, sharply-bounded zones but complex, overlapping, gradual
transitions. This concept has no further downstream unlocks in the current curriculum but completes
the sociolinguistics sequence's specialized regional-variation branch.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) explain what an isogloss is (a mapped boundary line
for ONE specific linguistic feature) and that different isoglosses for different features rarely align
perfectly, (b) explain that dialect regions are not sharply bounded, uniform zones but involve gradual,
overlapping transition areas, and (c) explain that a "dialect" is not a single, monolithic category but
a bundle of many individually-varying features, some shared with neighboring dialects and some not.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-DIALECT-BOUNDARIES-ARE-SHARP-LINES-WHERE-EVERYONE-ON-ONE-SIDE-SPEAKS-ONE-WAY-AND-EVERYONE-ON-THE-OTHER-SPEAKS-DIFFERENTLY

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes dialect regions have sharp, clean boundaries (like a political
border on a map) where speech changes abruptly and uniformly at a single line, failing to recognize
that real dialect boundaries (isoglosses) are typically gradual, involve transition zones with mixed
or intermediate features, and that different individual linguistic features often have DIFFERENT
boundary lines that don't align with each other.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a real (or realistic, illustrative) dialect map showing
multiple isoglosses for different features (a vocabulary difference, a pronunciation difference, a
grammatical difference) crossing a region at DIFFERENT, non-aligned lines, rather than one single
unified boundary. Ask: "Do all these different feature boundaries fall on exactly the same line? What
does that suggest about assuming a single, sharp 'dialect border'?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming dialect boundaries are sharp, single lines is like assuming climate
zones change abruptly at a single line on a map — but real climate transitions are gradual, and
different climate FEATURES (temperature, rainfall, vegetation) each have their own somewhat different
transition zones that don't align perfectly. Dialect boundaries work the same way: each individual
linguistic feature has its OWN isogloss, and these isoglosses for different features rarely stack up
into one single, sharp dividing line."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't picture dialect boundaries as a single sharp line separating two
uniform regions. Picture instead many individual isoglosses (one per linguistic feature), each
following its own somewhat different path, creating gradual, overlapping transition zones rather than
one clean dividing line."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Assuming a single dialect border exists where speech uniformly changes at one line (misconception)
   vs. recognizing multiple different isoglosses for different features, each following its own
   somewhat different path across a region (correct, evidence-based understanding).
2. A student surprised that someone living right at a "dialect boundary" might mix features from both
   neighboring dialects (missing the transition-zone reality) vs. a student expecting this mixing as
   the normal, predictable pattern in a genuine transition zone (correct understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to picture sharp dialect boundaries after the bridge, show them
two or three individual isoglosses for different specific features in the same general region and have
them identify where the lines diverge from each other, reinforcing that no single unified boundary
exists.

### MC-B-A-DIALECT-IS-ONE-SINGLE-UNIFIED-THING-EVERYONE-IN-A-REGION-EITHER-HAS-OR-DOESNT-HAVE

**Trigger signal:** Student treats "a dialect" as a single, monolithic, all-or-nothing category that a
speaker either fully has or entirely lacks, failing to recognize that a named dialect is actually a
BUNDLE of many individual features (specific pronunciations, vocabulary items, grammatical patterns),
and real speakers typically have some but not all of the features associated with a named dialect,
with individual variation being the norm rather than the exception.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a description of two speakers both broadly identified
as speaking "the same regional dialect," but who differ from each other on several specific features
(one uses a specific vocabulary item the other doesn't; one has a specific pronunciation feature the
other lacks). Ask: "If both are considered speakers of the same named dialect, but they differ on
several specific features, does that mean the dialect label refers to one single, uniform, all-or-
nothing set of features every speaker shares identically?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Treating a dialect as one single unified thing everyone either has or lacks
is like treating 'being a jazz musician' as requiring mastery of every single jazz technique that has
ever existed — in reality, individual jazz musicians share a broad family resemblance while each
having their own specific mix of techniques, influences, and quirks. A named dialect is similarly a
BUNDLE of many individual features; real speakers typically share MOST but rarely ALL of the bundle,
and individual variation within a dialect label is completely normal."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When you encounter a named dialect, remember it's a convenient label for
a BUNDLE of many individual linguistic features, not a single monolithic thing. Expect real speakers
labeled with that dialect to vary — sharing many but rarely all of the associated features — rather
than assuming uniform, all-or-nothing membership."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Assuming every speaker of a named dialect must share every single associated feature identically
   (rigid, monolithic view) vs. recognizing that speakers typically share a majority of features with
   individual variation being normal and expected (correct, bundle-based understanding).
2. A student confused when two speakers of "the same dialect" show real differences (treating this as
   a contradiction) vs. a student expecting and explaining this variation as the normal reality of
   dialects being feature bundles, not monolithic categories (correct understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to treat dialects as monolithic after the bridge, have them list
five individual features associated with a named dialect they're familiar with, then identify which
specific features they personally have and which they don't, demonstrating the bundle-based, partial-
membership reality directly from their own experience.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.linguistics.sociolinguistics-intro`):** "Explain one social factor, besides
geography, that can produce systematic language variation." A student who cannot answer correctly
lacks the foundational sociolinguistic framing this concept specializes into regional variation —
route to `eng.linguistics.sociolinguistics-intro` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Climate-Zone-Gradual-Transition and Jazz-Musician-Family-Resemblance Anchor.** Combine two
images: a climate map showing gradual, non-aligned transitions for temperature, rainfall, and
vegetation rather than one sharp climate boundary — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (dialect
isoglosses for different features don't align into one sharp boundary). Second, jazz musicians sharing
a broad family resemblance while each having a unique mix of specific techniques, none identical to
any other — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (a dialect is a feature bundle with normal individual
variation, not a monolithic all-or-nothing category).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — The Isogloss: Mapping One Feature at a Time

Introduce the isogloss as a mapped boundary line for a SINGLE specific linguistic feature. Student
practice: given data on where a specific vocabulary feature is used across a region, sketch or
describe the likely isogloss for that one feature.

### TA-2 — Multiple Isoglosses Rarely Align (MC-A)

Using the climate-zone anchor, teach that different features' isoglosses typically follow different
paths, creating gradual transition zones rather than one sharp boundary. Student practice: given two
or three different features' isoglosses for the same region, identify where they diverge from each
other.

### TA-3 — Dialects as Feature Bundles, Not Monolithic Categories (MC-B)

Using the jazz-musician anchor, teach that a named dialect is a bundle of many individual features,
with normal speaker-to-speaker variation. Student practice: given two speakers both labeled with the
same named dialect but differing on some features, explain why this is expected, not contradictory.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Analyzing a Dialect Region

Give the student a new dataset describing several linguistic features across a region, with
isoglosses that don't perfectly align, and two speaker profiles showing partial, non-identical
membership in the region's named dialect. They must explain the non-aligned isoglosses (TA-2) and the
bundle-based, partial dialect membership (TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Data: a specific vocabulary term ("soda" vs. "pop") has one isogloss path
across a region; a specific pronunciation feature has a different isogloss path through the same
region; two speakers near the boundary each use some but not all of the region's associated dialect
features. Fully worked: the two isoglosses' non-alignment explained (each feature has its own boundary,
not one shared line); the two speakers' partial, differing feature sets explained as normal bundle-
based variation, not a contradiction of their shared dialect label. Annotation confirms both the
isogloss non-alignment (fixing MC-A) and the feature-bundle understanding (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New dataset with two different features' isoglosses and speaker profiles. Student
is prompted with guiding questions ("Do these two features' boundary lines fall in exactly the same
place? What does that suggest about picturing a single dialect border? Do these two speakers share
every single feature associated with their named dialect, or do they differ on some — is that a
problem, or expected?") to complete the analysis.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new dataset with multiple features' isoglosses and speaker profiles,
unaided. Student explains the non-aligned isoglosses and the bundle-based, partial dialect membership.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Understanding the Isogloss**
Explain what an isogloss is and what specific kind of linguistic information it maps.

**MP-2 [P36] — Explaining Non-Aligned Isoglosses**
Given data showing two different features' isoglosses following different paths through the same
region, explain why this occurs and what it means for the idea of a single "dialect boundary."

**MP-3 [P36] — Explaining Dialect as a Feature Bundle**
Given two speakers labeled with the same named dialect but differing on some specific features,
explain why this variation is expected rather than contradictory.

**MP-4 [P36] — Applying the Bundle Concept to a New Example**
Given a list of features associated with a named dialect, identify why a speaker sharing most but not
all of them can still legitimately be considered a speaker of that dialect.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Dialect Region Analysis**
Given a new dataset with multiple features' isoglosses and speaker profiles, explain the non-aligned
isoglosses and the bundle-based, partial dialect membership shown.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite. During
Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1/TA-2 (assuming sharp
boundaries) and MC-B during TA-3 (assuming monolithic dialects) — these are related but distinct
misconceptions and often need separate, explicit correction, since a student can accept gradual
transitions (fixing MC-A) while still viewing each dialect itself as internally uniform (still holding
MC-B). If either fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe
stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure indicates the basic isogloss concept (TA-1) needs review,
distinct from either misconception; MP-2 failure alone indicates residual MC-A; MP-3/MP-4 failures
indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap —
provide one more guided full-analysis example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student is themselves from a region with well-known dialect variation, or has family from
  different dialect regions, connect this concept directly and validatingly to their own experience —
  noticing which specific features they personally have or lack relative to a named regional dialect
  can make the abstract "feature bundle" concept concrete and personal.
- If the student masters the isogloss concept (MP-1, MP-2) but struggles specifically with the
  feature-bundle reasoning (MP-3, MP-4), revisit `eng.linguistics.sociolinguistics-intro`'s social-
  factor framing, since dialectology's feature-bundle concept builds directly on that foundational,
  non-monolithic view of language variation.
- If the student conflates "the boundaries are gradual and dialects are feature bundles" with "dialect
  distinctions aren't real or meaningful at all," clarify that gradual, bundle-based variation is
  still genuinely systematic and describable — the nuance is about HOW dialects work, not whether they
  exist.
- Students who enjoy maps or geography may find the isogloss concept especially engaging — consider
  drawing on real dialect survey maps (e.g., well-known regional vocabulary surveys) as concrete,
  motivating illustrations if available.
