## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-PHRASE-IS-JUST-ANY-GROUP-OF-WORDS
- **Trigger signal:** Student treats any random string of consecutive words as a "phrase," without recognizing that a genuine grammatical phrase must function as a single unified UNIT (acting as one part of speech — noun, verb, adjective, adverb — within the larger sentence) built around a HEAD word, rather than being an arbitrary word grouping.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said 'dog the barked at' from the sentence 'The dog barked at the mailman' is a phrase. Does this group of words function as ONE unified unit doing a single job in the sentence, the way 'the dog' (a noun phrase) or 'barked at' does?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "A grammatical phrase is a group of related words that function TOGETHER as a single unit (acting as a noun, verb, adjective, or adverb within the sentence), built around a HEAD word that the rest of the phrase modifies or complements — not just any consecutive string of words. 'The dog' is a noun phrase (head: dog); 'barked loudly' is a verb phrase (head: barked); but a random scrambled sequence isn't a phrase at all."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Check whether a word group functions as one unified grammatical unit built around a head word — not just whether the words happen to sit next to each other."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "the tall building" (genuine noun phrase — head "building," modified by "the" and "tall," functions as one unit) vs. an arbitrary scrambled sequence with no unified function.
  - "very quickly" (genuine adverb phrase — head "quickly," modified by "very") vs. a random non-functional word string.
- **s6_path:** "Since 'phrase' in everyday language often just means 'a few words,' it's very natural to expect any word grouping to count — but the grammatical definition specifically requires functional unity around a head word."

### MC-PHRASES-AND-CLAUSES-ARE-THE-SAME-THING
- **Trigger signal:** Student conflates phrases (word groups WITHOUT a subject-verb pair, functioning as a single part of speech) with clauses (word groups WITH a subject-verb pair, capable of expressing a complete or partial proposition), missing the defining structural difference — the presence or absence of a subject-verb relationship (e.g., "in the morning" is a phrase — no subject-verb pair; "when the sun rises" is a clause — contains the subject "sun" and verb "rises").
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You called 'in the morning' a clause. Does it have a subject doing an action (a subject-verb pair), the way 'when the sun rises' does? What's the key difference?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Phrases are word groups WITHOUT a subject-verb pair, functioning as a single grammatical unit ('in the morning' — a prepositional phrase, no subject or verb). Clauses are word groups WITH a subject-verb pair ('when the sun rises' — 'sun' is the subject, 'rises' is the verb), capable of forming at least a partial proposition. This subject-verb presence/absence is THE defining structural difference between the two categories."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Check for the presence of a subject-verb pair — its absence means a phrase, its presence means a clause — as the defining test."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "in the morning" (phrase — no subject-verb pair) vs. "when the sun rises" (clause — "sun" + "rises" is a subject-verb pair).
  - "running quickly" (phrase — no subject/verb pair, just a participial phrase) vs. "because she was running" (clause — "she" + "was running" is a subject-verb pair).
- **s6_path:** "Both phrases and clauses are 'groups of words smaller than a full sentence,' which makes them easy to conflate — but checking specifically for a subject-verb pair reliably distinguishes the two categories."

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The phrase-unit blocks**

> Present a sentence broken into individual word cards. Physically group related word cards into "unit blocks" with a rubber band or box — "the tall building" becomes one block (noun phrase), "walked quickly" becomes another (verb phrase). Discuss the HEAD word of each block (the core word the others modify). Then present a contrasting subject-verb pair ("when it rained") that is NOT groupable the same way, since it has its own internal subject-verb structure (a clause, not a phrase).

---

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Noun Phrases [C]**

Present sentences with noun phrases (the tall building, my best friend); student identifies the phrase, its head noun, and its modifiers.

**TA-2 — Verb Phrases [C]**

Present sentences with verb phrases (has been running, will finish soon); student identifies the phrase and its head verb, directly targeting MC-A-PHRASE-IS-JUST-ANY-GROUP-OF-WORDS by requiring identification of the unifying function.

**TA-3 — Prepositional, Adjectival, and Adverbial Phrases [C→P]**

Present sentences with prepositional phrases (in the morning), adjectival phrases (very tall), and adverbial phrases (quite quickly); student identifies each phrase type and its function.

**TA-4 — Distinguishing Phrases from Clauses [P]**

Present a mixed set of phrases and clauses; student applies the subject-verb-pair test to correctly classify each, directly targeting MC-PHRASES-AND-CLAUSES-ARE-THE-SAME-THING.

**TA-5 — Identifying Phrases in Extended Text [P]**

Student reads a longer passage and identifies multiple phrase types (noun, verb, prepositional, adjectival, adverbial), integrating all prior categories into a realistic reading context.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — noun phrase)**

> "In 'The old house creaked,' identify the noun phrase and its head."

Target response: "The old house" — head noun "house," modified by "the" and "old."

**WE-2 (Foundational — verb phrase)**

> "In 'She has been studying,' identify the verb phrase and its head."

Target response: "has been studying" — head verb "studying," with helping verbs "has" and "been."

**WE-3 (Intermediate — phrase vs. clause)**

> "Is 'after the game' a phrase or a clause? What about 'after the game ended'?"

Target response: "after the game" is a phrase (no subject-verb pair); "after the game ended" is a clause ("game" + "ended" is a subject-verb pair).

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Noun phrase, novel sentence**
"In 'My favorite book fell,' identify the noun phrase and its head."
*Target:* "My favorite book" — head noun "book."

**MP-2 [P36] — Verb phrase, novel sentence**
"In 'They will have finished by noon,' identify the verb phrase and its head."
*Target:* "will have finished" — head verb "finished."

**MP-3 [P36] — Prepositional phrase, novel sentence**
"In 'The cat slept under the table,' identify the prepositional phrase."
*Target:* "under the table."

**MP-4 [P36] — Phrase vs. clause, novel pair**
"Is 'before dinner' a phrase or a clause? What about 'before we ate dinner'?"
*Target:* "before dinner" is a phrase (no subject-verb pair); "before we ate dinner" is a clause ("we" + "ate" is a subject-verb pair).

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain phrases**
"In your own words: what makes a group of words a genuine grammatical phrase, and how is a phrase different from a clause?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that a phrase is a group of words functioning as one unit around a head word (not just any word grouping), and that phrases lack a subject-verb pair while clauses have one — the defining structural distinction.

**Protocol routing:** S0 (secure on word order, new to phrase-level grammatical analysis): dwell on TA-1/TA-2's most intuitive phrase types (noun, verb) before introducing prepositional/adjectival/adverbial phrases. S1 (already uses phrases fluently in speech/writing but hasn't formally analyzed them): use this concept to build explicit, transferable structural vocabulary for an already-present intuitive skill. S6 (overwhelmed by five distinct phrase types): explicitly organize by function (naming a thing, showing action, showing relationship, describing) rather than presenting all five as an undifferentiated list. S9 (L1 doesn't use a phrase/clause distinction the same way, or has a different internal phrase-structure system): expect this concept, especially the phrase-vs-clause discrimination (TA-4), to require dedicated, explicit contrastive teaching, since this structural analysis skill is foundational to later English clause/sentence-combining work and may not transfer directly.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Head-word unity is the defining test for a genuine phrase**: always require identifying the head word and confirming the group functions as one unit, directly countering the "any word group counts" default.
- **Subject-verb-pair presence/absence is the defining phrase/clause test**: teach this as an explicit, checkable rule, since phrases and clauses are easily conflated as "smaller than a sentence" groupings.
- **Function-based chunking manages the five phrase types' complexity**: organize instruction by function (naming, action, relationship, description) rather than presenting all five types simultaneously.
- **This concept is foundational for later clause/sentence-combining work**: for S9 learners, ensure genuine mastery here, since the upcoming clauses concept builds directly on the phrase/clause distinction established in this concept.
