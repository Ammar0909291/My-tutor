**Concept:** What Is Linguistics? — a survey introduction to linguistics as the scientific,
descriptive study of human language: how it is structured, how it varies, and how it is actually
used, as distinct from prescriptive rules about how language "should" be used.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Sentence Combining (prerequisite) and Prosody
(prerequisite) both give the student hands-on experience manipulating specific structural and sound
features of language. What Is Linguistics? teaches the student to step back and see these as
instances of a broader SCIENTIFIC discipline that studies language descriptively — documenting and
explaining how language actually works and varies, rather than prescribing how it "should" be used.
This concept unlocks `eng.linguistics.phonology-intro` (the systematic study of sound systems) and
`eng.linguistics.morphology-intro` (the systematic study of word structure), both of which require
first understanding linguistics as a descriptive science.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) explain the difference between descriptive
linguistics (documenting how language actually is used and varies) and prescriptive grammar
(rules about how language "should" be used), (b) identify at least three major subfields of
linguistics (e.g., phonetics/phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, sociolinguistics) and briefly
describe what each studies, and (c) explain why linguists study language variation (dialects,
registers, historical change) as legitimate data rather than as "errors" to be corrected.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-LINGUISTICS-IS-THE-STUDY-OF-CORRECT-GRAMMAR-RULES

**Trigger signal:** Student believes linguistics is fundamentally about teaching or enforcing
"correct" grammar rules (prescriptive grammar), rather than recognizing it as a descriptive science
that studies and explains how language actually works and varies, including "non-standard" forms —
e.g., assuming a linguist's job is to correct people's grammar mistakes.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a linguist's actual research question (e.g., studying
why a specific regional dialect consistently uses a particular grammatical construction, and what
that construction reveals about language change over time) alongside a prescriptive grammar rule from
a style guide ("never split an infinitive"). Ask: "Is the linguist's research question trying to
correct the dialect's speakers, or trying to understand and document how their language actually
works?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Linguistics is like biology, not like a rulebook for how animals 'should'
behave — a biologist studying wolf behavior doesn't judge wolves for not behaving like domestic
dogs; they document and explain the behavior that actually occurs. Linguistics studies language the
same way: describing and explaining how people actually use language, including 'non-standard'
dialects and forms, rather than judging or correcting it."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Ask: is this activity DESCRIBING how language actually works and varies
(linguistics), or PRESCRIBING how language should be used according to a style convention
(prescriptive grammar, which is a separate, much narrower activity)? Linguistics as a science is
almost entirely the former."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A linguist documenting and explaining a grammatical pattern in a regional dialect, treating it as
   legitimate data (descriptive) vs. a style guide insisting a specific construction is "incorrect"
   and should be avoided in formal writing (prescriptive).
2. A student assuming linguists correct people's "bad grammar" (misconception) vs. a student
   recognizing that a linguist studying "bad grammar" is actually documenting how that construction
   is used, by whom, and why, without judgment (correct understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues conflating linguistics with prescriptive grammar after the
bridge, have them identify, for three example activities (writing a style guide, studying how
children acquire their first language, correcting a friend's "incorrect" sentence), which are
genuinely linguistic (descriptive/scientific) and which are prescriptive.

### MC-B-LINGUISTICS-IS-JUST-ONE-SINGLE-SUBJECT-ABOUT-GRAMMAR

**Trigger signal:** Student believes linguistics is a single, narrow subject focused only on
grammar rules, unaware that it comprises multiple distinct subfields studying different aspects of
language (sounds, word structure, sentence structure, meaning, social variation, historical change) —
e.g., being surprised that linguistics includes the study of how children acquire language or how
languages change over centuries.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a list of genuinely different linguistic research
questions: "Why do babies universally babble certain sounds before others?" (phonetics/acquisition),
"Why does English form plurals differently for 'cat' and 'ox'?" (morphology), "Why is 'colorless
green ideas sleep furiously' grammatical but meaningless?" (syntax/semantics), "Why do teenagers in
one city develop a distinct vocabulary from adults?" (sociolinguistics). Ask: "Are these all the same
kind of question, or are they studying genuinely different aspects of language?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming linguistics is just 'grammar rules' is like assuming medicine is
just one single subject about broken bones — medicine actually branches into distinct fields
(cardiology, neurology, immunology) that each study a different body system. Linguistics branches
the same way: phonetics/phonology studies sounds, morphology studies word structure, syntax studies
sentence structure, semantics studies meaning, and sociolinguistics studies how social factors shape
language use — each a genuinely distinct area of study."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When you encounter a linguistics topic, ask which subfield it belongs
to: is this about SOUNDS (phonetics/phonology), WORD STRUCTURE (morphology), SENTENCE STRUCTURE
(syntax), MEANING (semantics), or SOCIAL VARIATION (sociolinguistics)? Recognizing these distinct
branches shows linguistics is a broad scientific discipline, not one narrow topic."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A student who thinks all linguistics questions are really "grammar rule" questions (narrow view)
   vs. a student who can correctly sort four different linguistic questions into four different
   subfields (broad, accurate view).
2. Surprise that a "linguistics course" includes a unit on how children learn language or how
   languages change over centuries (narrow expectation) vs. recognizing these as expected, central
   topics within the broader discipline (accurate expectation).

**s6_path:** If the student continues treating linguistics as a single narrow topic after the
bridge, have them sort eight genuinely distinct linguistic research questions into the five major
subfields, reinforcing the breadth of the discipline through direct categorization practice.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.grammar.sentence-combining`):** "Combine these two sentences using an
appropriate connector." A student who cannot perform basic sentence combining lacks hands-on
experience with sentence structure this concept references — route to `eng.grammar.sentence-
combining` review first.

**PD-2 (checks `eng.phonetics.prosody`):** "Identify where the stress falls in this short spoken
sentence." A student who cannot identify basic prosodic features lacks hands-on experience with sound
structure this concept references — route to `eng.phonetics.prosody` review before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Biologist-and-Medical-Specialties Anchor.** Combine two images: a biologist studying wolf
behavior without judgment, documenting what actually occurs rather than what "should" occur —
directly seeding the fix for MC-A (linguistics describes, doesn't prescribe). Second, a diagram of
medical specialties (cardiology, neurology, immunology) branching from one root discipline
(medicine) — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (linguistics similarly branches into distinct
subfields studying different aspects of language, not one narrow "grammar rules" topic).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Descriptive vs. Prescriptive: Linguistics as a Science (MC-A)

Using the biologist anchor, teach the core distinction between descriptive linguistics and
prescriptive grammar. Student practice: given six activities, sort into "descriptive linguistics" and
"prescriptive grammar."

### TA-2 — Language Variation as Legitimate Data, Not Error

Extend TA-1 by teaching that dialects, registers, and language change are treated by linguists as
legitimate, interesting data rather than "incorrect" deviations. Student practice: given three
examples of language variation (a regional dialect feature, a historical sound change, a teen
slang term), explain why a linguist would study each with the same rigor as "standard" forms.

### TA-3 — The Major Subfields of Linguistics (MC-B)

Using the medical-specialties anchor, introduce the major subfields (phonetics/phonology, morphology,
syntax, semantics, sociolinguistics) with a brief description and example question for each. Student
practice: given eight research questions, sort each into its correct subfield.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Analyzing a Language Phenomenon Through a Linguistic Lens

Give the student a real, observable language phenomenon (e.g., how a slang term spreads and changes
meaning, or why a language learner makes a specific systematic "error"). They must explain it using a
descriptive, non-judgmental lens (TA-1, TA-2) and correctly identify which subfield(s) the
phenomenon belongs to (TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Phenomenon: "Some English speakers say 'I seen it' instead of 'I saw it.'"
Fully worked: identified as a systematic, rule-governed dialectal feature (not an "error") — the
speaker is following their dialect's own consistent past-tense rule; classified as belonging to
morphology (verb form) and sociolinguistics (social/regional variation); framed descriptively
("linguists would study which communities use this form, how consistently, and how it relates to
historical English verb forms") rather than prescriptively ("this is wrong and should be
corrected"). Annotation confirms the descriptive framing (fixing MC-A) and correct subfield
identification (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New phenomenon: "Young children often say 'goed' instead of 'went.'" Student is
prompted with guiding questions ("Is this an error to correct, or does it reveal something
systematic about how children are applying a grammar rule? What does this phenomenon suggest about
how children acquire language rules? Which subfield(s) does this belong to — morphology? language
acquisition, which relates to several subfields?") to complete a descriptive, subfield-identified
explanation.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new language phenomenon, unaided. Student explains it descriptively
(non-judgmentally) and correctly identifies its relevant subfield(s).

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Descriptive vs. Prescriptive Sorting**
Given six activities or statements, sort into "descriptive linguistics" and "prescriptive grammar,"
justifying each classification.

**MP-2 [P36] — Explaining Variation Without Judgment**
Given an example of language variation (a dialect feature, slang, or historical change), explain why
a linguist would study it as legitimate data rather than treating it as an error.

**MP-3 [P36] — Subfield Identification**
Given six linguistic research questions, sort each into its correct subfield (phonetics/phonology,
morphology, syntax, semantics, or sociolinguistics).

**MP-4 [P36] — Distinguishing Subfields on a Shared Topic**
Given one broad topic (e.g., a new slang word), identify at least two different subfields that could
each study a different aspect of it, and briefly describe what each subfield's angle would be.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Descriptive Analysis**
Given a new, real language phenomenon, explain it using a descriptive (non-judgmental) framing and
correctly identify its relevant subfield(s).

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters the descriptive/prescriptive distinction and subfield identification in
  isolation (MP-1 through MP-4) but struggles to apply both simultaneously to a novel phenomenon
  (MP-5), provide a structured checklist (describe without judgment, then identify subfield(s)) as
  scaffolding before removing it.
- If the student has strong prior exposure to prescriptive grammar instruction (e.g., from formal
  grammar drills in earlier concepts), expect more initial resistance to MC-A's reframe — this is a
  genuine paradigm shift from "rules to follow" to "phenomena to study," not just new content, and may
  need more repeated exposure to settle.
  settle.
- If the student conflates "descriptive, non-judgmental" with "anything goes, no communication
  standards exist," clarify that linguistics describing variation neutrally doesn't mean formal
  writing/speaking contexts lack real communicative expectations — these are two separate questions
  (what IS versus what's expected in a given context), paralleling the accents-and-dialects concept's
  prestige-versus-correctness distinction.
- Students who found `eng.phonetics.accents-and-dialects` engaging (if previously taught) often
  transfer well to this concept's non-judgmental stance on variation — this is a natural connection
  point to draw on explicitly if the student completed that concept.
