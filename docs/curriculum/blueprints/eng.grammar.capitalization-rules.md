## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-CAPITALIZE-ANY-IMPORTANT-SOUNDING-WORD
- **Trigger signal:** Student capitalizes common nouns that FEEL important or significant (e.g., "I love my Mom" — capitalizing "Mom" as if it were always a proper name, or "We went to the Beach" — capitalizing "Beach" because the trip felt special), rather than reserving capitalization for genuine PROPER nouns (specific, named individuals/places) as opposed to common nouns (general categories), regardless of how emotionally significant the word feels.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You capitalized 'Beach' in 'We went to the Beach.' Is 'beach' naming one SPECIFIC, particular beach with its own official name (like 'Waikiki Beach'), or is it a general category of place, like 'park' or 'store'?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Capitalization for nouns follows the PROPER vs. COMMON distinction, not emotional importance — proper nouns (specific, named individuals: Sarah, Paris, Monday, the White House) are capitalized; common nouns (general categories: mom, beach, teacher, city), even ones you care about deeply, are NOT capitalized unless they're used as part of a direct address or title-substitute for a specific name ('I love my mom' — common noun, lowercase; 'I love you, Mom' — direct address, capitalized like a name)."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Capitalize based on whether a word names a SPECIFIC, particular individual/place (proper noun) versus a general category (common noun) — not based on how important or significant the word feels emotionally."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "I love my mom" (common noun, general category, lowercase) vs. "I love you, Mom" (direct address, functioning like a name, capitalized).
  - "We went to the beach" (common noun, general category) vs. "We went to Waikiki Beach" (proper noun, specific named place, capitalized).
- **s6_path:** "It's a very natural instinct to capitalize words that feel emotionally significant — but the rule is specifically about proper (specific/named) vs. common (general category) nouns, not about how much you care about the thing."

### MC-CAPITALIZE-ONLY-THE-VERY-FIRST-WORD-OF-A-TEXT
- **Trigger signal:** Student capitalizes the first word of an entire piece of writing but fails to capitalize the first word of EACH new sentence within it, treating capitalization-at-the-start as a one-time, whole-document rule rather than a per-sentence rule that applies at every sentence boundary.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You capitalized the very first word of your paragraph, but not the first word of the second sentence. Does the capitalize-the-first-word rule apply just once for the whole paragraph, or does it apply again every time a NEW sentence starts?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "The first-word-capitalization rule applies at the start of EVERY sentence, not just once at the beginning of an entire text — each new sentence (signaled by the period/question mark/exclamation point ending the previous one) needs its own capitalized first word."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Capitalize the first word after every sentence-ending punctuation mark (period, question mark, exclamation point), not just the very first word of the whole piece of writing."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "The dog barked. It ran to the door." (both sentences correctly capitalized at their start) vs. "The dog barked. it ran to the door." (second sentence incorrectly left lowercase).
  - A three-sentence paragraph with all three sentences correctly capitalized vs. only the very first sentence capitalized.
- **s6_path:** "Since the very first word of a text is the most obvious, prominent capitalization point, it's understandable to focus attention there — but the rule genuinely repeats at every single sentence boundary throughout the whole piece."

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The capitalization checklist walk**

> Present a short paragraph with several sentences and a mix of proper/common nouns. Walk through it sentence by sentence, physically circling the first letter of each new sentence (confirming capitalization at every sentence start, not just the paragraph's start) and separately underlining proper nouns (names, places) that need capitalization regardless of position in the sentence, contrasting with common nouns left lowercase.

---

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Capitalizing the First Word of Every Sentence [C]**

Present multi-sentence passages; student capitalizes the first word of EACH sentence, directly targeting MC-CAPITALIZE-ONLY-THE-VERY-FIRST-WORD-OF-A-TEXT.

**TA-2 — Capitalizing Proper Nouns (Names, Places, Days, Months) [C]**

Present sentences with proper nouns (people's names, cities, countries, days of the week, months); student correctly capitalizes these specific, named entities.

**TA-3 — Distinguishing Proper from Common Nouns [C→P]**

Present pairs contrasting common and proper nouns for similar concepts (mom/Mom, city/Paris, dog/Rex); student correctly capitalizes only the proper noun member, directly targeting MC-CAPITALIZE-ANY-IMPORTANT-SOUNDING-WORD.

**TA-4 — Capitalizing "I" and Titles [P]**

Present sentences using the pronoun "I" (always capitalized regardless of position) and titles (Dr., President, Aunt when used as part of a name); student applies these specific capitalization conventions.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — every sentence's first word)**

> "Capitalize correctly: 'the sun was shining. birds were singing.'"

Target response: "The sun was shining. Birds were singing." — both sentences' first words capitalized.

**WE-2 (Foundational — proper nouns)**

> "Capitalize correctly: 'we visited paris in may.'"

Target response: "We visited Paris in May." — "Paris" (place) and "May" (month) are proper nouns.

**WE-3 (Intermediate — common vs. proper noun)**

> "Which is correct: 'I saw my Teacher' or 'I saw my teacher'? What about 'I saw Mrs. Garcia'?"

Target response: "I saw my teacher" (common noun, lowercase) and "I saw Mrs. Garcia" (proper noun, capitalized) — both correct in their own contexts.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Every sentence's first word, novel passage**
"Capitalize correctly: 'she opened the box. inside was a surprise.'"
*Target:* "She opened the box. Inside was a surprise."

**MP-2 [P36] — Proper nouns, novel example**
"Capitalize correctly: 'my friend maria lives in canada.'"
*Target:* "My friend Maria lives in Canada."

**MP-3 [P36] — Common vs. proper, novel pair**
"Is it 'I love my Dog' or 'I love my dog'? What about 'I love Rex'?"
*Target:* "I love my dog" (common noun) and "I love Rex" (proper noun, the dog's specific name).

**MP-4 [P36] — "I" and titles**
"Capitalize correctly: 'i asked dr. smith a question.'"
*Target:* "I asked Dr. Smith a question." — "I" always capitalized; "Dr." as a title before a name capitalized.

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain capitalization rules**
"In your own words: when do you capitalize a word — does it depend on how important the word feels, or something else? And does the first-word rule apply once per document or more often?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that capitalization depends on whether a word is a proper noun (specific/named) versus a common noun (general category), not emotional importance, and that the first-word rule applies at the start of every sentence, not just once for the whole text.

**Protocol routing:** S0 (secure on sentence-types-by-function, new to capitalization conventions): dwell on TA-1's every-sentence rule before introducing proper/common noun distinctions. S1 (already capitalizes correctly in short writing but misses in longer multi-sentence passages): focus on TA-1's per-sentence application in extended text. S6 (frustrated by wanting to capitalize emotionally significant common nouns like "mom" or "dog"): validate the direct-address exception (capitalized when used AS a name-substitute, e.g., "Thanks, Mom!") to honor the underlying instinct within the correct rule. S9 (L1 has no capitalization system at all, e.g. many non-Latin-script languages, or a different capitalization convention, e.g. German capitalizes all nouns): expect this concept to require genuinely foundational instruction (not just a review), since the entire concept of case-based letter marking may be unfamiliar, or the German-style all-nouns-capitalized pattern may need explicit unlearning for English.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Every-sentence-boundary rule, not once-per-document**: explicitly reinforce that first-word capitalization repeats at every sentence start throughout a passage — the most common early-writer gap.
- **Proper vs. common is the defining test, not emotional significance**: teach this distinction explicitly and repeatedly, since the "important things get capitals" intuition is strong and persistent, especially for beloved people/places/pets.
- **Direct-address exception as a bridge, not a contradiction**: the "Mom"/"mom" distinction (capitalized when used as a name-substitute in direct address, lowercase as a general common noun) validates the underlying instinct while teaching the correct rule.
- **Foundational-level instruction for no-capitalization or all-caps L1 systems**: for S9 learners whose L1 has no case distinction or a different capitalization convention entirely (like German), treat this as needing full foundational teaching or active unlearning, not a quick review.
