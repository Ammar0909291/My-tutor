**Concept:** Distinguishing Sounds in Speech — perceiving fine phonetic contrasts between
similar-sounding words in connected, natural speech, rather than relying on sentence context alone or
assuming isolated-word listening skill transfers automatically to fast connected speech.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Minimal Pairs (prerequisite) taught recognizing the
phonetic contrast between similar-sounding word pairs in careful, isolated pronunciation. Active
Listening (prerequisite) taught sustained, attentive listening. Distinguishing Sounds in Speech
extends both into the harder, more realistic task: actually perceiving those same fine phonetic
contrasts within fast, connected, natural speech, where contrasts can blur or reduce and cannot
always be resolved by context alone. This concept has no further downstream unlocks in the current
curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) distinguish similar-sounding words by actually
attending to their phonetic difference, rather than guessing from sentence context alone, and (b)
recognize that phonetic contrasts audible in careful, isolated pronunciation can blur or reduce in
fast, connected natural speech, requiring dedicated practice beyond the isolated-word skill.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-YOU-CAN-ALWAYS-TELL-SIMILAR-SOUNDING-WORDS-APART-JUST-BY-USING-SENTENCE-CONTEXT

**Trigger signal:** Student relies entirely on sentence context and meaning to guess which of two
similar-sounding words was said, rather than actually attending to the phonetic contrast itself —
working reasonably well when context is unambiguous, but failing whenever context is genuinely
ambiguous or absent, because the ear was never actually trained to hear the sound difference.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present the student with a sentence where context does NOT disambiguate
between two minimal-pair words: "I saw the ship/sheep in the distance" spoken in a context (a foggy
harbor scene) where either word could plausibly fit. Ask: "Can you tell which word was said just from
the sentence's meaning here? If context can't tell you, what's left to rely on?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Relying entirely on context to distinguish similar sounds is like navigating
only by asking other people for directions instead of ever learning to read a map yourself — it works
fine as long as someone's there to help, but falls apart the moment you're in unfamiliar territory
with no one to ask. Context often helps, but a listener who never trains their ear to actually hear
the phonetic contrast has no fallback when context is ambiguous."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't rely only on sentence context to distinguish similar-sounding
words. Train your ear to actually hear the specific phonetic contrast (a vowel length, a consonant
sound) so you can distinguish the words even when context doesn't settle it for you."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "I saw the ship/sheep in the distance." spoken in an ambiguous foggy-harbor context (context
   provides no disambiguation — the listener must rely on the actual sound heard) vs. "I saw the
   sheep grazing in the field." (context clearly disambiguates — but the listener who only ever relies
   on context has no skill to fall back on when it doesn't).
2. A student who guesses correctly only when context happens to make the answer obvious
   (misconception — hasn't actually trained phonetic perception) vs. a student who can identify the
   word correctly even in an ambiguous context by attending to the actual sound (correct, trained
   perception).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to rely purely on context after the bridge, give them three
sentences with genuinely ambiguous context around a minimal pair, and have them identify the word
based on the audio alone, explicitly stating what phonetic feature (not context) told them the
answer.

### MC-B-IF-YOU-CAN-DISTINGUISH-A-MINIMAL-PAIR-IN-CAREFUL-ISOLATED-SPEECH-YOULL-AUTOMATICALLY-DISTINGUISH-IT-IN-FAST-CONNECTED-SPEECH

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes that because they can distinguish a minimal pair (ship/sheep,
would have/would of) when each word is pronounced carefully and in isolation, they will automatically
be able to distinguish the same pair in fast, connected, natural speech — failing to recognize that
phonetic contrasts can blur, reduce, or merge under natural speech's faster pace and connected
pronunciation, requiring separate, dedicated practice beyond the isolated-word skill.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Play (or present as description) the same minimal pair first spoken
carefully in isolation (clearly distinguishable) and then embedded in fast, natural connected speech
(where the contrast noticeably reduces or blurs, such as "would have" sounding close to "would of" in
casual speech). Ask: "Could you tell the words apart in the careful, isolated version? Is it as easy
in the fast, connected version? What changed?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming isolated-word skill transfers automatically to connected speech is
like assuming that because you can identify a single musical note played slowly and clearly, you can
automatically pick out that same note within a fast, complex piece of music — the skill of isolated
identification doesn't automatically include the skill of picking the same thing out amid speed and
context. Connected natural speech genuinely reduces and blurs contrasts that are clear in careful,
isolated pronunciation."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't assume that distinguishing a minimal pair in careful, isolated
speech means you can distinguish it in fast, connected speech too. Practice listening for the same
contrast specifically within natural, connected speech, where the sounds may blur or reduce compared
to careful pronunciation."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Ship" and "sheep" spoken carefully and separately (contrast clearly audible) vs. the same words
   embedded in fast, natural connected speech (contrast noticeably reduced or blurred).
2. A student who assumes their strong isolated-minimal-pair performance guarantees strong connected-
   speech performance (misconception) vs. a student who recognizes connected speech requires separate
   practice because the contrast behaves differently there (correct recognition).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to assume automatic transfer after the bridge, give them the
same minimal pair first in isolated form (which they distinguish correctly) and then in fast
connected speech (where they struggle), and have them explicitly describe what specifically became
harder to hear in the connected version.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.phonetics.minimal-pairs`):** "Distinguish this minimal pair spoken carefully in
isolation (e.g., 'ship' vs. 'sheep')." A student who cannot distinguish minimal pairs even in careful,
isolated speech lacks the foundation for the harder connected-speech task — route to
`eng.phonetics.minimal-pairs` review first.

**PD-2 (checks `eng.listening.active-listening`):** "Demonstrate sustained, attentive listening to
this short spoken passage by summarizing its content." A student who cannot sustain attentive
listening lacks the foundation for the focused perceptual attention this concept requires — route to
`eng.listening.active-listening` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Map-Reading-vs-Asking-for-Directions and Isolated-Note-vs-Fast-Music-Piece Anchor.** Navigating
only by asking others for directions instead of ever learning to read a map — directly seeding the
fix for MC-A (relying purely on context leaves no fallback when context is ambiguous; the ear must be
trained on the actual sound). Second, identifying a single musical note played slowly vs. picking the
same note out of a fast, complex piece — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (isolated-word skill
doesn't automatically transfer to connected, natural speech).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Attending to Phonetic Contrast Instead of Context Alone (MC-A)

Using the map-reading anchor, teach identifying the specific phonetic feature (vowel length,
consonant sound) that distinguishes a minimal pair, independent of context. Student practice: given a
sentence with ambiguous context around a minimal pair, identify the word from the audio alone,
stating the specific phonetic feature that gave it away.

### TA-2 — Recognizing Reduction in Connected Speech (MC-B)

Using the isolated-note-vs-fast-music anchor, teach that contrasts audible in isolation can blur in
connected speech. Student practice: given the same minimal pair first isolated then embedded in
connected speech, describe what became harder to hear in the connected version.

### TA-3 — Targeted Listening Practice for Connected Speech

Extend TA-2: practice distinguishing minimal pairs specifically within fast, connected sentences,
building the connected-speech-specific skill directly. Student practice: given five sentences with
embedded minimal pairs in connected speech, identify the correct word in each.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Distinguishing Sounds in a New Connected Passage

Give the student a new short passage of natural connected speech containing two or three minimal-pair
contrasts. They must identify each correct word using phonetic attention rather than context alone
(TA-1), applying connected-speech-specific listening practice (TA-2/TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A short passage containing the minimal pair "would have"/"would of" in fast
casual speech, fully worked: the isolated-form contrast confirmed as clear; the connected-speech form
identified as blurred, with the specific reduced feature named; correct identification made using
the residual phonetic cue rather than guessing from context, since the context in this passage is
genuinely ambiguous. Annotation confirms both the phonetic-attention-over-context principle (fixing
MC-A) and the connected-speech-reduction recognition (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new passage with a different minimal pair embedded in connected speech. Student
is prompted with guiding questions ("Does the context here actually tell you which word was said, or
do you need to rely on the sound itself? Does this pair sound as distinguishable here as it would in
careful, isolated speech?") to complete the identification.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new passage of connected speech with embedded minimal pairs, unaided.
Student identifies each word correctly using trained phonetic attention.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying a Word from Phonetic Contrast in Ambiguous Context**
Given a sentence with ambiguous context around a minimal pair, identify the word from the audio
alone, stating the specific phonetic feature that gave it away.

**MP-2 [P36] — Describing Contrast Reduction in Connected Speech**
Given the same minimal pair first isolated then embedded in connected speech, describe what became
harder to hear in the connected version.

**MP-3 [P36] — Distinguishing Embedded Minimal Pairs in Connected Sentences**
Given five sentences with embedded minimal pairs in connected speech, identify the correct word in
each.

**MP-4 [P36] — Explaining Why Isolated Skill Doesn't Automatically Transfer**
Given a student's strong isolated-pair performance paired with weak connected-speech performance,
explain why the two skills are not automatically the same.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Connected-Speech Sound Discrimination**
Given a new short passage of natural connected speech containing two or three minimal-pair
contrasts, identify each correct word.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a gap in either routes back to the
relevant prerequisite. During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during
TA-1 (relying on context alone) and MC-B during TA-2/TA-3 (assuming automatic transfer to connected
speech) — these are largely independent and often need separate, explicit correction. If either
fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route
by failure: MP-1 failure indicates residual MC-A; MP-2/MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B;
MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided
full-passage example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly identifies words in ambiguous-context sentences with clear audio (MP-1)
  but reverts to context-guessing on fast connected speech (MP-5), provide a mandatory "what did I
  actually hear, not what makes sense" checkpoint before answering.
- If the student's isolated minimal-pair recognition is strong (from prerequisite) but connected-
  speech performance (MP-3) lags significantly, provide slowed-down connected-speech audio as an
  intermediate scaffolding step before full-speed practice.
- If the student struggles to describe what specifically reduces in connected speech, provide a
  reference list of common connected-speech reduction patterns (contractions, blended consonants) as
  a guide.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.listening.active-listening`'s sustained attention should
  transfer readily to the focused perceptual attention this concept requires, since both demand
  resisting the pull toward passive, surface-level listening — draw this parallel explicitly if
  useful.
