**Concept:** Run-On Sentences and Comma Splices — errors where two independent clauses (each capable
of standing as its own complete sentence) are incorrectly joined without proper punctuation or a
conjunction (a true run-on), or joined with only a comma and no conjunction (a comma splice), and the
strategies for correcting each.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Compound Sentences (prerequisite) taught the CORRECT ways
to join independent clauses (a comma plus a coordinating conjunction, a semicolon, or a period).
Comma Usage (prerequisite) taught precise comma rules generally. This concept teaches recognizing the
specific ERRORS that occur when independent clauses are joined incorrectly — either with no
punctuation/conjunction at all (a run-on) or with only a comma and no conjunction (a comma splice) —
and applying the correct fix for each. This concept has no further downstream unlocks in the current
curriculum but is a foundational error-recognition skill for clear writing.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) identify a true run-on sentence (two independent
clauses joined with no punctuation or conjunction at all), (b) identify a comma splice (two
independent clauses joined with only a comma, no conjunction), correctly distinguishing it from a
properly formed compound sentence, and (c) correct each error using an appropriate strategy (adding a
conjunction, replacing the comma with a semicolon, or splitting into two separate sentences).

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-A-COMMA-SPLICE-IS-JUST-AS-CORRECT-AS-A-COMPOUND-SENTENCE-SINCE-BOTH-USE-A-COMMA

**Trigger signal:** Student believes that placing a comma between two independent clauses is
sufficient on its own to correctly join them, not recognizing that a comma ALONE (with no
coordinating conjunction like "and," "but," or "so" following it) creates a comma splice — an error —
rather than a correctly formed compound sentence, which requires the comma AND a conjunction together.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two sentences: "The rain stopped, we went outside."
(comma splice — comma alone, no conjunction) and "The rain stopped, and we went outside." (correct
compound sentence — comma plus the conjunction "and"). Ask: "Both use a comma between two complete
ideas. Is the comma alone actually enough to correctly join them, or is something else required?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming a comma alone can correctly join two independent clauses is like
assuming a single link of chain, with no connecting clasp, can hold two heavy objects together — the
comma is doing some of the connecting work, but on its own, without the 'clasp' of a coordinating
conjunction (and, but, so, or, nor, for, yet), the connection isn't actually secure. Two independent
clauses need the comma PLUS a conjunction together, not the comma alone."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Never join two independent clauses with a comma alone. Always add a
coordinating conjunction (and, but, so, or, nor, for, yet) after the comma, or use a different fix
(a semicolon, or splitting into two sentences) instead."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "The rain stopped, we went outside." (comma splice — comma alone, an error) vs. "The rain stopped,
   and we went outside." (correct compound sentence — comma plus conjunction).
2. A student who believes any comma between two complete ideas is automatically correct
   (misconception) vs. a student who checks specifically for a coordinating conjunction following the
   comma before accepting the sentence as correctly formed (correct, precise understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to accept comma splices as correct after the bridge, have them
take five comma-spliced sentences and, for each, identify the missing conjunction that would need to
be added directly after the comma to correctly fix it.

### MC-B-THE-ONLY-WAY-TO-FIX-A-RUN-ON-OR-COMMA-SPLICE-IS-TO-SPLIT-IT-INTO-TWO-SEPARATE-SENTENCES

**Trigger signal:** Student, upon identifying a run-on sentence or comma splice, always defaults to
the single fix of splitting the two clauses into two completely separate sentences, missing that
there are multiple equally valid fixes (adding a coordinating conjunction after a comma, using a
semicolon, or splitting into two sentences) that may suit the specific context and desired sentence
rhythm differently.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a run-on sentence ("The rain stopped we went outside")
alongside THREE different valid fixes: splitting into two sentences ("The rain stopped. We went
outside."), adding a comma and conjunction ("The rain stopped, and we went outside."), and using a
semicolon ("The rain stopped; we went outside."). Ask: "Are all three of these valid, correct fixes?
Do they all read exactly the same, or does each create a slightly different rhythm or emphasis?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming splitting into two sentences is the only fix is like assuming there's
only one way to repair a torn seam in fabric — sewing, using fabric glue, or applying a patch might all
genuinely fix the tear, each with a slightly different look and durability. A run-on or comma splice
can be fixed by splitting into two sentences, adding a conjunction after a comma, or using a semicolon
— all valid, with the best choice depending on the specific rhythm and relationship you want to convey
between the two ideas."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When fixing a run-on or comma splice, consider all three valid options —
splitting into two sentences, adding a comma plus conjunction, or using a semicolon — and choose based
on which best fits the relationship between the two ideas and the rhythm you want, rather than
automatically defaulting to just one fix every time."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Always splitting every run-on/splice into two separate sentences regardless of context (rigid,
   single-strategy approach) vs. considering all three valid fixes (splitting, comma-plus-
   conjunction, semicolon) and choosing the one that best fits the specific sentence's relationship and
   rhythm (flexible, context-aware approach).
2. A student who cannot generate any fix for a run-on other than splitting it (limited toolkit) vs. a
   student who can generate and compare all three valid fix options for the same run-on (full toolkit,
   flexible application).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to default only to splitting after the bridge, have them take
three run-on sentences and generate ALL THREE valid fixes for each, then choose which fix they think
reads best for that specific sentence and explain why.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.grammar.compound-sentences`):** "Correctly join these two independent clauses
into a compound sentence." A student who cannot form a basic compound sentence lacks the foundation
this concept builds on — route to `eng.grammar.compound-sentences` review first.

**PD-2 (checks `eng.grammar.comma-usage`):** "Explain when a comma alone is sufficient to join two
independent clauses." A student who believes a comma alone is ever sufficient needs
`eng.grammar.comma-usage` reviewed before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Chain-Clasp and Torn-Seam-Repair Anchor.** Combine two images: a chain link with no connecting
clasp, unable to securely hold two heavy objects together on its own — directly seeding the fix for
MC-A (a comma alone is insufficient; it needs the conjunction "clasp" to securely join independent
clauses). Second, a torn fabric seam repairable by sewing, fabric glue, or a patch, each a valid fix
with a different look — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (multiple valid fix strategies exist for
run-ons/splices, not just one).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Identifying True Run-Ons (No Punctuation at All)

Introduce the true run-on: two independent clauses joined with absolutely no punctuation or
conjunction. Student practice: given four sentences, identify which are true run-ons.

### TA-2 — Identifying Comma Splices (MC-A)

Using the chain-clasp anchor, teach students to identify comma splices — a comma alone joining two
independent clauses with no conjunction. Student practice: given four sentences (mixing comma
splices and correct compound sentences), identify which are comma splices.

### TA-3 — The Three Valid Fix Strategies (MC-B)

Using the torn-seam anchor, teach the three valid fixes: splitting into two sentences, adding a
comma-plus-conjunction, and using a semicolon. Student practice: given three run-ons/splices, generate
all three valid fixes for each and compare their rhythm and effect.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Identifying and Correcting Errors in Context

Give the student a short passage containing a mix of true run-ons, comma splices, and correctly formed
sentences. They must identify each error (TA-1, TA-2) and correct each using the most contextually
appropriate fix from the three valid strategies (TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Passage: "The test was hard I still passed it. The teacher was pleased, she
gave us extra credit." Fully worked: first sentence identified as a true run-on (no punctuation at
all); second identified as a comma splice (comma alone, no conjunction); both corrected using
different, contextually appropriate fixes (the run-on split into "The test was hard, but I still
passed it." using a comma-plus-conjunction to show contrast; the splice fixed with a semicolon: "The
teacher was pleased; she gave us extra credit." to show a closely related consequence). Annotation
confirms both error types were correctly distinguished (fixing MC-A for the splice) and different valid
fix strategies were applied based on context (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New passage with a true run-on and a comma splice. Student is prompted with guiding
questions ("Does this sentence have any punctuation or conjunction joining the two complete ideas, or
is it missing entirely — a true run-on? Does this other sentence have a comma but no conjunction — a
splice? What are all three possible fixes for each, and which fits the relationship between the ideas
best?") to identify and correct both errors.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new short passage containing a mix of true run-ons, comma splices, and
correct sentences, unaided. Student identifies and corrects each error using an appropriate,
contextually justified fix.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying True Run-Ons**
Given three sentences, identify which are true run-ons (no punctuation or conjunction joining two
independent clauses).

**MP-2 [P36] — Identifying Comma Splices**
Given three sentences, identify which are comma splices (comma alone, no conjunction) as distinct
from correctly formed compound sentences.

**MP-3 [P36] — Generating Multiple Valid Fixes**
Given one run-on or comma splice, generate all three valid fixes (splitting, comma-plus-conjunction,
semicolon) and briefly explain the different effect of each.

**MP-4 [P36] — Choosing the Most Appropriate Fix**
Given a run-on or comma splice in context, select and justify the fix (from the three valid options)
that best matches the relationship between the two ideas.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Passage Correction**
Given a new short passage containing a mix of true run-ons, comma splices, and correct sentences,
identify and correct each error using an appropriate, contextually justified fix.

residual MC-A; MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing
indicates an integration gap across error types and fix strategies — provide one more guided full-
passage example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters each error type and fix strategy in isolation (MP-1 through MP-4) but
  reverts to errors when all types are mixed together (MP-5), provide a structured two-step checklist
  (identify the error type, then select among the three valid fixes) as scaffolding during mixed
  practice.
- If the student's own writing shows a pattern of comma splices specifically (a very common real-
  world error even among otherwise skilled writers), flag this for targeted, ongoing attention in
  future writing assignments beyond this concept's mastery probes.
- If the student always chooses the semicolon fix regardless of whether the relationship between
  ideas actually suits a semicolon's typically close, parallel connection, clarify that semicolons
  work best for closely related independent clauses — very different ideas may read more naturally
  simply split into two sentences.
- Students with a strong ear for spoken sentence rhythm may intuitively sense when a sentence "runs
  together" awkwardly even before formally identifying the grammatical structure — allow this
  intuition as a valid entry point, then connect it to the formal subject/verb/independent-clause
  analysis for precision.
