**Concept:** Research Paper Writing — producing an extended, evidence-based paper that synthesizes
multiple sources around an original thesis, rather than merely reporting what each source
individually says.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Citations and Referencing (prerequisite) teaches
acknowledging individual sources correctly. Rhetorical Analysis (prerequisite) teaches evaluating how
a single text achieves its effect. Research Paper Writing teaches the culminating skill: combining
MULTIPLE sources' ideas, evaluated critically, into an original argument that is the student's OWN
synthesis, not a strung-together sequence of source summaries. This concept unlocks
`eng.composition.academic-writing-conventions` (the formal conventions of academic prose apply most
fully in the extended, source-based writing this concept produces).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) formulate an original, arguable thesis that is not
simply borrowed from a single source, (b) organize a paper by IDEA or SUB-ARGUMENT rather than by
source-by-source summary, integrating multiple sources within each section to support each point, and
(c) synthesize sources that agree, disagree, or partially overlap into a coherent account that
advances the student's own original argument.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-A-RESEARCH-PAPER-IS-A-SEQUENCE-OF-SOURCE-SUMMARIES

**Trigger signal:** Student organizes their paper source-by-source ("Source A says X. Source B says
Y. Source C says Z.") rather than idea-by-idea, producing a paper that reads as a series of book
reports strung together rather than a unified argument that draws on multiple sources within each
point.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two paper outlines on the same research question
(the effects of screen time on adolescent sleep): one organized source-by-source (paragraph 1
summarizes Source A entirely, paragraph 2 summarizes Source B entirely, paragraph 3 summarizes
Source C entirely), and one organized by sub-argument (paragraph 1 uses evidence from Sources A and C
together to establish the physiological mechanism; paragraph 2 uses Source B alongside a rebuttal
from Source A to address a complicating factor). Ask: "Which paper is making its OWN argument, and
which is just reporting what each source said in turn?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Organizing by source is like writing a book report for each of three
witnesses in a trial, one at a time, and never actually building a case — a lawyer instead organizes
by ARGUMENT, pulling relevant testimony from whichever witnesses support each specific point, in
whatever order the case needs. A research paper needs the same idea-first structure: organize by
YOUR points, and pull in whichever sources support (or complicate) each one."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before writing, outline your paper by SUB-ARGUMENT or IDEA, not by
source. For each section, ask: which of my sources have something relevant to say about THIS
specific point — not 'what does this source say overall,' but 'what does this source contribute to
this specific piece of my argument?'"

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. An outline with headings "Source A," "Source B," "Source C" (source-organized, book-report
   structure) vs. an outline with headings "The physiological mechanism," "The behavioral factor,"
   "The counterargument and response" (idea-organized, argument structure).
2. A paragraph that fully summarizes one source before moving to the next (sequential source
   reporting) vs. a paragraph that weaves two sources together to build one point, citing each where
   relevant (synthesized, idea-driven writing).

**s6_path:** If the student continues organizing source-by-source after the bridge, have them
physically create a table with sources as COLUMNS and their own sub-arguments as ROWS, filling in
which source contributes to which row — this literally requires them to think in terms of ideas the
sources serve rather than the sources themselves as the organizing unit.

### MC-B-SYNTHESIZING-SOURCES-MEANS-ONLY-USING-SOURCES-THAT-AGREE

**Trigger signal:** Student selects and cites only sources that agree with their thesis, ignoring or
omitting sources that complicate, partially contradict, or offer a different angle on the question —
believing that acknowledging disagreement among sources would weaken their own argument, rather than
recognizing that addressing genuine complexity strengthens credibility.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two paper excerpts on the same claim: one that cites
only agreeing sources and presents the question as if there were no complexity or disagreement in the
research, and one that acknowledges a source presenting a complicating finding, then explains how the
student's argument accounts for or responds to that complication. Ask: "Which paper seems more
credible and more like it has actually engaged with the full body of research, rather than
cherry-picking only what supports one side?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Citing only sources that agree with you is like a doctor only reading test
results that confirm their initial guess and ignoring the ones that don't fit — it feels safer in the
moment, but it produces a less accurate, less trustworthy diagnosis. A truly strong research paper
engages with the FULL, sometimes messy picture the sources present, and shows why your argument still
holds even accounting for the complications."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "As you research, deliberately look for sources that complicate, qualify,
or partially disagree with your thesis — and address them directly in your paper, either by showing
why the disagreement doesn't undermine your overall point or by refining your thesis to account for
it. Omitting inconvenient evidence doesn't strengthen an argument; it just makes it fragile once a
reader finds the missing piece."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A paper that presents a claim as if universally supported by all research, citing only agreeing
   sources (selective, fragile) vs. a paper that acknowledges a genuine complicating finding and
   explains how the overall argument accounts for it (engaged, credible).
2. A student choosing to simply not cite a source they found because it complicated their thesis
   (avoidance) vs. a student citing that same source and using a limited concession plus a
   qualification to strengthen the overall argument's precision (genuine synthesis).

**s6_path:** If the student continues omitting complicating sources after the bridge, require them
to find and cite at least one source that complicates or partially disagrees with their thesis as an
explicit, non-negotiable step in their research process, before drafting any section of the paper.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.writing.citations-and-referencing`):** "Produce a correctly formatted in-text
citation and reference entry for this source." A student who cannot cite correctly lacks the
mechanical foundation this concept assumes — route to `eng.writing.citations-and-referencing` review
first.

**PD-2 (checks `eng.composition.rhetorical-analysis`):** "Evaluate whether this short source passage
is a credible, well-reasoned piece of evidence." A student who cannot perform basic source evaluation
needs `eng.composition.rhetorical-analysis` reviewed before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Trial-Lawyer Anchor.** Present a simplified courtroom scenario: three witnesses each have
partial, sometimes overlapping, sometimes conflicting testimony about an event. A lawyer building a
case doesn't call each witness up to give a complete, standalone monologue in turn (source-by-source)
— instead, the lawyer organizes the closing argument by CLAIM, drawing on whichever witness's
testimony supports each specific claim, and directly addresses any witness whose testimony
complicates the case. This directly seeds fixes for both misconceptions: it shows that a strong case
is organized by argument, not by witness (fixing MC-A), and that a good lawyer addresses
inconvenient testimony directly rather than pretending it doesn't exist, since ignoring it leaves the
case vulnerable to being undermined later (fixing MC-B).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Formulating an Original, Arguable Thesis from Multiple Sources

Teach students to develop a thesis that emerges from considering several sources together, not
borrowed wholesale from one source. Student practice: given three source summaries on one research
question, draft an original thesis that isn't simply restating any single source's conclusion.

### TA-2 — Outlining by Idea, Not by Source (MC-A)

Using the witness-testimony table method, teach students to build an idea-first outline. Student
practice: given four sources and three candidate sub-arguments, build a source-by-idea table showing
which sources support which sub-argument.

### TA-3 — Synthesizing Multiple Sources Within One Paragraph

Teach the mechanics of weaving two or more sources together within a single paragraph to support one
point, rather than summarizing each source in its own paragraph. Student practice: given two source
excerpts supporting the same sub-argument, write one synthesized paragraph using both.

### TA-4 — Engaging Complicating or Disagreeing Sources (MC-B)

Using the doctor's-test-results bridge text, teach students to actively seek out and address sources
that complicate their thesis. Student practice: given a thesis and a complicating source, write a
paragraph that acknowledges the complication and explains how the overall argument accounts for it.

### TA-5 — Full Research Paper Section Construction

Give the student a research question, a thesis, and five sources (a mix of supporting and
complicating). They must build an idea-organized outline, write at least one fully synthesized
paragraph combining multiple sources, and explicitly address at least one complicating source — the
full synthesis task at a section, not full-paper, scale given the time constraint.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Research question: "Does homework improve academic achievement in middle
school?" Fully worked: an original thesis synthesizing three sources ("homework's benefit depends
heavily on quality and quantity, not presence alone"); an idea-organized outline (quality factors,
quantity thresholds, the counterargument that any homework helps); one fully synthesized paragraph
combining two sources on the quality factor; one paragraph directly addressing a complicating source
that found homework has no measurable effect, explaining that this source measured only quantity, not
quality, which the thesis specifically distinguishes. Annotation confirms the idea-first structure
(fixing MC-A) and the direct engagement with complicating evidence (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New research question ("Is remote learning as effective as in-person instruction?")
with four sources supplied. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("What thesis could you form
that isn't just repeating any one source? Which sources support which parts of that thesis — build
the table. Is there a source here that complicates your thesis, and how would you address it rather
than ignore it?") to complete the outline and one synthesized, complication-engaging paragraph.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new research question with five sources, unaided. Student formulates an
original thesis, builds an idea-organized outline, writes one fully synthesized paragraph, and
explicitly addresses at least one complicating source.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Original Thesis Formulation**
Given three source summaries on a research question, draft an original thesis that synthesizes them
rather than restating any single source.

**MP-2 [P36] — Idea-Organized Outlining**
Given four sources and three candidate sub-arguments, build a table or outline organized by idea,
showing which sources support which sub-argument.

**MP-3 [P36] — Synthesizing Multiple Sources in One Paragraph**
Given two source excerpts supporting the same point, write one paragraph that weaves both together
rather than summarizing each separately.

**MP-4 [P36] — Engaging a Complicating Source**
Given a thesis and a source that complicates or partially disagrees with it, write a paragraph that
acknowledges the complication and explains how the argument accounts for it.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Section Construction**
Given a new research question, a thesis, and five sources (mixed supporting and complicating),
produce an idea-organized outline, one fully synthesized paragraph, and one paragraph directly
engaging a complicating source.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a gap in either prerequisite routes
back before Component 3. During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during
TA-2 (outlining) and MC-B during TA-4 (complicating sources) — these are largely independent failure
modes and can surface in either order depending on the student's research habits. If either fires,
branch to the relevant s6_path drill before resuming. Given this concept's length (4 hours) and
expert difficulty, monitor cognitive load carefully across TAs — this is an appropriate concept to
split across multiple sessions if fatigue signals appear, resuming at the next TA rather than
restarting. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure indicates the thesis-
formulation step (TA-1) needs review; MP-2 failure alone indicates residual MC-A; MP-3 failure alone
indicates the synthesis-mechanics step (TA-3), distinct from either misconception; MP-4 failure alone
indicates residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap at
scale — provide one more fully-guided section-construction example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- Given the 4-hour length and expert difficulty of this concept, actively monitor for fatigue or
  cognitive overload across the TA sequence — this is an explicitly appropriate concept to split
  across two or more sessions, resuming at the next TA with a brief review of the prior session's
  work rather than restarting from TA-1.
- If the student can synthesize sources correctly at the paragraph level (MP-3) but their full
  section (MP-5) reverts to source-by-source organization under the added complexity, treat this as
  a load-related regression, not a conceptual gap — provide one additional worked example at full
  section scale before re-attempting independent work.
- If the student's complicating-source paragraphs (MP-4) devolve into fully abandoning the original
  thesis rather than refining or qualifying it, clarify that engaging a complication can mean
  narrowing or qualifying a thesis's scope, not necessarily abandoning the argument altogether.
- Students with strong single-source analytical skills (from Rhetorical Analysis) but weak
  multi-source integration should be given explicit practice with the source-by-idea table (TA-2) as
  a mandatory planning artifact for several subsequent assignments, since this is a genuinely new
  cognitive demand beyond single-text analysis.
