**Concept:** Reading Across Genres — synthesizing understanding across multiple texts and genres on
a related topic or theme, integrating insights into one unified understanding rather than merely
summarizing each text separately or treating genre difference as a barrier to connection.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Evaluating Sources (prerequisite) taught assessing a
single source's credibility. Compare and Contrast Texts (prerequisite) taught comparing two texts
directly. Reading Across Genres extends both into true synthesis: combining understanding from
several texts — which may be in entirely different genres (a poem, a news article, a scientific
report) on the same topic or theme — into one integrated understanding, rather than either
summarizing each separately or assuming genre difference prevents meaningful connection. This
concept has no further downstream unlocks in the current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) genuinely synthesize insights across multiple
texts into one unified understanding, rather than producing separate summaries placed side by side,
and (b) connect meaningfully related content across texts in different genres, rather than assuming
genre difference is a barrier to synthesis.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-SYNTHESIZING-MULTIPLE-TEXTS-MEANS-SUMMARIZING-EACH-ONE-SEPARATELY-BACK-TO-BACK

**Trigger signal:** Student "synthesizes" multiple texts by producing a separate summary of each
text placed one after another, never actually integrating or connecting the texts' insights into one
unified understanding — the same block-structure problem as simple text comparison, but at the level
of genuine cross-text synthesis, where the actual integrating work never happens on the page.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a "synthesis" consisting of three separate paragraph
summaries (one per text, in isolation, with no connecting statements) next to a genuine synthesis
paragraph that draws on all three texts together to make one integrated point ("all three sources
agree that X causes Y, though the poem frames it emotionally while the report frames it statistically").
Ask: "Does the first version ever combine the three texts' insights into one idea? Does the second?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Summarizing each text separately and calling it synthesis is like laying
three separate photographs side by side and calling it a single portrait — the photographs are all
present, but nothing has actually been combined into one unified image. Genuine synthesis integrates
insights from multiple texts into one connected understanding, not three separate summaries placed
next to each other."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't summarize each text separately and stop there. After
understanding each text individually, ask: what do these texts, taken together, tell me that no
single one tells me alone? Write statements that draw on multiple texts at once, not one summary per
text."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Three separate paragraph summaries with no connecting statement between them (summaries placed
   side by side, no genuine synthesis) vs. one integrated paragraph drawing on all three sources
   together to make a combined point (genuine synthesis).
2. A student whose "synthesis" essay could be split into three independent, unconnected sections
   (misconception) vs. a student whose synthesis explicitly states what multiple sources reveal
   together (correct synthesis).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to produce separate summaries after the bridge, have them take
their own separate-summary draft and, for one specific claim, identify what all three texts
collectively suggest about it, writing one integrated sentence naming all three sources together.

### MC-B-TEXTS-IN-DIFFERENT-GENRES-CANNOT-BE-MEANINGFULLY-CONNECTED-OR-SYNTHESIZED

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes that because texts belong to different genres (a poem and a news
report, a short story and a scientific article), their content cannot be meaningfully connected or
synthesized, treating genre difference itself as a barrier to synthesis rather than recognizing that
texts in very different genres can address the same underlying topic or theme and be integrated into
one unified understanding.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a poem about the emotional experience of drought and a
scientific report on drought's measurable environmental effects. Ask: "Are these texts in the same
genre? Do they address a genuinely related topic? Could understanding the emotional experience help
you understand why the measurable effects matter to the people experiencing them, or are the two
texts simply unrelated because their genres differ?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming different genres can't be synthesized is like assuming a
photograph and a written description of the same event have nothing to do with each other because one
is a picture and one is words — different forms of the same underlying subject can absolutely inform
and connect to each other. Genre is the FORM a text takes, not necessarily a wall around its content
— a poem and a report can both illuminate the same real topic from different angles."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't assume texts in different genres can't be connected. Ask: what
underlying topic or theme do these texts share, regardless of their different forms? Look for how
each genre's distinct approach (emotional, factual, narrative) illuminates a different facet of the
same shared subject."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A poem about the emotional experience of drought and a scientific report on drought's measurable
   effects (different genres, but a genuinely shared underlying topic that can be synthesized) vs.
   two completely unrelated texts that happen to be in the same genre (same genre, no shared topic to
   synthesize).
2. A student who assumes a poem and a report "can't go together" in a synthesis essay simply because
   of genre difference (misconception) vs. a student who identifies the shared topic and integrates
   both genres' distinct perspectives on it (correct cross-genre synthesis).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to treat genre difference as a barrier after the bridge, give
them two texts in clearly different genres sharing an underlying topic, and have them state
explicitly what shared topic connects the two despite their different forms, then write one
sentence integrating both.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.reading.evaluating-sources`):** "Assess the credibility of this source and
explain your reasoning." A student who cannot evaluate a single source's credibility lacks the
foundation for weighing multiple sources within a synthesis — route to `eng.reading.evaluating-sources`
review first.

**PD-2 (checks `eng.reading.compare-and-contrast-texts`):** "Identify one similarity and one
difference between these two texts." A student who cannot directly compare two texts lacks the
foundation for the more complex multi-text, multi-genre synthesis this concept requires — route to
`eng.reading.compare-and-contrast-texts` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Three-Separate-Photographs-vs-Single-Portrait and Photograph-vs-Written-Description Anchor.**
Three separate photographs laid side by side, never combined into one unified portrait — directly
seeding the fix for MC-A (genuine synthesis integrates insights, not just places summaries
side-by-side). Second, a photograph and a written description of the same event, different forms
but genuinely connected — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (different genres can inform and connect
to the same shared underlying topic).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Understanding Each Text Individually First

Teach establishing a solid individual understanding of each text before attempting synthesis.
Student practice: given three short texts on a related theme, summarize each one's main point
individually.

### TA-2 — Genuine Synthesis vs. Separate Summaries (MC-A)

Using the photographs-vs-portrait anchor, teach integrating insights across texts into one unified
statement. Student practice: given three individually-summarized texts, write one integrated
sentence drawing on all three together.

### TA-3 — Connecting Texts Across Different Genres (MC-B)

Using the photograph-vs-written-description anchor, teach identifying a shared underlying topic
across texts in different genres. Student practice: given a poem and a report on a related topic,
identify the shared topic and write one sentence integrating both.

### TA-4 — Identifying Points of Agreement and Tension Across Sources

Extend TA-2/TA-3: teach identifying where multiple synthesized sources agree, and where they offer
different or even conflicting perspectives on the same shared topic. Student practice: given three
sources on one topic, identify one point of agreement and one point of differing perspective.

### TA-5 — Full Application: Synthesizing Across Multiple Genres

Give the student three new texts in different genres on a shared topic. They must understand each
individually (TA-1), integrate their insights genuinely (TA-2), connect across the genre differences
(TA-3), and identify agreement/tension across the sources (TA-4) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Three texts on a shared theme (the value of community, presented as a short
story, a persuasive op-ed, and a historical account), fully worked: each summarized individually;
one integrated synthesis sentence written drawing on all three; the shared theme identified across
genres despite very different forms; one point of agreement (community support matters in crisis)
and one point of differing perspective (the story emphasizes individual bonds, the historical account
emphasizes institutional structures) both identified. Annotation confirms both the genuine-synthesis
principle (fixing MC-A) and the cross-genre-connection principle (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new set of three texts in different genres. Student is prompted with guiding
questions ("What does each text say individually? What do all three, taken together, reveal that no
single one reveals alone? What underlying topic connects them despite their different genres?") to
complete the synthesis.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new set of texts in different genres, unaided. Student synthesizes across
all texts, integrating insights and connecting across genre differences.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Summarizing Multiple Texts Individually**
Given three short texts on a related theme, summarize each one's main point individually.

**MP-2 [P36] — Writing a Genuinely Integrated Synthesis Statement**
Given three individually-summarized texts, write one integrated sentence drawing on all three
together.

**MP-3 [P36] — Connecting Texts Across Genre Difference**
Given a poem and a report on a related topic, identify the shared underlying topic and write one
sentence integrating both.

**MP-4 [P36] — Identifying Agreement and Tension Across Sources**
Given three sources on one topic, identify one point of agreement and one point of differing
perspective across them.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Cross-Genre Synthesis**
Given three new texts in different genres on a shared topic, produce a genuine synthesis integrating
insights, connecting across genres, and identifying agreement and tension.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a gap in either routes back to the
relevant prerequisite. During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during
TA-2 (separate summaries mistaken for synthesis) and MC-B during TA-3 (treating genre difference as a
barrier) — these are largely independent and often need separate, explicit correction. If either
fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route
by failure: MP-1/MP-2 failures indicate residual MC-A; MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B;
MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided
full cross-genre example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly integrates provided summaries (MP-2) but reverts to separate summaries on
  a new original set of texts (MP-5), provide a mandatory "what do all of these say TOGETHER that
  none says alone?" prompt before finalizing any synthesis.
- If the student's within-genre synthesis is strong (MP-2) but cross-genre synthesis (MP-3) stalls,
  require an explicit "what underlying topic connects these despite their different forms?" question
  before attempting to connect any two differently-formed texts.
- If the student struggles to identify agreement/tension across sources at all, provide a two-column
  scaffold ("where do these agree?" / "where do these differ?") before free synthesis writing.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.reading.compare-and-contrast-texts`'s two-text comparison
  should transfer readily to multi-text, multi-genre synthesis, since both require holding more than
  one text's content in mind simultaneously — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
