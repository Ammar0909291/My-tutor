**Concept:** Critical Listening — evaluating a speaker's argument, evidence, and possible bias while
listening, rather than simply following what is being said.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Listening Comprehension Strategies (prerequisite)
taught understanding the content of spoken language accurately. Critical Listening extends this from
understanding WHAT is said to evaluating HOW WELL it holds up: is the argument well-supported, is the
evidence credible, and is the speaker's confident delivery being mistaken for the actual strength of
their case. This concept has no further downstream unlocks in the current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) evaluate the strength of a spoken argument
independently of the speaker's delivery confidence or fluency, and (b) distinguish credible,
well-supported evidence from unsupported claims or weak anecdotal evidence within a spoken argument.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-A-CONFIDENT-FLUENT-SPEAKER-IS-MAKING-A-STRONGER-ARGUMENT

**Trigger signal:** Student judges an argument's quality by the speaker's confidence, fluency, or
delivery polish rather than by the actual logical strength and evidentiary support of what is being
said, mistaking eloquence for correctness — a confidently-delivered weak argument is rated as
stronger than a hesitant but well-supported one.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Play (or present as transcript) two short arguments on the same topic:
one delivered with confident, fluent, persuasive-sounding language but resting on a single unsupported
personal anecdote, and one delivered more hesitantly but citing a specific study with named
methodology. Ask: "Which speaker sounded more convincing? Which speaker's argument is actually better
supported by evidence?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Judging an argument by how confidently it's delivered is like judging a
bridge's safety by how shiny its paint job is — the paint tells you nothing about whether the
structure underneath can hold weight. A confident, fluent delivery can wrap around a weak,
unsupported argument, and a hesitant delivery can carry a well-supported one. Evaluate the structure
(the evidence and reasoning), not the paint (the delivery)."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't rate an argument's quality by how confidently or fluently it's
delivered. Ask separately: what evidence is actually being offered, and does the reasoning connect
that evidence to the conclusion? A polished delivery and a strong argument are two different things
that don't always travel together."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A confidently, fluently delivered argument resting on one unsupported personal anecdote (polished
   delivery, weak evidentiary support) vs. a more hesitant argument citing a specific named study
   (less polished delivery, strong evidentiary support).
2. A student who rates the confident speaker as "more convincing" without examining the evidence
   (misconception) vs. a student who separately evaluates delivery and evidentiary strength, and
   rates the argument by the evidence (correct critical-listening discipline).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to conflate confidence with correctness after the bridge, give
them a confidently-delivered weak argument and a hesitantly-delivered strong one, and have them list
the actual evidence offered in each, independent of delivery style, before judging which argument is
better supported.

### MC-B-ANY-EVIDENCE-A-SPEAKER-MENTIONS-PROVES-THEIR-POINT-EQUALLY-WELL

**Trigger signal:** Student treats any evidence a speaker cites as equally strong support for their
claim, failing to evaluate the credibility of the source, the relevance of the evidence to the
specific claim, or whether it is a single unsupported anecdote versus a well-documented, credible
source — accepting "they cited something" as proof the point is established.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present two spoken claims each with cited "evidence": one citing "my
neighbor told me this happened to them" as support for a broad general claim, and one citing a
named, peer-reviewed study with a clear sample size as support for a similar claim. Ask: "Are both of
these equally strong evidence for their claims? What's different about the credibility of each
source?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Treating any mentioned evidence as equally strong is like treating a single
handwritten note and an official notarized document as equally valid proof of the same claim — both
are technically 'evidence,' but they carry very different weight. A single anecdote from one person
is far weaker evidence than a documented, credible, relevant source — evaluate the source and
relevance, not just whether something was cited at all."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When a speaker cites evidence, don't just note that evidence was
mentioned — ask: how credible is this source? Is it a single anecdote or a documented study? Is it
actually relevant to the specific claim being made? Weigh the evidence's quality, not just its
presence."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "My neighbor told me this happened to them." (a single unsupported anecdote, weak evidence for a
   broad claim) vs. "A peer-reviewed study of 500 participants found..." (a credible, documented,
   relevant source, strong evidence).
2. A student who accepts any cited evidence as equally proving the point (misconception) vs. a
   student who evaluates each piece of cited evidence for source credibility and relevance before
   weighing how much it actually supports the claim (correct discipline).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to treat all cited evidence as equally strong after the
bridge, give them a spoken argument citing three different pieces of "evidence" of varying quality
(an anecdote, an unnamed "expert," and a specific documented study) and have them rank the three by
credibility and relevance to the claim.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.listening.listening-comprehension-strategies`):** "Summarize the main claim and
one supporting point of this short spoken passage." A student who cannot accurately follow the
content of spoken language lacks the foundation to evaluate it critically — route to
`eng.listening.listening-comprehension-strategies` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Shiny-Paint-Bridge and Notarized-Document-vs-Handwritten-Note Anchor.** A bridge judged safe
purely by its shiny paint job, with no attention to the structure underneath — directly seeding the
fix for MC-A (a confident delivery is the "paint," not the argument's actual structural strength).
Second, a single handwritten note treated as equally valid proof as an official notarized document —
directly seeding the fix for MC-B (not all cited evidence carries equal weight; source credibility
and relevance must be evaluated).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Separating Delivery Confidence from Argument Strength (MC-A)

Using the shiny-paint-bridge anchor, teach evaluating an argument's actual evidence and reasoning
independent of delivery polish. Student practice: given a confidently-delivered weak argument and a
hesitantly-delivered strong one, list the actual evidence in each before judging.

### TA-2 — Evaluating Source Credibility (MC-B)

Using the notarized-document anchor, teach assessing whether cited evidence comes from a credible,
documented source or an unsupported anecdote. Student practice: given three pieces of cited evidence
of varying quality, rank them by credibility.

### TA-3 — Evaluating Relevance of Evidence to the Specific Claim

Extend TA-2: teach checking whether cited evidence, even if credible, actually supports the specific
claim being made (rather than a related but different point). Student practice: given a claim and a
piece of credible but only tangentially relevant evidence, explain the mismatch.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Critically Evaluating a Spoken Argument

Give the student a new short spoken argument (as audio or transcript) citing two or three pieces of
evidence. They must evaluate the argument's strength independent of delivery (TA-1), assess each
piece of evidence's credibility (TA-2), and check its relevance to the specific claim (TA-3) — the
full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A transcript of a confidently-delivered argument citing one weak anecdote and
one irrelevant-but-credible statistic, fully worked: delivery confidence explicitly set aside; each
piece of evidence assessed for credibility and relevance; overall argument strength judged as weak
despite the polished delivery. Annotation confirms both the delivery-vs-strength separation (fixing
MC-A) and the credibility/relevance evaluation (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new transcript with a different mix of delivery style and evidence quality.
Student is prompted with guiding questions ("Is this speaker convincing because of what they're
actually saying, or how confidently they're saying it? Is each piece of cited evidence a credible,
documented source or something weaker? Does the evidence actually support this specific claim?") to
complete the evaluation.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new spoken argument, unaided. Student evaluates argument strength
independent of delivery and assesses each piece of cited evidence for credibility and relevance.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Separating Delivery from Argument Strength**
Given a confidently-delivered weak argument and a hesitantly-delivered strong one, identify which
argument is actually better supported, independent of delivery style.

**MP-2 [P36] — Ranking Evidence by Source Credibility**
Given three pieces of cited evidence of varying quality, rank them by credibility.

**MP-3 [P36] — Evaluating Evidence Relevance**
Given a claim and a piece of credible but only tangentially relevant evidence, explain why the
evidence does not fully support the specific claim.

**MP-4 [P36] — Detecting the Eloquence-Equals-Truth Trap**
Given a transcript of a highly polished but evidence-thin argument, identify specifically what
evidence is missing despite the confident delivery.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Critical Evaluation of a Spoken Argument**
Given a new spoken argument citing two or three pieces of evidence, evaluate its overall strength,
assessing delivery independence, evidence credibility, and evidence relevance.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite.
During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1 (rating confident
delivery as stronger) and MC-B during TA-2/TA-3 (accepting any cited evidence uncritically) — these
are largely independent and often need separate, explicit correction. If either fires, branch to the
relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1/MP-4
failures indicate residual MC-A; MP-2/MP-3 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1
through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-argument example
before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly separates delivery from strength on provided contrastive pairs (MP-1) but
  reverts to trusting a confident speaker on a new, unscaffolded argument (MP-5), provide a mandatory
  "list the actual evidence first, before rating convincingness" step until the habit is automatic.
- If the student's credibility ranking is strong (MP-2) but relevance evaluation (MP-3) remains weak,
  require an explicit "does this evidence address this specific claim, or a different one?" check
  before accepting any piece of evidence as support.
- If the student struggles to evaluate source credibility at all, provide a starter credibility
  hierarchy (documented study > named expert testimony > single anecdote) as a scaffolding reference.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.speaking.debate-skills`'s evidence-evaluation work should
  transfer readily to critical listening, since both require assessing argument and evidence quality
  independent of presentation — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
