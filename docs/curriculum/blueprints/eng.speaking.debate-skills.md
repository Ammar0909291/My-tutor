**Concept:** Debate Skills — constructing and ORALLY defending an argument, and rebutting an opposing
position, within a structured FORMAT with defined roles, time limits, and turn order, requiring active,
in-the-moment listening to the opponent's actual case rather than delivering a pre-scripted response
regardless of what the opponent says.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Discussion Skills (prerequisite) taught collaborative,
open-ended spoken exchange. Presentation Skills (prerequisite) taught structured, prepared spoken
delivery. Debate Skills combines and extends both into a genuinely different format: a competitive,
structured exchange where success requires actively listening to and directly engaging with the
opponent's SPECIFIC, actual arguments in real time — not simply delivering prepared remarks regardless
of what the other side says. This concept has no further downstream unlocks in the current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) construct a clear, well-supported opening argument
suited to a structured debate format's specific constraints (time limit, defined role), (b) actively
listen to an opponent's specific argument during a debate and construct a rebuttal that directly
engages with what was ACTUALLY said, rather than delivering a pre-planned response regardless of the
opponent's actual case, and (c) maintain a respectful, professional tone even when directly and firmly
challenging an opponent's argument, distinguishing forceful argumentation from personal hostility.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-WINNING-A-DEBATE-MEANS-DELIVERING-YOUR-PREPARED-ARGUMENTS-REGARDLESS-OF-WHAT-THE-OPPONENT-ACTUALLY-SAYS

**Trigger signal:** Student prepares a fixed script of arguments and points before a debate and
delivers them regardless of what the opponent actually argues during the debate, failing to actively
LISTEN to the opponent's specific case and directly engage with it — producing a rebuttal that
addresses points the opponent never actually made, or ignores points they specifically did make,
because the student is following a pre-planned script rather than responding to the live exchange.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a debate transcript where one speaker's "rebuttal"
clearly ignores the specific points the opponent just made, instead delivering pre-planned remarks
that don't actually engage with what was said, alongside a rebuttal that directly quotes and responds
to the opponent's SPECIFIC argument. Ask: "Does the first rebuttal actually respond to what the
opponent said, or does it sound like it would be delivered the same way regardless of the opponent's
actual case? Which rebuttal would a judge find more persuasive?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Delivering a fixed script regardless of what the opponent says is like a
tennis player swinging at the exact same spot every time regardless of where the ball actually lands —
technically executing a shot, but completely disconnected from the actual, live exchange happening in
front of them. A debate rebuttal has to directly respond to the SPECIFIC argument the opponent just
made, requiring active, in-the-moment listening, not a pre-planned script executed regardless of the
opponent's actual case."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "During a debate, actively listen to your opponent's SPECIFIC argument as
they make it, and construct your rebuttal to directly engage with what they ACTUALLY said — not a
pre-planned response that would work regardless of their actual case. Preparation should inform your
flexibility, not replace your listening."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A rebuttal that addresses a point the opponent never actually made, following a pre-planned script
   (disconnected from the live exchange) vs. a rebuttal that directly quotes and responds to the
   opponent's specific argument just made (engaged, responsive rebuttal).
2. A student who prepares only fixed remarks and delivers them regardless of the opposing argument
   (misconception) vs. a student who prepares flexible content but actively adapts their specific
   rebuttal based on what the opponent actually argues (correct, responsive practice).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to deliver pre-planned rebuttals regardless of the opponent's
actual case after the bridge, have them practice with a partner: the partner delivers an argument, and
the student must construct a rebuttal that begins by accurately restating the SPECIFIC point just made
before responding to it, making the direct engagement structurally required.

### MC-B-BEING-FORCEFUL-OR-DIRECTLY-CHALLENGING-AN-OPPONENTS-ARGUMENT-MEANS-BEING-DISRESPECTFUL-OR-HOSTILE

**Trigger signal:** Student conflates being FORCEFUL and directly challenging in argumentation
(firmly disagreeing with and directly refuting an opponent's specific claims) with being personally
disrespectful or hostile toward the opponent, either avoiding direct, forceful engagement out of fear
of seeming rude, or conversely becoming genuinely personally hostile when trying to argue forcefully —
failing to recognize that forceful, direct argumentative disagreement and personal respect are fully
compatible and both expected in a well-conducted debate.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two debate rebuttals disagreeing forcefully with the
same opposing argument: one that directly and firmly refutes the argument's reasoning while
maintaining respectful, professional language ("The evidence my opponent cites doesn't actually support
their conclusion, because..."), and one that becomes personally hostile or dismissive toward the
opponent as a person ("My opponent clearly doesn't understand basic logic..."). Ask: "Is the first
version forceful and direct? Is it personally disrespectful? Can you be firmly, directly disagreeing
with an argument WITHOUT being hostile toward the person making it?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Confusing forceful argumentation with personal hostility is like confusing a
skilled boxer's powerful, direct punches (within the rules, aimed at winning the match) with an
unsportsmanlike attack outside the ring — both involve real force and directness, but one operates
within respectful, professional bounds targeting the ARGUMENT, while the other targets the PERSON. A
debate rewards forceful, direct engagement with an opponent's REASONING, while personal hostility
toward the opponent themselves is neither necessary nor appropriate."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Be as forceful and direct as needed in challenging your opponent's
ARGUMENT and REASONING — this is expected and rewarded in debate. But always keep your language
targeted at the argument, not the person, maintaining respectful, professional tone even during firm
disagreement."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "The evidence my opponent cites doesn't actually support their conclusion, because..." (forceful,
   direct, targeting the argument, respectful) vs. "My opponent clearly doesn't understand basic
   logic." (personally dismissive, targeting the person, disrespectful).
2. A student who softens or avoids directly challenging a flawed argument out of fear of seeming rude
   (under-forceful, missing legitimate argumentative opportunities) vs. a student who firmly and
   directly refutes the argument's specific flaws while maintaining respectful language throughout
   (correct, both forceful and respectful).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to conflate forcefulness with hostility (in either direction)
after the bridge, have them take three example rebuttals varying in both forcefulness and
personal-hostility level, and independently rate each on both dimensions separately, confirming that
high forcefulness and low hostility can coexist.

**PD-2 (checks `eng.speaking.presentation-skills`):** "Deliver a brief, well-structured spoken

lacks the foundation this concept builds on — route to `eng.speaking.presentation-skills` review
before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Tennis-Swinging-at-Fixed-Spot and In-Ring-Boxing-vs-Outside-the-Ring Anchor.** Combine two
images: a tennis player swinging at a fixed spot regardless of where the ball actually lands,
completely disconnected from the live exchange — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (rebuttals must
directly respond to the opponent's specific, actual argument, not a pre-planned script). Second, a
skilled boxer's powerful, direct punches within the rules and ring versus an unsportsmanlike attack
outside them — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (forceful, direct argumentative engagement and
personal respect are fully compatible).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Constructing a Clear Opening Argument for a Structured Format

Teach students to build a well-supported opening argument suited to a debate format's specific
constraints (time limit, defined role). Student practice: given a debate topic and format constraints,
construct a brief opening argument.

### TA-2 — Active Listening and Direct Engagement in Rebuttal (MC-A)

Using the tennis-swinging anchor, teach students to construct rebuttals that directly engage with the
opponent's specific, actual argument. Student practice: given a recorded or transcribed opposing
argument, construct a rebuttal that begins by accurately restating the specific point before
responding to it.

### TA-3 — Forceful Argumentation Without Personal Hostility (MC-B)

Using the boxing-ring anchor, teach students to be direct and forceful while maintaining respectful,
argument-focused language. Student practice: given three rebuttal drafts (some hostile, some
under-forceful, one correctly forceful-and-respectful), identify and revise any hostile or
under-forceful examples.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Conducting a Structured Debate Exchange

Give the student a debate scenario with an assigned position and a partner's (or provided) opposing
argument. They must construct an opening argument (TA-1), listen to and directly rebut the specific
opposing points (TA-2), and maintain forceful but respectful language throughout (TA-3) — the full
synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Topic: "Should school days start later?" Fully worked: an opening argument
constructed within a stated time constraint, citing one piece of evidence; an opposing argument
provided (citing a specific concern about after-school activity scheduling); a rebuttal constructed
that begins by accurately restating this specific concern before directly addressing it with a
counter-point, using firm but respectful language throughout ("My opponent raises a real concern about
activity scheduling, but the evidence shows..."). Annotation confirms both the direct-engagement
rebuttal structure (fixing MC-A) and the forceful-yet-respectful tone (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New topic and a new opposing argument. Student is prompted with guiding questions
("What SPECIFIC point did the opponent just make — restate it accurately before responding. How can
you directly and firmly challenge this specific point while keeping your language focused on the
argument, not the person?") to construct the rebuttal.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new debate topic and opposing argument, unaided. Student constructs an
opening argument, a direct, engaged rebuttal, and maintains forceful-but-respectful language throughout.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Constructing a Structured Opening Argument**
Given a debate topic and time constraint, construct a brief, well-supported opening argument.

**MP-2 [P36] — Restating Before Rebutting**
Given an opposing argument, accurately restate its specific point before constructing a rebuttal to
it.

**MP-3 [P36] — Detecting Disconnected Rebuttals**
Given a rebuttal that ignores or misrepresents the opponent's actual argument, identify the
disconnection and revise the rebuttal to directly engage with what was actually said.

**MP-4 [P36] — Distinguishing Forcefulness from Hostility**
Given three rebuttal drafts varying in forcefulness and personal-hostility level, rate each on both
dimensions separately and identify which is correctly forceful-yet-respectful.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Debate Exchange**
Given a new debate topic and opposing argument, construct an opening argument, a direct and engaged
rebuttal, and maintain forceful-but-respectful language throughout.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a gap in either prerequisite routes
back before Component 3. During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during
TA-2 (scripted, disconnected rebuttals) and MC-B during TA-3 (either avoiding forceful engagement or
becoming hostile) — these are largely independent and often need separate, explicit correction. If
either fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9),
route by failure: MP-1 failure indicates the basic opening-argument construction (TA-1) needs review,
distinct from either misconception; MP-2/MP-3 failures indicate residual MC-A; MP-4 failure alone
indicates residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap at
full-exchange scale — provide one more guided debate exchange before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters isolated rebuttal-construction and tone-calibration (MP-2, MP-4) but reverts
  to scripted or hostile responses under the real-time pressure of a live debate exchange (MP-5),
  provide additional practice specifically under time pressure, since the skills may be secure in
  untimed analysis but fragile under live performance conditions.
- If the student is naturally conflict-avoidant and consistently under-forceful even after correction,
  provide explicit reassurance and modeling that firm, direct argumentative disagreement is expected
  and valued in this format, distinct from their personal communication style in other contexts.
- If the student is naturally combative and slips into personal hostility even after correction,
  provide a concrete self-check habit (before speaking, silently confirm the response targets the
  argument's content, not the opponent's character) as an ongoing practice tool.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.listening.critical-listening` should transfer the
  evaluative-listening skills readily to this concept's active-engagement requirement — draw this
  cross-link connection explicitly if that concept has been completed.
