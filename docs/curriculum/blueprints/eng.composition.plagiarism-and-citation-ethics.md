**Concept:** Plagiarism and Citation Ethics — the ethical and academic-integrity principles governing
proper attribution of others' ideas and words, distinguishing genuine plagiarism from honest citation
mistakes, and understanding WHY proper attribution matters beyond just following a formatting rule.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Academic Writing Conventions (prerequisite) taught the
formal register and mechanics of academic prose, including citation basics. Plagiarism and Citation
Ethics teaches the deeper ETHICAL dimension: understanding plagiarism as a genuine integrity violation
(misrepresenting someone else's intellectual work as one's own), while also recognizing that not every
citation slip is equally serious — distinguishing minor, honest formatting errors from genuine,
intentional misrepresentation. This concept has no further downstream unlocks in the current
curriculum but completes the citation-ethics foundation for all academic writing.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) explain why plagiarism is considered a genuine
ethical violation (misrepresenting someone else's intellectual work as one's own) rather than merely a
technical rule violation, (b) distinguish plagiarism (a genuine misrepresentation of authorship) from
an honest citation formatting error (a genuine attempt to attribute correctly, but with a minor
technical mistake), and (c) identify self-plagiarism (resubmitting one's own previous work as if it
were new, without disclosure) as also a genuine integrity concern in most academic contexts.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-PLAGIARISM-IS-JUST-A-TECHNICAL-FORMATTING-RULE-NOT-A-REAL-ETHICAL-ISSUE

**Trigger signal:** Student treats plagiarism as an arbitrary academic formatting rule to follow
(like a specific citation style's exact punctuation) rather than recognizing it as a genuine ethical
violation — misrepresenting someone else's ideas, research, or words as one's own, which is a form of
dishonesty with real consequences for the original author and for the integrity of the shared
academic/intellectual record.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two scenarios: a student submitting an essay entirely
copied from an online source with no attribution, presented as their own original analysis, alongside
a scenario of a business person taking credit for a colleague's original idea in a meeting without
acknowledgment. Ask: "Is the academic case fundamentally different in KIND from the business case, or
is it the same underlying ethical issue (taking credit for someone else's work) just occurring in a
different context?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Treating plagiarism as just a formatting technicality is like treating
signing someone else's name on a check as just a technical paperwork issue — but it's not really about
the SIGNATURE'S formatting, it's about falsely claiming ownership of something that isn't yours.
Plagiarism is the same underlying ethical violation as taking credit for someone else's work in any
context — it just happens to occur in academic writing."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Understand plagiarism as a genuine ethical issue — misrepresenting
someone else's ideas or words as your own — not merely a technical citation-formatting rule. The harm
is real: it deprives the original author of credit and undermines the honesty of the shared
intellectual record."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A student viewing plagiarism as just "forgetting to add a citation," a minor technical slip with
   no real ethical weight (misconception) vs. a student recognizing that submitting someone else's
   work as your own is a genuine act of taking credit for something you didn't do (correct, ethically
   grounded understanding).
2. Comparing academic plagiarism to a business person taking credit for a colleague's idea and seeing
   them as unrelated issues (missing the underlying parallel) vs. recognizing both as the same
   underlying ethical violation of misrepresenting authorship, occurring in different contexts
   (correct, principle-level understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to view plagiarism as merely technical after the bridge, have
them articulate, in their own words, who is specifically harmed when a piece of writing is plagiarized
(the original author, whose work goes uncredited; the reader, who is misled about the source of the
ideas; the broader academic community, whose trust in attributed work is undermined).

### MC-B-ANY-CITATION-MISTAKE-EVEN-A-MINOR-FORMATTING-ERROR-IS-EQUALLY-SERIOUS-AS-DELIBERATE-PLAGIARISM

**Trigger signal:** Student, having learned that plagiarism is a serious ethical violation,
over-generalizes and treats EVERY citation mistake — including minor, honest formatting errors made
while genuinely attempting to attribute a source correctly — as equally serious as deliberate,
uncredited copying, failing to distinguish genuine misrepresentation of authorship from an honest
attempt at attribution that simply contains a technical error.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two scenarios: a student who copies an entire paragraph
from a source with no citation at all, presenting it as their own original writing (genuine
plagiarism), and a student who correctly paraphrases a source's idea, genuinely attempts to cite it,
but makes a minor formatting mistake in the citation format (e.g., a missing page number, or an
incorrect punctuation mark in the citation). Ask: "Are these two situations the same kind of
violation, or does one represent a genuine attempt at honest attribution with a minor technical slip,
while the other represents an actual misrepresentation of authorship?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Treating every citation mistake as equally serious as deliberate plagiarism is
like treating a driver who makes a minor paperwork error on a parking permit the same as a driver who
deliberately steals a car — both involve a car-related 'mistake,' but the intent and the actual harm
are completely different. A genuine, honest attempt to attribute a source, even with a minor technical
error, is fundamentally different from deliberately presenting someone else's work as your own."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When evaluating a citation issue, ask: was there a genuine, honest attempt
to attribute the source, with only a minor technical or formatting slip? Or is there no attempt at
attribution at all, with the source's work presented as if it were the writer's own? These are
different in kind, not just degree, and should be treated differently."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A citation missing a page number, but clearly and correctly crediting the right source and author
   (an honest, minor formatting error) vs. an entire paragraph copied with absolutely no attribution
   at all, presented as original writing (genuine plagiarism, a real misrepresentation).
2. A student who treats a minor citation formatting slip as equally severe as deliberate, uncredited
   copying (over-generalized misconception) vs. a student who recognizes the fundamental difference in
   intent and actual misrepresentation between the two (correct, nuanced understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to conflate minor errors with genuine plagiarism after the
bridge, give them five example citation scenarios (ranging from honest minor formatting slips to
egregious, uncredited copying) and have them rank each by severity, articulating what specifically
makes the more severe cases worse (absence of any genuine attribution attempt, versus a technical
slip within a genuine attempt).

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.composition.academic-writing-conventions`):** "Explain the purpose of a citation

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Signed-Check and Parking-Permit-vs-Stolen-Car Anchor.** Combine two images: someone signing
another person's name on a check, falsely claiming ownership of funds that aren't theirs — directly
seeding the fix for MC-A (plagiarism is fundamentally about false claims of ownership/authorship, not
formatting). Second, a minor parking-permit paperwork error versus an actual stolen car — both
"car-related mistakes" but vastly different in intent and harm — directly seeding the fix for MC-B
(honest citation slips and deliberate uncredited copying are different in kind, not just degree).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Plagiarism as a Genuine Ethical Violation (MC-A)

Using the signed-check anchor, teach that plagiarism is fundamentally about misrepresenting
authorship, a real ethical harm, not an arbitrary formatting rule. Student practice: given three
scenarios (academic and non-academic), identify the shared underlying ethical violation across
contexts.

### TA-2 — Distinguishing Genuine Plagiarism from Honest Citation Errors (MC-B)

Using the parking-permit-vs-stolen-car anchor, teach students to distinguish honest, minor citation
mistakes from genuine, uncredited misrepresentation. Student practice: given five citation scenarios,
rank each by severity and justify the ranking based on presence or absence of a genuine attribution
attempt.

### TA-3 — Self-Plagiarism as a Related Integrity Concern

Introduce self-plagiarism (resubmitting one's own previous work as new, without disclosure) as a
related but distinct integrity concern. Student practice: given three scenarios involving reused
work, identify which constitute self-plagiarism and explain why disclosure would resolve the concern.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Evaluating a Set of Academic Integrity Scenarios

Give the student a new set of scenarios (mixing genuine plagiarism, honest citation errors, and
self-plagiarism cases). They must correctly classify each (TA-1, TA-2, TA-3) and explain the reasoning
behind each classification — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Scenario set: (1) a student copies three paragraphs from a website with no
citation, presenting them as original analysis; (2) a student paraphrases a source correctly and
cites it, but uses the wrong citation style formatting; (3) a student submits the same essay they
wrote for a different class last semester without telling either instructor. Fully worked: scenario 1
identified as genuine plagiarism (no attribution attempt at all, a real misrepresentation of
authorship); scenario 2 identified as an honest, minor citation error (a genuine attribution attempt
exists); scenario 3 identified as self-plagiarism (reusing one's own work without disclosure).
Annotation confirms the ethical-violation framing (fixing MC-A) and the severity distinction (fixing
MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New scenario set with different specific details for each of the three categories.
Student is prompted with guiding questions ("Is there any genuine attempt to attribute the source
here, even if imperfect? Is this presenting someone else's work as the writer's own, or is it the
writer's own work being reused without disclosure?") to classify each scenario correctly.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new set of academic integrity scenarios, unaided. Student classifies each as
genuine plagiarism, an honest citation error, or self-plagiarism, and justifies each classification.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Explaining the Ethical Harm of Plagiarism**
Explain, in the student's own words, who is specifically harmed by plagiarism and why it constitutes a
genuine ethical violation rather than a mere formatting rule.

**MP-2 [P36] — Distinguishing Plagiarism from Honest Citation Errors**
Given two scenarios (one genuine plagiarism, one an honest minor citation error), correctly classify
each and explain the key distinguishing factor.

**MP-3 [P36] — Ranking Citation Scenarios by Severity**
Given four citation scenarios of varying severity, rank them and justify the ranking based on the
presence or absence of a genuine attribution attempt.

**MP-4 [P36] — Identifying Self-Plagiarism**
Given three scenarios involving reused work, identify which constitute self-plagiarism and explain
what disclosure step would resolve the concern.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Scenario Classification**
Given a new set of academic integrity scenarios, classify each as genuine plagiarism, an honest
citation error, or self-plagiarism, and justify each classification.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student's severity-ranking (MP-3) shows genuine understanding but they remain anxious about
  ever making an honest citation mistake, reassure them explicitly that the goal of this concept is
  informed, careful attribution practice, not fear — honest, good-faith effort with an occasional
  minor slip is categorically different from what this concept treats as a real integrity concern.
- If the student conflates group-work collaboration (a legitimate, disclosed collaborative process)
  with plagiarism, clarify that properly disclosed and permitted collaboration is a distinct, non-
  problematic category from misrepresenting SOLE, uncredited authorship.
- If the student's self-plagiarism classifications (MP-4) miss cases where reuse would be genuinely
  fine with simple disclosure (e.g., building on one's own previously graded project with the
  instructor's knowledge), emphasize that disclosure — not avoidance of all reuse — is the resolving
  action.
- Students preparing for or already engaged in original research work should be encouraged to apply
  this concept's distinctions directly to their own citation practices going forward, since the
  ethical stakes become more concrete and personally relevant in genuine research writing.
