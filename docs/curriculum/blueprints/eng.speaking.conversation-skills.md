## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-GOOD-CONVERSATION-MEANS-TALKING-A-LOT
- **Trigger signal:** Student equates conversational skill with volume of speech — talking at length, filling every silence, and treating pauses as failures to be avoided — rather than recognizing turn-taking and balanced participation as the actual measure of a good conversationalist.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Play or describe two short conversation samples: one where Speaker A talks continuously for two minutes without pausing for Speaker B to respond, and one where Speaker A and B exchange shorter turns, each responding to and building on what the other said. Ask which conversation the OTHER person would likely enjoy more and feel more heard in.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "A conversation is a turn-taking activity, like a game of catch — if one person holds the ball the whole time, it stops being a game. Good conversationalists don't talk the most; they create space for both people to contribute roughly equally and build on what the other person says."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Instead of measuring conversation success by how much you said, measure it by whether both people got a fair turn and whether you responded to what the other person actually said, rather than just waiting for your turn to talk about something else."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Volume-based success (talking more/longer signals conversational skill) vs. (b) Turn-balance-based success (roughly equal, responsive turn-taking signals conversational skill, regardless of who talks more in raw word count).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1 re-check, then re-run TA-1's turn-taking observation task before returning to practice.

### MC-SILENCE-IN-A-CONVERSATION-MEANS-SOMETHING-WENT-WRONG
- **Trigger signal:** Student treats any pause or silence during a conversation as an awkward failure requiring an immediate rescue (blurting out a new topic, filling with nervous chatter), rather than recognizing brief natural pauses as a normal, even comfortable, part of real conversation, especially between people who know each other.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Describe a scenario: two friends are talking, one finishes a story, and there's a 3-second pause before the other responds thoughtfully. Ask the student whether something "went wrong" in that pause, then contrast with an example where an awkward silence really does signal a problem (both people have clearly run out of anything to say and seem uncomfortable, avoiding eye contact).
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Not all silence is the same. A short pause while someone thinks about what to say next, or a comfortable quiet moment between people who know each other, is completely normal — it's not a sign of failure. The kind of silence that IS a problem is different: it comes with visible discomfort, avoided eye contact, and no one seeming to know what to do next."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Before rushing to fill a silence, ask: is this a thinking pause or a comfortable moment (normal, leave it alone), or does it come with visible discomfort and a stuck feeling (worth addressing, e.g., with a follow-up question)? Only the second kind needs rescuing."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) All-silence-is-failure (any pause signals something has gone wrong and must be immediately filled) vs. (b) Silence-type discrimination (a thinking/comfortable pause is normal and healthy; only a visibly uncomfortable, stuck silence signals a real problem worth addressing).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1, then re-present the Concrete Anchor's tennis-rally framing with its built-in pause example before continuing to TA-3.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

### PD-1: Pronunciation-in-Conversation Readiness
- **Tests:** Can the student produce clearly intelligible speech at a conversational pace — since turn-taking and topic-maintenance skills are meaningless if the student's speech itself isn't reliably understood by a conversation partner?
- **Probe:** Ask the student to describe their morning routine in 4-5 sentences at a natural conversational pace; assess whether a listener could follow without repeated requests for clarification.
- **Pass condition:** Speech is intelligible with no more than one clarification request needed.
- **Fail routing:** If intelligibility is a persistent problem, route to `eng.speaking.pronunciation-in-conversation` review — conversation skills assume the mechanical speech act is already reliable.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**"The Tennis Rally."** Use a real or imagined tennis-ball exchange: two people hit a ball back and forth, each hit representing one conversational turn. Demonstrate three rally patterns: (1) a healthy rally — the ball goes back and forth roughly evenly, each hit responding to where the last one landed (a good conversation, responsive turn-taking); (2) one player refusing to return the ball, instead holding it and talking about it endlessly while the other stands waiting (the volume-monopoly problem, seeding MC-GOOD-CONVERSATION-MEANS-TALKING-A-LOT's fix); (3) a natural, brief pause between hits while a player considers their next shot — completely normal, not a broken rally (seeding MC-SILENCE-IN-A-CONVERSATION-MEANS-SOMETHING-WENT-WRONG's fix) — versus both players standing frozen, neither reaching for the ball, visibly unsure what to do (a genuinely stalled rally, the real problem case). Have the student physically act out or narrate a short rally exchange, narrating each "hit" as a conversational turn that responds to the previous one.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1: Observing Turn-Balance
Present a transcript or audio sample of a monopolized conversation (one speaker dominating) and a balanced one. Student identifies which is balanced and estimates the turn-count/time ratio in each, directly countering MC-GOOD-CONVERSATION-MEANS-TALKING-A-LOT.

### TA-2: Responsive Turns — Building on What Was Said
Teach the difference between a responsive turn (references or builds on the previous speaker's content: "That reminds me of..." / "How did that make you feel?") and a topic-hijacking turn (ignores what was just said and pivots to an unrelated topic about the listener's own interests). Student practices generating responsive follow-ups to sample statements.

### TA-3: Reading Silence — Thinking Pause vs. Stalled Conversation
Using the discrimination pairs from MC-SILENCE-IN-A-CONVERSATION-MEANS-SOMETHING-WENT-WRONG, present short scenario descriptions of pauses and have the student classify each as "normal/comfortable" or "genuinely stalled — needs a rescue" and, for the stalled case, generate an appropriate rescue move (a follow-up question, a new related topic).

### TA-4: Initiating and Closing Conversations
Teach appropriate conversation openers (context-appropriate greetings, shared-context references) and closers (signaling the conversation is ending without an abrupt cutoff: "Well, I should get going, but it was great catching up!"). Student practices both in role-play scenarios.

### TA-5: Full Conversation Simulation
Student engages in or narrates a full short simulated conversation (5-6 turns) incorporating balanced turn-taking, at least one responsive follow-up, appropriate handling of a natural pause, and an appropriate closing — the generative capstone integrating all four prior TAs.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

### WE-1: Balanced vs. Monopolized Turn-Taking (Scaffolded)
**Sample A (monopolized):** Speaker 1 talks for six sentences about their weekend without pausing; Speaker 2 never gets a turn. **Sample B (balanced):** Speaker 1 and 2 exchange 2-3 sentences each, alternating.
**Walkthrough:** Count turns and estimate speaking time in each sample. Sample A shows a 6:0 imbalance — a monopoly. Sample B shows roughly even alternation — balanced, healthy turn-taking.

### WE-2: Responsive vs. Hijacking Follow-Up (Guided)
**Statement:** "I finally finished reading that mystery novel — the ending completely surprised me."
**Two possible follow-ups:** (a) "Oh, that reminds me, I need to return my library books today." (hijacking — ignores the content, pivots to an unrelated topic) vs. (b) "Really? What was the twist — did you see it coming at all?" (responsive — builds directly on what was said).
**Walkthrough:** Identify (b) as responsive and (a) as a topic hijack, even though (a) is grammatically fine and socially "polite" on the surface.

### WE-3: Pause Classification and Rescue (Independent)
**Scenario 1:** Two old friends finish reminiscing about a shared trip; there's a comfortable 4-second pause before one says, "Anyway, we should plan another one sometime." **Scenario 2:** Two people who just met run out of things to say; both look away, and 8 seconds pass with visible discomfort.
**Walkthrough:** Student classifies Scenario 1 as a normal/comfortable pause (no rescue needed) and Scenario 2 as genuinely stalled, then generates an appropriate rescue move for Scenario 2 (e.g., a new question about a shared context, like the event they're both at).

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identify a turn-monopoly**
Given a conversation sample where one speaker dominates, student identifies the imbalance and explains why it undermines the conversation, regardless of how interesting the content was. *(Directly targets MC-GOOD-CONVERSATION-MEANS-TALKING-A-LOT.)*

**MP-2 [P36] — Classify a pause correctly**
Given two pause scenarios (one comfortable, one genuinely stalled), student correctly classifies each and explains the distinguishing signals (visible discomfort vs. thoughtful quiet). *(Directly targets MC-SILENCE-IN-A-CONVERSATION-MEANS-SOMETHING-WENT-WRONG.)*

**MP-3 [P36] — Generate a responsive follow-up**
Given a statement, student generates a follow-up that builds on the specific content rather than pivoting to an unrelated topic.

**MP-4 [P36] — Initiate and close a conversation appropriately**
Given a scenario (running into an acquaintance), student produces an appropriate opener and, after a short simulated exchange, an appropriate closer.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full conversation simulation**
Student conducts or narrates a 5-6 turn simulated conversation demonstrating balanced turn-taking, at least one responsive follow-up, and an appropriate close.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** At session open (S0), confirm PD-1 passes — conversational turn-taking and responsiveness are meaningless to assess if the student's basic speech intelligibility is unreliable. During instruction (S1), watch for both misconception patterns: a student who dominates practice exchanges (MC-GOOD-CONVERSATION-MEANS-TALKING-A-LOT) or who rushes to fill every pause (MC-SILENCE-IN-A-CONVERSATION-MEANS-SOMETHING-WENT-WRONG) is applying the same underlying "more talking = better" heuristic in two different situations, and both should be redirected to the Tennis Rally anchor's turn-balance framing. If the student struggles to distinguish a comfortable pause from a stalled one during TA-3 (S6), drop back to the Concrete Anchor's explicit discomfort-signal contrast before returning to open-ended scenarios. At mastery-probe stage (S9), do not certify mastery unless MP-1 and MP-2 both pass — a student who can generate good responsive turns (MP-3) but still over-talks or panics at silence has not internalized the core turn-taking judgment this concept teaches.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student dominates conversation practice, weight remediation toward MC-GOOD-CONVERSATION-MEANS-TALKING-A-LOT's bridge text and re-run TA-1's turn-balance observation before returning to open dialogue practice.
- If the student rushes to fill every pause anxiously, weight remediation toward MC-SILENCE-IN-A-CONVERSATION-MEANS-SOMETHING-WENT-WRONG's bridge text and TA-3's pause-classification practice specifically, building tolerance for brief comfortable silence.
- This concept is foundational for three direct downstream unlocks (`eng.speaking.asking-and-answering-questions`, `eng.speaking.storytelling-orally`, `eng.speaking.non-verbal-communication`) — do not certify mastery without TA-5's full simulation, since all three downstream concepts assume the student can already sustain a balanced, responsive multi-turn exchange.
- Reuse the "tennis rally" turn-taking framing explicitly when introducing `eng.speaking.asking-and-answering-questions` — that concept refines one specific type of conversational turn (the question-answer exchange) within the same balanced-rally structure.
