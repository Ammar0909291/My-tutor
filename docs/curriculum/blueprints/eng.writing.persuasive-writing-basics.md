**Concept:** Persuasive Writing Basics — writing that states a position and supports it with reasons
genuinely intended to convince a reader, rather than merely asserting the position forcefully or
restating personal preference.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Transitions and Cohesion (prerequisite) taught linking
ideas smoothly across sentences and paragraphs. Persuasive Writing Basics extends this into the
specific discipline of argument: stating a clear position and backing it with reasons a neutral
reader would find compelling, distinct from simply asserting an opinion loudly or restating a
personal preference as if it were a reason. This concept has no further downstream unlocks in the
current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) distinguish a forcefully stated opinion from an
actual supported argument, recognizing that intensity of language does not substitute for reasons,
and (b) generate genuine reasons — points a neutral reader could find compelling — rather than
restating personal preference as if it were a reason.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-STATING-AN-OPINION-FORCEFULLY-OR-WITH-STRONG-LANGUAGE-MAKES-IT-PERSUASIVE

**Trigger signal:** Student relies on forceful language, intensifiers, repetition, or exclamation
points ("This is absolutely, definitely, one hundred percent true!!!") to make a position sound
convincing, rather than backing the position with actual reasons — mistaking the volume or intensity
of an assertion for persuasive strength.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two short passages arguing the same position: one
using intense, repeated assertion with no reasons ("School uniforms are terrible! Everyone hates
them! They are just awful and wrong!") and one stating the position calmly with two specific reasons
("School uniforms limit self-expression, and research shows they don't measurably improve academic
outcomes."). Ask: "Which passage actually gives you a reason to agree? Does saying something loudly
make it more true?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Trying to persuade through forceful language alone is like trying to win a
courtroom case by shouting 'Guilty! Guilty! Absolutely guilty!' without presenting any evidence — the
volume changes nothing about whether the claim is actually supported. A real persuasive argument
convinces through the strength of its reasons, not the intensity of its wording."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't rely on strong language or repetition to persuade. State your
position calmly and clearly, then back it with specific reasons a reader could actually weigh and
consider — the reasons do the persuading, not the volume of your wording."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "School uniforms are terrible! Everyone hates them! They are just awful and wrong!" (forceful
   assertion, zero actual reasons) vs. "School uniforms limit self-expression, and research shows
   they don't measurably improve academic outcomes." (calm statement, two specific reasons).
2. A student who adds exclamation points and intensifiers to make a weak argument sound stronger
   (misconception) vs. a student who states a position plainly and lets specific reasons carry the
   persuasive weight (correct discipline).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to rely on forceful wording after the bridge, give them a
forcefully-worded passage with no reasons and have them rewrite it in calm, plain language while
adding at least two specific reasons — proving the reasons, not the wording, are what changed the
persuasive strength.

### MC-B-RESTATING-A-PERSONAL-PREFERENCE-COUNTS-AS-A-REASON

**Trigger signal:** Student offers reasons that are simply a restatement of personal preference or
the position itself ("School uniforms are bad because I don't like them" / "because they're just not
good") rather than a genuine reason a neutral reader — who does not share the writer's preference —
could find compelling, failing to distinguish circular restatement from actual supporting reasoning.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two candidate "reasons" for the same position: "School
uniforms are bad because I don't like wearing them." (restated preference) and "School uniforms limit
students' ability to express their individual identity, which research links to lower student
engagement." (a reason independent of the writer's personal taste). Ask: "Would someone who doesn't
share your personal preference find the first reason convincing? What about the second?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Offering 'because I don't like it' as a reason is like a lawyer's entire
courtroom argument being 'because my client says so' — it asks the listener to simply trust the
speaker's preference rather than offering anything they could independently evaluate. A genuine
reason gives a neutral reader — someone who doesn't already share your preference — something real
to weigh."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before writing a reason, ask: would this convince someone who doesn't
already share my opinion? If your 'reason' just restates that you personally dislike or prefer
something, it isn't a reason yet — dig for the actual effect, evidence, or consequence behind your
preference."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "School uniforms are bad because I don't like wearing them." (restated personal preference, not a
   reason a neutral reader could weigh) vs. "School uniforms limit students' ability to express their
   individual identity, which research links to lower student engagement." (a genuine reason
   independent of personal taste).
2. A student whose every "reason" reduces to "because I like/dislike it" (misconception) vs. a
   student who digs beneath their preference to identify an effect, consequence, or evidence a
   neutral reader could evaluate (correct reasoning discipline).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to offer restated preference as reasoning after the bridge,
have them take one of their own preference-based "reasons" and ask "why does this matter to someone
who doesn't share my opinion?" repeatedly until they surface a genuine, independently-weighable
reason.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.writing.transitions-and-cohesion`):** "Add an appropriate transition connecting
these two sentences." A student who cannot link ideas cohesively lacks the foundation to connect a
position to its supporting reasons clearly — route to `eng.writing.transitions-and-cohesion` review
first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Shouting-Courtroom and "Because-My-Client-Says-So" Anchor.** A lawyer trying to win a case by
shouting "Guilty! Guilty!" with no evidence — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (persuasion comes
from the strength of reasons, not the volume or intensity of wording). Second, a lawyer's entire
argument reducing to "because my client says so," asking the listener to trust personal assertion
rather than offering anything to weigh — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (a genuine reason must be
something a neutral reader who doesn't already share the writer's preference could evaluate).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Stating a Clear Position

Teach writing a plain, direct position statement without relying on intensifiers or forceful
language. Student practice: given a forcefully-worded position, rewrite it as a calm, direct
statement.

### TA-2 — Reasons vs. Forceful Assertion (MC-A)

Using the shouting-courtroom anchor, teach that persuasive strength comes from reasons, not wording
intensity. Student practice: given a forceful, reason-free passage, rewrite it in calm language while
adding at least one specific reason.

### TA-3 — Genuine Reasons vs. Restated Preference (MC-B)

Using the "because-my-client-says-so" anchor, teach digging beneath personal preference to find a
genuine, independently-weighable reason. Student practice: given a preference-restating "reason,"
dig to surface the actual effect or consequence behind it.

### TA-4 — Organizing Position and Reasons Cohesively

Extend TA-1/TA-3: using transitions (prerequisite skill), organize a position statement with two or
three genuine reasons into a cohesive short paragraph. Student practice: given a position and a list
of genuine reasons, organize them into one cohesive paragraph with appropriate transitions.

### TA-5 — Full Application: Writing a Persuasive Paragraph

Give the student a new topic. They must state a clear position (TA-1), generate genuine reasons
rather than restated preference (TA-3), avoid relying on forceful wording (TA-2), and organize the
whole into a cohesive paragraph (TA-4) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A topic (extending the school day), fully worked: an initial forceful,
reason-free draft rewritten into a calm position statement; two candidate reasons generated, one
identified as restated preference ("because I don't want to") and discarded, one identified as
genuine ("students already show declining focus in the final class period, so extending the day may
reduce learning quality") and kept; the position and genuine reason organized into a cohesive
paragraph with a transition. Annotation confirms both the reasons-not-volume principle (fixing MC-A)
and the genuine-reason discipline (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new topic. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("Is your position stated
calmly, or does it rely on forceful wording? Would someone who doesn't share your opinion find this
reason convincing, or is it just your preference restated?") to complete the paragraph.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new topic, unaided. Student states a position, generates genuine reasons,
and organizes them into a cohesive persuasive paragraph.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Rewriting Forceful Language as a Calm Position Statement**
Given a forcefully-worded opinion, rewrite it as a plain, direct position statement.

**MP-2 [P36] — Distinguishing Reasons from Forceful Assertion**
Given a reason-free, forcefully-worded passage, add at least one specific reason while removing the
forceful wording.

**MP-3 [P36] — Identifying Restated Preference vs. a Genuine Reason**
Given two candidate reasons for the same position, identify which is restated personal preference
and which is a genuine, independently-weighable reason.

**MP-4 [P36] — Digging Beneath Preference to a Genuine Reason**
Given a preference-restating "reason," dig to surface the actual effect or consequence behind it.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Persuasive Paragraph**
Given a new topic, write a cohesive persuasive paragraph stating a calm position and supporting it
with genuine reasons.

MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-paragraph example before
re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly rewrites forceful passages on provided examples (MP-1) but reverts to
  intensifiers when writing an original position (MP-5), provide a mandatory "read your position
  aloud without any exclamation points or intensifiers" check before finalizing.
- If the student's calm tone is strong (MP-1, MP-2) but their reasons (MP-4) remain restated
  preference, require the explicit "would someone who disagrees with me find this convincing?"
  question before accepting any reason as finished.
- If the student struggles to generate any reason beyond preference, provide sentence starters
  ("This matters because it affects...", "Evidence shows that...") as scaffolding before free
  reason-generation.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.writing.transitions-and-cohesion`'s idea-linking work
  should transfer readily to organizing position and reasons cohesively, since both require
  connecting ideas so a reader can follow the logic — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
