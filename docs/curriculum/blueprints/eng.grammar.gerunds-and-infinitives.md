## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-GERUND-IS-JUST-PRESENT-CONTINUOUS
- **Trigger signal:** Student confuses a gerund (-ing form functioning as a NOUN: "Swimming is fun") with the -ing form used in present continuous verb tense ("I am swimming"), not recognizing that despite identical spelling, a gerund plays a completely different grammatical role — it acts as a NOUN (subject, object, or complement), not part of a verb phrase showing ongoing action.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "In 'Swimming is fun,' is 'swimming' the main VERB of the sentence (like in 'I am swimming'), or is it acting as a NOUN — the subject that 'is fun' describes?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "A gerund looks identical to the -ing form used in present continuous (both add '-ing' to the base verb), but it plays a completely different grammatical ROLE: a gerund functions as a NOUN (Swimming is fun — 'swimming' is the subject; I enjoy swimming — 'swimming' is the object), while the present continuous '-ing' form is part of the VERB phrase showing ongoing action (I am swimming — 'am swimming' is the verb). Check the word's JOB in the sentence, not just its spelling."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Check whether an '-ing' word is functioning as a noun (subject/object/complement — gerund) or as part of the verb phrase showing action (present continuous) — identical spelling, different grammatical roles."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "Swimming is my favorite hobby" (gerund — subject, a noun role) vs. "I am swimming right now" (present continuous — part of the verb phrase).
  - "I enjoy reading" (gerund — object of "enjoy," a noun role) vs. "I am reading a book" (present continuous — part of the verb phrase).
- **s6_path:** "Since both forms look identical (base verb + -ing), it's very natural to conflate them — but checking whether the word is doing a NOUN's job or a VERB's job in that specific sentence reliably distinguishes them."

### MC-GERUND-AND-INFINITIVE-ARE-ALWAYS-INTERCHANGEABLE
- **Trigger signal:** Student assumes gerunds (-ing) and infinitives (to + base verb) can always substitute for each other after any verb, missing that specific verbs GOVERN one form or the other (some verbs only take gerunds: enjoy, avoid, finish, mind; some only take infinitives: want, decide, hope, plan; some take either with the SAME meaning: like, start, begin; and some take either with a DIFFERENT meaning: stop + gerund vs. stop + infinitive) — producing errors like "I enjoy to swim" (should be "I enjoy swimming") or confusion about meaning-changing cases.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said 'I enjoy to swim' and 'I enjoy swimming' are both fine and mean the same. Is 'enjoy to swim' actually something fluent English speakers say? What's the correct form?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Specific verbs GOVERN whether the following verb takes the gerund or infinitive form — this must be learned per verb, since it's not always predictable from meaning alone: 'enjoy,' 'avoid,' 'finish,' 'mind' require a GERUND (I enjoy swimming, not 'to swim'); 'want,' 'decide,' 'hope,' 'plan' require an INFINITIVE (I want to swim, not 'swimming'); some verbs like 'like,' 'start,' 'begin' accept EITHER with little meaning difference; and a few verbs (like 'stop,' 'remember,' 'try') change MEANING depending on which form follows (I stopped smoking = I quit; I stopped to smoke = I paused another activity in order to smoke)."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Learn which specific form (gerund-only, infinitive-only, either, or meaning-changing) a given verb governs, rather than assuming they're always interchangeable."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "I enjoy swimming" (gerund-required verb) vs. attempting "I enjoy to swim" (incorrect).
  - "I stopped smoking" (quit the habit) vs. "I stopped to smoke" (paused to take a smoke break) — same verb "stop," different following form, genuinely different meaning.
- **s6_path:** "Since both forms describe similar ideas (an activity), assuming interchangeability is a reasonable first guess — but specific verb-governance patterns (and occasionally genuine meaning differences) must be learned directly, similar to prepositional collocations."

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The noun-job vs. verb-job sort**

> Present a set of "-ing" sentence cards. For each, ask: "Is the -ing word doing a NOUN's job (subject/object — could I replace it with 'it' or 'that activity'?) or a VERB's job (part of what's happening right now)?" Sort into two piles. Then introduce a set of verb cards (enjoy, want, avoid, decide) with color-coded tags (gerund-only, infinitive-only, either) to build the verb-governance pattern visually.

---

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Gerunds as Subjects and Objects [C]**

Present sentences using gerunds as subjects (Running is healthy) and objects (I love running); student identifies the gerund's noun-role function, directly targeting MC-GERUND-IS-JUST-PRESENT-CONTINUOUS.

**TA-2 — Infinitives as Subjects, Objects, and Purpose [C]**

Present sentences using infinitives (To exercise is important; I want to exercise; I exercise to stay healthy); student identifies the infinitive's various functions, including expressing purpose.

**TA-3 — Gerund-Only and Infinitive-Only Verbs [C→P]**

Present a set of common gerund-only verbs (enjoy, avoid, finish, mind, suggest) and infinitive-only verbs (want, decide, hope, plan, agree); student produces the correct following form for each, directly targeting MC-GERUND-AND-INFINITIVE-ARE-ALWAYS-INTERCHANGEABLE.

**TA-4 — Verbs Accepting Either Form (Same Meaning) [P]**

Present verbs accepting either gerund or infinitive with little meaning difference (like, start, begin, continue); student practices both forms correctly.

**TA-5 — Meaning-Changing Verbs (Stop, Remember, Try) [P]**

Present the small set of verbs where gerund vs. infinitive genuinely changes meaning (stop, remember, forget, try); student distinguishes the two meanings for each verb.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — gerund as subject)**

> "In 'Reading is my favorite hobby,' is 'reading' a gerund (noun role) or part of a present continuous verb?"

Target response: gerund — it's the subject of the sentence, a noun role.

**WE-2 (Foundational — infinitive-only verb)**

> "Fill in: 'I decided ___ (study) abroad.'"

Target response: "to study" — "decide" governs the infinitive.

**WE-3 (Intermediate — meaning-changing verb)**

> "What's the difference between 'I remembered to lock the door' and 'I remembered locking the door'?"

Target response: "remembered to lock" means she recalled the need to do it (and did it, or was about to); "remembered locking" means she recalls the memory of the past act of locking it (already done, recalling it afterward).

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Gerund vs. present continuous, novel example**
"In 'Swimming keeps me healthy,' is 'swimming' a gerund or part of the verb?"
*Target:* gerund — subject of the sentence, a noun role.

**MP-2 [P36] — Gerund-only verb, novel example**
"Fill in: 'She avoided ___ (answer) the question.'"
*Target:* "answering" — "avoid" governs the gerund.

**MP-3 [P36] — Infinitive-only verb, novel example**
"Fill in: 'They agreed ___ (help) us.'"
*Target:* "to help" — "agree" governs the infinitive.

**MP-4 [P36] — Either-form verb, novel example**
"Fill in both ways: 'It started ___ (rain).'"
*Target:* "raining" or "to rain" — both acceptable, "start" governs either.

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain gerunds/infinitives and verb governance**
"In your own words: is an '-ing' word always part of the verb tense, and can every verb be followed by either a gerund or infinitive interchangeably?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that gerunds function as nouns (distinct from the present-continuous verb form despite identical spelling), and that specific verbs govern whether a gerund, infinitive, either, or a meaning-changing choice follows — this must be learned per verb.

**Protocol routing:** S0 (secure on present tenses, new to gerund/infinitive distinctions): dwell on TA-1's gerund-noun-role identification before introducing verb-governance patterns. ## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Function (noun-role vs. verb-role) is the key gerund/present-continuous discriminator**: always require checking grammatical function, not spelling, to distinguish these identical-looking forms.
- **Verb governance is a genuine, bounded memorization task, parallel to preposition collocations**: frame it explicitly this way to reduce frustration and redirect effort toward effective memorization of the highest-frequency governing verbs (enjoy, avoid, finish, mind, suggest / want, decide, hope, plan, agree / like, start, begin / stop, remember, forget, try).
- **Meaning-changing verbs deserve dedicated, memorable contrast pairs**: teach stop/remember/forget/try explicitly as a small, named exception category where the choice of form carries genuine semantic weight, not just grammatical correctness.
- **One of the most persistent advanced-learner challenge areas — budget accordingly**: for S9 learners, recognize gerund/infinitive verb governance as idiosyncratic even by English's own standards, requiring sustained, spaced practice well beyond a single session.
