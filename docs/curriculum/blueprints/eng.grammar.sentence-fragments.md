**Concept:** Sentence Fragments — incomplete sentences lacking a subject, a verb, or a complete
thought (leaving a subordinate/dependent clause standing alone as if it were a full sentence), and
strategies for correcting them.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Simple Sentences (prerequisite) established what a
complete sentence needs: a subject and a verb expressing a complete thought. Sentence Fragments
teaches recognizing when that completeness requirement is NOT met — a group of words punctuated as a
sentence but missing a subject, missing a verb, or (a subtler case) containing both but still not
expressing a complete thought because it's a subordinate clause left standing alone. This concept has
no further downstream unlocks in the current curriculum but is a foundational error-recognition skill
underlying clear, correct writing throughout.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) identify a sentence fragment missing a subject or a
verb, (b) identify the subtler fragment type — a subordinate/dependent clause punctuated alone as if
it were a complete sentence, even though it contains both a subject and a verb, and (c) correctly
revise a fragment by either supplying the missing element or joining it to an adjacent independent
clause.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-A-GROUP-OF-WORDS-IS-A-COMPLETE-SENTENCE-AS-LONG-AS-IT-HAS-A-SUBJECT-AND-A-VERB-SOMEWHERE-IN-IT

**Trigger signal:** Student believes that any group of words containing a subject and a verb
somewhere within it must be a complete sentence, missing the subtler fragment type where a
subordinate/dependent clause DOES contain a subject and a verb but still fails to express a complete
thought because it depends on something else to complete its meaning (e.g., "Because the rain
started." has a subject "rain" and a verb "started," but is still a fragment, since "because" makes
the reader expect more information to follow).

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student "Because the rain started." (containing a subject and a
verb, but still a fragment) alongside "The rain started." (a genuinely complete sentence with the same
subject and verb, minus "because"). Ask: "Both have a subject and a verb. Does the first one feel
finished, or does it leave you expecting more information — like 'because the rain started... WHAT
happened'?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Having a subject and verb somewhere in a group of words is like having flour
and water somewhere in your kitchen — necessary ingredients, but not automatically a finished dish. A
word like 'because,' 'although,' or 'when' at the start of a clause creates an expectation that MORE
is coming — the clause depends on something else to complete the thought, even though it has its own
subject and verb. That dependence is what makes it a fragment, not a complete sentence."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't just check for a subject and a verb — check whether the group of
words expresses a genuinely COMPLETE thought that doesn't leave the reader expecting more. If it starts
with a word like 'because,' 'although,' 'when,' or 'since' and stands alone with nothing following, it's
very likely a fragment, even with a subject and verb present."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Because the rain started." (has a subject and verb, but is a fragment — leaves the reader
   expecting what happened as a result) vs. "The rain started." (a genuinely complete sentence,
   subject and verb, no dangling expectation).
2. A student who accepts "Because the rain started." as complete simply because it has a subject and
   verb (misconception) vs. a student who recognizes the "because" creates an unfulfilled expectation,
   making it a fragment despite having a subject and verb (correct, deeper understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to accept subordinate-clause fragments as complete after the
bridge, have them read five candidate sentences aloud and physically pause at the end of each,
asking "does this feel finished, or am I still waiting for something?" — using the felt sense of
incompleteness as a concrete, repeatable test.

### MC-B-FIXING-A-FRAGMENT-ALWAYS-MEANS-ADDING-MORE-WORDS-TO-THE-SAME-STANDALONE-GROUP

**Trigger signal:** Student, when correcting a fragment, always attempts to fix it by adding more
words to make the fragment stand alone as its own complete sentence, even in cases where the more
natural and effective fix is to JOIN the fragment to an adjacent sentence (often the one right before
or after it) rather than padding it out into an artificially separate, complete sentence.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a fragment in context: "The team celebrated. Because
they had finally won the championship." alongside two fix options: one that awkwardly pads the
fragment into its own artificial sentence ("Because they had finally won the championship, it was
true."), and one that joins the fragment to the adjacent sentence it clearly belongs with ("The team
celebrated because they had finally won the championship."). Ask: "Which fix actually reads naturally,
and which feels forced and artificial?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Always trying to pad a fragment into its own standalone sentence is like
trying to repair a puzzle piece that clearly belongs attached to its neighbor by gluing random extra
cardboard onto it instead of just connecting it to the piece it was always meant to join. Often, the
most natural fix for a fragment is to JOIN it to the adjacent sentence it logically belongs with, not
to artificially inflate it into its own separate sentence."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When you find a fragment, first check: does it logically belong attached
to the sentence right before or after it? If so, the most natural fix is usually to JOIN them into one
sentence, rather than padding the fragment out to stand alone."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Because they had finally won the championship, it was true." (an awkward, padded standalone fix
   that doesn't read naturally) vs. "The team celebrated because they had finally won the
   championship." (a natural join to the adjacent sentence the fragment clearly belonged with).
2. A student who always tries to make a fragment stand alone by adding filler content (misconception)
   vs. a student who checks whether joining to an adjacent sentence produces a more natural result
   before considering other fixes (correct, flexible approach).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to pad fragments into artificial standalone sentences after the
bridge, have them take three fragments in context and try BOTH fixes (join to adjacent sentence,
versus pad into a standalone sentence) for each, then identify which fix reads more naturally in every
case.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.grammar.simple-sentences`):** "Does this group of words form a complete sentence:
'The dog ran'?" A student who cannot confirm basic sentence completeness lacks the foundation this
concept builds on — route to `eng.grammar.simple-sentences` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Flour-and-Water and Puzzle-Piece Anchor.** Combine two images: flour and water sitting separately
in a kitchen, ingredients present but not yet a finished dish — directly seeding the fix for MC-A
(subject and verb present doesn't guarantee a complete thought). Second, a puzzle piece that clearly
belongs connected to its neighbor, rather than having random extra cardboard glued onto it to make it
stand alone — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (join a fragment to its natural neighbor rather than
padding it into an artificial standalone sentence).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Fragments Missing a Subject or a Verb

Introduce the most basic fragment type: a group of words missing a subject entirely, or missing a
verb entirely. Student practice: given four groups of words, identify which are missing a subject,
which are missing a verb, and which are complete.

### TA-2 — Subordinate Clause Fragments (MC-A)

Using the flour-and-water anchor and the "does this feel finished?" test, teach the subtler fragment
type: a subordinate clause with both a subject and verb, still incomplete because of a dependent
word like "because," "although," "when." Student practice: given five clauses beginning with
subordinating words, identify which stand as fragments and which are followed by enough content to be
complete.

### TA-3 — Fixing a Fragment by Joining to an Adjacent Sentence (MC-B)

Using the puzzle-piece anchor, teach students to check whether a fragment naturally joins to an
adjacent sentence before considering other fixes. Student practice: given three fragments in context,
join each to the more appropriate adjacent sentence (before or after).

### TA-4 — Full Application: Identifying and Correcting Fragments

Give the student a short passage containing a mix of complete sentences and both fragment types
(missing subject/verb, and subordinate-clause-alone). They must identify each fragment (TA-1, TA-2)
and correct it using the most natural fix — joining to an adjacent sentence where appropriate, or
supplying the missing element (TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Passage: "Ran quickly down the street. The dog. Because it was chasing a
squirrel." Fully worked: "Ran quickly down the street." identified as missing a subject; "The dog."
identified as missing a verb; "Because it was chasing a squirrel." identified as a subordinate clause
fragment (has subject and verb, but incomplete due to "because"); all three joined and corrected into
"The dog ran quickly down the street because it was chasing a squirrel." Annotation confirms both
fragment types were correctly identified (fixing MC-A) and the natural joining fix was applied rather
than three awkward standalone patches (fixing MB-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New short passage with a missing-subject fragment, a missing-verb fragment, and a
subordinate-clause fragment. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("Does this group of words have
both a subject and a verb? If so, does it still feel incomplete — is there a word like 'because' or
'when' creating an unfulfilled expectation? Does this fragment naturally belong joined to the sentence
next to it?") to identify and correct each fragment.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new short passage containing a mix of complete sentences and both fragment
types, unaided. Student identifies and corrects each fragment using the most natural fix.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying Missing Subject/Verb Fragments**
Given four groups of words, identify which are missing a subject, which are missing a verb, and which
are complete sentences.

**MP-2 [P36] — Identifying Subordinate Clause Fragments**
Given four clauses beginning with subordinating words (because, although, when, since), identify
which stand as fragments and which are followed by enough content to be complete.

**MP-3 [P36] — Choosing Between Joining and Standalone Fixes**
Given a fragment in context, determine whether joining it to an adjacent sentence or supplying a
missing element independently produces the more natural fix.

**MP-4 [P36] — Correcting by Joining**
Given a fragment and its adjacent sentence, join them into one correctly punctuated, complete
sentence.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Identification and Correction**
Given a new short passage containing a mix of complete sentences and both fragment types, identify
and correct each fragment using the most natural fix.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite.
During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-2 (subordinate clause
fragments, the subtler case) and MC-B during TA-3 (fixing preference) — MC-A generally needs
resolving first since a student who can't identify the subtler fragment type has nothing yet to
practice fixing correctly. If either fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At
the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure indicates the basic missing-subject/verb
recognition (TA-1) needs review, distinct from either misconception; MP-2 failure alone indicates
residual MC-A; MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing
indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-passage example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters basic missing-subject/verb fragments (MP-1) but consistently misses
  subordinate clause fragments (MP-2), continue extending practice specifically with subordinating
  words (because, although, when, since, if) until the "feels finished?" test becomes automatic.
- If the student's corrections default to always joining to the PREVIOUS sentence even when joining to
  the FOLLOWING sentence would be more natural, explicitly practice checking both directions before
  choosing a fix.
- If the student over-applies fragment detection to intentional, stylistically effective fragments used
  by professional writers for emphasis (a legitimate but advanced stylistic choice, distinct from the
  unintentional error this concept addresses), note this as a genuine exception worth a brief
  mention, without diluting the core rule for standard academic writing.
- Students with a strong ear for spoken language rhythm may find the "does this feel finished?" test
  from TA-2 more intuitive than the formal subject/verb identification from TA-1 — allow this as a
  valid entry point, then connect it to the formal grammatical explanation for precision.
