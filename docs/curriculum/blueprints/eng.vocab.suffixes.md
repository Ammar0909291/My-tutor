## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-SUFFIXES-ONLY-CHANGE-MEANING-NOT-WORD-CLASS
- **Trigger signal:** Student focuses only on meaning changes from suffixes (e.g., "-less" meaning "without") without recognizing that many suffixes primarily change the WORD CLASS (part of speech) of the base word, often with a more predictable grammatical function than a purely semantic one — e.g., "-tion" turns a verb into a noun (create → creation), "-ive" turns a verb/noun into an adjective (create → creative), "-ly" turns an adjective into an adverb (quick → quickly).
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said '-tion' in 'creation' just adds meaning. But look at 'create' (a verb) and 'creation' (what word class is this now)? Did the suffix change more than just the meaning?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Many suffixes have a primarily GRAMMATICAL function — they change the word's CLASS (part of speech): '-tion/-ment/-ness' typically create nouns, '-ive/-ous/-ful' typically create adjectives, '-ly' typically creates adverbs, '-ize/-ify' typically create verbs. This grammatical-conversion function is often more predictable and important than any subtle meaning shift."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Check whether a suffix changes the word's grammatical CLASS (noun/verb/adjective/adverb), not just its shade of meaning — this is often the suffix's primary job."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "create" (verb) + "-tion" → "creation" (noun) — word-class change from verb to noun.
  - "quick" (adjective) + "-ly" → "quickly" (adverb) — word-class change from adjective to adverb.
- **s6_path:** "Focusing on meaning first is a natural instinct since meaning feels like the 'main point' — but noticing that many suffixes are really doing GRAMMATICAL conversion work (changing the word's job in a sentence) is an equally important, often more reliable pattern."

### MC-SUFFIX-SPELLING-CHANGES-ARE-RANDOM
- **Trigger signal:** Student treats spelling changes that occur when adding a suffix (dropping a final "e," changing "y" to "i," doubling a final consonant) as unpredictable or random, rather than recognizing the systematic spelling rules governing these changes (e.g., drop silent final "e" before a vowel suffix: "hope" + "-ing" → "hoping"; change "y" to "i" before most suffixes when preceded by a consonant: "happy" + "-ness" → "happiness"; double a final consonant in specific stressed, short-vowel contexts: "run" + "-ing" → "running").
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You spelled 'hopeing' by just adding '-ing' to 'hope' without changing anything. But do we usually keep a silent 'e' right before another vowel sound, or does English typically drop it? What's the correct spelling?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Spelling changes when adding suffixes follow genuine, learnable rules, not randomness: drop a silent final 'e' before a vowel-starting suffix ('hope' + '-ing' = 'hoping'), change a final 'y' to 'i' before most suffixes when preceded by a consonant ('happy' + '-ness' = 'happiness,' but 'play' + '-ing' = 'playing' since 'y' follows a vowel), and double a final consonant in short, stressed, single-vowel-ending words ('run' + '-ing' = 'running')."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Check the base word's ending (silent e? y after a consonant? single stressed short vowel + consonant?) against the specific spelling rule before adding a suffix, rather than just attaching it directly."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "hope" + "-ing" = "hoping" (drop the silent e) vs. simply attaching to get the incorrect "hopeing."
  - "run" + "-ing" = "running" (double the final consonant, since "run" is short/stressed/single-vowel) vs. "walk" + "-ing" = "walking" (no doubling needed, since "walk" ends in two consonants already).
- **s6_path:** "Spelling changes can feel arbitrary since they interrupt the simple 'just add the suffix' pattern — but each rule (drop-e, y-to-i, double-consonant) applies to a specific, checkable pattern in the base word, making them genuinely learnable rather than memorized case-by-case."

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The suffix job-change costume**

> Present a base word card (e.g., "create," labeled "VERB"). Attach a suffix card ("-tion") and flip the label to "NOUN," discussing how the word's grammatical job changed. Repeat with "-ive" (→ "ADJECTIVE") and other suffixes, building a visual "costume change" metaphor: the suffix changes what JOB the word can do in a sentence, like a costume change for a different role.

---

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Noun-Forming Suffixes [C]**

Present base words with noun-forming suffixes (-tion, -ment, -ness, -ity: creation, movement, happiness, activity); student identifies the suffix and the resulting word class.

**TA-2 — Adjective- and Adverb-Forming Suffixes [C]**

Present base words with adjective-forming (-ful, -ous, -ive, -able) and adverb-forming (-ly) suffixes; student identifies the suffix and resulting word class, directly targeting MC-SUFFIXES-ONLY-CHANGE-MEANING-NOT-WORD-CLASS by requiring explicit word-class identification.

**TA-3 — Verb-Forming Suffixes [C→P]**

Present base words with verb-forming suffixes (-ize, -ify, -en: modernize, simplify, strengthen); student identifies the suffix and resulting word class, completing the four-word-class pattern.

**TA-4 — Spelling Rule Application (Drop-E, Y-to-I, Double Consonant) [P]**

Present base words requiring specific spelling adjustments when a suffix is added; student correctly applies the drop-e, y-to-i, or double-consonant rule, directly targeting MC-SUFFIX-SPELLING-CHANGES-ARE-RANDOM.

**TA-5 — Combining Word-Class Change with Correct Spelling [P]**

Student both identifies the word-class change AND correctly spells the suffixed form for a set of words requiring spelling adjustments, integrating both skill strands.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — noun-forming suffix)**

> "What word class is 'movement,' and what suffix created it from 'move'?"

Target response: noun; suffix "-ment" turned the verb "move" into the noun "movement."

**WE-2 (Foundational — adjective-forming suffix)**

> "What word class is 'colorful,' and what suffix created it from 'color'?"

Target response: adjective; suffix "-ful" turned the noun "color" into the adjective "colorful."

**WE-3 (Intermediate — spelling rule)**

> "Spell 'happy' + '-ness.' What spelling rule applies?"

Target response: "happiness" — change "y" to "i" before adding "-ness," since "y" follows a consonant.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Noun-forming suffix, novel word**
"What word class is 'activity,' and what suffix formed it from 'active'?"
*Target:* noun; suffix "-ity."

**MP-2 [P36] — Adjective-forming suffix, novel word**
"What word class is 'dangerous,' and what suffix formed it from 'danger'?"
*Target:* adjective; suffix "-ous."

**MP-3 [P36] — Verb-forming suffix, novel word**
"What word class is 'simplify,' and what suffix formed it from 'simple'?"
*Target:* verb; suffix "-ify."

**MP-4 [P36] — Spelling rule, novel word**
"Spell 'big' + '-est.' What spelling rule applies?"
*Target:* "biggest" — double the final consonant, since "big" is short, stressed, and ends in a single vowel + consonant.

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain suffixes and spelling rules**
"In your own words: what's the main job of most suffixes, and are spelling changes when adding them random?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that suffixes often change a word's grammatical CLASS (not just its meaning), and that spelling adjustments (drop-e, y-to-i, double-consonant) follow specific, learnable rules based on the base word's ending, not randomness.

S9 (L1 doesn't mark word-class changes through suffixation, e.g. many isolating languages, or has very different spelling-adjustment conventions): expect both the word-class-conversion concept and the English-specific spelling rules to require dedicated, explicit teaching, since these may have no direct L1 parallel.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Word-class conversion is often the suffix's PRIMARY function**: explicitly teach and test word-class identification alongside meaning, directly countering the meaning-only default.
- **Spelling rules are systematic, tied to base-word endings**: teach the three core rules (drop-e, y-to-i, double-consonant) as checkable tests based on the base word's specific ending pattern, not arbitrary exceptions.
- **Four word-class categories (noun/adjective/adverb/verb) give a complete, organized framework**: sequence suffix introduction by target word class, mirroring the prefix concept's category-based approach for consistency across the vocab-morphology strand.
- **Morphological transfer for L1s without suffix-based word-class conversion**: for S9 learners whose L1 doesn't build words this way, provide foundational scaffolding on the very concept before assuming ready transfer of the pattern-recognition skill.
