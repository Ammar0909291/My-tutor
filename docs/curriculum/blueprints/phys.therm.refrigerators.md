## Component 1 — Concept Explanation (Multi-Tier)

### Tier 1 — Concrete / Intuitive (S0)

A refrigerator does something remarkable: it moves heat from cold to hot — the opposite of what heat naturally does. This costs work (your electricity bill). Think of it as a heat engine running backwards: instead of heat flowing from hot to cold to produce work, you put in work to push heat from cold to hot. The "coefficient of performance" (COP) tells you how much heat you move per unit of work — a good fridge has COP around 3–5, meaning you move 3–5 joules of heat for every joule of electricity you pay for. A heat pump does the same thing but in the useful direction: you pump heat from the cold outdoors into a warm building.

### Tier 2 — Conceptual / Mechanistic (S1)

**Refrigerator energy balance:**
A refrigerator absorbs heat Q_C from the cold reservoir (inside the fridge, T_C) and rejects heat Q_H to the hot reservoir (kitchen air, T_H), powered by work input W.

By the first law for a cycle (ΔU = 0):
$$Q_H = Q_C + W$$

**Coefficient of Performance — refrigerator (COP_R):**
What you want: Q_C (heat removed from cold space)
What you pay: W (work input)
$$\text{COP}_R = \frac{Q_C}{W} = \frac{Q_C}{Q_H - Q_C}$$

**Coefficient of Performance — heat pump (COP_HP):**
What you want: Q_H (heat delivered to warm space)
What you pay: W (work input)
$$\text{COP}_{HP} = \frac{Q_H}{W} = \frac{Q_H}{Q_H - Q_C}$$

**Key identity:**
$$\text{COP}_{HP} = \text{COP}_R + 1$$

This identity holds always (it follows directly from Q_H = Q_C + W).

**Carnot limits:**
The Carnot cycle reversed gives maximum COP (minimum work for a given heat transfer):
$$\text{COP}_{R,\text{Carnot}} = \frac{T_C}{T_H - T_C}$$
$$\text{COP}_{HP,\text{Carnot}} = \frac{T_H}{T_H - T_C}$$

No real refrigerator or heat pump can exceed the Carnot COP between the same temperatures.

**Clausius statement of the second law:**
"Heat cannot spontaneously flow from a cold body to a hot body." A refrigerator does this — but it requires work input. Without work, Q_C → Q_H would violate the second law: COP → ∞ is forbidden.

**Second-law efficiency:**
$$\varepsilon_R = \frac{\text{COP}_R}{\text{COP}_{R,\text{Carnot}}}$$

Real refrigerators: ε_R ≈ 0.20–0.40 (20–40% of Carnot limit).

### Tier 3 — Formal / Thermodynamic Cycle

**Vapour-compression refrigeration cycle** (standard refrigerator):
1. **Evaporation** (T_C, constant P): Refrigerant absorbs Q_C from cold space; liquid → vapour (latent heat)
2. **Compression** (adiabatic): Compressor does work W on gas; T rises from T_C to T_H
3. **Condensation** (T_H, constant P): Refrigerant rejects Q_H to hot reservoir; vapour → liquid
4. **Expansion valve** (throttling): Isenthalpic expansion (ΔH = 0), P drops, T falls to T_C

This is an irreversible cycle (throttling is irreversible; compression imperfect) → COP < COP_Carnot.

**Entropy analysis:**
For Carnot refrigerator: Q_C/T_C = Q_H/T_H → ΔS_universe = 0
For real refrigerator: Q_H/T_H > Q_C/T_C → ΔS_universe > 0 (entropy generated by irreversibility)

**EER (Energy Efficiency Ratio):** Used in industry; EER = COP_R × 3.412 (converts to BTU/W·hr).
Seasonal EER (SEER) averages over a full season.

**Heat pump economics:**
Moving heat from outside (−5°C = 268 K) to inside (20°C = 293 K):
COP_HP,Carnot = 293/(293−268) = 293/25 = 11.7
Even at 40% of Carnot: COP_HP = 4.7 → for every 1 kWh of electricity, deliver 4.7 kWh of heat. Electric resistance heating has COP = 1. Heat pumps are thermodynamically superior.

## Component 2 — Worked Examples

### WE-1 (Foundational — basic COP calculation)

**Problem:** A refrigerator removes 150 J of heat from its cold interior for every 50 J of work the compressor performs. Find: (a) Q_H, (b) COP_R.

**Worked solution:**

*Step 1 — First law for a refrigeration cycle:*
$$Q_H = Q_C + W = 150\text{ J} + 50\text{ J} = 200\text{ J}$$

*Step 2 — COP for refrigerator:*
$$\text{COP}_R = \frac{Q_C}{W} = \frac{150}{50} = 3.0$$

*Step 3 — Interpret:*
For every joule of electricity consumed, the refrigerator removes 3 joules from the cold space and deposits 4 joules (Q_H = Q_C + W) into the kitchen air. This is why kitchens warm up when refrigerator doors are left open — the net effect is heating the room.

**Answer:** Q_H = 200 J; COP_R = 3.0

---

### WE-2 (Core — Carnot COP and comparison to real device)

**Problem:** A refrigerator operates between T_C = 5°C and T_H = 35°C. (a) Find the Carnot COP. (b) The actual COP is 2.4. What is the second-law efficiency?

**Worked solution:**

*Step 1 — Convert temperatures to Kelvin:*
T_C = 278 K, T_H = 308 K

*Step 2 — Carnot COP for refrigerator:*
$$\text{COP}_{R,\text{Carnot}} = \frac{T_C}{T_H - T_C} = \frac{278}{308 - 278} = \frac{278}{30} = 9.27$$

*Step 3 — Second-law efficiency:*
$$\varepsilon_R = \frac{\text{COP}_R}{\text{COP}_{R,\text{Carnot}}} = \frac{2.4}{9.27} = 0.259 = 25.9\%$$

*Step 4 — Interpret:*
The real refrigerator achieves about 26% of the theoretical maximum. The gap is due to irreversibilities: non-quasi-static compression, throttling losses, heat conduction across finite temperature gaps, friction in the compressor.

**Answer:** COP_Carnot = 9.27; ε_R = 25.9%

---

### WE-3 (Advanced — heat pump economics)

**Problem:** A heat pump heats a house by extracting heat from outside air at −10°C and pumping it inside at 22°C. The heat pump requires 2.5 kW of electrical power. (a) Find COP_HP,Carnot. (b) If the actual COP_HP = 3.2, what is the rate of heat delivery to the house?

**Worked solution:**

*Step 1 — Temperatures in Kelvin:*
T_C = 263 K, T_H = 295 K

*Step 2 — Carnot COP (heat pump):*
$$\text{COP}_{HP,\text{Carnot}} = \frac{T_H}{T_H - T_C} = \frac{295}{295 - 263} = \frac{295}{32} = 9.22$$

*Step 3 — Actual rate of heat delivery:*
$$\dot{Q}_H = \text{COP}_{HP} \times \dot{W} = 3.2 \times 2.5\text{ kW} = 8.0\text{ kW}$$

*Step 4 — Compare to electric resistance heater:*
Electric resistance heating: COP = 1.0 → 2.5 kW of electricity delivers only 2.5 kW of heat.
Heat pump: 2.5 kW of electricity delivers 8.0 kW of heat → 3.2× more effective.

**Answer:** COP_HP,Carnot = 9.22; Q̇_H = 8.0 kW (heat pump delivers 3.2× more heat than resistance heater)

## Component 3 — Misconception Profiles

### MC-REFRIGERATOR-CREATES-COLD

**Trigger signal:** Student says "the refrigerator makes cold air" or "the refrigerator creates coldness" or thinks the fridge produces something called "cold."

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** "The interior of a fridge at 5°C contains the same molecules as room air at 25°C — they haven't been replaced with 'cold molecules.' What changed is energy: the refrigerator moved thermal energy OUT of the interior. Cold is the absence of thermal energy, not a substance. Measurement: Q_C = 150 J removed from interior; the interior drops in temperature because it lost energy, not because cold was added."

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Heat is what moves — it flows from the cold interior to the hot kitchen via the refrigeration cycle. The interior gets colder because it has LESS thermal energy. 'Cold' is a relative term for low thermal energy, not a substance that enters from somewhere."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Refrigerators move heat, not cold. The cycle extracts thermal energy Q_C from the cold reservoir (interior) and rejects Q_H = Q_C + W to the hot reservoir (room). The interior cools because it loses thermal energy. There is no 'cold substance' — only heat transfer driven by work input."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
| Valid | Invalid |
|---|---|
| "Q_C is heat removed from the cold interior" | "Q_C is cold added to the interior" |
| "The fridge interior cools because heat is removed" | "The fridge interior cools because cold is pumped in" |
| "COP_R = Q_C/W (heat removed per unit work)" | "COP_R = cold created per unit work" |

**s6_path:** Use the energy flow diagram explicitly — show Q_C leaving the interior (arrow out), W entering the compressor, Q_H leaving the condenser coils (arrow to room). Trace energy, not temperature.

---

### MC-COP-CANNOT-EXCEED-ONE

**Trigger signal:** Student is confused that COP > 1 is possible and says "efficiency can't be more than 100%" or thinks COP > 1 violates thermodynamics.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** "COP is NOT efficiency in the heat-engine sense. Efficiency η = W_out/Q_H is bounded by 1 because you can't get more work out than heat in. But COP_R = Q_C/W measures something different: how much heat you MOVE per unit work input. You move Q_C AND also bring along the work energy — so Q_H = Q_C + W > W, and COP = Q_C/W can be >> 1 without violating any law. A typical fridge: COP = 3–5. No contradiction."

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Heat engines convert heat to work: efficiency = output/input ≤ 1 makes sense for a conversion process. Refrigerators move heat using work — you're not converting anything, you're transporting. Moving more heat than the work you spend is perfectly legal thermodynamically — the second law only sets the Carnot ceiling, it doesn't prevent COP > 1."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "COP is not the same as thermodynamic efficiency. COP_R = Q_C/W can exceed 1 because Q_C + W = Q_H — you move Q_C of heat with W of work, and the second law simply requires COP ≤ COP_Carnot = T_C/(T_H−T_C). This Carnot limit is often >> 1 for small temperature differences."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
| Valid | Invalid |
|---|---|
| "COP_R = Q_C/W can be 3, 5, or higher (legal)" | "COP > 1 violates conservation of energy" |
| "Carnot COP sets the maximum, not a value of 1" | "The maximum possible COP is 1" |
| "Heat pump COP_HP = COP_R + 1 always > 1" | "COP > 1 would be a perpetual motion machine" |

**s6_path:** Show the energy balance explicitly: Q_H = Q_C + W. Both Q_C and W enter the hot side. You pay W but move Q_C + W worth of heat. COP = Q_C/W > 1 if Q_C > W, which is normal.

## Component 4 — Practice Set

### P4-a (Retrieval — COP formulas)
Write the COP formula for (a) a refrigerator and (b) a heat pump. Show that COP_HP = COP_R + 1.

**Answer key:**
(a) COP_R = Q_C/W
(b) COP_HP = Q_H/W
Proof: COP_HP − COP_R = Q_H/W − Q_C/W = (Q_H − Q_C)/W = W/W = 1. ∴ COP_HP = COP_R + 1. ✓

---

### P4-b (Application — refrigerator analysis)
A refrigerator has COP_R = 4.5 and consumes 300 W of electrical power. Find: (a) rate of heat removal from the interior, (b) rate of heat rejected to the room.

**Answer key:**
(a) Q̇_C = COP_R × Ẇ = 4.5 × 300 = **1350 W** (1.35 kW removed from interior)
(b) Q̇_H = Q̇_C + Ẇ = 1350 + 300 = **1650 W** rejected to kitchen

---

### P4-c (Carnot limit — refrigerator)
A refrigerator operates between 2°C (interior) and 30°C (kitchen). Find the maximum possible COP_R.

**Answer key:** T_C = 275 K, T_H = 303 K; COP_R,Carnot = 275/(303−275) = 275/28 = **9.82**

---

### P4-d (Multi-step — heat pump vs. electric heater)
A heat pump operates between outdoor air at 0°C and indoor air at 20°C, with an actual COP_HP = 4.0. Compare (a) the Carnot COP_HP, (b) the second-law efficiency, and (c) the power savings vs. a 5 kW electric resistance heater providing the same 20 kW of heat.

**Answer key:**
T_C = 273 K, T_H = 293 K
(a) COP_HP,Carnot = 293/(293−273) = 293/20 = **14.65**
(b) ε_HP = 4.0/14.65 = **0.273 = 27.3%**
(c) Electric heater: P = Q̇_H/COP = 20 kW/1 = 20 kW
Heat pump: P = Q̇_H/COP_HP = 20 kW/4.0 = **5 kW**
Savings: 20 − 5 = **15 kW** (75% reduction in electricity use)

---

### P4-e (Analysis — second law constraint)
A manufacturer claims a refrigerator with T_C = 0°C and T_H = 30°C has COP_R = 12. Is this possible?

**Answer key:**
COP_R,Carnot = 273/(303−273) = 273/30 = 9.1.
Claimed COP = 12 > 9.1 → **impossible**, violates Carnot's theorem (second law). The manufacturer's claim is false.

---

### P4-f (Synthesis — entropy check)
A refrigerator removes heat at rate Q̇_C = 800 W from a cold space at 5°C and rejects Q̇_H = 1100 W to the room at 30°C. Is this cycle reversible, irreversible but legal, or illegal?

**Answer key:**
ΔṠ_cold = −Q̇_C/T_C = −800/278 = −2.878 W/K (entropy removed from cold reservoir)
ΔṠ_hot = +Q̇_H/T_H = +1100/303 = +3.630 W/K (entropy added to hot reservoir)
ΔṠ_universe = −2.878 + 3.630 = +0.752 W/K > 0 → **irreversible but legal** (real refrigerator, generates entropy)
For a reversible (Carnot) refrigerator: ΔṠ_universe = 0 exactly.

## Component 6 — Assessment Items (Mastery Probes)

### AP-1 (Bloom: Remember)
State the Clausius statement of the second law and explain how it applies to refrigerators.

**Answer:** "Heat cannot spontaneously flow from a cold body to a hot body." A refrigerator moves heat from cold to hot — but this is not spontaneous; it requires work input W. Remove the compressor (W = 0) and heat would not flow cold → hot. The second law requires W > 0 for any refrigerator, setting COP < ∞.

---

### AP-2 (Bloom: Understand)
A heat pump and an electric resistance heater both maintain a house at 21°C. The outdoor temperature is 5°C. The heat pump has COP_HP = 3.5. Which device uses less electricity for the same heating output? By what factor?

**Answer:**
Electric heater: COP = 1.0 (all electricity → heat, 1:1)
Heat pump: COP_HP = 3.5 (3.5 J heat per J electricity)
For the same Q_H: the heat pump uses 1/3.5 = 0.286 as much electricity → uses **3.5× less electricity** than the resistance heater.

---

### AP-3 (Bloom: Apply)
A Carnot refrigerator operates between −20°C and 30°C. If it removes 5000 J of heat from the cold reservoir, how much work is required?

**Answer:**
T_C = 253 K, T_H = 303 K
COP_Carnot = 253/(303−253) = 253/50 = 5.06
W = Q_C/COP = 5000/5.06 = **988 J**

---

### AP-4 (Bloom: Analyze)
A refrigerator runs for 1 hour consuming 0.5 kWh of electricity. In that hour, 1.8 kWh of heat is removed from the cold compartment. (a) Calculate COP_R. (b) Calculate Q_H. (c) Suppose T_C = 270 K and T_H = 300 K. Find the Carnot COP and compare.

**Answer:**
W = 0.5 kWh, Q_C = 1.8 kWh
(a) COP_R = 1.8/0.5 = **3.6**
(b) Q_H = Q_C + W = 1.8 + 0.5 = **2.3 kWh**
(c) COP_Carnot = 270/(300−270) = 270/30 = **9.0**
Second-law efficiency = 3.6/9.0 = 40% — the real refrigerator operates at 40% of the Carnot ideal.
