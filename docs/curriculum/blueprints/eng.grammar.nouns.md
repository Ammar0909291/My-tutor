## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-NOUNS-ARE-ONLY-PHYSICAL-THINGS
- **Trigger signal:** Student defines nouns only as physical, touchable objects ("things you can hold"), failing to recognize abstract nouns (ideas, qualities, states — "happiness," "freedom," "honesty") as a legitimate noun category, since they can't be seen or touched.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said nouns are only things you can touch. Can you touch 'happiness'? Can you touch 'freedom'? Do these words still name things — just not physical things?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Nouns name people, places, THINGS, and IDEAS — abstract nouns (happiness, freedom, honesty, courage) name concepts, qualities, or states that can't be physically touched but are still genuine 'things' in a grammatical sense — they can take articles ('the honesty', 'a feeling of freedom') and function as subjects/objects just like concrete nouns."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Check if a word names ANY kind of thing — a person, place, physical object, OR an idea/quality/state — not just physical objects."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "table" (concrete noun, physical object) vs. "happiness" (abstract noun, an idea/state) — both function as nouns grammatically.
  - "book" (concrete) vs. "knowledge" (abstract) — "I have a book" and "I have knowledge" both use the noun in the same grammatical slot.
- **s6_path:** "It's a very natural starting point to think of nouns as physical objects, since those are the easiest to picture — but abstract nouns are just as real a category, and recognizing them is an important expansion of the concept."

### MC-COUNTABLE-VS-UNCOUNTABLE-IS-ARBITRARY
- **Trigger signal:** Student treats the countable/uncountable distinction as random or unlearnable, applying plural forms or "a/an" incorrectly to uncountable nouns (e.g., "an information," "many furnitures") without recognizing the underlying pattern — uncountable nouns typically name substances, abstract concepts, or collections treated as a single mass rather than individually countable units.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said 'many furnitures.' Can you count 'one furniture, two furnitures'? Or do we usually think of furniture as a whole category/mass, like 'water' or 'rice' — things you measure rather than count as separate 'ones'?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Countable nouns name things that can be individually counted (one book, two books). Uncountable nouns typically name substances (water, rice), abstract concepts (information, advice), or categories treated as a whole mass (furniture, luggage) — these don't take plural '-s' or 'a/an' in standard usage, and are quantified differently ('some furniture,' 'a piece of furniture,' 'much information')."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Before pluralizing a noun or adding 'a/an,' check whether it names individually countable units or a mass/substance/abstract category treated as a whole."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "chair" (countable: "one chair, two chairs") vs. "furniture" (uncountable: "furniture," "a piece of furniture," never "a furniture" or "furnitures").
  - "fact" (countable: "one fact, several facts") vs. "information" (uncountable: "information," "a piece of information," never "an information" or "informations").
- **s6_path:** "The countable/uncountable split can feel arbitrary since it's not always predictable from meaning alone — but recognizing the general pattern (substances, abstract concepts, and mass categories tend to be uncountable) makes it much more learnable than pure memorization word-by-word."

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The noun museum**

> Set up four labeled "exhibit" areas: People, Places, Things, Ideas. For each, place example object/picture cards (a person's photo, a picture of a park, a toy, and a card simply labeled with an abstract word like "courage" or "friendship"). Walk through each exhibit, discussing why each item belongs there. For the "Ideas" exhibit, explicitly discuss that these can't be touched but are still real nouns. This anchors the four traditional noun categories (person/place/thing/idea) as a concrete, spatial classification system, including the abstract category.

---

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Concrete Noun Categories (Person, Place, Thing) [C]**

Present a set of words/pictures; student sorts into person, place, or thing categories, building the foundational concrete-noun recognition.

**TA-2 — Abstract Nouns (Ideas, Qualities, States) [C]**

Present a set of abstract nouns (love, freedom, courage, honesty); student identifies them as nouns despite their non-physical nature, directly targeting MC-NOUNS-ARE-ONLY-PHYSICAL-THINGS.

**TA-3 — Common vs. Proper Nouns [C→P]**

Present pairs (dog/Rex, city/Paris, teacher/Ms. Smith); student identifies which is the general/common noun and which is the specific/proper noun (capitalized, naming a particular individual instance).

**TA-4 — Countable vs. Uncountable Nouns [P]**

Present a mixed set of nouns (book, water, chair, rice, idea, furniture); student sorts into countable (can be pluralized/counted individually) vs. uncountable (measured as a mass/substance/category), directly targeting MC-COUNTABLE-VS-UNCOUNTABLE-IS-ARBITRARY.

**TA-5 — Collective Nouns [P]**

Introduce collective nouns (team, flock, family, herd) — singular-form nouns naming a group treated as one unit; student identifies collective nouns and practices appropriate agreement (e.g., "the team IS playing well" vs. sometimes "the team ARE" in certain varieties, noting this is a more advanced/variable pattern).

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — concrete noun categories)**

> "In 'The teacher walked to the school,' which words are nouns, and are they people, places, or things?"

Target response: "teacher" (person), "school" (place).

**WE-2 (Foundational — abstract nouns)**

> "Is 'happiness' a noun? Can you touch it?"

Target response: yes, "happiness" is a noun (an abstract/idea noun); no, it can't be touched, but it still functions as a noun grammatically.

**WE-3 (Intermediate — countable vs. uncountable)**

> "Can you say 'two waters' the way you'd say 'two books'? Why or why not?"

Target response: not in the same way — "water" is typically uncountable (a substance), so you'd say "two glasses of water" rather than "two waters," unlike "book," which is countable.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Concrete noun identification, novel sentence**
"In 'The children played in the park,' identify the nouns and their categories (person/place/thing)."
*Target:* "children" (person), "park" (place).

**MP-2 [P36] — Abstract noun identification, novel word**
"Is 'bravery' a noun? What kind?"
*Target:* yes, an abstract noun.

**MP-3 [P36] — Common vs. proper noun, novel pair**
"In 'My friend Maria lives in Canada,' identify the common nouns and the proper nouns."
*Target:* "friend" (common); "Maria," "Canada" (proper).

**MP-4 [P36] — Countable vs. uncountable, novel word**
"Is 'advice' countable or uncountable? How do you know?"
*Target:* uncountable — you can't say "one advice, two advices"; you'd say "a piece of advice" or "some advice."

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain noun categories**
"In your own words: what are the different kinds of nouns, and can a noun be something you can't touch?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that nouns name people, places, things, and ideas (including abstract, untouchable concepts), and can be further classified as common/proper and countable/uncountable.

**Protocol routing:** S0 (secure on word-classes-overview, new to noun subcategories): dwell on TA-1's concrete categories before introducing abstract nouns. S1 (already identifies concrete nouns confidently, needs subcategory nuance): compress TA-1, focus on TA-2/TA-4's more nuanced distinctions. S6 (frustrated by the seemingly arbitrary countable/uncountable rules): validate that the pattern IS learnable (substances/abstracts/mass-categories tend uncountable) rather than framing it as pure memorization. S9 (L1 has no countable/uncountable grammatical distinction, e.g. many languages don't mark this the same way, or L1 has different countability defaults for cognate concepts, e.g. "information" is countable in some languages): expect TA-4 to be a genuinely difficult, high-value area requiring explicit contrastive practice, since countability mismatches between L1 and English are a very common, persistent source of learner errors ("informations," "advices," "furnitures").

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Abstract nouns are a core category, not an edge case**: always include abstract nouns explicitly alongside concrete ones from early instruction, rather than treating them as an advanced afterthought — this directly counters the intuitive "nouns = physical things" default.
- **Countable/uncountable has a learnable pattern, not just an exception list**: teach the general semantic pattern (substances, abstracts, mass-categories tend uncountable) as a genuine aid, even though the mapping isn't 100% predictable from meaning alone.
- **Proper noun capitalization should be taught alongside the common/proper distinction**: reinforce the visual/orthographic marker (capitalization) as a concrete signal supporting the common/proper category, useful for both reading and writing.
- **Countability transfer errors are extremely common and worth dedicated practice**: for S9 learners, budget significant explicit practice time for the specific high-frequency uncountable nouns that are commonly miscounted (information, advice, furniture, luggage, news) since these transfer errors are persistent and well-documented across many L1 backgrounds.
