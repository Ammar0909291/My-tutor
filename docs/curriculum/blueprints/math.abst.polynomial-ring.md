## Component 1 — Learning Objectives

- LO1: Define $R[x]$ — polynomials with coefficients drawn from an arbitrary ring $R$ (not just $\mathbb{R}$), under standard polynomial addition and multiplication — as itself satisfying the ring axioms R1-R5 from `math.abst.ring-theory`, verified directly by reusing that concept's own axiom-checking procedure applied to $R[x]$'s two operations.
- LO2: State that $F[x]$ (coefficients in a FIELD $F$) is a **Euclidean domain**, admitting a **polynomial division algorithm** (division with remainder, remainder's degree strictly less than the divisor's), and recognize that this depends CRITICALLY on $F$ being a field — a division that requires inverting a leading coefficient can fail entirely over a general ring $R$ that is not a field.
- LO3 (orientation level — full ideal theory beyond this corpus's current scope): recognize that an **irreducible** polynomial in $F[x]$ generates a **maximal** ideal, illustrated concretely by $\langle x^2+1\rangle$ in $\mathbb{R}[x]$ (whose quotient ring is exactly $\mathbb{C}$), contrasted with a **reducible** polynomial's ideal, which is never maximal.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Check

Assumes mastery of `math.abst.ring-theory` (a ring $(R,+,\cdot)$ satisfies axioms R1 through R5 — $(R,+)$ an abelian group, $\cdot$ closed and associative, and distributivity — with multiplicative identity, commutativity, and inverses all OPTIONAL extras, not definitional requirements).

## Component 3 — Core Explanation

**From real coefficients to an arbitrary ring $R$**: `math.alg.polynomial` studied polynomials $a_nx^n+\cdots+a_0$ with REAL-number coefficients, noting they are closed under addition and multiplication. $R[x]$ generalizes this directly: the coefficients $a_i$ are drawn from ANY ring $R$ (not necessarily $\mathbb{R}$, not necessarily even commutative or containing multiplicative inverses), with addition and multiplication of polynomials defined exactly the same way (add/multiply coefficients of matching-degree terms, using $R$'s own $+$ and $\cdot$). Verifying $R[x]$ satisfies the ring axioms R1-R5 uses the EXACT SAME checking procedure `math.abst.ring-theory` already established — the only new content is applying that procedure to this specific, richer object.

**$F[x]$ is a Euclidean domain — but this needs $F$ to be a FIELD**: when the coefficient ring is specifically a FIELD $F$ (every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse), $F[x]$ supports a **polynomial division algorithm**: for any $f,g\in F[x]$ with $g\ne0$, there exist unique $q,r\in F[x]$ with $f=qg+r$ and $\deg(r)<\deg(g)$ — exactly like ordinary long division of integers, but for polynomials. This division process, at each step, needs to DIVIDE by the divisor's leading coefficient — an operation that requires that coefficient to have a multiplicative inverse. If $R$ is NOT a field (some nonzero element lacks an inverse), this division can genuinely FAIL to produce a polynomial quotient with coefficients still in $R$ — the algorithm is not merely harder over a general ring, it can be entirely unavailable.

**Irreducibility and maximal ideals**: an irreducible polynomial in $F[x]$ (one that cannot be factored into two lower-degree polynomials over $F$) generates a **maximal ideal** — an ideal (per `math.abst.ring-theory`'s own ideal concept) not properly contained in any ideal other than $F[x]$ itself. A maximal ideal's quotient ring is always a FIELD, so factoring $F[x]$ by an irreducible polynomial's ideal produces a genuinely new field — a powerful, constructive way to BUILD field extensions directly from polynomial factorization. A REDUCIBLE polynomial's ideal, by contrast, is never maximal — it sits properly inside a larger ideal generated by one of its own factors.

## Component 4 — Worked Examples

**Example 1 (LO1 — verifying ring axioms for $R[x]$ over a non-field $R$)**: Let $R=\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$ (integers mod $4$, a ring where $2$ has no multiplicative inverse). In $R[x]$, take $f(x)=2x+1$, $g(x)=3x+2$. Addition: $f+g = 5x+3 \equiv 1x+3\pmod4$ (since $5\equiv1$). Multiplication: $f\cdot g = (2x+1)(3x+2) = 6x^2+4x+3x+2 = 6x^2+7x+2 \equiv 2x^2+3x+2\pmod4$ (since $6\equiv2$, $7\equiv3$). Both results stay in $R[x]$ (coefficients reduced mod $4$), directly confirming closure — the same axiom-checking procedure from `math.abst.ring-theory`, applied here to polynomials rather than numbers.

**Example 2 (LO1/LO2 — x has no inverse even in a field's polynomial ring, and division fails over a non-field, breaking MC-1 and MC-2)**: In $\mathbb{Q}[x]$ ($\mathbb{Q}$ IS a field): does $x$ have a multiplicative inverse? That would require some $p(x)\in\mathbb{Q}[x]$ with $x\cdot p(x)=1$ — but for nonzero polynomials over a field, $\deg(x\cdot p(x)) = \deg(x)+\deg(p(x)) \ge 1$, while $\deg(1)=0$ — no such $p(x)$ can exist. So $x$ has **NO** inverse in $\mathbb{Q}[x]$, confirming $\mathbb{Q}[x]$ is **NOT** a field, even though $\mathbb{Q}$ itself is one. Now the division algorithm: in $\mathbb{Q}[x]$, dividing $x^2+3x+2$ by $x+1$ gives $x^2+3x+2=(x+1)(x+2)+0$ — clean, exact division. Contrast this with $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ ($\mathbb{Z}$ is NOT a field): dividing $x^2$ by $2x$ would require a quotient $q(x)=x/2$ — **not** a polynomial with integer coefficients at all — the division algorithm genuinely fails here, precisely because $2$ has no inverse in $\mathbb{Z}$.

**Example 3 (LO3 — irreducible generates maximal, reducible does not, orientation level)**: In $\mathbb{R}[x]$, $x^2+1$ is **irreducible** (no real roots, so it cannot be factored into two degree-$1$ real polynomials). The ideal $\langle x^2+1\rangle$ (all real-polynomial multiples of $x^2+1$) is **maximal**, and the quotient ring $\mathbb{R}[x]/\langle x^2+1\rangle$ is exactly $\mathbb{C}$ — setting $x^2+1=0$ (i.e. $x^2=-1$) forces $x$ to behave exactly like $i$, constructing the complex numbers directly from real polynomials factored by this one irreducible polynomial's ideal. Contrast with $x^2-1=(x-1)(x+1)$, **reducible** in $\mathbb{R}[x]$: the ideal $\langle x^2-1\rangle$ is **NOT** maximal — it sits properly inside the larger ideal $\langle x-1\rangle$, since every multiple of $x^2-1$ is automatically a multiple of $x-1$ too.

## Component 5 — Teaching Actions

### Teaching Action A01 — R[x] Is a Ring, Verified the Same Way as Always (Primitive P11: Representation Shift)

State: "`math.alg.polynomial` used real coefficients; $R[x]$ just swaps in ANY ring $R$ for the coefficients — and you check the ring axioms for $R[x]$ using the EXACT SAME procedure you already know from `math.abst.ring-theory`." Work Example 1's full addition and multiplication computation in $(\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z})[x]$.

### Teaching Action A02 — x Has No Inverse Even Over a Field, and Division Needs a Field (Primitive P28: Conflict Evidence)

Present Example 2's direct evidence: $x$ has no multiplicative inverse in $\mathbb{Q}[x]$ (proven via a degree argument), so $\mathbb{Q}[x]$ is not a field despite $\mathbb{Q}$ being one; and dividing $x^2$ by $2x$ in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ fails to produce an integer-coefficient quotient at all. State: "$R[x]$ never automatically inherits every property of $R$ — being a field is one property it NEVER gets, no matter how nice $R$ is; and the division algorithm specifically needs to invert leading coefficients, which requires $F$ to be a field."

- **MC-1 hook**: ask "if $F$ is a field, is $F[x]$ also automatically a field?" — a "yes" answer reveals MC-1 (assuming $R[x]$ inherits all of $R$'s properties, missing that $x$ itself can never have an inverse in any polynomial ring).
- **MC-2 hook**: ask "does the polynomial division algorithm (division with remainder) work over any ring $R[x]$, not just a field $F[x]$?" — a "yes" answer reveals MC-2 (missing that division specifically requires inverting the divisor's leading coefficient, which needs a field).

### Teaching Action A03 — Irreducible Generates Maximal; Reducible Does Not (Primitive P06: Contrast Pair)

Contrast Example 3's $x^2+1$ (irreducible, generating a maximal ideal, quotient $=\mathbb{C}$) against $x^2-1$ (reducible, its ideal properly contained in $\langle x-1\rangle$, not maximal). State: "irreducibility in $F[x]$ isn't just 'can't factor it' as an isolated fact — it directly determines whether the resulting quotient ring is a field, which is exactly what 'maximal ideal' means."

- **MC-3 hook**: ask "does an irreducible polynomial's ideal being 'maximal' mean something stronger than just 'prime,' or is 'irreducible generates a prime ideal' already the whole story in F[x]?" — an answer that treats "prime" as the full story reveals MC-3 (missing that in $F[x]$ specifically, irreducible generates the STRONGER property of a maximal ideal, not merely a prime one).

### Teaching Action A04 — Mastery Gate (Primitive P91)

P91 = P77 → P55 → P76 → P55 → P75 → P55 → P74 → P55 → P78.

- **P77 (4-problem set)**:
  1. In $(\mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z})[x]$, compute $(2x+3)+(4x+4)$ and $(2x+3)(4x+4)$, reducing coefficients mod 5.
  2. Explain why $x$ has no multiplicative inverse in $\mathbb{R}[x]$, using the same degree argument as Example 2.
  3. Attempt to divide $x^2+1$ by $3x$ in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, and explain why the division algorithm fails to produce an integer-coefficient quotient.
  4. Is $x^2+4$ irreducible or reducible in $\mathbb{R}[x]$? State whether the ideal it generates is maximal, and if reducible, name a larger ideal containing it.
- **P76 (Transfer Probe, mode = cross-link probe against `math.alg.polynomial`)**: "Recall from `math.alg.polynomial` that real-coefficient polynomials are closed under addition, subtraction, and multiplication, forming a ring — and that a degree-$n$ polynomial has at most $n$ real roots. (a) Explain precisely how $R[x]$'s definition in this lesson generalizes that concept's real-coefficient polynomials to an arbitrary ring $R$, and what stays EXACTLY the same in the process (which operations, which closure properties). (b) That lesson's root-count theorem was stated specifically for real (or complex) coefficients. Explain why the argument that $x$ has no multiplicative inverse in $\mathbb{Q}[x]$ (a degree-counting argument) would work identically for $\mathbb{R}[x]$ or $\mathbb{C}[x]$, but would need MORE care to state correctly over a general ring $R$ that is not a field or integral domain. (c) Using $\mathbb{R}[x]/\langle x^2+1\rangle\cong\mathbb{C}$ from this lesson, explain in your own words how factoring by an irreducible polynomial's ideal can be seen as a natural extension of `math.alg.polynomial`'s own root-finding concerns — turning 'this polynomial has no real root' into 'this polynomial's ideal builds a new field where it DOES have a root.'"
- **P55 (Score)** after each stage.
- **P75 (Mastery Assessment against MAMR = 4/5)**.
- **P74 (Routing Decision)**: MAMR met → P78 (Completion). MAMR not met → route to the specific missed primitive's repair action before re-attempting P77.

## Component 6 — Misconception Registry

| ID | Label | Description | Severity |
|---|---|---|---|
| MC-1 | POLYNOMIAL-RING-ASSUMED-TO-INHERIT-FIELD-PROPERTY | Believing R[x] is automatically a field whenever R is a field, missing that x itself never has a multiplicative inverse in any polynomial ring | Foundational |
| MC-2 | DIVISION-ALGORITHM-ASSUMED-TO-WORK-OVER-ANY-RING | Believing the polynomial division algorithm (division with remainder) works over any ring R[x], missing that it specifically requires the coefficient ring to be a field, to invert leading coefficients | Foundational |
| MC-3 | IRREDUCIBLE-IDEAL-ASSUMED-ONLY-PRIME-NOT-MAXIMAL | Treating "irreducible polynomial generates a prime ideal" as the complete fact, missing that in F[x] specifically, irreducible generates the stronger property of a maximal ideal | Moderate |

### Protocol B — Repair Actions

- **B01 (targets MC-1)**: P27 (name it: "Polynomial Ring Assumed to Inherit Field Property") → P41 (detect: ask a student whether $F[x]$ is a field whenever $F$ is, and check for a "yes") → P64 (conceptual shift: re-walk Example 2's degree argument showing $x$ has no inverse in $\mathbb{Q}[x]$, re-anchoring on "a polynomial ring never becomes a field, no matter how nice the coefficient ring is — $x$ itself is always a permanent obstruction").
- **B02 (targets MC-2)**: P27 (name it: "Division Algorithm Assumed to Work Over Any Ring") → P41 (detect: ask a student to divide two polynomials in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ where the divisor's leading coefficient isn't $\pm1$, and check whether they expect a clean integer-coefficient quotient regardless) → P64 (conceptual shift: re-walk Example 2's failed $x^2\div2x$ computation in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, re-anchoring on "the division algorithm needs to INVERT the divisor's leading coefficient at each step — that's only guaranteed possible over a field").
- **B03 (targets MC-3)**: P27 (name it: "Irreducible Ideal Assumed Only Prime, Not Maximal") → P41 (detect: ask a student what kind of ideal an irreducible polynomial generates in $F[x]$, and check for an answer that stops at "prime") → P64 (conceptual shift: re-walk Example 3's $x^2+1$ case, showing the quotient $\mathbb{R}[x]/\langle x^2+1\rangle\cong\mathbb{C}$ is a genuine FIELD, re-anchoring on "maximal ideal means the quotient is a field — that's the stronger, specific fact for irreducibles in F[x]").

## Component 8 — Teaching Notes

- estimated_hours = 5 with an expert/apply tag places this at a "3 TAs + gate" tier — A01 (verifying $R[x]$'s ring axioms), A02 (the field-property and division-algorithm failures), and A03 (irreducible vs. reducible ideals) each target a distinct LO, appropriate for a concept that directly generalizes one already-mastered concrete case (`math.alg.polynomial`'s real coefficients) using one already-mastered abstract framework (`math.abst.ring-theory`'s axioms).
- LO3 is deliberately grounded in ONE fully-worked, canonical concrete instance ($\mathbb{R}[x]/\langle x^2+1\rangle\cong\mathbb{C}$, the standard first example in every abstract algebra course) rather than the general theory of maximal ideals in arbitrary rings, since the general ideal-lattice theory is a substantially larger topic than this concept's estimated_hours=5 can support in full generality.
- Examples 1 and 2 were deliberately built using DIFFERENT coefficient rings ($\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$, then $\mathbb{Q}$ and $\mathbb{Z}$) specifically to demonstrate that the ring-axiom-verification procedure (Example 1) and the field-dependence of stronger properties (Example 2) are genuinely separate concerns — $R[x]$ is ALWAYS a ring regardless of $R$, but properties like "has a division algorithm" depend sharply on whether $R$ is a field.
