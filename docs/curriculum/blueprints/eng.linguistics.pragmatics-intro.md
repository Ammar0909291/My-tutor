**Concept:** Introduction to Pragmatics — the study of how CONTEXT shapes meaning in actual language
use, beyond a sentence's literal, context-independent semantic meaning: what a speaker actually
INTENDS and what a listener actually INFERS in a specific situation.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Semantics (prerequisite) taught literal, context-
independent sentence meaning and previewed the semantics/pragmatics boundary. Pragmatics develops that
boundary fully: a speaker's INTENDED meaning in context often differs systematically from a sentence's
literal semantic meaning (e.g., "Can you pass the salt?" is literally a question about ability, but
pragmatically a request), and these differences follow predictable patterns, not random guesswork.
This concept unlocks `eng.linguistics.discourse-analysis-intro` (analyzing how meaning is constructed
across multi-turn conversations builds on single-utterance pragmatic inference) and
`eng.linguistics.sociolinguistics-intro` (how social context shapes language use draws directly on
pragmatic principles).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) distinguish a sentence's literal semantic meaning
from its pragmatically INTENDED meaning in a given context, using the classic indirect-speech-act
example, (b) explain the concept of an implicature — meaning that is conveyed but not literally
stated, inferred by the listener based on conversational expectations — and identify at least one
example, and (c) explain that successful pragmatic inference depends on SHARED context and
conversational expectations between speaker and listener, which is why the same literal sentence can
be interpreted correctly in one context and misunderstood in another.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-WHAT-A-SPEAKER-LITERALLY-SAYS-IS-ALWAYS-EXACTLY-WHAT-THEY-MEAN

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes a speaker's literal, semantic sentence meaning is always
identical to their actual communicative intent, failing to recognize indirect speech acts — where a
speaker uses a sentence with one literal meaning (a question about ability) to accomplish a different
communicative purpose (a request) — e.g., interpreting "Can you pass the salt?" purely as a literal
yes/no question about physical ability, missing that it functions as a request.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student the sentence "Can you pass the salt?" asked at a dinner
table, and ask: "Is the speaker actually just curious about whether you're PHYSICALLY CAPABLE of
passing the salt, or are they asking you to actually pass it? If someone literally just answered 'Yes,
I can' and did nothing else, would that satisfy what the speaker wanted?" Guide the student to see
the literal question and the actual communicative intent (a polite request) are systematically
different.

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming literal meaning always equals intended meaning is like assuming a
knock on a door is JUST the physical sound of knuckles on wood, with no communicative purpose beyond
that — but everyone understands a knock is actually a request ('let me in' or 'I'm here'), a
conventional signal that goes beyond its literal physical nature. 'Can you pass the salt?' works the
same way: its literal form (a question about ability) is a conventional way of accomplishing a
different communicative act (a request)."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When interpreting an utterance, ask: is the speaker's literal, semantic
question or statement actually what they intend to communicate, or are they using a conventional
INDIRECT form to accomplish something else (like a request, a suggestion, or a criticism)? Context and
convention often signal this gap between literal form and actual intent."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Can you pass the salt?" answered literally with just "Yes" and no action (missing the indirect
   request) vs. correctly recognizing this as a conventional indirect request and passing the salt
   (correct pragmatic inference).
2. A student who cannot explain why "It's cold in here" at a party often functions as an indirect
   request to close a window (missing the pragmatic layer) vs. a student who recognizes the literal
   statement about temperature is being used to accomplish a request (correct pragmatic
   understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to interpret utterances purely literally after the bridge, give
them three more indirect speech act examples ("Do you know what time it is?", "I wonder if there's
any coffee left", "This soup needs salt") and have them identify the actual communicative intent
behind each literal form.

### MC-B-IMPLICATURE-MEANS-THE-SAME-AS-LITERAL-VAGUENESS-OR-JUST-BEING-UNCLEAR

**Trigger signal:** Student conflates "implicature" (meaning that is systematically, predictably
conveyed but not literally stated, inferred based on shared conversational expectations) with simple
vagueness, ambiguity, or unclear communication — failing to recognize that implicature is a precise,
RELIABLE, and predictable inference a competent listener draws, not a fuzzy guess about unclear
speech.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student an exchange: "Are you coming to the party?" "I have to
work that night." Ask: "Did the second speaker literally say 'no'? Yet would almost any listener
confidently infer 'no' from this response? Is this confident, reliable inference the same thing as
the response being vague or unclear?" Guide the student to see that the inference ("no, I'm not
coming") is actually highly reliable and predictable, based on the conversational expectation that an
answer should be relevant to the question — this is the OPPOSITE of vague or unclear communication.

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Confusing implicature with vagueness is like confusing a chess player's subtle
but deliberate strategic move with a random, confused mistake — both might not be the 'obvious' most
direct move, but one is a precise, calculated communication within the rules of the game (the
conversational expectation that answers should be relevant), while genuine vagueness is actually
unclear or ambiguous. Implicature is a RELIABLE inference competent listeners confidently draw, not
fuzzy guesswork."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Implicature is meaning that's reliably CONVEYED but not literally
STATED, based on a shared expectation that speakers will be relevant, informative, and cooperative.
Test it: would a competent listener confidently and consistently draw this inference, or is the
statement genuinely ambiguous with no clear intended meaning? Only the former is implicature."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "I have to work that night." in response to a party invitation (reliably implicates 'no,' a
   genuine, confident implicature) vs. a genuinely ambiguous statement with no clear intended
   meaning that different listeners would interpret in incompatible ways (actual vagueness, not
   implicature).
2. A student treating any indirect or non-literal response as "just being vague" (misconception) vs.
   a student recognizing that a specific indirect response reliably conveys a precise inferred
   meaning that virtually any competent listener would draw (correct understanding of implicature).

**s6_path:** If the student continues conflating implicature with vagueness after the bridge, have
them test three example exchanges by asking "would ten different listeners all draw the SAME
inference from this response?" — a consistent yes across listeners indicates genuine, reliable
implicature; genuine disagreement indicates actual ambiguity.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.linguistics.semantics-intro`):** "State the literal semantic meaning of 'Can you
pass the salt?' separately from how it might actually be used." A student who cannot separate literal
meaning from contextual use lacks the foundational distinction this concept develops — route to
`eng.linguistics.semantics-intro` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Door-Knock-and-Chess-Move Anchor.** Combine two images: a knock on a door, understood by
everyone as a conventional signal ("let me in") that goes beyond its literal physical sound — directly
seeding the fix for MC-A (literal form and communicative intent can systematically differ). Second, a
chess player's subtle, deliberate strategic move, precise and rule-governed despite not being the most
"obvious" direct move — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (implicature is a precise, reliable
inference, not vague or unclear communication).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Literal Meaning vs. Speaker Intent: Indirect Speech Acts (MC-A)

Using the door-knock anchor, teach the concept of indirect speech acts, where a sentence's literal
form (a question, a statement) conventionally accomplishes a different communicative purpose (a
request, a suggestion). Student practice: given four common indirect speech act examples, identify the
literal meaning and the actual communicative intent for each.

### TA-2 — The Cooperative Principle: Why Listeners Can Reliably Infer Beyond the Literal

Introduce the basic idea that conversation relies on speakers being expected to be relevant,
informative, and cooperative, which is what allows listeners to reliably infer meaning beyond the
literal words. Student practice: given three conversational exchanges, identify what conversational
expectation (relevance, sufficient information) is being relied upon for the inference to work.

### TA-3 — Implicature as Reliable, Not Vague (MC-B)

Using the chess-move anchor and the "would ten listeners agree" test, teach that implicature is a
precise, reliable inference, distinct from genuine ambiguity. Student practice: given four exchanges
(some genuine implicature, some genuinely ambiguous), classify each and justify using the
ten-listeners test.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Analyzing Context-Dependent Meaning

Give the student a new conversational exchange or short dialogue. They must identify any indirect
speech act present (TA-1), explain the conversational expectation enabling the inference (TA-2), and
confirm whether an implicature present is genuinely reliable rather than merely ambiguous (TA-3) —
the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Exchange: "Did you like the movie?" "Well, the popcorn was great." Fully
worked: literal meaning of the response identified (a statement about popcorn quality, not literally
about the movie); communicative intent inferred (likely a negative or lukewarm opinion of the movie
itself, avoiding a direct negative statement); conversational expectation identified (the response
should be relevant to the question asked — if the speaker liked the movie, a relevant answer would
address the movie directly); confirmed via the ten-listeners test that most listeners would draw the
same inference (genuine implicature, not vagueness). Annotation confirms both the indirect-meaning
identification (fixing MC-A) and the reliability confirmation (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New exchange: "Are you hungry?" "I just ate a huge lunch." Student is prompted
with guiding questions ("What's the literal content of this response? What does it likely imply about
whether they're hungry? What conversational expectation makes this inference reliable? Would most
listeners draw the same conclusion, or is this genuinely ambiguous?") to complete the analysis.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new conversational exchange, unaided. Student identifies any indirect
speech act, explains the enabling conversational expectation, and confirms the reliability of any
implicature present.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying Indirect Speech Acts**
Given three sentences with conventional indirect uses, identify the literal meaning and the likely
actual communicative intent for each.

**MP-2 [P36] — Explaining the Enabling Conversational Expectation**
Given a conversational exchange relying on implicature, identify what conversational expectation
(relevance, sufficient information) makes the inference possible.

**MP-3 [P36] — Testing Implicature Reliability**
Given two exchanges (one genuine implicature, one genuinely ambiguous), apply the "would ten
listeners agree" test to classify each correctly.

**MP-4 [P36] — Distinguishing Implicature from Vagueness**
Given a statement, determine whether a specific inferred meaning is a reliable implicature or
whether the statement is actually too ambiguous to support a confident inference.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Contextual Meaning Analysis**
Given a new conversational exchange, identify any indirect speech act, explain the enabling
conversational expectation, and confirm whether any implicature present is genuinely reliable.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters isolated indirect-speech-act identification (MP-1) but struggles to explain
  WHY the inference is reliable (MP-2), this indicates the cooperative-principle mechanism needs more
  concrete grounding — return to the door-knock anchor's "conventional signal" framing rather than
  advancing to more examples.
- If the student is multilingual and their other language(s) have different indirect-request
  conventions than English (some languages are more direct, others more indirect in comparable
  situations), leverage this as a powerful comparative example, since pragmatic conventions are
  notably variable across languages and cultures even when the underlying cooperative principle is
  universal.
- If the student conflates politeness or social tact with pragmatics broadly (assuming pragmatics is
  just "being polite"), clarify that pragmatics is the broader systematic study of context-dependent
  meaning, of which politeness-motivated indirectness is just one application.
- Students who found the cross-linked `eng.vocab.idioms` concept engaging (if completed) may
  transfer well to this concept's non-literal-meaning focus — draw the connection explicitly, since
  idioms are a related but distinct phenomenon (conventionalized non-literal meaning at the phrase
  level, versus pragmatic inference at the utterance/context level).
