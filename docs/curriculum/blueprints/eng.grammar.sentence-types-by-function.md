## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-PUNCTUATION-MARK-ALONE-DEFINES-SENTENCE-TYPE
- **Trigger signal:** Student classifies sentence type purely by the ending punctuation mark rather than by the sentence's grammatical FUNCTION/STRUCTURE, missing that the same content can sometimes be punctuated differently for emphasis, and that function (statement, question, command, exclamation) is the true defining feature, with punctuation as a supporting signal, not the sole determinant (e.g., "Stop!" is an imperative regardless of whether it ends in "!" or "." — "Stop." is still a command/imperative).
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said 'Stop!' is exclamatory because it has an exclamation point. But is this sentence actually giving a command (telling someone to do something), or expressing strong emotion about a fact? What if I wrote 'Stop.' instead — would it still be a command?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Sentence type is defined by grammatical FUNCTION — declarative (states a fact), interrogative (asks a question), imperative (gives a command, often with an implied 'you' subject), and exclamatory (expresses strong emotion) — not simply by which punctuation mark appears at the end. Punctuation supports and signals the function, but a command stays a command whether it ends in a period or an exclamation point; the underlying structure and purpose is what actually defines the type."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Determine sentence type by its grammatical structure and communicative purpose (statement, question, command, strong emotion) first, then treat punctuation as a supporting signal, not the sole test."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "Stop!" (imperative — a command, punctuated with emphasis) vs. "Stop." (still imperative — a command, just less emphatic punctuation) — same function, different punctuation.
  - "What a wonderful day!" (exclamatory — expresses strong feeling) vs. "What is today?" (interrogative — asks a genuine question, despite both starting with "What").
- **s6_path:** "Punctuation is a very visible, easy-to-check signal, so it's natural to rely on it heavily — but the underlying grammatical function (and communicative purpose) is what truly defines the sentence type, with punctuation as a helpful but secondary clue."

### MC-QUESTIONS-ALWAYS-START-WITH-A-QUESTION-WORD
- **Trigger signal:** Student assumes all interrogative sentences must start with a wh-word (who, what, where, when, why, how), missing yes/no questions that invert the subject and auxiliary verb without any question word at all ("Are you coming?" "Did she leave?" "Can they help?") — these are equally genuine interrogative sentences.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said questions always start with a question word like 'who' or 'what.' Does 'Are you coming?' start with one of those words? Is it still a question?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Interrogative sentences (questions) can be formed in TWO main ways: with a wh-word at the start (What is your name?) OR by inverting the subject and auxiliary/helping verb with no question word at all (Are you ready? Did she call? Can he swim?) — both are genuine, equally common question structures."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Recognize a sentence as interrogative if it either starts with a wh-word OR inverts the subject and auxiliary verb, not just the wh-word pattern alone."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "What time is it?" (wh-question) vs. "Is it late?" (yes/no question via subject-auxiliary inversion, no question word).
  - "Where did you go?" (wh-question) vs. "Did you go somewhere?" (yes/no question, inversion only).
- **s6_path:** "Wh-questions are often taught and noticed first since the question word is such a clear signal — but yes/no questions (formed by inversion alone) are just as common and genuinely interrogative, even without a question word."

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The four-function sorting boxes**

> Set up four labeled boxes: "Tells (Declarative)," "Asks (Interrogative)," "Commands (Imperative)," "Exclaims (Exclamatory)." Present a set of sentence cards (some varying only in ending punctuation, e.g., "Stop." and "Stop!") and sort each by its FUNCTION, discussing cases where punctuation might seem to suggest one type but the underlying structure/purpose reveals another.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Declarative Sentences (Statements) [C]**

Present sentences making statements; student identifies them as declarative and notes the standard period ending.

**TA-2 — Interrogative Sentences (Both Types) [C]**

Present both wh-questions and yes/no (inversion) questions; student identifies both as interrogative, directly targeting MC-QUESTIONS-ALWAYS-START-WITH-A-QUESTION-WORD.

**TA-3 — Imperative Sentences (Commands) [C→P]**

Present commands/requests, including some with implied "you" subject ("Close the door" = "[You] close the door"); student identifies these as imperative regardless of politeness level or punctuation.

**TA-4 — Exclamatory Sentences (Strong Emotion) [P]**

Present sentences expressing strong emotion; student identifies these as exclamatory and distinguishes them from a similarly-punctuated imperative or declarative sentence, directly targeting MC-PUNCTUATION-MARK-ALONE-DEFINES-SENTENCE-TYPE.

**TA-5 — Classifying Mixed Sentence Sets by Function, Not Punctuation Alone [P]**

Present a mixed set including sentences where punctuation could mislead (a command ending in a period; an exclamatory statement); student classifies each by underlying function, integrating all four types.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — declarative)**

> "What type of sentence is 'The sun is shining today'?"

Target response: declarative — it states a fact.

**WE-2 (Foundational — both interrogative types)**

> "Are 'What is your name?' and 'Is she coming?' both interrogative, even though only one has a question word?"

Target response: yes — both are interrogative; one uses a wh-word, the other uses subject-auxiliary inversion.

**WE-3 (Intermediate — punctuation vs. function)**

> "Is 'Please sit down.' a command, even though it ends in a period, not an exclamation point?"

Target response: yes — it's imperative (a command/request), regardless of the period ending.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Declarative, novel sentence**
"What type is 'The library closes at 9pm'?"
*Target:* declarative.

**MP-2 [P36] — Interrogative, novel yes/no question**
"Is 'Have you finished?' interrogative, even without a wh-word?"
*Target:* yes — interrogative via subject-auxiliary inversion.

**MP-3 [P36] — Imperative, novel example**
"What type is 'Turn off the lights before you leave'?"
*Target:* imperative — a command with an implied "you" subject.

**MP-4 [P36] — Exclamatory vs. punctuation-alone judgment**
"Is 'What a beautiful sunset!' exclamatory or interrogative, even though it starts with 'What'?"
*Target:* exclamatory — despite starting with "What," it expresses strong emotion/reaction rather than asking a genuine question.

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain the four sentence types**
"In your own words: what are the four sentence types by function, and does punctuation alone determine the type?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates the four types (declarative, interrogative, imperative, exclamatory) defined by grammatical function/purpose, and that punctuation supports but doesn't solely determine the type — and that questions can be formed either with a wh-word or via subject-auxiliary inversion.

**Protocol routing:** S0 (secure on word order, new to sentence-function classification): dwell on TA-1's declarative sentences before introducing the more nuanced interrogative/imperative/exclamatory distinctions. ## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Function over punctuation is the central lesson**: every classification task should require identifying grammatical structure/purpose first, with punctuation treated as a secondary, supporting signal — directly countering the natural punctuation-first shortcut.
- **Both question-formation types deserve equal, explicit attention**: wh-questions and inversion-based yes/no questions are equally legitimate and common — don't let the more visually obvious wh-word pattern dominate instruction.
- **Implied subjects in imperatives are a genuine structural feature worth naming**: explicitly teach that imperative sentences have an implied "you" subject, connecting this concept back to the subject-and-predicate foundation.
- **Subject-auxiliary inversion for questions is a distinctive English structural feature**: for S9 learners from L1s using question particles or intonation-only marking, treat this as a genuinely novel structural pattern requiring dedicated practice, not an assumed intuitive transfer.
