**Concept:** Media Literacy — critically analyzing and evaluating messages, sources, and persuasive/
production TECHNIQUES used across print, broadcast, and digital media, recognizing that all media
(including news) is PRODUCED by someone with choices, purposes, and often commercial or persuasive
interests, not a neutral, unmediated window onto reality.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Evaluating Sources (prerequisite) taught assessing the
credibility of written sources generally. Media Literacy extends this specifically to the broader
media landscape (print, broadcast, digital, social), teaching students to recognize PRODUCTION
techniques (framing, selection, editing choices) that shape a message, and to distinguish between
media that is TRYING to inform versus media that is primarily trying to persuade, entertain, or
generate engagement/profit — often while appearing to simply "show what happened." This concept has
no further downstream unlocks in the current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) explain that all media messages are CONSTRUCTED
through deliberate choices (what to include, how to frame it, which images or clips to select) rather
than simply, neutrally reflecting reality, (b) identify the likely PURPOSE and business/incentive
model behind a media message (to inform, persuade, entertain, or generate engagement/advertising
revenue) and explain how that purpose can shape the content, and (c) evaluate a media message for signs
of selective framing or missing context, without assuming that emotionally engaging or attention-
grabbing content is automatically less trustworthy, or that plain, unemotional content is automatically
more trustworthy.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-NEWS-AND-MEDIA-COVERAGE-SIMPLY-SHOWS-WHAT-HAPPENED-WITH-NO-CONSTRUCTED-CHOICES-INVOLVED

**Trigger signal:** Student treats media coverage (a news report, a documentary segment, a social
media post) as a neutral, direct window onto reality, failing to recognize that EVERY media message
involves deliberate PRODUCTION CHOICES — what footage or facts to include, how to frame the story,
which quotes to select, what headline to use — that shape what the audience actually experiences,
even when the underlying facts reported are accurate.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two headlines describing the identical underlying event
(e.g., a protest) with different framing choices ("Protesters Clash with Police" vs. "Community Members
Demand Accountability"), both technically accurate but emphasizing different aspects. Ask: "Do these
two headlines describe the same actual event? Do they create the same overall impression in a reader
who only sees the headline?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming media coverage simply shows what happened, with no constructed
choices, is like assuming a photograph taken from a specific angle, with a specific framing, shows the
'whole' unedited reality — but the photographer's choice of angle, what's included in the frame, and
what's cropped out are all deliberate decisions that shape what the viewer sees, even if nothing in the
photo itself is technically 'faked.' Media coverage similarly involves constructed choices — framing,
selection, emphasis — at every stage, even when reporting genuinely accurate facts."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When evaluating any media message, ask: what SPECIFIC choices (framing,
word choice, what's included or left out) were made in producing this? Even accurate, fact-based
coverage involves deliberate construction, not a neutral, unmediated view of reality."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Protesters Clash with Police" (a framing choice emphasizing conflict) vs. "Community Members
   Demand Accountability" (a framing choice emphasizing the protesters' cause) — both describing the
   same underlying event with different constructed emphasis.
2. A student who treats a news report as simply "the facts, no interpretation involved"
   (misconception) vs. a student who identifies specific framing and selection choices even within an
   accurate, fact-based report (correct, media-literate understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to view media as unconstructed after the bridge, have them find
two different headlines or framings of the same real event and identify the SPECIFIC word choices or
emphasis differences that shape the reader's impression differently.

### MC-B-EMOTIONALLY-ENGAGING-OR-ATTENTION-GRABBING-CONTENT-IS-AUTOMATICALLY-LESS-TRUSTWORTHY-THAN-PLAIN-CONTENT

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes that media content which is emotionally engaging, dramatic, or
attention-grabbing must automatically be less trustworthy or more manipulative than plain,
unemotional content, and conversely that plain, dry, unemotional presentation is automatically more
trustworthy — failing to recognize that trustworthiness depends on the ACCURACY, sourcing, and
transparency of the underlying content, not on its emotional tone or presentation style, which are
separate questions.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two media pieces on the same well-documented, accurate
topic: one presented with an emotionally engaging, well-produced narrative style (real, well-sourced
human stories illustrating an accurate statistic), and one presented in a dry, plain, seemingly
"neutral" style that actually contains inaccurate, poorly sourced claims. Ask: "Does the emotionally
engaging piece's style make it automatically less trustworthy than the plain piece? Which piece
actually contains more accurate, well-sourced information?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming emotional or dramatic presentation is automatically untrustworthy,
while plain presentation is automatically trustworthy, is like assuming a well-designed, professional-
looking website must be legitimate while a plain, text-only one must be a scam — but design polish (or
its absence) is a separate question from whether the underlying information is actually accurate.
Trustworthiness depends on sourcing, accuracy, and transparency — not directly on emotional tone or
presentation style, which can vary independently in either direction."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't judge a media message's trustworthiness by its emotional tone or
presentation style. Instead, ask: is the underlying information accurately sourced and transparent
about its evidence? Both dramatic, emotionally engaging content and plain, dry content can be
accurate or inaccurate — check the substance, not the style."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. An emotionally engaging, well-produced piece built on accurate, well-sourced facts (trustworthy
   despite dramatic presentation) vs. a plain, dry-sounding piece containing inaccurate or poorly
   sourced claims (untrustworthy despite unemotional presentation).
2. A student who assumes "if it's trying to make me feel something, it must be manipulative"
   (over-generalized skepticism of emotional content) vs. a student who evaluates emotional and plain
   content by the same accuracy/sourcing standard, regardless of tone (correct, substance-focused
   evaluation).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to judge trustworthiness by tone after the bridge, give them
two more media pieces (one emotionally engaging and accurate, one plain and inaccurate) and have them
evaluate BOTH using only accuracy/sourcing criteria, explicitly setting aside the emotional tone as
irrelevant to the trustworthiness judgment.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.reading.evaluating-sources`):** "Evaluate whether this source is likely reliable,
citing specific criteria." A student who cannot perform basic source evaluation lacks the foundation
this concept extends to the broader media landscape — route to `eng.reading.evaluating-sources`
review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Photograph-Framing-Choice and Website-Design-Polish Anchor.** Combine two images: a photograph
whose framing, angle, and cropping are deliberate choices shaping what's shown, even without any
technical "faking" — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (media is constructed through deliberate
production choices, even when accurate). Second, a professionally designed website that could still be
a scam, and a plain, text-only website that could still be legitimate — directly seeding the fix for
MC-B (presentation style and trustworthiness are separate questions).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Media Is Constructed Through Deliberate Choices (MC-A)

Using the photograph-framing anchor, teach students to identify specific production choices (framing,
word choice, selection) in media messages. Student practice: given two different framings of the same
event, identify the specific choices that shape each impression differently.

### TA-2 — Identifying a Media Message's Purpose and Incentive Model

Teach students to identify the likely purpose (inform, persuade, entertain, generate engagement/
revenue) behind a media message and how that purpose can shape content. Student practice: given four
media examples, identify the likely primary purpose of each and one way that purpose shapes the
content.

### TA-3 — Separating Trustworthiness from Emotional Tone or Presentation Style (MC-B)

Using the website-design anchor, teach students to evaluate trustworthiness based on accuracy and
sourcing, independent of emotional tone or production polish. Student practice: given four media
examples varying in emotional tone and accuracy independently, evaluate each on accuracy/sourcing
grounds alone.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Evaluating a New Media Message

Give the student a new media message (a news segment, an article, or a social media post
description). They must identify the specific production choices shaping it (TA-1), the likely
purpose/incentive behind it (TA-2), and evaluate its trustworthiness based on accuracy and sourcing
rather than tone (TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Two headlines about the same protest event, one emphasizing conflict, one
emphasizing the protesters' cause. Fully worked: specific framing choices identified in each (word
choice, emphasis); likely purpose identified for a hypothetical outlet known for sensationalism
(engagement/advertising revenue, favoring the more dramatic "clash" framing) versus one known for
advocacy journalism (informing/persuading around the cause, favoring the "accountability" framing);
both evaluated on accuracy grounds separately from their emotional tone, confirming neither framing
is automatically more or less trustworthy purely due to its dramatic or plain presentation. Annotation
confirms both the constructed-choices analysis (fixing MC-A) and the tone/trustworthiness separation
(fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New media example with a dramatic, emotionally engaging presentation style and
well-sourced, accurate content. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("What specific choices —
framing, word choice, selected details — shape this piece? What's this outlet's likely purpose or
incentive? Does the emotional, dramatic presentation style tell you anything about whether the
underlying facts are accurate — or do you need to check the sourcing separately?") to complete the
analysis.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new media message, unaided. Student identifies production choices, likely
purpose, and evaluates trustworthiness based on accuracy/sourcing rather than tone.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying Constructed Production Choices**
Given two different framings of the same event, identify the specific production choices (word
choice, emphasis, selection) that shape each impression.

**MP-2 [P36] — Identifying Purpose and Incentive Model**
Given three media examples, identify the likely primary purpose (inform, persuade, entertain,
generate engagement/revenue) behind each.

**MP-3 [P36] — Separating Tone from Trustworthiness**
Given two media examples with different emotional tones but the same actual accuracy level, evaluate
both using accuracy/sourcing criteria alone, explaining why tone alone doesn't determine
trustworthiness.

**MP-4 [P36] — Detecting Tone-Based Misjudgment**
Given a plain-toned but inaccurate media example and a dramatic but accurate one, correctly identify
which is actually more trustworthy based on substance, not tone.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Media Message Evaluation**
Given a new media message, identify its production choices, likely purpose, and evaluate its
trustworthiness based on accuracy and sourcing rather than tone.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite. During
Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1 (treating media as
unconstructed) and MC-B during TA-3 (judging by tone) — these are largely independent and often need
separate, explicit correction. If either fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill before resuming.
At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure indicates residual MC-A; MP-2 failure
alone indicates the purpose-identification step (TA-2) needs review, distinct from either
misconception; MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing
indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-evaluation example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters isolated construction-analysis and tone/trustworthiness separation (MP-1,
  MP-3) but struggles to integrate all three steps on genuinely current, real media examples (MP-5),
  provide a structured checklist (what choices were made? what's the likely purpose? is the
  underlying content accurate regardless of tone?) as scaffolding for real-world application.
- If the student becomes globally distrustful of all media after this concept (a common
  overcorrection), recalibrate by emphasizing that constructed choices and purpose don't automatically
  mean a message is FALSE — many well-produced, purpose-driven media messages are also accurate; the
  goal is informed evaluation, not blanket cynicism.
- If the student's purpose-identification (MP-2) defaults to assuming all media has purely negative
  or manipulative incentives, broaden the discussion to include legitimate informational and public-
  service purposes alongside commercial/persuasive ones.
- Students with active social media use should be encouraged to apply this concept's framework
  directly to content they encounter regularly, since social media specifically blends personal,
  commercial, and engagement-driven purposes in ways that reward explicit, deliberate analysis.
