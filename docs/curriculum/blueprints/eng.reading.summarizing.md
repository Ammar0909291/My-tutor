## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-SUMMARY-MEANS-COPYING-THE-IMPORTANT-SENTENCES
- **Trigger signal:** Student produces a "summary" by selecting and copying several sentences verbatim from the original text and stringing them together, rather than restating the essential content in their own words and their own sentence structure.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present a short passage and two "summaries": one made of 3 sentences copied word-for-word from the passage, and one that restates the same content in the student's own words at roughly the same length. Ask which one demonstrates that the reader actually UNDERSTOOD the passage versus just located important-looking sentences.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Copying sentences, even the 'important' ones, only proves you can find text — it doesn't prove you understood it. A real summary requires you to process the meaning and rebuild it in your own words. If you can't restate it without looking at the original, that's a sign you haven't fully digested the content yet."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "After identifying the main idea and key details, close or cover the original text and restate what you remember in your own words and sentence structure. If you find yourself needing to peek back at exact phrasing, that's a signal to re-read for understanding rather than for exact words to copy."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Copy-and-select summarizing (choosing and reproducing exact original sentences) vs. (b) Restate-in-own-words summarizing (processing the content's meaning and rebuilding it using the reader's own language and sentence structure).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1 re-check, then re-run TA-1's cover-and-restate practice before continuing.

### MC-A-GOOD-SUMMARY-INCLUDES-EVERY-DETAIL-FROM-THE-ORIGINAL
- **Trigger signal:** Student attempts to include as many details as possible from the original text in their summary, producing something nearly as long as the original and failing to distinguish essential content (main idea and truly load-bearing details) from minor or illustrative details that can be safely omitted.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present a passage with one main idea, 3 essential supporting details, and 2 minor illustrative details (a specific color mentioned once, an incidental aside). Ask the student to write a summary roughly one-quarter the length of the original and see whether they can produce one without the minor details.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "A summary's whole purpose is compression — capturing the essential content in much less space. If your summary is nearly as long as the original, it hasn't done its job. Not every detail deserves a place; you have to judge which details are truly load-bearing for the main idea versus which are just flavor or illustration that can be safely dropped."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Before including a detail in your summary, ask: does the main idea actually depend on this detail, or is it just interesting extra flavor? Keep only the details that are load-bearing — the ones that would change the reader's understanding of the main idea if removed. Aim for real compression, not a slightly shorter copy."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Comprehensive-inclusion summarizing (a good summary preserves as many original details as possible) vs. (b) Essential-compression summarizing (a good summary retains only the main idea and load-bearing details, deliberately dropping minor/illustrative details to achieve real compression).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1, then re-present the Concrete Anchor's suitcase-packing framing before continuing to TA-3.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

### PD-1: Main Idea and Detail-Weighting Readiness
- **Tests:** Can the student already distinguish a main idea from supporting details and identify which details are essential versus minor — the exact judgment summarizing depends on to decide what to keep and what to compress away?
- **Probe:** Give a short paragraph and ask the student to state the main idea and identify which 2 of 4 details are most essential to that main idea.
- **Pass condition:** Correctly states the main idea and identifies the 2 most essential details.
- **Fail routing:** If detail-weighting fails, route to `eng.reading.main-idea-and-details` review — summarizing is essentially main-idea/detail judgment applied to compression.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**"Packing the Weekend Suitcase."** Present the student with a full closet's worth of item cards (15+ items: several outfits, shoes, toiletries, a favorite souvenir, extra "just in case" items) and one small weekend suitcase that can only fit 5 items. Have the student choose which 5 items are truly essential for a weekend trip, physically packing only those into the suitcase and setting the rest aside. Emphasize two lessons directly: (1) packing means genuinely leaving things behind, not trying to cram everything in a slightly smaller bag (seeding MC-A-GOOD-SUMMARY-INCLUDES-EVERY-DETAIL-FROM-THE-ORIGINAL's fix — a summary is a suitcase, not a slightly-trimmed closet); (2) describe what they packed in their OWN words to a friend rather than reading the closet's inventory list aloud (seeding MC-A-SUMMARY-MEANS-COPYING-THE-IMPORTANT-SENTENCES's fix — a summary is a fresh description, not a recited list).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1: Cover-and-Restate Practice
Have the student read a short paragraph, then physically cover or set aside the original text and restate its content aloud or in writing using their own words, without looking back. Compare their restatement to the original, confirming it captures the same meaning without matching sentence structure. Directly counters MC-A-SUMMARY-MEANS-COPYING-THE-IMPORTANT-SENTENCES.

### TA-2: Essential vs. Minor Detail Sorting
Present a passage and have the student sort its details into "essential — main idea depends on this" and "minor — flavor/illustration, safely droppable," directly building the judgment needed for real compression.

### TA-3: The Suitcase Compression Test
Using the discrimination pairs from MC-A-GOOD-SUMMARY-INCLUDES-EVERY-DETAIL-FROM-THE-ORIGINAL, have the student write a summary at an assigned target length (e.g., one-quarter of the original) and self-check it against the essential/minor sort from TA-2, cutting any minor detail that snuck in.

### TA-4: Combining Own-Words and Compression
Present a longer passage (2-3 paragraphs) and have the student produce a single summary paragraph that is both entirely in their own words (TA-1's skill) and appropriately compressed (TA-2/TA-3's skill), applying both requirements simultaneously.

### TA-5: Summarizing a Novel Multi-Paragraph Text
Student independently summarizes a new, unscaffolded 3-paragraph passage to an assigned length constraint, then peer/self-checks it against a checklist (own words? essential details only? correct main idea captured?) — the generative capstone.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

### WE-1: Cover-and-Restate (Scaffolded)
**Original:** "The town's annual harvest festival drew a record crowd this year, with over three thousand visitors enjoying live music, local food vendors, and a pumpkin-carving contest that awarded prizes to the most creative designs."
**Walkthrough:** After covering the original, a student restates: "This year's harvest festival had its biggest crowd ever, with music, food, and a pumpkin-carving contest." Confirm this captures the same essential meaning in different words and a shorter form, without needing to reference back to the original's exact phrasing.

### WE-2: Essential vs. Minor Sorting (Guided)
**Passage:** "The new community garden opened last spring. Volunteers planted tomatoes, peppers, and herbs in raised beds donated by a local hardware store. The garden's opening ceremony featured a ribbon-cutting by the mayor, who wore a bright yellow jacket that day. Produce from the garden is now distributed free to neighborhood families every Saturday."
**Walkthrough:** Sort details: essential = garden opened, volunteers grow produce, produce distributed free weekly (all load-bearing for the main idea of "a community garden now provides free produce"); minor = raised beds from a specific hardware store, the mayor's yellow jacket (droppable illustrative details).

### WE-3: Full Compressed Own-Words Summary (Independent)
**Passage (2 paragraphs, ~120 words)** describing a school's new recycling program, including specific bin colors, a launch date, participating grades, and a quote from a student volunteer.
**Walkthrough:** Student produces a ~30-word own-words summary capturing only the essential content (a new recycling program launched, which grades participate, the basic goal), omitting the specific bin colors and the individual quote as minor/illustrative.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Reject a copied-sentence "summary"**
Given a passage and a "summary" made of copied sentences, student identifies why it isn't a true summary and rewrites it in original language. *(Directly targets MC-A-SUMMARY-MEANS-COPYING-THE-IMPORTANT-SENTENCES.)*

**MP-2 [P36] — Achieve real compression**
Given a passage and a target length constraint, student produces a summary that is genuinely compressed (not merely a slightly shorter near-copy), correctly omitting minor details. *(Directly targets MC-A-GOOD-SUMMARY-INCLUDES-EVERY-DETAIL-FROM-THE-ORIGINAL.)*

**MP-3 [P36] — Essential vs. minor sorting**
Given a passage, student correctly sorts its details into essential and minor categories with justification.

**MP-4 [P36] — Cover-and-restate**
Given a short passage, student restates its content from memory in their own words after covering the original, without significant loss of meaning.

**MP-5 [P36] — Independent multi-paragraph summary**
Student produces a complete, appropriately compressed, own-words summary of a novel 3-paragraph passage under a specified length constraint.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** At session open (S0), confirm PD-1 passes — summarizing is main-idea/detail-weighting judgment applied under a compression constraint, so a student who cannot distinguish essential from minor details will not know what to keep. During instruction (S1), watch for both misconception patterns: copying original sentences (MC-A-SUMMARY-MEANS-COPYING-THE-IMPORTANT-SENTENCES) and over-including details (MC-A-GOOD-SUMMARY-INCLUDES-EVERY-DETAIL-FROM-THE-ORIGINAL) both avoid the harder cognitive work of processing-and-compressing meaning — redirect both to the Suitcase anchor's pack-and-describe-in-your-own-words framing. If the student produces a near-copy or an overly long summary during TA-3/TA-4 (S6), drop back to the Concrete Anchor's physical packing exercise before returning to written practice. At mastery-probe stage (S9), do not certify mastery unless MP-1 and MP-2 both pass — a student who sorts essential/minor details correctly in isolation (MP-3) but still copies phrasing or over-includes in an actual summary has not yet integrated both required skills.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student copies original phrasing, weight remediation toward MC-A-SUMMARY-MEANS-COPYING-THE-IMPORTANT-SENTENCES's bridge text and re-run TA-1's cover-and-restate practice before returning to full summary writing.
- If the student over-includes minor details, weight remediation toward MC-A-GOOD-SUMMARY-INCLUDES-EVERY-DETAIL-FROM-THE-ORIGINAL's bridge text and TA-2/TA-3's essential-vs-minor sorting and compression practice specifically.
- This concept feeds one direct downstream unlock (`eng.reading.compare-and-contrast-texts`) — do not certify mastery without TA-5's independent multi-paragraph summary, since comparing texts assumes the student can already reliably distill each text's essential content before comparing them.
- Reuse the "suitcase, not a slightly smaller closet" framing explicitly when introducing `eng.reading.compare-and-contrast-texts` — that concept requires summarizing each text first before the comparison itself can happen.
