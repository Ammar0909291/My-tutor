**Concept:** Phrasal Verbs — verb-plus-particle combinations, such as "give up" or "look after," that
function as a SINGLE semantic unit with a meaning that is often not derivable from the individual verb
and particle alone, and whose two parts can sometimes be SEPARATED by an object, depending on the
specific verb.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Collocations (prerequisite) taught that certain words
habitually co-occur. Phrasal Verbs teach a specialized, very common case: a verb plus one or more
small particles (up, off, in, after) that together function as ONE unit of meaning, often quite
different from either word's literal sense, and with specific, sometimes tricky rules about whether
an object can or must go between the verb and particle. This concept has no further downstream
unlocks in the current curriculum but is a foundational, high-frequency vocabulary category in
everyday spoken and written English.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) recognize a phrasal verb as a single semantic unit and
explain its meaning, distinguishing it from a literal reading of the verb plus a simple preposition,
(b) correctly determine whether a specific phrasal verb is separable (the object can go between the
verb and particle, e.g., "give it up" or "give up the fight," both correct) or inseparable (the object
must follow the whole unit, e.g., "look after her," never "look her after"), and (c) correctly use a
pronoun object with a separable phrasal verb, recognizing that a pronoun object MUST go between the
verb and particle (e.g., "give it up," never "give up it").

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-A-PHRASAL-VERBS-MEANING-CAN-USUALLY-BE-FIGURED-OUT-FROM-THE-VERB-AND-PARTICLES-LITERAL-MEANINGS

**Trigger signal:** Student attempts to interpret a phrasal verb by combining the literal meanings of
the verb and the particle separately, rather than recognizing the combination as a fixed semantic
unit with its own, often non-derivable meaning — e.g., interpreting "give up" (meaning "to quit or
surrender") as somehow related to physically giving something in an upward direction.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student the sentence "After trying for hours, she finally gave
up." and ask: "Does 'gave up' here involve physically giving anything, or moving anything upward?
What does the WHOLE phrase mean as a single unit?" Guide the student to see the phrase means "to quit
or stop trying," a meaning with no direct, derivable connection to "give" or "up" individually.

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Trying to derive a phrasal verb's meaning from its individual parts is like
trying to understand the word 'breakfast' by combining the literal meanings of 'break' and 'fast' —
technically related historically (breaking an overnight fast), but a modern speaker just learns
'breakfast' as one whole word with one meaning. Phrasal verbs work the same way: learn the WHOLE
combination as one semantic unit, not a sum of its two literal parts."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When you encounter a verb-plus-particle combination, don't try to
derive its meaning from the individual words. Learn the WHOLE combination's meaning as a single
vocabulary unit, the same way you'd learn any new word."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "She gave up." (a phrasal verb meaning "quit," with no literal connection to physically giving
   something upward) vs. "She gave the ball up to the referee." (a literal reading where "gave" and
   "up" happen to appear near each other but aren't functioning as one fixed phrasal-verb unit in the
   same idiomatic way — context and specific usage determine which reading applies).
2. A student attempting to guess "look after" means something about looking in a direction called
   "after" (literal, incorrect derivation) vs. a student who has learned "look after" as a fixed unit
   meaning "to take care of" (correct, whole-unit learning).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to attempt literal derivation after the bridge, give them three
more common phrasal verbs in clear context and have them first attempt (and notice the failure of) a
literal interpretation, then learn and confirm the actual, non-derivable meaning from context or a
reference.

### MC-B-YOU-CAN-ALWAYS-PLACE-THE-OBJECT-EITHER-BETWEEN-THE-VERB-AND-PARTICLE-OR-AFTER-BOTH-REGARDLESS-OF-WHICH-PHRASAL-VERB-IT-IS

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes ALL phrasal verbs allow the object to be placed either between the
verb and particle or after the whole unit, failing to recognize that some phrasal verbs are
INSEPARABLE (the object must always follow the whole unit, e.g., "look after her," never "look her
after") while others are SEPARABLE (allowing both orders with a noun object, e.g., "give up the fight"
or "give the fight up") — and that pronoun objects specifically REQUIRE the separated position for
separable phrasal verbs.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student "She looked after the children." alongside the
incorrect attempted alternative "She looked the children after." and ask: "Does this second version
sound correct to a fluent speaker? Now compare 'She gave up the fight' with 'She gave the fight up' —
are both of THESE versions acceptable?" Guide the student to see that "look after" never allows the
object to split it, while "give up" allows the object either way (with a noun object).

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming every phrasal verb allows the object to move around freely is like
assuming every zipper can be opened from either end — some zippers genuinely can (separable), but
others are fixed and can only be operated one specific way (inseparable). Each phrasal verb has its
own specific behavior that must be learned individually — some allow the object to split the verb and
particle, others require the object to always follow the whole unit together."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "For each phrasal verb you learn, also learn whether it is SEPARABLE
(object can go between the verb and particle, or after both, if it's a noun) or INSEPARABLE (object
must always follow the whole unit). Don't assume all phrasal verbs behave the same way."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "She looked after the children." (correct — "look after" is inseparable) vs. "She looked the
   children after." (incorrect — the object cannot split this inseparable phrasal verb).
2. "She gave up the fight." and "She gave the fight up." (both correct — "give up" is separable,
   allowing a noun object either position) vs. only one of these orders being correct for an
   inseparable phrasal verb like "look after."

**s6_path:** If the student continues to assume uniform behavior after the bridge, give them five
phrasal verbs with example sentences and have them test each one by attempting both object placements
(between the parts, and after the whole unit), determining separable versus inseparable status for
each based on which placements sound correct to a reference or fluent-speaker check.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.vocab.collocations`):** "Identify a natural collocation from these word pairs:
'make a decision' or 'do a decision.'" A student who cannot identify basic collocations lacks the
foundation this concept extends to a more specialized case — route to `eng.vocab.collocations` review
first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Breakfast-Word-Fusion and Fixed-vs-Openable-Zipper Anchor.** Combine two images: the word
"breakfast," learned as one fused whole-word unit rather than derived from "break" plus "fast" each
time — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (learn phrasal verbs as whole semantic units). Second, a
zipper that can be opened from either end versus one fixed to only work one specific way — directly
seeding the fix for MC-B (some phrasal verbs are separable, others are inseparable, and each must be
learned individually).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Recognizing Phrasal Verbs as Single Semantic Units (MC-A)

Using the breakfast-fusion anchor, teach students to treat verb-plus-particle combinations as whole
units rather than attempting literal derivation. Student practice: given five sentences containing
phrasal verbs, state the whole-unit meaning of each.

### TA-2 — Separable vs. Inseparable Phrasal Verbs (MC-B)

Using the zipper anchor, teach students to test each phrasal verb for separability. Student practice:
given six phrasal verbs in context, test both object placements (between parts, after whole unit) and
classify each as separable or inseparable.

### TA-3 — Pronoun Objects Must Split Separable Phrasal Verbs

Extend TA-2: for separable phrasal verbs specifically, a PRONOUN object must always go between the
verb and particle (never after the whole unit). Student practice: given four separable phrasal verbs
with a noun object, convert the noun to a pronoun and correctly place it.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Using Phrasal Verbs Correctly in Context

Give the student a new short passage or set of sentences using phrasal verbs with various object
types (nouns, pronouns, or no object). They must identify each phrasal verb's meaning (TA-1),
determine separability (TA-2), and correctly place any pronoun object (TA-3) — the full synthesis
task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Sentence: "She decided to give up smoking, so she threw the cigarettes out and
never looked back." Fully worked: "give up" identified as a whole-unit phrasal verb meaning "to quit,"
tested as separable ("give smoking up" also works, though less natural with a gerund object); "threw
out" identified as separable, tested with the pronoun object correctly placed ("threw them out," never
"threw out them"); "looked back" identified as an idiomatic phrasal verb (inseparable, no object).
Annotation confirms both the whole-unit meaning recognition (fixing MC-A) and the correct separability
testing plus pronoun placement (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New sentence set with a mix of separable and inseparable phrasal verbs, plus a noun
object needing conversion to a pronoun. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("What does this
verb-plus-particle combination mean as a whole? Can the object go between the two parts, or must it
follow the whole unit — test both placements. If the object is a pronoun, where must it go?") to
complete the analysis and pronoun placement.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new short passage using phrasal verbs with various object types, unaided.
Student identifies each phrasal verb's meaning, determines separability, and correctly places any
pronoun objects.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying Whole-Unit Meaning**
Given three sentences containing phrasal verbs, state the whole-unit meaning of each, confirming it
is not simply derivable from the individual words.

**MP-2 [P36] — Testing Separability**
Given three phrasal verbs in context with a noun object, test both possible object placements and
classify each as separable or inseparable.

**MP-3 [P36] — Correct Pronoun Placement**
Given three separable phrasal verbs with a noun object, convert the noun to a pronoun and correctly
place it between the verb and particle.

**MP-4 [P36] — Distinguishing Separable from Inseparable**
Given four phrasal verbs, correctly identify which are separable (allowing both object placements
with a noun) and which are inseparable (requiring the object to follow the whole unit).

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Contextual Application**
Given a new short passage using phrasal verbs with various object types, identify each phrasal verb's
meaning, determine separability, and correctly place any pronoun objects.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite. During
Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1 (literal derivation
attempts) and MC-B during TA-2 (assuming uniform separability) — MC-A generally needs resolving first
since a student who hasn't accepted the whole-unit meaning principle has less motivation to carefully
learn each phrasal verb's individual separability behavior. If either fires, branch to the relevant
s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure
indicates residual MC-A; MP-2/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-3 failure alone indicates the
pronoun-placement extension (TA-3) needs review, distinct from either misconception; MP-5 failure with
MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided contextual example
before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student is a language learner whose first language lacks phrasal verbs or has a very
  different equivalent structure, expect this concept to require more repetition than for a native
  speaker, since the whole-unit, non-derivable nature of phrasal verbs is often genuinely
  unfamiliar cross-linguistically.
- If the student masters separability testing (MP-2, MP-4) but consistently forgets the pronoun-
  specific rule (MP-3), provide additional dedicated practice on this specific sub-rule, since it is a
  common, persistent error even among otherwise proficient speakers.
- If the student's own speech or writing already uses phrasal verbs fluently and correctly
  (common for native or highly proficient speakers), focus mastery probes on the EXPLICIT
  articulation of the separability rules rather than production, since implicit correct use doesn't
  guarantee explicit metalinguistic awareness.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.vocab.idioms` should transfer the "learn as a whole unit,
  don't derive from parts" principle readily to phrasal verbs — draw this parallel explicitly, since
  both concepts share this core insight about fixed, non-compositional expressions.
