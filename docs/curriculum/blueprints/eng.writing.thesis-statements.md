**Concept:** Thesis Statements — a single sentence that states an essay's central argument or
purpose and previews the essay's development, functioning as the essay's spine.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Essay Structure (prerequisite) teaches the container —
introduction, body paragraphs, conclusion — but that container needs a load-bearing sentence to hold
it together, or the body paragraphs drift with no shared destination. Thesis Statements teaches the
student to write that single sentence and to test whether it is strong enough to carry an entire
essay. This concept unlocks `eng.writing.citations-and-referencing` (a citation only matters in
service of a claim — you can't teach students to support a thesis with sourced evidence before they
can write the thesis itself).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can write a one-sentence thesis statement for a given essay
prompt that (a) makes a specific, arguable or explanatory claim rather than a bare statement of
topic, (b) is neither too broad to be defended in the assigned length nor too narrow to sustain
multiple body paragraphs, and (c) previews the essay's main supporting points. The student can also
evaluate a given thesis statement and identify whether it is too vague, too broad, too narrow, or
just a fact, and revise it accordingly.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-THESIS-IS-JUST-THE-TOPIC-ANNOUNCEMENT

**Trigger signal:** Student writes a "thesis" that only announces the topic — e.g., "In this essay
I will talk about the causes of the American Revolution" or "This essay is about video game
violence" — with no actual claim or position stated.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two sentences: "This essay is about school lunch
programs" and "School lunch programs should offer more plant-based options because they cost less
and reduce food waste." Ask: "If I only read the first sentence, do I know what the essay is going
to ARGUE? If I only read the second, do I?" The student discovers the first sentence promises a
topic but makes no commitment about what will be said about it — a reader cannot predict the essay's
content or take a position to agree or disagree with.

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Announcing a topic is like saying 'I'm going to talk about weather' before a
weather report — it tells the listener the general subject but gives them nothing to hold onto. A
thesis is the actual forecast: 'It will rain heavily this afternoon.' That's a specific, checkable
claim. Your thesis needs to be the forecast, not just the announcement that a forecast is coming."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "A thesis makes a CLAIM — something a reasonable person could disagree
with, or something specific the essay will prove or explain — not just a preview that a topic exists.
Test your thesis: could someone respond 'I disagree' or 'prove it'? If not, it's still just an
announcement."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "This essay will discuss climate change." (announcement — no claim) vs. "Individual recycling
   habits matter less to climate change than corporate emissions policy." (thesis — a specific,
   arguable claim).
2. "There are many types of renewable energy." (a fact, not disputable, not a claim about anything
   specific) vs. "Solar energy is the most viable renewable option for suburban homeowners because of
   falling installation costs and available tax incentives." (a specific, defensible claim with
   built-in reasons).

**s6_path:** If the student still writes announcements after the bridge, use the "disagree test"
directly and mechanically: hand them their own sentence and ask them to write a one-sentence
rebuttal to it. If they cannot write a rebuttal because there is nothing to rebut, the sentence is
an announcement, not a thesis — have them add the missing claim before moving on.

### MC-B-THESIS-JUST-NEEDS-TO-BE-AN-OPINION

**Trigger signal:** Student writes a thesis that IS an opinion but is too broad or too narrow to be
supported by the essay's actual length — e.g., "Social media is bad" (unsupportable in any length
without massive qualification) or "My friend's Instagram post last Tuesday got 12 likes" (too narrow
to sustain multiple body paragraphs), believing that having "an opinion" alone makes a thesis
correct.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Give the student the thesis "Social media is bad" and ask them to list
every possible body paragraph topic that could support it. They typically generate an unmanageable,
sprawling list (mental health, privacy, misinformation, addiction, cyberbullying...) that no single
essay could cover. Then give them "That one Instagram post got 12 likes" and ask the same — they
find almost nothing to say beyond restating the fact. Both fail, for opposite reasons — one is
un-shrinkable, one is un-expandable.

**Bridge text [P30]:** "A good thesis is like a properly sized container for water — too big (like
'social media is bad') and the water (your evidence) spreads too thin to fill it in the space you
have. Too small (a single tiny fact) and there's nothing left to pour in after the first sentence.
Your thesis has to be sized to exactly fit the essay you're actually going to write — not a whole
book's worth of claim, not a single fact's worth."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Test your thesis by listing the 2-4 body paragraphs it would require.
If you can't limit the list to a manageable few, the thesis is too broad — narrow it by adding a
specific reason, angle, or context. If you can't find more than one thing to say, it's too narrow —
broaden it by connecting the fact to a larger claim."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Social media is bad." (too broad — supports dozens of unrelated paragraphs) vs. "Social media
   use before bedtime harms teenagers' sleep quality by increasing screen exposure and mental
   stimulation." (properly scoped — supports 2-3 specific paragraphs: screen exposure, mental
   stimulation, resulting sleep effects).
2. "My friend's post got 12 likes." (too narrow — one fact, no further development possible) vs.
   "Posts with personal storytelling, like my friend's recent one, consistently earn more engagement
   than posts with only images." (broadened into a claim with a pattern to support across multiple
   paragraphs).

**s6_path:** If the student cannot resize their thesis after the bridge, use the "list your body
paragraphs" test directly: have them write three lines, one per intended body paragraph topic,
BEFORE finalizing the thesis wording — then write the thesis as the sentence that ties those three
lines together, rather than trying to invent the paragraphs after writing the thesis.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.writing.essay-structure`):** "Label the introduction, body, and conclusion of
this short scrambled essay." A student who cannot identify these parts lacks the structural
container a thesis is meant to anchor — route to `eng.writing.essay-structure` review first.

**PD-2 (checks claim vs. fact recognition, informal pre-check):** "Which of these three sentences
could someone reasonably disagree with: 'Water boils at 100°C,' 'The moon orbits Earth,' or
'Homework should be optional in middle school'?" A student who cannot identify the disagreeable
sentence needs a brief primer on claims vs. facts before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Spine-and-Ribs Anchor.** Draw (or physically model with a ruler and index cards) a simple
skeleton: one long "spine" card down the middle labeled with the thesis sentence, and three shorter
"rib" cards branching off it, each labeled with a body paragraph's main point. Physically move a rib
card that doesn't connect back to the spine sentence — it visibly dangles, unattached, unsupported.
This concretely fixes both misconceptions: it makes visible that the spine (thesis) must be
specific enough that ribs (body paragraphs) can actually attach to it — a vague "announcement" spine
(MC-A) gives the ribs nothing precise to connect to, and a spine sized wrong — too broad or too
narrow (MC-B) — either can't hold three ribs steady (too broad, ribs slide off in every direction) or
has no room for more than one rib (too narrow, nothing else attaches).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Claim vs. Announcement

Teach the "disagree test": a thesis must be a claim a reasonable person could push back on, not a
bare announcement that a topic exists. Practice: sort ten sentences into "announcement" and "claim"
piles.

### TA-2 — Sizing a Thesis (Too Broad, Too Narrow, Just Right)

Teach the "list your body paragraphs" sizing test from Bridge Text MC-B. Practice: given three
theses (one too broad, one too narrow, one well-sized) for the same general topic, sort and explain
each classification.

### TA-3 — Building a Thesis from Evidence Up

Reverse the process: give the student three pieces of evidence/observations about a single topic and
have them write ONE sentence that ties all three together as a claim — practicing the "ribs before
spine" method from the s6_path fallback, now taught as a primary strategy rather than only a repair.

### TA-4 — Previewing Structure Within the Thesis

Teach the "preview" function — a strong thesis often signals, in miniature, the shape of the body
paragraphs to come (e.g., naming the 2-3 reasons directly in the sentence). Practice: expand three
bare claims into full previewing theses by adding "because..." clauses that name the supporting
reasons.

### TA-5 — Revising a Weak Thesis

Give the student five flawed theses (mixing announcement-only, too-broad, too-narrow, and
non-previewing problems). For each, diagnose the specific flaw and revise it into a strong thesis.
This is the synthesis task — diagnosis plus repair, not just production from scratch.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Prompt: "Write a thesis about whether students should have homework on
weekends." Fully worked: Draft attempt — "Homework is a big topic for students." Diagnosis —
announcement only, no claim, no size test possible yet. Revision — "Weekend homework should be
eliminated for elementary students because it reduces family time without measurably improving test
scores." Annotation calls out: the disagree test now passes (someone could argue the opposite), and
the "because" clause previews two supporting reasons (family time, test scores) sized for exactly two
body paragraphs.

**WE-2 (Guided):** Same prompt type, new topic supplied ("Should school uniforms be mandatory?").
Student is given a first-draft thesis with a size flaw ("Uniforms affect everything about school
culture and society.") and prompted with guiding questions ("How many body paragraphs would 'affect
everything about school culture and society' actually require? Is that sized correctly? What's a
narrower, more specific claim about ONE effect of uniforms?") to arrive at a properly scoped revision.

**WE-3 (Independent):** New prompt ("Should the school day start later?") with no draft thesis
supplied. Student must produce their own thesis, unaided, then self-check it against both the
disagree test and the sizing test before submitting.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Claim vs. Announcement**
Given four sentences, identify which are bare topic announcements and which are actual arguable
claims, and rewrite one announcement into a claim.

**MP-2 [P36] — Sizing Diagnosis**
Given three thesis statements on the same topic (one too broad, one too narrow, one well-sized),
label each and explain the sizing flaw for the two flawed ones using the "list your body paragraphs"
test.

**MP-3 [P36] — Build a Thesis from Evidence**
Given three related observations about a topic, write one sentence that ties them together into a
single properly scoped claim.

**MP-4 [P36] — Add a Preview Clause**
Given a bare claim with no preview ("Recess time should be extended."), revise it to add a
"because..." clause that previews at least two specific supporting reasons.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Diagnosis and Revision**
Given a flawed thesis statement with an unnamed defect (could be an announcement, too broad, too
narrow, or missing a preview), identify the specific flaw type and revise the sentence into a
fully qualifying thesis statement.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a structural gap routes to Essay
Structure, a claim-recognition gap gets a brief primer rather than a full detour. During Component 4
(S1, active instruction), monitor for both misconception signals — a student who writes bare
announcements (MC-A) often also mis-sizes their first genuine claim attempt (MC-B) once they learn
to make a claim at all, since sizing only becomes visible after a claim exists to size. If a trigger
fires mid-sequence, branch to the relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage
(S9), route by which probes fail: MP-1 failure alone indicates the claim/announcement distinction
(TA-1) needs review; MP-2/MP-3 failures indicate sizing (TA-2/TA-3) needs review; MP-4 failure alone
indicates the preview mechanism (TA-4) specifically, not the underlying claim; MP-5 failure with
MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates a diagnosis-under-ambiguity gap — provide one more mixed-flaw
worked example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student can correctly critique OTHERS' theses (as in MP-2 and MP-5) but cannot produce a
  well-sized thesis of their own from scratch (WE-3), this indicates a production-versus-recognition
  gap — have them explicitly use the "list your body paragraphs first" method (TA-3) as a required
  scaffold rather than assuming free production.
- If the student consistently defaults to overly broad theses regardless of topic, suspect they are
  equating "important-sounding" with "well-scoped" — anchor sizing concretely with the rib-count test
  every time until the habit transfers without prompting.
- If the student produces claims that pass the disagree test but are factually indefensible or
  extreme (not just broad, but untrue), flag this as an evidence-quality issue distinct from thesis
  mechanics — note it for the downstream `claim-evidence-reasoning` concept rather than re-teaching
  thesis structure itself.
- Students who struggle with abstract claim generation may benefit from starting at TA-3 (building
  from evidence up) before attempting TA-1's abstract claim/announcement sort — reorder the sequence
  for concrete-first learners while keeping all five TAs.
