**Concept:** Cross-Cultural Communication — adapting communication style and expectations across
different cultural and linguistic communities, recognizing other cultures' norms as equally valid
conventions rather than deviations from one's own default, while also avoiding treating any culture
as a single monolithic style.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Sociolinguistics Intro (prerequisite) taught how
language varies across social groups. Digital Communication (prerequisite) taught adapting
communication for digital contexts. Cross-Cultural Communication extends both by teaching the
specific discipline of adapting communication across cultural boundaries — recognizing that one's own
culture's norms are not the universal default, while also avoiding the equal and opposite error of
treating an entire culture as one uniform style. This concept has no further downstream unlocks in
the current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) recognize their own culture's communication norms
(directness, formality, turn-taking) as one valid convention among several, rather than the universal
default against which other cultures' norms are deviations, and (b) recognize genuine variation
within any cultural group rather than treating an entire culture as having one single, uniform
communication style.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-MY-OWN-CULTURES-COMMUNICATION-NORMS-ARE-THE-UNIVERSAL-DEFAULT-AND-CORRECT-WAY

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes their own culture's communication norms (a preference for direct
versus indirect speech, a particular level of formality, specific turn-taking expectations) are the
universal, "correct" default, treating other cultures' different norms as deviations, errors, or
"less clear" communication rather than equally valid different conventions.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present two communication styles for delivering critical feedback: one
culture's convention of direct, explicit criticism, and another culture's convention of indirect,
face-saving feedback delivered through implication and context. Ask: "Is the direct style objectively
'more honest' or 'clearer,' or is it simply one convention among several equally valid ways different
cultures handle feedback?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming your own culture's norms are the universal default is like assuming
your home country's traffic laws (which side of the road to drive on) are the objectively 'correct'
side, and every other country's opposite convention is simply 'wrong' — both are actually valid,
internally consistent systems; neither is the universal truth. Communication norms work the same way:
different cultures' conventions for directness, formality, and turn-taking are different valid
systems, not one correct default and several deviations."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't treat your own culture's communication norms as the universal
default. Recognize that directness, formality, and turn-taking expectations vary by culture as
equally valid conventions — a different norm isn't a worse or less clear one, just a different one."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A culture's convention of direct, explicit critical feedback treated as objectively "more honest"
   (misconception — treats one convention as the universal standard) vs. recognizing that indirect,
   face-saving feedback in another culture is an equally valid, different convention for the same
   underlying goal (correct recognition).
2. A student who describes another culture's communication style as "less direct" or "vaguer" as if
   that were a deficiency (misconception, ethnocentric framing) vs. a student who describes it as a
   different convention serving a different set of social priorities (correct, non-judgmental
   framing).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to treat their own norms as default after the bridge, give
them three communication scenarios from cultures other than their own and have them describe each
convention's internal logic and purpose without evaluating it against their own culture's norms as
the standard.

### MC-B-AN-ENTIRE-CULTURE-HAS-ONE-SINGLE-UNIFORM-COMMUNICATION-STYLE-WITH-NO-INTERNAL-VARIATION

**Trigger signal:** Student, in the course of trying to avoid ethnocentrism, overcorrects into
treating an entire culture or linguistic community as a single monolithic block with one uniform
communication style, ignoring genuine regional, generational, contextual, and individual variation
within any cultural group — stereotyping in the name of cultural awareness.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present two individuals from the same broadly-labeled cultural group who
communicate quite differently from each other (one more direct, one more indirect, reflecting
individual, regional, or generational variation within that same culture). Ask: "Do these two people
share a cultural label? Do they communicate identically? What does this tell you about assuming one
'style' represents an entire culture?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming a whole culture shares one communication style is like assuming
everyone from a single large country speaks with the exact same accent and uses the exact same slang
— in reality, huge variation exists by region, generation, and individual personality within any
national or cultural group. Being culturally aware means recognizing broad tendencies AND their
genuine internal variation, not replacing one stereotype (my culture is the default) with another
(their whole culture is one uniform style)."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't treat an entire culture as having one single communication style.
Recognize broad tendencies as starting points for awareness, but expect genuine variation by region,
generation, and individual within any cultural group — adapt to the actual person in front of you,
not an assumed uniform stereotype."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Assuming every member of a cultural group communicates in exactly the same way (misconception —
   monolithic stereotype) vs. recognizing broad tendencies exist alongside real individual, regional,
   and generational variation within that group (correct, nuanced awareness).
2. A student who prepares one fixed "communication script" for an entire cultural group regardless of
   the individual (misconception) vs. a student who uses broad cultural awareness as a starting point
   while remaining attentive to the actual individual's specific communication style (correct
   approach).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to treat cultures as monolithic after the bridge, give them a
scenario involving two individuals from the same broad cultural group who communicate quite
differently, and have them explain what factors (region, generation, individual personality) might
account for the difference, reinforcing that cultural awareness is a starting point, not a fixed
rule.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.linguistics.sociolinguistics-intro`):** "Identify one way language use varies
across different social groups." A student who cannot recognize basic social variation in language
lacks the foundation for cross-cultural communication awareness — route to
`eng.linguistics.sociolinguistics-intro` review first.

**PD-2 (checks `eng.communication.digital-communication`):** "Identify one way communication style
adapts for a digital context compared to face-to-face." A student who cannot adapt communication for
different contexts lacks part of the foundation for adapting across cultural contexts — route to
`eng.communication.digital-communication` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Driving-Side-of-the-Road and Whole-Country-One-Accent Anchor.** Different countries driving on
different sides of the road, both valid, internally consistent systems, neither the universal
"correct" side — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (different cultures' communication norms are
equally valid conventions, not deviations from one's own default). Second, assuming everyone in one
large country shares an identical accent and slang — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (genuine
variation exists within any cultural group; broad tendencies are a starting point, not a uniform
rule).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Recognizing One's Own Norms as a Convention, Not the Default (MC-A)

Using the driving-side-of-the-road anchor, teach identifying one's own communication norms as one
valid convention among several. Student practice: given a communication scenario from a different
culture, describe its internal logic without evaluating it against one's own culture as the standard.

### TA-2 — Adapting Communication Style Across Cultural Contexts

Extend TA-1: teach adjusting one's own communication approach (directness, formality) when
interacting across a cultural boundary. Student practice: given a feedback scenario, adapt the
delivery style appropriately for a specified different cultural context.

### TA-3 — Recognizing Variation Within a Cultural Group (MC-B)

Using the whole-country-one-accent anchor, teach that broad cultural tendencies coexist with real
internal variation. Student practice: given two individuals from the same broad cultural group who
communicate differently, explain possible reasons for the difference.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Navigating a Cross-Cultural Communication Scenario

Give the student a new cross-cultural communication scenario. They must recognize their own norms as
one convention among several (TA-1), adapt their approach appropriately (TA-2), and remain attentive
to individual variation rather than applying a fixed stereotype (TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A workplace feedback scenario across two cultures, fully worked: the direct-
feedback culture's convention described without treating it as the universal default; the indirect-
feedback culture's convention described as an equally valid, different system serving the same
underlying goal; the delivery adapted appropriately for the second culture's convention; a note added
that individuals within either culture may vary from the broad tendency, so attentiveness to the
specific person remains necessary. Annotation confirms both the equal-validity recognition (fixing
MC-A) and the internal-variation awareness (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new cross-cultural scenario. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("Am I
treating my own norm as the 'correct' one, or as one valid convention among several? Am I assuming
this entire cultural group communicates identically, or am I staying attentive to this specific
person's actual style?") to complete the analysis.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new cross-cultural scenario, unaided. Student recognizes convention
validity, adapts communication appropriately, and remains attentive to individual variation.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Describing a Different Culture's Convention Without Ethnocentric Judgment**
Given a communication scenario from a different culture, describe its internal logic without
evaluating it against one's own culture as the standard.

**MP-2 [P36] — Adapting Communication Style Across a Cultural Context**
Given a feedback scenario, adapt the delivery style appropriately for a specified different cultural
context.

**MP-3 [P36] — Explaining Variation Within a Cultural Group**
Given two individuals from the same broad cultural group who communicate differently, explain
possible reasons for the difference.

**MP-4 [P36] — Detecting an Ethnocentric or Monolithic Assumption**
Given a statement treating one culture's norm as universal or an entire culture as uniform, identify
which misconception is present and correct it.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Cross-Cultural Communication Navigation**
Given a new cross-cultural communication scenario, recognize convention validity, adapt
communication appropriately, and remain attentive to individual variation.

example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly describes other cultures' conventions non-judgmentally in isolation
  (MP-1) but reverts to ethnocentric framing in a new full scenario (MP-5), provide a mandatory "am I
  treating my norm as the standard?" self-check before finalizing any cross-cultural analysis.
- If the student's equal-validity recognition is strong (MP-1) but they apply a fixed, uniform
  assumption to an entire cultural group in a new scenario (MP-5), require an explicit "could this
  specific person vary from the broad tendency?" check before finalizing any adaptation strategy.
- If the student struggles to adapt communication style concretely, provide a scaffolding template
  contrasting direct and indirect feedback phrasing as a starting reference.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.speaking.non-verbal-communication`'s cultural-variability
  recognition (for gesture and eye contact) should transfer readily to verbal cross-cultural
  communication, since both require recognizing culturally-variable convention rather than one
  universal norm — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
