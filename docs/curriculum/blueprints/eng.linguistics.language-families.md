**Concept:** Language Families — the genealogical classification of the world's languages into
related families (like the Indo-European family) based on shared historical descent from a common
ancestor language, established through systematic comparison of sound correspondences and shared
vocabulary/grammar, not surface-level resemblance alone.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Historical Linguistics (prerequisite) established that
languages change systematically over time following observable patterns. Language Families teaches
the CULMINATING application of that principle: grouping related languages into families based on
their shared historical descent, established through rigorous, systematic comparison (regular sound
correspondences across many words) rather than superficial resemblance, which can be coincidental or
due to borrowing rather than genuine common ancestry. This concept has no further downstream unlocks
in the current curriculum but completes the historical-linguistics sequence.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) explain that language family classification is based
on systematic, regular sound correspondences across many words (the comparative method), not
superficial word-similarity, (b) explain that languages can share similar-sounding words WITHOUT being
genealogically related (due to coincidence or borrowing), and distinguish this from genuine cognates
(words descended from a shared ancestor), and (c) identify that a shared writing system or geographic
proximity does NOT by itself indicate a genealogical language family relationship.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-ANY-TWO-LANGUAGES-WITH-SIMILAR-SOUNDING-WORDS-MUST-BE-RELATED

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes that any two languages sharing a similar-sounding word for the
same concept must be genealogically related (part of the same language family), failing to recognize
that similar-sounding words can arise from BORROWING (one language adopting a word from another,
unrelated language) or pure COINCIDENCE, and that genuine genealogical relationship requires
SYSTEMATIC, regular sound correspondences across MANY words, established through the comparative
method, not a single similar-sounding pair.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present the student with a well-known example of two genuinely unrelated
languages that happen to share a similar-sounding word for a basic concept purely by coincidence,
alongside an example of a systematic set of regular sound correspondences across dozens of words
between two languages known to be genuinely related (e.g., a regular pattern where a specific
consonant in one language consistently corresponds to a different, specific consonant in the related
language, across many word pairs). Ask: "Does one single similar-sounding word pair prove a genuine
family relationship? What does the systematic correspondence pattern across many words suggest that a
single coincidental match doesn't?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming any similar-sounding word proves a language relationship is like
assuming two unrelated people who happen to share a rare surname must be related — a single
coincidental match proves nothing on its own. Genuine family relationship, like genuine genealogical
descent, requires a systematic pattern of evidence (in linguistics, REGULAR sound correspondences
across dozens or hundreds of words), not a single similar-looking match."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before concluding two languages are related, look for a SYSTEMATIC,
REGULAR pattern of sound correspondence across MANY words (the comparative method), not just one or
two similar-sounding words. A single similar word could be coincidence or borrowing, not genuine
shared ancestry."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Two unrelated languages happening to share one similar-sounding word for a common concept by pure
   coincidence (no systematic pattern, not evidence of a family relationship) vs. two genuinely
   related languages showing a systematic, regular sound correspondence across dozens of words
   (genuine evidence of shared ancestry).
2. A student who concludes two languages are related after finding one similar word (over-hasty
   conclusion) vs. a student who requires systematic correspondence evidence across many words before
   concluding a genuine family relationship (correct, rigorous methodology).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to over-conclude relationship from single word matches after the
bridge, give them a dataset of ten word pairs from two languages, only some of which show a genuinely
systematic correspondence pattern, and have them identify which specific pairs support (or don't
support) a genuine family relationship claim.

### MC-B-LANGUAGES-USING-THE-SAME-WRITING-SYSTEM-OR-LOCATED-IN-THE-SAME-REGION-ARE-AUTOMATICALLY-IN-THE-SAME-LANGUAGE-FAMILY

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes that languages sharing a writing system (e.g., both using the Latin
alphabet, or both using Arabic script) or located in the same geographic region must automatically
belong to the same genealogical language family, failing to recognize that writing systems and
geographic proximity are historically and culturally contingent facts SEPARATE from genealogical
descent — languages from entirely different, unrelated families can share a writing system (often
through cultural contact or colonization) or be geographically neighboring without being related.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present the student with two examples of genuinely unrelated languages
(from different, well-established language families) that nonetheless share the same writing system,
alongside an example of two genuinely related languages (same family) that use entirely different
writing systems. Ask: "Does sharing a writing system tell us anything about genealogical descent? Does
being unrelated (different families) prevent two languages from using the same script?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming shared writing system or geography means shared language family is
like assuming two countries that both use the same currency must be part of the same political
federation — currency adoption (like writing-system adoption) is a separate, historically contingent
choice, not proof of the deeper genealogical/political relationship. Writing systems spread through
cultural contact, trade, religion, and colonization, completely independent of genealogical language
relationships."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Never infer a genealogical language family relationship from a shared
writing system or geographic proximity alone. These are separate, historically contingent facts.
Genuine family relationship requires the systematic sound-correspondence evidence from the
comparative method, regardless of what script is used or where the languages are spoken today."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Two languages from entirely different, unrelated families that happen to share a writing system
   due to historical cultural contact (no genealogical relationship despite the shared script) vs. two
   languages that ARE genealogically related (same family) but use entirely different writing systems
   today (genealogical relationship despite different scripts).
2. A student assuming two neighboring countries' languages must be related just because they're
   geographically close (misconception) vs. a student recognizing that geographic proximity alone
   doesn't establish genealogical relationship, which requires actual comparative evidence (correct
   understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to conflate writing system/geography with genealogical
relationship after the bridge, have them research (or be shown) one specific real-world example of
genuinely unrelated languages sharing a script, and one example of genuinely related languages using
different scripts, articulating why script/geography and genealogy are independent facts.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.linguistics.historical-linguistics-intro`):** "Explain why language change follows
systematic patterns rather than random, word-by-word chaos." A student who cannot answer correctly
lacks the systematic-pattern foundation this concept's comparative method depends on — route to
`eng.linguistics.historical-linguistics-intro` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Coincidental-Surname and Shared-Currency-Not-Federation Anchor.** Combine two images: two
unrelated people sharing a rare surname purely by coincidence, proving nothing about actual family
relationship on its own — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (a single similar word doesn't prove
genealogical language relationship; systematic patterns across many words are required). Second, two
countries sharing a currency without being part of the same political federation — directly seeding
the fix for MC-B (shared writing system or geography is a separate, contingent fact from genuine
genealogical language relationship).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — The Comparative Method: Systematic Sound Correspondences (MC-A)

Using the coincidental-surname anchor, teach that genuine language family relationships require
systematic, regular sound correspondences across many words, not a single similar-sounding match.
Student practice: given two datasets of word pairs (one showing systematic correspondence, one showing
only isolated coincidental similarity), distinguish which supports a genuine family relationship claim.

### TA-2 — Borrowing vs. Genuine Cognates

Extend TA-1 by teaching the distinction between borrowed words (adopted from an unrelated or distantly
related language, often for cultural/technological reasons) and genuine cognates (descended from a
shared ancestor, following systematic correspondence patterns). Student practice: given four word
pairs, identify which are more likely borrowings versus genuine cognates based on the presence or
absence of a systematic correspondence pattern.

### TA-3 — Writing Systems and Geography Are Independent of Genealogy (MC-B)

Using the shared-currency anchor, teach that writing systems and geographic proximity don't indicate
genealogical relationship. Student practice: given three scenarios (shared script but different
families; different scripts but same family; neighboring but unrelated languages), correctly classify
each.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Evaluating a Language Family Claim

Give the student a new dataset with word-pair correspondences, information about writing systems, and
geographic information for two languages. They must evaluate whether the evidence supports a genuine
family relationship, correctly disregarding writing system and geography as irrelevant evidence and
correctly weighing the sound-correspondence data (TA-1, TA-2, TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Data: five word pairs between two languages showing a consistent, regular
sound correspondence pattern (a specific consonant sound systematically corresponding to a different
specific consonant across all five pairs), alongside the fact that the two languages use different
writing systems and are spoken in non-adjacent regions. Fully worked: the systematic correspondence
pattern identified as strong evidence for genuine genealogical relationship (fixing MC-A); the
different writing systems and non-adjacent geography explicitly noted as irrelevant to this conclusion
(fixing MC-B) — the languages are related DESPITE these superficial differences, because the actual
evidence (sound correspondence) supports it.

**WE-2 (Guided):** New dataset with a mix of genuinely correspondence-supported word pairs and one
isolated coincidental-looking match, plus writing-system information. Student is prompted with guiding
questions ("Which of these word pairs show a systematic pattern repeated across multiple examples, and
which is just one isolated similar-looking word? Does the writing system information change your
conclusion about genealogical relationship?") to complete the evaluation.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new dataset with word-pair correspondences, writing system, and geographic
information, unaided. Student evaluates whether the evidence supports a genuine language family
relationship.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying Systematic Correspondence**
Given two sets of word pairs, identify which set shows a systematic, regular sound correspondence
pattern supporting a genuine family relationship claim.

**MP-2 [P36] — Distinguishing Cognates from Borrowings**
Given three word pairs, identify which are more likely genuine cognates (systematic correspondence)
versus borrowings (isolated similarity with no systematic pattern).

**MP-3 [P36] — Writing System and Geography as Irrelevant Evidence**
Given a scenario describing two languages' writing systems and geographic locations, explain why
neither fact alone indicates genealogical relationship.

**MP-4 [P36] — Classifying Language Pair Scenarios**
Given three scenarios (shared script/different families; different scripts/same family; neighboring/
unrelated), correctly classify each based on the actual genealogical relationship rather than surface
features.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Language Family Evaluation**
Given a new dataset with word-pair correspondences, writing system, and geographic information,
evaluate whether the evidence supports a genuine language family relationship.

re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student is multilingual and speaks languages from different families or the same family,
  leverage this directly as a concrete, personally-relevant example for exploring real correspondence
  patterns (or their absence) between their own languages.
- If the student masters the comparative-method reasoning (MP-1, MP-2) in the abstract but struggles
  to apply it to genuinely unfamiliar language data (MP-5), provide one more fully-worked example with
  languages entirely new to the student before independent evaluation.
- If the student overcorrects into skepticism about ALL language relationships, doubting even
  well-established families with abundant systematic evidence, clarify that the skepticism should be
  proportional to the evidence — well-documented families like Indo-European rest on extensive,
  rigorously verified correspondence data, not single coincidental matches.
- Students with an interest in etymology (from `eng.vocab.etymology`) may enjoy tracing specific words'
  cognates across a documented language family as a motivating, concrete extension of this concept's
  abstract principles.
