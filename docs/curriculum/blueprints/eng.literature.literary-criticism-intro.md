**Concept:** Introduction to Literary Criticism — a survey of major critical APPROACHES (lenses) for
interpreting literature, including historical/biographical criticism, formalist criticism, and
reader-response criticism, each asking a genuinely different kind of question about the same text.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Survey of Literary Periods (prerequisite) teaches
situating a work within its historical-literary context. Introduction to Literary Criticism teaches
that historical context is only ONE of several possible LENSES for interpreting a text — formalist
criticism instead focuses purely on the text's internal structure and language, while reader-response
criticism focuses on how meaning is co-created by the individual reader. This concept unlocks
`eng.literature.comparative-literature-intro` (comparing works across cultures or traditions
requires being explicit about which critical lens is doing the comparing).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) describe at least three major critical approaches
(historical/biographical, formalist, reader-response) and the distinct KIND OF QUESTION each one asks
about a text, (b) apply at least two different critical lenses to the SAME short text and produce
genuinely different, lens-appropriate interpretations, and (c) explain that no single critical
approach is the one "correct" or "complete" way to read a text — different lenses reveal different,
often complementary, aspects of meaning.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-THERE-IS-ONE-SINGLE-CORRECT-INTERPRETATION-OF-A-TEXT-THAT-CRITICISM-UNCOVERS

**Trigger signal:** Student treats literary criticism as a search for the single, objectively
"correct" hidden meaning of a text — as if formalist, historical, and reader-response critics are all
competing to find the same one right answer, rather than recognizing that each approach asks a
genuinely different question and can legitimately produce different, non-competing insights about the
same text.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a historical/biographical reading of a poem (focusing
on what the author's life circumstances reveal about the poem's meaning) alongside a formalist
reading of the SAME poem (focusing purely on its internal structure, imagery patterns, and language,
with no reference to the author's biography at all). Ask: "Are these two readings competing to find
the ONE correct answer, or are they asking two different questions that can both be legitimately
answered about the same poem?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming there's one single correct interpretation criticism is racing to
find is like assuming a doctor, a nutritionist, and a personal trainer are all competing to give the
'one correct' assessment of a person's health — but they're actually each asking a different,
legitimate question (medical condition, diet, fitness) about the same person, and all three
assessments can be true and useful at once. Different critical lenses similarly ask different
legitimate questions about the same text."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Instead of asking 'which critical approach is right about this text?',
ask 'what specific KIND OF QUESTION is each approach asking, and what does each one reveal that the
others don't focus on?' Multiple readings can be simultaneously valid because they're not actually
competing for the same answer."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "The formalist reading is correct and the historical reading is wrong." (assumes one right
   answer, treats the two as competing) vs. "The formalist reading reveals how the poem's internal
   structure creates its emotional effect, while the historical reading reveals how the author's
   circumstances shaped its content — both are valid, complementary insights." (recognizes distinct,
   non-competing questions).
2. A student confused or frustrated when two critics reach different conclusions about the same text
   (treating divergence as a problem to resolve) vs. a student recognizing that the divergence often
   reflects genuinely different critical questions being asked (correct, lens-aware understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues seeking one "correct" interpretation after the bridge, have
them write down, for two different critical readings of the same text, the SPECIFIC QUESTION each
one is actually trying to answer — making explicit that the questions themselves differ, not just the
answers.

### MC-B-ANY-PERSONAL-REACTION-TO-A-TEXT-COUNTS-AS-A-VALID-READER-RESPONSE-INTERPRETATION

**Trigger signal:** Student conflates reader-response criticism (a rigorous approach examining how a
reader's engagement co-creates meaning, still grounded in textual evidence and defensible reasoning)
with "any personal reaction is automatically a valid interpretation, no textual support needed" —
producing purely subjective reactions with no connection back to specific features of the text.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two reader-response-style readings of the same short
story: one that connects a personal reaction to SPECIFIC textual features (e.g., "the story's
ambiguous ending made me feel anxious, and I think this is because the author deliberately withholds
resolution at several earlier points too, building a pattern of unresolved tension"), and one that is
purely a personal reaction with no textual grounding at all ("I didn't like this story, it was
boring"). Ask: "Is the second response actually doing the analytical work reader-response criticism
requires, or is it just an unsupported opinion?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Reader-response criticism examining how a reader's engagement shapes meaning
is like a sommelier describing how a wine's specific acidity and tannins created their particular
tasting experience — it's personal and experiential, but still grounded in specific, describable
features of the wine. Just saying 'I didn't like it' with no reference to any specific feature isn't
sommelier-level analysis — it's just a preference. Reader-response criticism requires connecting your
reaction to SPECIFIC textual features, not just stating an unsupported reaction."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When using reader-response criticism, always connect your reaction back
to a SPECIFIC feature of the text (a particular scene, a pattern of imagery, a structural choice) that
you believe produced that reaction in you. An unsupported 'I liked/didn't like it' is not yet a
reader-response interpretation — it's the raw material one could start from."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "I didn't like this story." (unsupported personal reaction, no textual connection) vs. "The
   story's shifting narrator made me feel disoriented, which I think mirrors the protagonist's own
   confusion — a deliberate effect created by that specific structural choice." (a reader-response
   interpretation grounded in a specific textual feature).
2. A student treating any gut reaction as automatically a complete critical interpretation
   (misconception) vs. a student using their gut reaction as a STARTING POINT, then identifying the
   specific textual feature that produced it (correct application of reader-response criticism).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to offer ungrounded personal reactions after the bridge, have
them take their own stated reaction and complete the sentence "I felt/thought this because the text
specifically ___" — requiring the connection to a textual feature before the reaction counts as an
interpretation.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.literature.literary-periods-survey`):** "Identify one characteristic typically
associated with a named literary period." A student who cannot situate basic period characteristics
lacks context this concept builds on (particularly for historical/biographical criticism) — route to
`eng.literature.literary-periods-survey` review first.

**PD-2 (checks basic interpretive awareness, informal pre-check):** "Can two people read the same
story and reasonably disagree about what it means? Why might that happen?" A student who insists
there must always be exactly one correct reading needs a brief primer before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Doctor-Nutritionist-Trainer Anchor.** Present three professionals examining the same person —
a doctor assessing medical condition, a nutritionist assessing diet, a trainer assessing fitness —
each asking a different legitimate question about the same subject, none "more correct" than the
others. This directly seeds the fix for MC-A — different critical lenses similarly ask different,
non-competing questions about the same text. Extend the anchor for MC-B with the sommelier framing:
a sommelier's tasting notes are personal and experiential, but still grounded in specific, describable
features of the wine — not an unsupported "I don't like it."

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Historical/Biographical Criticism

Introduce historical/biographical criticism: interpreting a text in light of the author's life,
historical context, and the conditions of its production. Student practice: given a short text and
brief biographical/historical context, produce a historical/biographical reading.

### TA-2 — Formalist Criticism

Introduce formalist criticism: interpreting a text based purely on its internal structure, language,
and form, without reference to the author's biography or historical context. Student practice: given
the same short text from TA-1, produce a formalist reading focusing only on internal textual
features.

### TA-3 — Reader-Response Criticism (MC-B)

Introduce reader-response criticism, using the sommelier anchor to emphasize that reactions must be
grounded in specific textual features. Student practice: given the same short text, produce a
reader-response reading that explicitly connects a personal reaction to at least one specific
textual feature.

### TA-4 — Multiple Lenses as Complementary, Not Competing (MC-A)

Using the doctor-nutritionist-trainer anchor, teach students to compare the three readings produced
in TA-1 through TA-3 and articulate what SPECIFIC QUESTION each lens was answering, showing they are
complementary rather than competing for one "correct" answer.

### TA-5 — Full Application: Multi-Lens Analysis of a New Text

Give the student a new short text. They must apply at least two different critical lenses (chosen
from historical/biographical, formalist, reader-response) and produce genuinely distinct, lens-
appropriate interpretations, then explicitly articulate what different question each lens answered —
the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A short poem with brief author-biography context provided. Fully worked:
historical/biographical reading (connecting a specific biographical detail to the poem's central
image); formalist reading (analyzing the poem's structure and imagery pattern with no reference to
biography); reader-response reading (a personal reaction to the poem's ending, explicitly connected
to its specific ambiguous final image). Annotation confirms each lens asks a genuinely different
question (fixing MC-A) and that the reader-response reading is grounded in a specific textual feature,
not just an unsupported reaction (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new short text with brief context. Student is prompted with guiding questions
("What does this text's historical/biographical context suggest about its meaning? Now, ignoring that
context entirely, what does the text's internal structure alone suggest? What's your personal
reaction to this text, and what SPECIFIC feature produced that reaction?") to complete all three
readings.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new short text, unaided. Student applies at least two critical lenses,
producing genuinely distinct interpretations, and explains what different question each lens
answered.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying Critical Approaches by Their Questions**
Given three brief critical statements about a text, identify which critical approach
(historical/biographical, formalist, reader-response) each represents based on the KIND OF QUESTION
it asks.

**MP-2 [P36] — Producing a Formalist Reading**
Given a short text, produce a formalist interpretation based purely on its internal structure and
language, with no reference to author or historical context.

**MP-3 [P36] — Grounding a Reader-Response Interpretation**
Given a personal reaction to a text with no textual grounding, revise it into a genuine reader-
response interpretation by connecting the reaction to a specific textual feature.

**MP-4 [P36] — Explaining Complementary (Not Competing) Lenses**
Given two different critical readings of the same text, explain why both can be simultaneously
valid rather than one being "more correct" than the other.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Multi-Lens Analysis**
Given a new short text, apply at least two critical lenses to produce genuinely distinct
interpretations, and explain what different question each lens answered.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a periods gap routes back to that
prerequisite, an interpretive-plurality gap gets a brief primer. During Component 4 (S1, active
instruction), MC-B typically surfaces during TA-3 (ungrounded reactions) and MC-A during TA-4
(seeking one "correct" reading when comparing lenses) — MC-B often needs resolving first since
producing a genuine reader-response reading is a prerequisite for meaningfully comparing it against
the other two lenses in TA-4. If either fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill before resuming.
At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure indicates basic lens-identification
needs review; MP-2 failure alone indicates the formalist-mechanics step (TA-2) needs review, distinct
from either misconception; MP-3 failure alone indicates residual MC-B; MP-4 failure alone indicates
residual MC-A; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one
more guided multi-lens example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student produces technically distinct readings across lenses (MP-1, MP-2, MP-3) but still
  privately favors one lens as "the real answer" when asked directly, revisit MC-A explicitly with a
  text the student has strong personal opinions about, to test whether the plurality principle holds
  under genuine investment.
- If the student's formalist readings (TA-2) inadvertently smuggle in biographical assumptions (a
  common early error, since formalist analysis requires actively suppressing outside context), flag
  this specifically and have the student explicitly list what outside information they are
  deliberately setting aside before writing a formalist reading.
- If the student's reader-response readings remain vague even after grounding attempts ("the ending
  made me feel something because of how it was written" — still not specific), push for naming the
  EXACT textual feature (a specific word choice, a specific structural moment) rather than accepting
  a still-general connection.
- Given this concept's research-level difficulty and 3-hour length, monitor for cognitive fatigue
  across the three-lens sequence — this is an appropriate concept to split across sessions if needed,
  resuming at the next TA with a brief recap of the lens(es) already covered.
