**Concept:** Professional Communication — integrated written AND spoken communication skills
appropriate to professional and workplace settings, recognizing that a single professional situation
often requires coordinating both channels consistently rather than treating them as unrelated skills.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Business Writing (prerequisite) teaches written
workplace communication. Presentation Skills (prerequisite) teaches spoken delivery. Professional
Communication teaches the INTEGRATION of both: recognizing that a real workplace situation (a
meeting, a project update, a proposal) typically requires coordinated written and spoken components
that must be CONSISTENT with each other, and choosing the right channel (written vs. spoken, or both)
for a given professional purpose. This concept unlocks `eng.communication.negotiation-language`
(negotiating professionally draws on both written and spoken integrated skill).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) choose an appropriate communication channel (written,
spoken, or both) for a given professional scenario based on the message's urgency, complexity, and
need for a documented record, (b) ensure consistency between a written document and an accompanying
spoken presentation of the same content (matching key points, tone, and level of formality), and (c)
adapt the SAME underlying professional message appropriately across both channels, recognizing that
spoken delivery and written text have different natural conventions even when conveying identical
content.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-THE-CHANNEL-WRITTEN-VS-SPOKEN-DOESNT-MATTER-AS-LONG-AS-THE-MESSAGE-CONTENT-IS-RIGHT

**Trigger signal:** Student treats channel choice (email versus a phone call versus an in-person
conversation versus a formal written report) as an arbitrary or irrelevant decision, believing that as
long as the message content is accurate, it doesn't matter which channel delivers it — e.g., sending
an urgent, time-sensitive message by email when a phone call would reach the recipient immediately, or
delivering a sensitive personal matter by a impersonal group email rather than a private conversation.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two scenarios with the same content delivered through
different channels: an urgent same-day deadline change sent via email (which the recipient might not
check for hours) versus the same message delivered via a quick phone call or instant message (reaching
them immediately), and a sensitive piece of critical feedback delivered via a group email (public,
impersonal, potentially embarrassing) versus the same feedback delivered in a private one-on-one
conversation (appropriately private and personal). Ask: "Does the channel choice actually affect
whether this message achieves its purpose, even with identical content?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming channel doesn't matter as long as content is accurate is like
assuming it doesn't matter whether you deliver an urgent warning by shouting it directly at someone or
by mailing them a letter — the WORDS could be identical, but one reaches them in time to act and one
doesn't. Channel choice is a functional decision (urgency, privacy, need for a record), not a cosmetic
one, and gets it wrong even with perfect content."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before communicating, ask: how urgent is this, does it need a
documented written record, and how sensitive or personal is the content? These questions should
determine your channel choice (email, phone call, in-person conversation, formal report) — channel
choice is itself a communication decision, not an afterthought."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. An urgent same-day deadline change sent only by email, which the recipient doesn't see in time
   (wrong channel for urgency) vs. the same message delivered by an immediate phone call or instant
   message (channel matched to urgency).
2. Sensitive critical feedback delivered via a public group email (wrong channel for sensitivity) vs.
   the same feedback delivered in a private conversation (channel matched to sensitivity).

**s6_path:** If the student continues treating channel as irrelevant after the bridge, have them
evaluate five professional scenarios and select the most appropriate channel for each based
explicitly on urgency, need for a record, and sensitivity, articulating the specific reasoning for
each choice.

### MC-B-A-SPOKEN-PRESENTATION-SHOULD-BE-A-WORD-FOR-WORD-READING-OF-THE-WRITTEN-DOCUMENT

**Trigger signal:** Student, when asked to present spoken content that accompanies a written document
(a report, a proposal), simply reads the written text aloud verbatim, failing to recognize that spoken
delivery has different natural conventions (shorter sentences, more repetition of key points for a
listening audience, conversational transitions) even when conveying identical underlying content and
maintaining consistency with the written document.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a written report excerpt with a dense, complex sentence
structure appropriate for a reader who can pause and re-read, alongside a spoken version of the SAME
content adapted for a listening audience (shorter sentences, a brief verbal signpost like "so here's
the key takeaway," slight repetition of the main point). Ask: "If you read the written version aloud
exactly as written, would a listening audience follow it as easily as a reader could follow it on the
page?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Reading a written document aloud verbatim is like showing someone a detailed
map by holding it up briefly instead of describing the route in a way suited to someone who can't
pause and study it — a reader can re-read a dense sentence if needed, but a listener only gets one
pass and needs the structure adapted for real-time listening (shorter units, verbal signposts,
strategic repetition of the most important points). Spoken delivery of written content needs genuine
adaptation, not verbatim reading."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When presenting spoken content based on a written document, keep the
KEY POINTS and overall message consistent, but adapt the delivery: use shorter sentences, add verbal
signposts ('the main point here is...'), and consider repeating the most important information once
for a listening audience that can't pause or re-read."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Reading a dense, complex written sentence aloud verbatim, word for word (unadapted, hard for
   listeners to follow) vs. presenting the same underlying point in shorter, spoken-friendly phrasing
   with a verbal signpost (adapted for listening, same core content).
2. A student assuming consistency between written and spoken versions means using identical wording
   (misconception) vs. a student recognizing that consistency means matching KEY POINTS and tone
   while adapting sentence-level phrasing for each channel's natural conventions (correct
   understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues reading written text verbatim after the bridge, have them take
one dense sentence from their own written document and orally paraphrase it in shorter, more
conversational spoken phrasing while confirming the core content and key point remain identical.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.communication.business-writing`):** "Write a front-loaded, scannable business
email for this scenario." A student who cannot produce basic business writing lacks the foundation
this concept builds on — route to `eng.communication.business-writing` review first.

**PD-2 (checks `eng.speaking.presentation-skills`):** "Deliver a brief spoken summary of this short
piece of information." A student who cannot produce basic spoken delivery lacks the foundation this
concept builds on — route to `eng.speaking.presentation-skills` review before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Shout-vs-Letter and Map-Description Anchor.** Combine two images: an urgent warning delivered
by shouting directly at someone (reaches them immediately) versus the identical warning mailed as a
letter (arrives too late) — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (channel choice functionally affects
whether a message achieves its purpose). Second, someone verbally describing a route to a listener who
cannot see a map, adapting the description for real-time listening, versus simply holding up the map
and reading its labels verbatim — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (spoken delivery requires genuine
adaptation, not verbatim reading of written text).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Channel Selection Criteria (MC-A)

Using the shout-vs-letter anchor, teach the three key channel-selection criteria: urgency, need for a
documented record, and sensitivity/privacy. Student practice: given five professional scenarios,
select and justify the most appropriate channel for each based on these criteria.

### TA-2 — Maintaining Content Consistency Across Channels

Teach that written and spoken versions of the same professional message must convey the same key
points and overall tone, even when phrasing differs. Student practice: given a written document
excerpt, identify the 2-3 key points that must remain consistent if presented spoken as well.

### TA-3 — Adapting Delivery for Spoken Convention (MC-B)

Using the map-description anchor, teach spoken-delivery adaptations: shorter sentences, verbal
signposts, and strategic repetition of key points. Student practice: given a dense written sentence,
orally paraphrase it into spoken-friendly phrasing while preserving the core content.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Coordinating a Written and Spoken Professional Communication

Give the student a professional scenario requiring both a written component (e.g., a brief report or
email) and an accompanying spoken component (e.g., a short verbal summary or presentation). They must
select appropriate channels (TA-1), ensure the written and spoken versions share consistent key
points (TA-2), and adapt the spoken delivery appropriately rather than reading verbatim (TA-3) — the
full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Scenario: presenting a project status update to a team, both via a written
summary email and a brief spoken update in a meeting. Fully worked: channel choice justified (written
email for the documented record and details; spoken summary for the live meeting's real-time
discussion); three key points identified as consistent across both versions (status, one blocker,
one next step); the written version's denser phrasing adapted into shorter, signposted spoken
phrasing ("So, quick update — we're on track, but there's one blocker I want to flag..."). Annotation
confirms both the channel-selection reasoning (fixing MC-A) and the spoken-delivery adaptation
(fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New scenario (announcing a policy change, both via a written memo and a spoken
announcement). Student is prompted with guiding questions ("Given the sensitivity and need for a
record here, what channels make sense? What are the 2-3 key points that must appear in both versions?
How would you say this dense sentence from the memo out loud to a listening group, instead of just
reading it?") to complete the coordinated communication.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new professional scenario, unaided. Student selects appropriate channels,
ensures written/spoken consistency of key points, and adapts spoken delivery appropriately.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Channel Selection**
Given three professional scenarios, select and justify the most appropriate channel(s) for each
based on urgency, record-keeping need, and sensitivity.

**MP-2 [P36] — Identifying Consistent Key Points**
Given a written document excerpt, identify the 2-3 key points that must remain consistent if the same
content were also delivered spoken.

**MP-3 [P36] — Adapting a Sentence for Spoken Delivery**
Given a dense written sentence, orally (or in writing, transcribed) paraphrase it into spoken-friendly
phrasing while preserving the core content.

**MP-4 [P36] — Detecting Verbatim-Reading Problems**
Given a spoken presentation script that is a word-for-word reading of a written document, identify at
least one place where it should be adapted for listening rather than read verbatim.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Coordinated Communication**
Given a new professional scenario requiring both channels, select appropriate channels, ensure
consistent key points across written and spoken versions, and adapt spoken delivery appropriately.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a gap in either prerequisite routes
back before Component 3. During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during
TA-1 (arbitrary channel choice) and MC-B during TA-3 (verbatim spoken reading) — these are largely
independent and often need separate, explicit correction. If either fires, branch to the relevant
s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure
indicates residual MC-A; MP-2 failure alone indicates the key-point-identification step (TA-2) needs
review, distinct from either misconception; MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure
with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided coordinated
example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters channel selection and spoken adaptation in isolation (MP-1, MP-3) but
  struggles to keep written and spoken versions genuinely consistent under the combined demand of
  MP-5, provide a structured checklist (select channel, list key points, then draft each version
  against that shared list) as scaffolding.
- If the student's spoken adaptations (MP-3, MP-4) drift so far from the written content that
  consistency is lost (over-correcting MC-B into an unrelated, inconsistent spoken version), clarify
  that adaptation means changing PHRASING and PACING, not changing the actual key points or overall
  message.
- If the student defaults to email/written channels even for scenarios requiring urgency or
  sensitivity (a comfort-zone bias toward the previously-mastered written register), explicitly
  prompt with the three-criteria checklist (urgency, record-need, sensitivity) until channel selection
  becomes more balanced.
- Students with real group-project or extracurricular leadership experience may already have
  intuitive channel-selection habits — leverage this directly by having them recall a real example
  and analyze the channel choice against this concept's criteria.
