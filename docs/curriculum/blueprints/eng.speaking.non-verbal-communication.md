**Concept:** Non-Verbal Communication — body language, eye contact, gesture, and facial expression
as components of spoken communication that carry meaning alongside, and sometimes instead of, the
words being spoken.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Conversation Skills (prerequisite) taught managing
spoken exchange (turn-taking, responding appropriately). Non-Verbal Communication extends this by
teaching that meaning in a spoken exchange is not carried by words alone — body language, eye
contact, gesture, and facial expression all communicate too, can vary by culture, and can even
override or contradict the spoken words themselves. This concept has no further downstream unlocks
in the current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) recognize that non-verbal cues such as eye contact
and gesture vary in meaning across cultures rather than carrying one universal meaning, and (b)
recognize that non-verbal communication carries real communicative weight — sometimes contradicting
or overriding the spoken words — rather than being a minor, secondary addition to speech.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-NON-VERBAL-CUES-LIKE-EYE-CONTACT-AND-GESTURE-MEAN-THE-SAME-THING-IN-EVERY-CULTURE

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes a specific non-verbal cue (direct eye contact, a particular
gesture, physical distance during conversation) carries one universal meaning everywhere, failing to
recognize that non-verbal communication is culturally variable — the same cue can signal respect in
one culture and disrespect or aggression in another.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present two accounts of direct eye contact during a conversation with an
elder: in one cultural context, maintaining direct eye contact signals attentiveness and respect; in
another, sustained direct eye contact with an elder is considered disrespectful or confrontational,
and looking down or away signals respect instead. Ask: "Does direct eye contact mean the same thing
in both descriptions? What does this tell you about whether non-verbal cues are universal?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming a gesture or eye-contact norm means the same thing everywhere is
like assuming a thumbs-up means 'good job' in every country — in some cultures, the same gesture is
actually offensive. Non-verbal cues are like words in a language: their meaning is set by cultural
convention, not by some universal, built-in signal that means the same thing to everyone."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't assume a non-verbal cue means the same thing across every
culture. Recognize that eye contact norms, gestures, and personal space all vary by cultural context
— what signals respect in one setting can signal the opposite in another."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Direct eye contact with an elder signaling respect and attentiveness in one cultural context vs.
   the same direct eye contact signaling disrespect or confrontation in another cultural context (the
   identical cue, opposite meanings).
2. A student who assumes a gesture they know means the same thing everywhere (misconception) vs. a
   student who recognizes that the same gesture's meaning depends on cultural context (correct
   recognition).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to assume universal non-verbal meaning after the bridge, give
them three non-verbal cues (a gesture, an eye-contact norm, a personal-space distance) and have them
research or identify at least one cultural context in which each cue's meaning differs from their own
assumed default.

### MC-B-NON-VERBAL-COMMUNICATION-IS-JUST-A-MINOR-ADDITION-TO-THE-REAL-MESSAGE-WHICH-IS-THE-SPOKEN-WORDS

**Trigger signal:** Student treats non-verbal cues as a minor decoration on top of the "real"
message carried by spoken words, failing to recognize that non-verbal communication carries real,
sometimes decisive, communicative weight — including situations where contradictory body language
overrides or reverses how a spoken message is actually received.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present a scenario: a speaker says "I'm really excited to be here" while
looking at the floor, speaking in a flat monotone, with arms crossed. Ask: "What message does the
listener actually walk away believing — the words, or what the body language suggested? Which one do
you think the listener trusts more when the two disagree?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Treating body language as a minor add-on to the real message is like
thinking the tone of a text message doesn't matter as long as the words are technically polite — but
everyone knows a flat 'fine.' can mean the opposite of fine. When spoken words and body language
disagree, listeners very often trust the non-verbal signal over the words — non-verbal communication
isn't a minor decoration; it can carry the real message."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't treat non-verbal communication as secondary to your words.
Recognize that your body language, tone, and expression communicate right alongside — and sometimes
instead of — what you're saying, and a listener will often trust the non-verbal signal when the two
contradict each other."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "I'm really excited to be here," said with a flat, disengaged voice, eyes on the floor, arms
   crossed (words and non-verbal signal contradict — listener likely trusts the non-verbal cue over
   the words) vs. the same words said with genuine energy, eye contact, and open posture (words and
   non-verbal signal align, reinforcing the message).
2. A student who assumes their polite words alone will communicate sincerity regardless of tone or
   posture (misconception) vs. a student who recognizes their body language must align with their
   words for the message to land as intended (correct recognition).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to treat non-verbal cues as minor after the bridge, have them
role-play delivering the same short spoken message twice — once with contradictory body language and
once with aligned body language — and describe, from a listener's perspective, how differently the
message actually comes across each time.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.speaking.conversation-skills`):** "Identify one turn-taking or responding
strategy used in this short conversation exchange." A student who cannot manage basic conversational
exchange lacks the foundation for layering non-verbal awareness on top of it — route to
`eng.speaking.conversation-skills` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Thumbs-Up-Is-Not-Universal and Flat-"Fine."-Text-Message Anchor.** A thumbs-up gesture meaning
approval in some cultures but registering as offensive in others — directly seeding the fix for MC-A
(non-verbal cues are culturally variable, not universal). Second, a flat "fine." text message that
everyone recognizes doesn't actually mean "fine" — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (non-verbal/tonal
signals can carry the real message, overriding the literal words when the two contradict).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Non-Verbal Cues Vary by Culture (MC-A)

Using the thumbs-up anchor, teach that specific non-verbal cues (gesture, eye contact, personal
space) carry culturally variable, not universal, meaning. Student practice: given one non-verbal cue,
identify at least one cultural context where its meaning differs from an assumed default.

### TA-2 — Non-Verbal Signals Carry Real Communicative Weight (MC-B)

Using the flat-"fine."-text-message anchor, teach that non-verbal signals communicate real meaning,
sometimes decisively. Student practice: given a spoken line with described contradictory body
language, state what message the listener most likely walks away believing.

### TA-3 — Recognizing Alignment and Contradiction Between Words and Body Language

Extend TA-2: teach identifying whether a speaker's words and non-verbal signals align or contradict
each other. Student practice: given three short described scenarios, classify each as aligned or
contradictory.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Analyzing a Complete Spoken Exchange

Give the student a new short scenario describing both spoken words and non-verbal behavior across a
brief exchange. They must identify any culturally-variable cues present (TA-1) and determine whether
words and body language align or contradict, explaining what message the listener likely receives
(TA-2/TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A workplace scenario, fully worked: a manager saying "great job" while
avoiding eye contact and using a flat tone, identified as a contradiction (listener likely doubts the
sincerity of the praise); a separate note on how the same avoided-eye-contact cue might carry a
different, more neutral or even respectful meaning in a different cultural context. Annotation
confirms both the cultural-variability recognition (fixing MC-A) and the real-communicative-weight
recognition (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new scenario. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("Could this specific
gesture or eye-contact pattern mean something different in another cultural context? Do the words and
the body language in this scenario align, or contradict each other — and if they contradict, which
one does the listener likely trust?") to complete the analysis.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new scenario, unaided. Student identifies culturally-variable cues and
determines whether words and non-verbal signals align or contradict, explaining the likely message
received.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying Cultural Variability in a Non-Verbal Cue**
Given one non-verbal cue, identify at least one cultural context in which its meaning differs from
an assumed default.

**MP-2 [P36] — Determining the Message a Listener Actually Receives**
Given a spoken line paired with described contradictory body language, state what message the
listener most likely walks away believing.

**MP-3 [P36] — Classifying Alignment vs. Contradiction**
Given three short scenarios describing words and body language, classify each as aligned or
contradictory.

**MP-4 [P36] — Explaining Why Non-Verbal Signals Can Override Words**
Given a scenario where words and body language contradict, explain why the listener is likely to
trust the non-verbal signal over the literal words.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Exchange Analysis**
Given a new scenario describing both words and non-verbal behavior, identify any culturally-variable
cues and determine whether the exchange is aligned or contradictory, explaining the likely message
received.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite.
During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1 (assuming universal
cue meaning) and MC-B during TA-2/TA-3 (treating non-verbal signals as minor) — these are largely
independent and often need separate, explicit correction. If either fires, branch to the relevant
s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure
indicates residual MC-A; MP-2/MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1
through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-exchange example
before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly identifies cultural variability on provided single cues (MP-1) but
  assumes universal meaning when analyzing a full new scenario (MP-5), provide a mandatory "could
  this cue mean something different elsewhere?" checklist step before finalizing any analysis.
- If the student's alignment/contradiction classification is strong (MP-3) but they underweight the
  non-verbal signal's actual impact on the listener (MP-2, MP-4), push for an explicit "which signal
  would I trust if I were the listener?" framing on every contradiction case.
- If the student struggles to notice contradictions between words and body language at all, flag the
  specific contradicting non-verbal detail explicitly before asking them to classify the scenario.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.speaking.conversation-skills`'s exchange-management work
  should transfer readily to non-verbal awareness, since both require attending to more than just the
  literal content of what is said — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
