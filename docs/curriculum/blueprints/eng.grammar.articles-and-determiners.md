## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-AN-DEPENDS-ON-SPELLING-NOT-SOUND
- **Trigger signal:** Student chooses "a" vs. "an" based on whether the following word's first LETTER is a vowel or consonant, rather than the following word's first SOUND — leading to errors with words where spelling and pronunciation diverge (e.g., "an university" — "university" starts with the letter "u" but the SOUND /j/ is a consonant sound, so "a university" is correct; or "an hour" — "hour" starts with a silent "h," so the sound is a vowel /aʊ/, making "an" correct despite the consonant letter).
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said 'an university' because 'university' starts with the letter 'u.' But say 'university' out loud — does it start with a vowel SOUND, or does it actually start with a /j/ (y) consonant sound?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "The a/an choice depends on the following word's starting SOUND, not its starting LETTER. 'University' is spelled with a vowel letter but pronounced starting with a consonant sound (/j/), so it takes 'a' ('a university'). 'Hour' is spelled with a consonant letter but the 'h' is silent, so it starts with a vowel sound, taking 'an' ('an hour')."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Before choosing 'a' or 'an,' say the following word aloud and check its actual starting SOUND, not just its first written letter."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "a university" (spelled with vowel letter 'u,' but starts with consonant sound /j/) vs. "an umbrella" (starts with genuine vowel sound /ʌ/).
  - "an hour" (silent 'h,' starts with vowel sound) vs. "a horse" (pronounced 'h,' starts with consonant sound).
- **s6_path:** "Checking the letter is a very reasonable shortcut that works most of the time — but the sound-based rule is what actually determines correctness, and a few common exception words (university, hour, honest, European) are worth learning by ear."

### MC-THE-IS-ALWAYS-NEEDED-OR-NEVER-NEEDED
- **Trigger signal:** Student either over-uses "the" (adding it to every noun, including general/non-specific references and most proper nouns) or under-uses it (omitting it even for specific, previously-mentioned, or uniquely-identifiable nouns), rather than applying the definite/indefinite distinction correctly — "the" signals a SPECIFIC, already-identified or unique referent, while "a/an" or no article signals a general/non-specific one.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said 'I saw the dog' the first time you mentioned a dog, with no earlier context. If I don't know WHICH dog you mean yet, should it be 'the dog' or 'a dog'? Now, after we've both been talking about one specific dog for a while, would 'the dog' make sense?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "'The' signals that the listener/reader can identify a SPECIFIC referent — because it was already mentioned, is uniquely identifiable from context, or is one-of-a-kind (the sun, the moon). 'A/an' (or no article for plurals/uncountables) signals a general, non-specific, or first-time-introduced referent. The choice depends on whether shared, specific identification is possible, not a fixed habit of always/never using 'the.'"
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Before choosing 'the' vs. 'a/an'/no article, check whether the listener/reader can already identify a SPECIFIC referent (use 'the') or whether you're introducing something general/new (use 'a/an' or no article)."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "I saw A dog" (first mention, non-specific) followed later by "THE dog was friendly" (now specific, already introduced) — same noun, different article based on shared identifiability.
  - "THE sun is bright today" (unique, one-of-a-kind referent, always "the") vs. "I ate AN apple" (one of many, non-specific).
- **s6_path:** "Article choice can feel like an all-or-nothing habit, especially if your first language doesn't use articles the same way — but the underlying logic (can the listener already identify exactly which one you mean?) is a genuine, learnable test to apply each time."

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

- Pass: correctly identifies "building" as the noun (per `eng.grammar.nouns`).
- Fail → [P52]: brief nouns refresher before proceeding. Persistent failure → route to eng.grammar.nouns for reteaching.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The specific-vs-general picture sort**

> Present pairs of pictures: one showing a GENERAL/unspecified item (any apple, any dog) and one showing a SPECIFIC, already-identified item (the exact apple mentioned earlier, the exact dog we've been discussing). Practice narrating each with the correct article ("I want an apple" for any apple; "I want THE apple [pointing to a specific one]" for a specific one). Extend with a sound-check exercise for a/an — say pairs of words aloud (university/umbrella, hour/horse) and physically mark whether the SOUND is vowel or consonant.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — A vs. An by Sound [C]**

Present a set of words, some with spelling/sound-matching starts and some with the classic mismatches (university, hour, honest, European, unicorn); student says each aloud and chooses a/an based on the actual sound, directly targeting MC-A-AN-DEPENDS-ON-SPELLING-NOT-SOUND.

**TA-2 — The for Specific/Already-Identified Referents [C]**

Present short two-sentence contexts where a noun is first introduced with "a/an" then referred to again with "the"; student applies the correct article at each stage.

**TA-3 — The for Unique/One-of-a-Kind Referents [C→P]**

Present nouns that are inherently unique (the sun, the moon, the internet, the president [of a specific country in context]); student explains why these always take "the" regardless of prior mention.

**TA-4 — Zero Article (No Article Needed) [P]**

Present sentences with plural/uncountable nouns used generally (no article needed: "Dogs are loyal," "I like music") vs. specific instances requiring "the" ("The dogs in the shelter need homes"); student distinguishes general/generic reference (no article) from specific reference (the), directly targeting MC-THE-IS-ALWAYS-NEEDED-OR-NEVER-NEEDED.

**TA-5 — Other Determiners (This/That/These/Those, Possessives, Quantifiers) [P]**

Introduce other determiner types (demonstratives: this/that/these/those; possessives: my/your/her; quantifiers: some/many/few) as words that also specify/quantify nouns, briefly connecting them to the article system as a broader determiner category.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — a vs. an by sound)**

> "Is it 'a hour' or 'an hour'? Say 'hour' aloud — what sound does it start with?"

Target response: "an hour" — "hour" starts with a silent "h," so the actual sound is a vowel.

**WE-2 (Foundational — the for specific referent)**

> "'I saw a cat yesterday. ___ cat was black.' Fill in the blank."

Target response: "The" — the cat has already been introduced, now specific/identifiable.

**WE-3 (Intermediate — zero article for general reference)**

> "'___ Cats are independent animals' vs. '___ cats next door are noisy.' Fill in both blanks."

Target response: first blank — no article (general statement about cats as a category); second blank — "The" (specific cats, identified by "next door").

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — A vs. an, novel example**
"Is it 'a European country' or 'an European country'? Say 'European' aloud — what sound does it start with?"
*Target:* "a European country" — "European" starts with a consonant /j/ sound despite the vowel letter.

**MP-2 [P36] — The for specific referent, novel context**
"'I read a book last week. ___ book was fascinating.' Fill in the blank."
*Target:* "The" — already-introduced, specific referent.

**MP-3 [P36] — The for unique referent**
"Fill in the blank: 'I read an article about ___ internet.'"
*Target:* "the" — the internet is a unique, one-of-a-kind referent.

**MP-4 [P36] — Zero article vs. the, novel context**
"'___ Water is essential for life' vs. '___ water in this bottle is cold.' Fill in both blanks."
*Target:* first blank — no article (general statement); second blank — "The" (specific water, identified by "in this bottle").

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain article choice**
"In your own words: how do you decide between 'a,' 'an,' 'the,' and no article at all?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that a/an depends on the following word's starting SOUND (not letter), and that "the" vs. "a/an"/no article depends on whether the referent is specific/already-identified (the) or general/non-specific/newly-introduced (a/an or no article).

**Protocol routing:** S0 (secure on nouns, new to the article system): dwell on TA-1's a/an sound rule with clear examples before introducing the more conceptually complex the/zero-article distinction. S1 (already uses articles fluently in speech but struggles with formal/written precision): focus on TA-3/TA-4's more nuanced unique-referent and generic-reference cases. S6 (frustrated by the specificity-based reasoning required for "the"): validate that the underlying test (can the listener identify exactly which one?) is a genuine, consistent, applicable rule, even though it requires more judgment than a simple always/never rule. S9 (L1 has no article system at all, e.g. many Slavic languages, Japanese, Korean, Mandarin — one of the most well-documented, persistent challenges for English learners from these backgrounds): expect this concept to require substantially more sustained practice than the standard session suggests, since the entire concept of obligatory articles marking specificity may be structurally unfamiliar; prioritize the specific/general distinction (TA-2/TA-4) as the core transferable logic, and expect this to remain a source of occasional errors even at advanced proficiency — treat this as normal, not a sign of insufficient effort.

---

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Sound, not spelling, determines a/an — teach the exceptions explicitly**: the classic mismatch words (university, hour, honest, European, unicorn, one) deserve dedicated, memorable practice since they're the primary source of errors even for otherwise fluent speakers.
- **Specificity is the core logical test for the/zero-article choice**: always frame the decision around "can the listener already identify exactly which one?" rather than teaching article use as fixed per-noun habits.
- **This is a genuinely difficult, persistent-error area for article-less-L1 learners**: for S9 learners from L1s without an article system, treat this concept as needing extended, ongoing practice well beyond a single session, and normalize continued occasional errors as expected rather than a sign of insufficient learning.
- **Determiners as a broader category worth brief introduction**: TA-5 connects articles to the larger determiner family (demonstratives, possessives, quantifiers) without requiring full mastery of each — this sets up future, more detailed determiner-specific concepts.
