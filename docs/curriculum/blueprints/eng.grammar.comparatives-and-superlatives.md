## Component 1 ‚Äî Misconception Register

### MC-ALWAYS-ADD-ER-EST-OR-ALWAYS-USE-MORE-MOST
- **Trigger signal:** Student applies only one comparison strategy universally ‚Äî either always adding "-er/-est" (producing errors like "beautifuler," "difficultest") or always using "more/most" (producing errors like "more tall," "most fast") ‚Äî rather than applying the correct rule based on the adjective/adverb's SYLLABLE LENGTH (short words: -er/-est; longer words, typically 3+ syllables or certain 2-syllable patterns: more/most).
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said 'beautifuler.' Let's count the syllables in 'beautiful': beau-ti-ful ‚Äî three syllables. Do longer, multi-syllable words like this usually take '-er,' or do they usually use 'more' instead?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Short adjectives/adverbs (usually 1 syllable, and many common 2-syllable ones) take '-er/-est' (tall‚Üítaller‚Üítallest, happy‚Üíhappier‚Üíhappiest). Longer adjectives/adverbs (usually 3+ syllables, and some 2-syllable ones ending in patterns like '-ful,' '-ous,' '-ing') take 'more/most' instead (beautiful‚Üímore beautiful‚Üímost beautiful). The choice depends on syllable length and specific word patterns, not personal preference or a single universal rule."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Check the syllable count and ending pattern of the adjective/adverb before choosing '-er/-est' or 'more/most' ‚Äî don't apply one strategy to every word."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "tall ‚Üí taller ‚Üí tallest" (short, 1 syllable, takes -er/-est) vs. "beautiful ‚Üí more beautiful ‚Üí most beautiful" (3 syllables, takes more/most).
  - "happy ‚Üí happier ‚Üí happiest" (2 syllables ending in -y, takes -er/-est with a spelling change) vs. "careful ‚Üí more careful ‚Üí most careful" (2 syllables ending in -ful, takes more/most).
- **s6_path:** "It's a reasonable instinct to want one consistent rule for all comparisons ‚Äî but English genuinely splits this by word length/pattern, and checking syllable count is a reliable, learnable test."

### MC-IRREGULAR-COMPARATIVES-ARE-JUST-MISTAKES
- **Trigger signal:** Student "corrects" genuinely irregular comparative/superlative forms (good‚Üíbetter‚Üíbest, bad‚Üíworse‚Üíworst, far‚Üífarther/further‚Üífarthest/furthest) back to the regular pattern, producing errors like "gooder," "goodest," "badder," "baddest," not recognizing these as a small, fixed set of true exceptions that must be memorized rather than derived from the regular rule.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said 'gooder' instead of 'better.' Is 'gooder' actually a word fluent English speakers use, or is there a special, different word used instead?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "A small set of very common adjectives/adverbs have IRREGULAR comparative and superlative forms that don't follow either the '-er/-est' or 'more/most' pattern: good‚Üíbetter‚Üíbest, bad‚Üíworse‚Üíworst, far‚Üífarther/further‚Üífarthest/furthest, little‚Üíless‚Üíleast, much/many‚Üímore‚Üímost. These must be memorized directly as a fixed, learnable list, since they can't be derived from the regular rules."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "For a small set of very common words (good, bad, far, little, much/many), use the memorized irregular forms rather than applying the regular -er/-est or more/most rule."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "good ‚Üí better ‚Üí best" (irregular, memorized) vs. attempting "gooder/goodest" (incorrect, regular pattern misapplied).
  - "bad ‚Üí worse ‚Üí worst" (irregular) vs. attempting "badder/baddest" (incorrect).
- **s6_path:** "Since regular rules work for the vast majority of words, it's very natural to expect them to apply everywhere ‚Äî but a handful of extremely common, frequently-used words are genuine, learnable exceptions worth memorizing directly."

## Component 3 ‚Äî Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene ‚Äî The height-line comparison**

> Line up three objects of different heights (or picture cards). Compare TWO of them using comparative form ("This one is taller than that one"), then all THREE using superlative ("This one is the tallest"). Repeat with a longer adjective (e.g., comparing three levels of "interesting" book covers) using "more interesting" / "most interesting," discussing why the strategy differs based on word length.

---

## Component 4 ‚Äî Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 ‚Äî Short Word Comparatives/Superlatives (-er/-est) [C]**

Present short adjectives (tall, fast, big ‚Äî with attention to spelling changes: big‚Üíbigger, happy‚Üíhappier); student produces comparative and superlative forms.

**TA-2 ‚Äî Long Word Comparatives/Superlatives (more/most) [C]**

Present longer adjectives (beautiful, difficult, interesting); student produces comparative and superlative forms using "more/most," directly targeting MC-ALWAYS-ADD-ER-EST-OR-ALWAYS-USE-MORE-MOST.

**TA-3 ‚Äî Irregular Comparatives/Superlatives [C‚ÜíP]**

Present the fixed irregular set (good/better/best, bad/worse/worst, far/farther-further/farthest-furthest, little/less/least, much-many/more/most); student produces correct irregular forms, directly targeting MC-IRREGULAR-COMPARATIVES-ARE-JUST-MISTAKES.

**TA-4 ‚Äî Using Comparatives/Superlatives in Context (Two vs. Three+ Items) [P]**

Present scenarios comparing exactly two items (requiring comparative + "than") vs. three or more items (requiring superlative + "the"); student selects and produces the correct form based on the number of items being compared.

## Component 5 ‚Äî Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational ‚Äî short word)**

> "Compare two people's heights using 'tall': 'Tom is ___ than Jerry.'"

Target response: "taller."

**WE-2 (Foundational ‚Äî long word)**

> "Compare two books using 'interesting': 'This book is ___ than that one.'"

Target response: "more interesting."

**WE-3 (Intermediate ‚Äî irregular form)**

> "Fill in: 'This cake is ___ than that one' (using 'good')."

Target response: "better" ‚Äî not "gooder."

## Component 6 ‚Äî Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] ‚Äî Short word, novel example**
"Fill in: 'This box is ___ than that one' (using 'big')."
*Target:* "bigger" (with correct spelling ‚Äî doubled consonant).

**MP-2 [P36] ‚Äî Long word, novel example**
"Fill in: 'This is the ___ movie I've seen' (using 'exciting,' superlative for many movies compared)."
*Target:* "most exciting."

**MP-3 [P36] ‚Äî Irregular form, novel example**
"Fill in: 'Of all the options, this is the ___' (using 'bad,' superlative)."
*Target:* "worst" ‚Äî not "baddest."

**MP-4 [P36] ‚Äî Two vs. three+ items**
"If comparing just two apples, do you say 'the reddest apple' or 'the redder apple'? What about comparing five apples?"
*Target:* two apples ‚Äî "the redder apple" (comparative); five apples ‚Äî "the reddest apple" (superlative).

**MP-5 [P36] ‚Äî Explain the comparison system**
"In your own words: how do you decide between '-er/-est' and 'more/most,' and are there any words that don't follow either rule?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that short words typically take -er/-est while longer words take more/most (based on syllable count/pattern), and that a small set of common words (good, bad, far, little, much/many) have irregular forms that must be memorized.

## Component 8 ‚Äî Adaptive Flags

- **Syllable-count check is the reliable decision rule**: teach this explicitly as a concrete, checkable test rather than expecting intuition to develop unprompted.
- **Irregular forms are a small, fixed, memorizable list**: treat good/bad/far/little/much-many as a named exception set worth direct memorization, not a source of ongoing confusion to work around.
- **Comparative requires "than"; superlative requires "the"**: reinforce these companion-word patterns explicitly (TA-4) as part of correct usage, not just the adjective/adverb form itself.
- **Lower session_cap (4 TAs) reflects the concept's genuine scope**: comparatives-and-superlatives is a smaller, single-hour concept per the KG's `estimated_hours: 1` ‚Äî the TA sequence is proportionally shorter, consistent with the established convention for concise concepts.
