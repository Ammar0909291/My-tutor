## Component 1 — Cognitive Map

### Prior Knowledge Anchors
- **math.abst.ideal**: ideal definition (absorbs multiplication by ring elements: aI⊆I); principal ideals ⟨n⟩ in ℤ; quotient ring R/I construction; ideals as kernels of ring homomorphisms; examples ⟨6⟩, ⟨2⟩, ⟨0⟩, ⟨1⟩=R in ℤ

### Target Knowledge State
Student can state the definitions of prime ideal and maximal ideal; apply the quotient-ring criterion (P prime iff R/P is an integral domain; M maximal iff R/M is a field); determine whether a given ideal in ℤ or ℤ[x] is prime, maximal, both, or neither using the criterion; distinguish the two types by counterexample (every maximal is prime, but not conversely); and correctly classify ideals in concrete rings without conflating primeness of an element with primeness of the ideal it generates.

### Conceptual Obstacles
1. Conflating "p is a prime element" with "⟨p⟩ is a prime ideal" — primeness of an element implies primeness of the ideal it generates (in integral domains), but students often apply the element-primeness definition directly to the ideal, bypassing the quotient criterion
2. Assuming every prime ideal is maximal — ⟨x⟩ in ℤ[x] is prime (ℤ[x]/⟨x⟩≅ℤ, an integral domain) but not maximal (ℤ is not a field); the implication runs one way only: maximal ⟹ prime (in commutative rings with identity)
3. Not using the quotient-ring criterion — students attempt element-wise verification of the definition directly on large or abstract ideals, missing the algebraic shortcut and making classification intractable

## Component 2 — Misconception Registry

| ID | Label | Description | Trigger |
|----|-------|-------------|---------|
| MC-1 | PRIME-ELEMENT-PRIME-IDEAL-CONFLATION | Student decides primeness of ⟨p⟩ by checking whether p is a prime integer/element, not by checking the ideal absorption property or quotient criterion; misses ideals like ⟨x⟩ in ℤ[x] where x is irreducible but the primeness claim requires a different argument | Any ideal generated by a single element in a non-PID or quotient context |
| MC-2 | EVERY-PRIME-IS-MAXIMAL | Student assumes prime ⟹ maximal; claims ⟨x⟩ in ℤ[x] is maximal because it is prime; does not check whether the quotient ring is a field | Rings where prime-maximal equivalence fails, especially ℤ[x], polynomial rings |
| MC-3 | QUOTIENT-CRITERION-UNUSED | Student attempts direct element-wise verification (checking every pair ab∈P) instead of computing R/P and identifying its ring type; classification becomes impractical and error-prone | Abstract rings, principal ideal rings where the quotient is recognisable but the direct check is infinite |

**Foundational Misconception:** MC-1 (PRIME-ELEMENT-PRIME-IDEAL-CONFLATION) — bypassing the quotient criterion for concrete element-based reasoning produces correct answers for ℤ but fails in polynomial rings and other settings where element primeness and ideal primeness diverge.

## Component 4 — Protocol A (Main Teaching Sequence)

### Teaching Action A01 — From Concrete Divisibility to the Quotient Criterion

**Primitive:** P11 REPRESENTATION SHIFT
**Purpose:** Ground prime/maximal ideal definitions in concrete ℤ examples before the abstract criterion; make the quotient-ring criterion the natural tool (not an afterthought); distinguish the ideal primeness property from element primeness at the concrete stage

---

**[P11 — REPRESENTATION SHIFT]**

**Stage C — Concrete (direct element check in ℤ):**

Consider the ideal ⟨4⟩ = {…,−8,−4,0,4,8,…} in ℤ.

Is ⟨4⟩ a prime ideal? Test the definition: does ab∈⟨4⟩ force a∈⟨4⟩ or b∈⟨4⟩?

- Take a=2, b=2: ab=4∈⟨4⟩. But 2∉⟨4⟩ (since 4 does not divide 2) and 2∉⟨4⟩.
- Counterexample found: 2·2∈⟨4⟩ but neither factor lies in ⟨4⟩.

⟹ **⟨4⟩ is NOT a prime ideal in ℤ.**

Now consider ⟨5⟩ = {…,−10,−5,0,5,10,…}.

Test: suppose ab∈⟨5⟩, i.e., 5|ab. Since 5 is prime, 5|a or 5|b, so a∈⟨5⟩ or b∈⟨5⟩.

⟹ **⟨5⟩ IS a prime ideal in ℤ.**

Key observation: 4 is not a prime integer (4=2·2), and ⟨4⟩ is not a prime ideal. 5 is a prime integer, and ⟨5⟩ is a prime ideal. In ℤ this correspondence holds — but it is NOT the definition, and it fails in other rings (addressed in A02).

**Stage P — Pictorial (table of ideals in ℤ):**

| Ideal | Quotient ring | Integral domain? | Field? | Prime? | Maximal? |
|-------|---------------|------------------|--------|--------|----------|
| ⟨0⟩ | ℤ/⟨0⟩ ≅ ℤ | Yes | No | Yes | No |
| ⟨2⟩ | ℤ/⟨2⟩ ≅ ℤ₂ | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| ⟨4⟩ | ℤ/⟨4⟩ ≅ ℤ₄ | No (2·2=0) | No | No | No |
| ⟨5⟩ | ℤ/⟨5⟩ ≅ ℤ₅ | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| ⟨6⟩ | ℤ/⟨6⟩ ≅ ℤ₆ | No (2·3=0) | No | No | No |
| ⟨1⟩=ℤ | ℤ/ℤ ≅ {0} | No (trivial) | No | No | No |

Pattern in ℤ: prime ideals ↔ {0} and ⟨p⟩ for prime p; maximal ideals ↔ ⟨p⟩ for prime p only.

**Stage A — Abstract (formal definitions and the quotient criterion):**

**Definition (prime ideal):** An ideal P in a commutative ring R (P ≠ R) is **prime** if for all a,b∈R:
$$ab \in P \implies a \in P \text{ or } b \in P$$

**Equivalent criterion:** P is prime if and only if the quotient ring R/P is an integral domain (no zero divisors, P ≠ R).

**Definition (maximal ideal):** An ideal M in a commutative ring R (M ≠ R) is **maximal** if no ideal I satisfies M ⊊ I ⊊ R (M is maximal in the poset of proper ideals).

**Equivalent criterion:** M is maximal if and only if R/M is a field.

**Theorem:** Every maximal ideal is prime (since every field is an integral domain).
**Converse is FALSE:** ⟨0⟩ in ℤ is prime but not maximal (ℤ is not a field). See A02 for ℤ[x] examples.

**Standard workflow:**
1. Compute R/I (identify the quotient ring)
2. Check: integral domain? → I prime. Field? → I maximal. Both? → maximal (and prime). Neither? → neither.

---

**[P49 — ADAPTIVE CHECKPOINT]**

**Prompt:** Is ⟨3⟩ a prime ideal in ℤ? Is it maximal? Apply the quotient criterion.

**CORRECT:** ℤ/⟨3⟩ ≅ ℤ₃, a field (3 is prime → ℤₚ is a field). So ⟨3⟩ is both prime AND maximal.
→ "Correct. In ℤ, ⟨p⟩ is maximal (hence prime) for every prime integer p." → Proceed to A02.

**PARTIAL:** Student says "prime because 3 is a prime number" without citing the quotient criterion (MC-1 / MC-3 pattern).
→ "Your answer is correct here, but the reason is: ℤ/⟨3⟩≅ℤ₃ is a field, which makes ⟨3⟩ maximal and prime. In ℤ, prime integer ↔ prime ideal is a theorem, not the definition. In other rings this shortcut fails — always apply the quotient criterion."

**INCORRECT:** Student says ⟨3⟩ is not prime (or gives ℤ/⟨3⟩≅ℤ₄ incorrectly).
→ "ℤ/⟨3⟩ has cosets 0̄,1̄,2̄: exactly 3 elements, so it is ℤ₃. ℤ₃ has no zero divisors (check: 1·1=1, 1·2=2, 2·2=4≡1; none are 0̄) and every nonzero element has an inverse, so ℤ₃ is a field. Hence ⟨3⟩ is maximal and prime."

**NO_RESPONSE:** → "Compute the quotient: ℤ/⟨3⟩ partitions ℤ into cosets {0̄,1̄,2̄}, giving the ring ℤ₃. Since 3 is prime, ℤ₃ is a field (every nonzero element is invertible). A field is always an integral domain, so ⟨3⟩ is both maximal and prime."

### Teaching Action A02 — Contrast Pair: Prime vs Maximal in ℤ[x]

**Primitive:** P06 CONTRAST PAIR
**Purpose:** Expose the prime-not-maximal case using ⟨x⟩ in ℤ[x]; contrast with ⟨2,x⟩ which is maximal; cement the one-way implication maximal ⟹ prime; address MC-2 and MC-3

---

**[P06 — CONTRAST PAIR]**

**Setup — the ring ℤ[x]:**
ℤ[x] = all polynomials with integer coefficients. It is an integral domain but NOT a PID (has ideals needing 2 generators, like ⟨2,x⟩).

**Case 1 — ⟨x⟩: prime but NOT maximal**

*Claim: ⟨x⟩ is a prime ideal in ℤ[x].*
Quotient: ℤ[x]/⟨x⟩ ≅ ℤ (evaluation at x=0: send f(x) ↦ f(0)).
- ℤ is an integral domain → ⟨x⟩ is prime. ✓

*Claim: ⟨x⟩ is NOT maximal.*
ℤ is not a field (2 has no inverse in ℤ). So ⟨x⟩ is not maximal. ✗
Alternatively: ⟨x⟩ ⊊ ⟨2,x⟩ ⊊ ℤ[x] (the ideal ⟨2,x⟩ properly contains ⟨x⟩ and is proper), so ⟨x⟩ cannot be maximal.

**Case 2 — ⟨2,x⟩: prime AND maximal**

⟨2,x⟩ = {2a(x)+x·b(x) : a,b∈ℤ[x]} — all polynomials with even constant term.
Quotient: ℤ[x]/⟨2,x⟩ ≅ ℤ₂ (send f(x) ↦ f(0) mod 2).
- ℤ₂ is a field → ⟨2,x⟩ is maximal (and prime). ✓

**Contrast table:**

| Ideal | Quotient ring | Integral domain? | Field? | Prime? | Maximal? |
|-------|---------------|------------------|--------|--------|----------|
| ⟨x⟩ in ℤ[x] | ℤ | Yes | **No** | Yes | **No** |
| ⟨2,x⟩ in ℤ[x] | ℤ₂ | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| ⟨x²−x⟩ in ℤ[x] | ℤ[x]/⟨x²−x⟩ | No (x·(x−1)=0) | No | **No** | No |

**Implication summary:**

$$\text{maximal} \implies \text{prime} \quad (\text{field} \implies \text{integral domain})$$
$$\text{prime} \centernot\implies \text{maximal} \quad (\langle x \rangle \text{ in } \mathbb{Z}[x] \text{ is the canonical counterexample})$$

**Why MC-2 fails:** "⟨x⟩ is prime" does NOT imply "⟨x⟩ is maximal." The converse of the maximal-implies-prime theorem requires extra conditions (e.g., the ring is a PID). Never assume the converse without checking the quotient ring.

---

**[P49 — ADAPTIVE CHECKPOINT]**

**Prompt:** In ℤ[x], is the ideal ⟨x²+1⟩ prime? Is it maximal? Apply the quotient criterion.

**CORRECT:** ℤ[x]/⟨x²+1⟩ ≅ ℤ[i] (Gaussian integers); ℤ[i] is an integral domain → prime ✓. Is ℤ[i] a field? No (2 has no inverse: there is no Gaussian integer z with 2z=1, since |2z|²=4|z|²≥4≠1). So ⟨x²+1⟩ is prime but NOT maximal.
→ "Correct. ℤ[i] is a PID and an integral domain but not a field — its units are ±1 and ±i, so integers like 2 are non-invertible. Hence prime-not-maximal." → Proceed to A03.

**PARTIAL:** Student correctly identifies ℤ[i] but claims it's a field (MC-2 tendency).
→ "ℤ[i] is not a field. Check: does 2 have a multiplicative inverse in ℤ[i]? You need a+bi with (a+bi)·2=1, i.e., 2a=1, 2b=0. But 2a=1 has no integer solution. So ℤ[i] has non-invertible elements — it's an integral domain, not a field. ⟨x²+1⟩ is prime, not maximal."

**INCORRECT:** Student cannot compute the quotient (MC-3 — trying element-wise check).
→ "Use the quotient criterion. In ℤ[x]/⟨x²+1⟩, the relation x²+1=0 means x²=−1. Every element reduces to a+bx where a,b∈ℤ — the ring ℤ[x]/⟨x²+1⟩ is isomorphic to ℤ[i] via x↦i. Check: is ℤ[i] an integral domain? Yes (it's a subring of ℂ). Is it a field? No. So ⟨x²+1⟩ is prime but not maximal."

**NO_RESPONSE:** → "Step 1: compute R/I=ℤ[x]/⟨x²+1⟩. Set x²=−1. Elements become a+bx (a,b∈ℤ), multiplication uses x²=−1: (a+bx)(c+dx)=(ac−bd)+(ad+bc)x. This is ℤ[i] (set i=x). Step 2: ℤ[i]⊂ℂ, so no zero divisors → integral domain → I prime. Step 3: 2 has no inverse in ℤ[i] → not a field → I not maximal."

### Teaching Action A03 — Mastery Gate (P91)

**Primitive:** P91 = P77→P55→P76→P55→P75→P55→P74→P55→P78
**Purpose:** Assess fluent classification of prime and maximal ideals using the quotient criterion

---

**[P77 — MULTI-PROBLEM SET]** *(4 problems)*

**Problem 1:** Is ⟨6⟩ a prime ideal in ℤ? Apply the quotient criterion.

*Solution:* ℤ/⟨6⟩ ≅ ℤ₆. In ℤ₆: 2·3=6≡0 but 2≠0 and 3≠0 — zero divisors exist. ℤ₆ is not an integral domain. Therefore **⟨6⟩ is NOT prime** (and not maximal).

**Problem 2:** Is ⟨7⟩ a prime ideal in ℤ? Is it maximal?

*Solution:* ℤ/⟨7⟩ ≅ ℤ₇. Since 7 is prime, ℤ₇ is a field. Therefore **⟨7⟩ is both prime and maximal**.

**Problem 3:** In ℤ[x], is ⟨x−2⟩ prime? Maximal?

*Solution:* ℤ[x]/⟨x−2⟩ ≅ ℤ (evaluation at x=2). ℤ is an integral domain → **⟨x−2⟩ is prime**. ℤ is not a field → **not maximal**. (Same structure as ⟨x⟩ by translation.)

**Problem 4:** In ℤ[x], is ⟨x²−x⟩ a prime ideal?

*Solution:* x²−x = x(x−1). In the quotient, x·(x−1)=0 but x≠0 and (x−1)≠0 in ℤ[x]/⟨x²−x⟩ — the images of x and x−1 are zero divisors. Therefore **⟨x²−x⟩ is NOT prime**.

**[P76 — TRANSFER PROBE]** *(P76_mode: independence)*

**Prompt:** Let R = ℤ[x] and consider the ideal I = ⟨2, x²+1⟩ (all polynomials of the form 2a(x)+(x²+1)b(x) with a,b∈ℤ[x]).

(a) Compute the quotient ring R/I and identify it as a familiar ring.
(b) Determine whether I is prime, maximal, both, or neither.
(c) Is I the same as ⟨x²+1⟩? Explain whether I properly contains ⟨x²+1⟩.

**Expected solution:**

(a) In R/I, set 2=0 (from the 2-generator) and x²+1=0 (from the other generator). Working mod 2: x²+1=0 becomes x²=1 (over ℤ₂), i.e., x²+1 factors as (x+1)² over ℤ₂... 

Wait — let's be careful. ℤ[x]/⟨2,x²+1⟩: first reduce mod 2 (coefficients in ℤ₂), then mod (x²+1). In ℤ₂[x], x²+1=(x+1)². So ℤ₂[x]/⟨(x+1)²⟩. This is NOT a field (the element (x+1) is nilpotent: (x+1)²=0) and NOT an integral domain.

Therefore: **(b) I is neither prime nor maximal.**

Wait, but I should double-check this. ℤ[x]/⟨2,x²+1⟩: the generators set 2≡0 and x²+1≡0. So we're working over ℤ₂ with x²=1. In ℤ₂[x]/⟨x²+1⟩ = ℤ₂[x]/⟨(x+1)²⟩. The element (x+1) satisfies (x+1)²=x²+1=0 (mod 2, since 2x=0). So (x+1) is nilpotent and the ring has zero divisors.

Actually, let me recheck x²+1 over ℤ₂: (x+1)² = x²+2x+1 = x²+0+1 = x²+1 (since 2≡0). So x²+1=(x+1)² in ℤ₂[x]. Yes, (x+1)²=0 in ℤ₂[x]/⟨x²+1⟩, so (x+1)·(x+1)=0 but x+1≠0. Not an integral domain.

(c) **I properly contains ⟨x²+1⟩:** The polynomial 2 lies in I (take a(x)=1, b(x)=0) but 2∉⟨x²+1⟩ (no polynomial times x²+1 gives 2, since deg((x²+1)·q)≥2 for q≠0 and deg(0)=-∞; 2 has degree 0 but ≠0). So ⟨x²+1⟩ ⊊ I.

*Summary:* ⟨x²+1⟩ is prime but not maximal (quotient ≅ ℤ[i], integral domain not field). I=⟨2,x²+1⟩ properly contains ⟨x²+1⟩ and has quotient ℤ₂[x]/⟨(x+1)²⟩, which has zero divisors — so I is neither prime nor maximal. The containment ⟨x²+1⟩ ⊊ I ⊊ ℤ[x] confirms ⟨x²+1⟩ is not maximal (I is a proper ideal properly between ⟨x²+1⟩ and ℤ[x]).

## Component 5 — Protocol B (Misconception Repair)

### Repair Action B01 — PRIME-ELEMENT-PRIME-IDEAL-CONFLATION (MC-1)

**[P27 — MISCONCEPTION NAMING]**
"There is a specific error: deciding that ⟨p⟩ is a prime ideal because p is a prime element, without applying the quotient criterion. This is PRIME-ELEMENT-PRIME-IDEAL-CONFLATION. In ℤ it happens to give the right answer, but in ℤ[x] and other rings it breaks down."

**[P41 — MISCONCEPTION DETECTOR]**
Diagnostic: "Why is ⟨x⟩ a prime ideal in ℤ[x]?"
- MC-1 response: "Because x is a prime/irreducible polynomial."
- Correct response: "Because ℤ[x]/⟨x⟩≅ℤ is an integral domain."

**[P64 — CONCEPTUAL SHIFT]**
"The primeness property must be checked in the ideal, not the element. The quotient criterion is the bridge: P is prime iff R/P has no zero divisors. For ⟨x⟩: R/⟨x⟩≅ℤ (substitute x=0 in every polynomial). ℤ has no zero divisors, so ⟨x⟩ is prime. The fact that x is irreducible gives you a hint but is not the proof."

Practice: Classify ⟨x−3⟩ in ℤ[x] using the quotient criterion (not element primeness).

---

### Repair Action B02 — EVERY-PRIME-IS-MAXIMAL (MC-2)

**[P27 — MISCONCEPTION NAMING]**
"Assuming that every prime ideal is also maximal is the EVERY-PRIME-IS-MAXIMAL error. The implication runs one way: maximal implies prime. The converse requires extra conditions (PID + 1D structure) that ℤ[x] does not have."

**[P41 — MISCONCEPTION DETECTOR]**
Diagnostic: "Is ⟨x⟩ maximal in ℤ[x]?"
- MC-2 response: "Yes, because it's prime."
- Correct: "No — ℤ[x]/⟨x⟩≅ℤ is not a field, so ⟨x⟩ is not maximal."

**[P64 — CONCEPTUAL SHIFT]**
"Always check the field question separately. After confirming R/P is an integral domain (prime), ask: is R/P a field? If yes: maximal. If no: prime only. Draw the one-way arrow: maximal → prime. Write a ✗ on the reverse arrow."

Practice: Use the table from A01 Stage P and circle the ideals that are prime-but-not-maximal. Confirm ⟨0⟩ and ⟨x⟩ (in ℤ[x]) are the canonical examples.

### Repair Action B03 — QUOTIENT-CRITERION-UNUSED (MC-3)

**[P27 — MISCONCEPTION NAMING]**
"Attempting to verify prime-ideal membership by checking every pair ab∈P is the QUOTIENT-CRITERION-UNUSED error. For most rings, you cannot check infinitely many pairs directly — the quotient criterion is the practical and correct tool."

**[P41 — MISCONCEPTION DETECTOR]**
Diagnostic: "Is ⟨4⟩ a prime ideal in ℤ?"
- MC-3 response: Long manual search with specific pairs; no quotient ring computed.

**[P64 — CONCEPTUAL SHIFT]**
"One-step test: compute ℤ/⟨4⟩≅ℤ₄. Check ℤ₄: does 2·2=4≡0? Yes, but 2≠0 in ℤ₄. So ℤ₄ has a zero divisor → ⟨4⟩ is not prime. The pair 2·2 found by the quotient ring perfectly corresponds to the element-level counterexample — but you found it in one step, not by searching."

Practice: Classify ⟨9⟩ and ⟨11⟩ in ℤ using only the quotient criterion. Time how long the direct check would take by contrast.

## Component 8 — Teaching Notes

**Structural decisions:**
- h=4 → lean structure (2 main TAs + gate)
- bloom=analyze → V-4 = N/A (no P07 required; analysis of ideal structure, not procedural execution)
- CPA_entry = C for expert learner: concrete ℤ element-check in A01 before the quotient abstraction

**Key teaching insight:** The quotient criterion is the conceptual engine of this blueprint. Every misconception (MC-1, MC-2, MC-3) reduces to bypassing this criterion. A01 introduces it via the Stage A theorem after the concrete ℤ check; A02 makes it the tool for distinguishing ⟨x⟩ and ⟨2,x⟩ in ℤ[x]. By the gate, students should reach for the quotient ring immediately upon seeing any ideal classification problem.

**P76 design note:** The transfer probe uses ⟨2,x²+1⟩ in ℤ[x], a 2-generated ideal that contains (but does not equal) the prime ideal ⟨x²+1⟩. Part (c) directly connects to the A02 lesson that ⟨x²+1⟩ is prime-not-maximal: students must find an ideal properly between ⟨x²+1⟩ and ℤ[x] to confirm non-maximality, and the probe provides exactly that case. The quotient ℤ₂[x]/⟨(x+1)²⟩ has nilpotents — showing that "bigger ideal" does not mean "better quotient."

**ℤ[x] ring choice:** ℤ[x] is the minimal ring that separates prime from maximal in an accessible way. Students who have only seen ℤ may believe prime = maximal (it holds in ℤ for nonzero ideals since ℤ is a PID of Krull dimension 1). The ℤ[x] examples (Krull dimension 2) are the essential correction.
