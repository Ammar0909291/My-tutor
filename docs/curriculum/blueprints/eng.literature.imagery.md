**Concept:** Imagery — language that evokes SENSORY experience (sight, sound, touch, taste, and
smell) in the reader, making writing vivid and immersive by appealing to the senses rather than
stating information abstractly.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Metaphor and Simile (prerequisite) taught non-literal
comparison as one tool for vivid writing. Imagery teaches a related but distinct tool: language that
appeals directly to the FIVE SENSES to create a vivid, immersive experience for the reader, which can
be entirely literal (a precise sensory description) or can work alongside figurative language, but is
defined by its sensory appeal, not by being non-literal. This concept has no further downstream
unlocks in the current curriculum but is a foundational tool for both reading and writing vivid
descriptive prose.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) identify imagery appealing to each of the five senses
in a text, citing the specific sensory words or phrases, (b) explain that imagery does not require
figurative language (metaphor or simile) — precise, literal sensory description is also imagery, and
(c) explain the specific effect a piece of imagery creates (what mood, atmosphere, or emphasis it
produces) rather than simply labeling which sense it appeals to.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-IMAGERY-MUST-BE-FIGURATIVE-USING-METAPHOR-OR-SIMILE-TO-COUNT

**Trigger signal:** Student, having just learned metaphor and simile, assumes imagery REQUIRES
figurative (non-literal) language to qualify, failing to recognize that precise, vivid, entirely
LITERAL sensory description is also genuine imagery — e.g., failing to identify "the sharp, acrid
smell of burnt toast filled the kitchen" as imagery because it contains no metaphor or simile, only
literal, specific sensory description.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two passages: one using a metaphor for smell ("the smell
hit her like a wall") and one using purely literal, precise sensory description ("the sharp, acrid
smell of burnt toast filled the kitchen"). Ask: "Does the second passage, with no metaphor or simile at
all, still create a vivid sensory experience for the reader? Is literal, precise sensory description
also a form of imagery?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming imagery must be figurative is like assuming a photograph only
counts as 'vivid' if it's been artistically distorted or stylized — but a perfectly clear, precisely
focused, literal photograph can be just as vivid and evocative as a stylized one. Imagery is defined by
its SENSORY specificity and vividness, not by whether it uses figurative comparison — precise literal
description ('the sharp, acric smell of burnt toast') is just as much imagery as a metaphor for the
same sensation."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When looking for imagery, don't require figurative language. Ask: does
this language appeal specifically and vividly to one of the five senses (sight, sound, touch, taste,
smell), whether through literal, precise description or through metaphor/simile? Both count as
imagery."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "The smell hit her like a wall." (figurative — a simile creating sensory imagery) vs. "The sharp,
   acrid smell of burnt toast filled the kitchen." (entirely literal, precise sensory description —
   still genuine imagery).
2. A student who only marks figurative sensory language as "imagery," missing precise literal
   sensory description entirely (misconception) vs. a student who identifies BOTH figurative and
   literal vivid sensory language as imagery (correct, broader understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to require figurative language for imagery after the bridge,
give them three passages containing ONLY literal (non-figurative) sensory description and have them
identify the specific sense each appeals to, confirming these count as imagery despite containing no
metaphor or simile.

### MC-B-IDENTIFYING-WHICH-SENSE-IMAGERY-APPEALS-TO-IS-THE-WHOLE-ANALYTICAL-TASK

**Trigger signal:** Student, upon identifying imagery, stops at labeling which of the five senses it
appeals to (e.g., "this is imagery appealing to smell") without going further to explain the SPECIFIC
EFFECT that imagery creates — what mood, atmosphere, association, or emphasis it produces for the
reader in that particular context — treating sense-identification as the complete analytical task
rather than its first step.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two analyses of the same piece of imagery ("the sharp,
acrid smell of burnt toast filled the kitchen"): one that stops at "this is imagery appealing to
smell," and one that continues, "this imagery appeals to smell, using the harsh words 'sharp' and
'acrid' to create an unpleasant, jarring atmosphere that suggests something has gone wrong in this
otherwise ordinary domestic scene." Ask: "Which analysis actually tells us something about how this
imagery is FUNCTIONING in the passage, not just what sense it targets?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Stopping at 'this appeals to smell' is like a music critic stopping at 'this
piece uses violins' without ever describing what mood or effect those violins actually create in the
piece — true analysis has to go further and explain the SPECIFIC EFFECT the sensory detail produces,
not just which instrument (or sense) is involved."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "After identifying which sense a piece of imagery appeals to, always add:
what SPECIFIC mood, atmosphere, or emphasis does this particular sensory detail create in this
particular context? Naming the sense is only the first half of the analysis."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "This is imagery appealing to smell." (naming only, no effect explained) vs. "This imagery appeals
   to smell, using harsh language to create an unpleasant, jarring atmosphere suggesting something has
   gone wrong." (naming plus specific effect).
2. A student analysis that lists five instances of imagery by sense with zero explanation of effect
   (surface-level labeling) vs. an analysis that identifies fewer instances but explains each one's
   specific mood or emphasis in context (deeper, effect-focused analysis).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to stop at sense-labeling after the bridge, require them to
complete the sentence stem "This imagery appeals to [sense], creating the effect of ___ because ___"
for every instance identified, making the effect-explanation step structurally unavoidable.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.literature.metaphor-and-simile`):** "Identify a metaphor or simile in this short
passage." A student who cannot identify basic figurative language lacks a related skill this concept
builds alongside — route to `eng.literature.metaphor-and-simile` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Unstylized-Photograph and Music-Critic Anchor.** Combine two images: a perfectly clear, precisely
focused, entirely literal photograph that is nonetheless vivid and evocative — directly seeding the
fix for MC-A (literal, precise sensory description is genuine imagery, not requiring figurative
distortion). Second, a music critic who stops at "this piece uses violins" without describing the mood
those violins create — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (naming the sense is only half the analysis;
the specific effect must be explained too).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Identifying Imagery Across the Five Senses

Introduce imagery appealing to each of the five senses with clear examples. Student practice: given
five short phrases, identify which sense each appeals to.

### TA-2 — Literal Sensory Description Also Counts as Imagery (MC-A)

Using the unstylized-photograph anchor, teach that imagery does not require figurative language.
Student practice: given four passages (some figurative, some purely literal sensory description),
identify the imagery in each regardless of whether it's figurative.

### TA-3 — Explaining the Specific Effect of Imagery (MC-B)

Using the music-critic anchor and the sentence-stem requirement, teach students to explain imagery's
specific mood or effect, not just name the sense. Student practice: given three pieces of imagery with
sense already identified, write the missing effect explanation for each.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Analyzing Imagery in a New Passage

Give the student a new short passage. They must identify at least two instances of imagery (TA-1),
recognizing both literal and figurative examples if present (TA-2), and explain the specific effect
each creates (TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Passage: "The sharp, acrid smell of burnt toast filled the kitchen. Outside,
the gentle rustle of leaves whispered against the window." Fully worked: first sentence identified as
literal (non-figurative) imagery appealing to smell, with the effect explained (the harsh words
create an unpleasant, jarring atmosphere); second sentence identified as imagery appealing to sound,
using personification-adjacent language ("whispered") with the effect explained (creates a calm,
gentle contrast to the harsh kitchen scene). Annotation confirms both the literal-imagery recognition
(fixing MC-A) and the effect explanation (fixing MC-B) for each instance.

**WE-2 (Guided):** New passage with two more instances of imagery, one literal and one lightly
figurative. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("Which sense does this appeal to? Does it
need to use a metaphor or simile to count as imagery? What specific mood or emphasis does this
sensory detail create in this context?") to complete the identification and effect analysis.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new short passage containing at least two instances of imagery, unaided.
Student identifies each, notes whether it's literal or figurative, and explains its specific effect.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying Imagery by Sense**
Given four short phrases, identify which sense (sight, sound, touch, taste, smell) each piece of
imagery appeals to.

**MP-2 [P36] — Recognizing Literal Sensory Description as Imagery**
Given three passages (some figurative, some purely literal), identify the imagery in each regardless
of whether it uses metaphor or simile.

**MP-3 [P36] — Explaining Imagery's Effect**
Given a piece of imagery with its sense already identified, write a specific explanation of the mood
or emphasis it creates in context.

**MP-4 [P36] — Combined Identification and Effect Analysis**
Given a new passage, identify one instance of imagery, name its sense, and explain its effect in one
combined response.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Imagery Analysis**
Given a new short passage, identify at least two instances of imagery (literal or figurative), name
the sense each appeals to, and explain the specific effect each creates.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite. During
Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1/TA-2 (requiring figurative
language) and MC-B during TA-3 (stopping at sense-naming) — MC-A generally needs resolving first since
a student who misses literal imagery entirely has an incomplete inventory to practice explaining
effects for. If either fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery
probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure indicates basic sense-identification (TA-1) needs
review, distinct from either misconception; MP-2 failure alone indicates residual MC-A; MP-3/MP-4
failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration
gap — provide one more guided full-analysis example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student can identify imagery accurately (MP-1, MP-2) but their effect explanations (MP-3)
  remain generic ("it makes the scene more vivid"), push for specificity — what EXACTLY does this
  particular sensory detail suggest or emphasize in THIS particular context.
- If the student conflates imagery with figurative language broadly (treating every metaphor or
  simile as automatically imagery even when it doesn't appeal to a specific sense), clarify that
  imagery specifically requires SENSORY appeal — a metaphor about an abstract concept with no sensory
  content is not imagery.
- If the student's own descriptive writing relies heavily on abstract statements ("it was a nice day")
  rather than specific sensory detail, connect this concept directly to writing practice, encouraging
  revision toward specific, vivid sensory language.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.composition.figurative-language-in-composition` should
  transfer well to the effect-explanation skill in TA-3, since both concepts require moving beyond
  identification to functional analysis — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
