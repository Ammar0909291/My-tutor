## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-ALL-CONJUNCTIONS-WORK-THE-SAME-WAY
- **Trigger signal:** Student treats all conjunctions as interchangeable connectors without recognizing that coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or, so — joining EQUAL, grammatically independent elements) behave differently from subordinating conjunctions (because, although, when, if — joining a dependent/subordinate clause to an independent one, creating an unequal relationship), leading to errors like joining two independent clauses with a subordinating conjunction and expecting it to work exactly like "and."
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You joined 'I was tired' and 'I went to bed' with 'because,' making 'Because I was tired, I went to bed.' Now try replacing 'because' with 'and.' Does 'I was tired and I went to bed' mean quite the same thing as showing a CAUSE-and-effect relationship?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or, so — remember 'FANBOYS': for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) join two EQUAL, independent ideas that could each stand alone. Subordinating conjunctions (because, although, when, if, since, while) join a DEPENDENT clause to an independent one, creating a specific logical relationship (cause, contrast, time, condition) — the dependent clause can't stand alone as a full sentence."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Check whether a conjunction joins two equal, independently-standing ideas (coordinating) or creates a dependent relationship showing cause/time/contrast/condition (subordinating) — they work differently and aren't simply interchangeable."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "I was tired, AND I went to bed" (coordinating — two equal, independent statements) vs. "BECAUSE I was tired, I went to bed" (subordinating — shows cause, "because I was tired" can't stand alone).
  - "She studied, BUT she still failed" (coordinating — contrast between two equal statements) vs. "ALTHOUGH she studied, she still failed" (subordinating — "although she studied" is dependent).
- **s6_path:** "It's a reasonable first assumption that all connecting words work the same way, since they're all called 'conjunctions' — but coordinating and subordinating conjunctions create genuinely different grammatical relationships, and telling them apart is a real, useful skill."

### MC-COMMA-PLACEMENT-WITH-CONJUNCTIONS-IS-RANDOM
- **Trigger signal:** Student places commas around conjunctions inconsistently/randomly rather than following the systematic rules — a comma typically comes BEFORE a coordinating conjunction joining two independent clauses ("I was tired, but I kept working"), while a comma typically comes AFTER a subordinating clause that STARTS the sentence ("Although I was tired, I kept working") but is often omitted when the subordinate clause comes second ("I kept working although I was tired").
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You put a comma after 'but' in 'I was tired but, I kept working.' Let's check: does the comma usually go BEFORE 'but' when joining two full sentences, or after it?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Comma placement with conjunctions follows learnable patterns, not randomness: a comma goes BEFORE a coordinating conjunction joining two independent clauses ('I was tired, but I kept working'). For subordinating conjunctions, a comma goes AFTER the subordinate clause when it comes FIRST ('Although I was tired, I kept working'), but is typically omitted when the subordinate clause comes SECOND ('I kept working although I was tired')."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Check the conjunction TYPE and clause ORDER to determine comma placement, rather than placing commas by feel."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "I was tired, but I kept working" (comma before coordinating conjunction) vs. "I was tired but, I kept working" (incorrect placement).
  - "Although I was tired, I kept working" (comma after a fronted subordinate clause) vs. "I kept working although I was tired" (no comma needed, subordinate clause comes second).
- **s6_path:** "Comma placement can feel like guesswork since native speakers often place them 'by ear' — but there are genuine, learnable patterns tied to conjunction type and clause order that make this far more predictable than it seems."

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The equal-bridge vs. leaning-bridge**

> Build two simple physical demonstrations with blocks or cards: an "equal bridge" — two same-height blocks connected by a flat card labeled "AND/BUT/OR" (representing coordinating conjunctions joining equal ideas) — and a "leaning bridge" — one taller block (independent clause) with a smaller, leaning block (dependent clause) connected by a card labeled "BECAUSE/ALTHOUGH/WHEN" (representing subordinating conjunctions, where one clause depends on/leans on the other). This anchors the equal-vs-dependent structural distinction visually and physically.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Coordinating Conjunctions (FANBOYS) [C]**

Present sentence pairs; student joins them using coordinating conjunctions (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so), confirming both original sentences could stand alone independently.

**TA-2 — Subordinating Conjunctions [C]**

Present sentence pairs; student joins them using subordinating conjunctions (because, although, when, if, since, while), identifying which clause becomes dependent, directly targeting MC-ALL-CONJUNCTIONS-WORK-THE-SAME-WAY.

**TA-3 — Correlative Conjunctions [C→P]**

Introduce correlative conjunction pairs (either...or, neither...nor, both...and, not only...but also); student uses them correctly to join parallel elements.

**TA-4 — Comma Placement with Coordinating Conjunctions [P]**

Student practices placing commas correctly before coordinating conjunctions joining two independent clauses, directly targeting MC-COMMA-PLACEMENT-WITH-CONJUNCTIONS-IS-RANDOM.

**TA-5 — Comma Placement with Subordinating Conjunctions (Clause Order) [P]**

Student practices placing commas correctly based on subordinate-clause position (comma after a fronted subordinate clause; no comma when it follows the independent clause), completing the comma-placement pattern.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — coordinating conjunction)**

> "Join these with a coordinating conjunction: 'I like tea.' 'I like coffee.'"

Target response: "I like tea and coffee" (or "I like tea, and I like coffee").

**WE-2 (Foundational — subordinating conjunction)**

> "Join these using 'because': 'I was tired.' 'I went to bed.'"

Target response: "I went to bed because I was tired" or "Because I was tired, I went to bed."

**WE-3 (Intermediate — comma placement)**

> "Where does the comma go in: 'Although it was raining we went outside'?"

Target response: "Although it was raining, we went outside" — comma after the fronted subordinate clause.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Coordinating conjunction, novel pair**
"Join these with 'but': 'She studied hard.' 'She still failed.'"
*Target:* "She studied hard, but she still failed."

**MP-2 [P36] — Subordinating conjunction, novel pair**
"Join these using 'when': 'The phone rang.' 'I was cooking.'"
*Target:* "The phone rang when I was cooking" or "When I was cooking, the phone rang."

**MP-3 [P36] — Correlative conjunction, novel example**
"Use 'either...or' to combine: 'We can go to the beach.' 'We can go to the park.'"
*Target:* "We can either go to the beach or go to the park" (or similar correct parallel structure).

**MP-4 [P36] — Comma placement, coordinating conjunction**
"Where does the comma go in: 'I finished my homework and I watched TV'?"
*Target:* "I finished my homework, and I watched TV" — comma before "and."

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain conjunction types and comma rules**
"In your own words: what's the difference between a coordinating and a subordinating conjunction, and how does that affect comma placement?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that coordinating conjunctions join equal independent clauses (comma before the conjunction), while subordinating conjunctions create a dependent relationship (comma depends on clause order — after the subordinate clause if it comes first, often omitted if it comes second).

**Protocol routing:** S0 (secure on word-classes-overview, new to conjunction subtypes): dwell on TA-1's coordinating conjunctions before introducing the more complex subordinating relationship. S1 (already joins clauses fluently in speech but writes run-ons or comma splices): focus on TA-4/TA-5's comma-placement rules, since spoken fluency doesn't guarantee correct written punctuation. S6 (frustrated that comma rules feel arbitrary): validate that the rules ARE systematic and tied to clause order/conjunction type (Component 1 s6_path), giving a concrete checklist rather than "just a feel." S9 (L1 has a different clause-joining/punctuation convention, e.g. different comma norms, or a language that doesn't distinguish coordinating/subordinating relationships the same way): expect this concept to require explicit, dedicated contrastive teaching, since comma-with-conjunction rules are English-specific conventions that don't reliably transfer from other punctuation systems.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Equal vs. dependent is the core structural distinction**: always frame coordinating vs. subordinating conjunctions around whether the joined clauses are grammatically equal/independent or one depends on the other — this is the conceptual anchor for the whole concept.
- **Comma rules are systematic, tied to conjunction type and clause order**: teach comma placement as a checkable rule (not a feel-based judgment), directly countering the perception of randomness.
- **FANBOYS as a memory aid**: the coordinating-conjunction mnemonic (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) is a genuinely useful, concrete memory tool worth explicitly teaching and reusing.
- **Comma-with-conjunction conventions are English-specific and need explicit teaching for L1 punctuation-system mismatches**: for S9 learners, don't assume punctuation conventions transfer from L1 — teach the specific English comma rules explicitly and with dedicated practice.
