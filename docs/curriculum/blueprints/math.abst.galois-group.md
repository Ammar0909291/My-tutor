## Component 1 — Learning Objectives

- LO1: Apply the equality $|\text{Gal}(K/F)|=[K:F]$ — recognizing this holds SPECIFICALLY for GALOIS extensions, and verifying it genuinely FAILS for a non-Galois extension of the same degree.
- LO2: Compute $\text{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p)\cong\mathbb{Z}_n$ concretely, generated by the **Frobenius automorphism** $\sigma(x)=x^p$ — recognizing $\sigma$ is not merely an example element but the specific GENERATOR of the entire cyclic group.
- LO3 *(orientation-level, given the research-level scope of this concept)*: Recognize Galois groups are not always as simple as the small abelian examples studied — the generic degree-5 polynomial's Galois group is the full, non-abelian symmetric group $S_5$ (order 120) — a caution against overgeneralizing from small, well-behaved cases.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Check

Assumes mastery of `math.abst.galois-theory` (the definition of $\text{Gal}(K/F)$ as $F$-fixing field automorphisms under composition; the Fundamental Theorem verified concretely on $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,\sqrt3)/\mathbb{Q}$, with $|\text{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q})|=4=[K:\mathbb{Q}]$).

## Component 3 — Core Explanation

**$|\text{Gal}(K/F)|=[K:F]$ requires the Galois-extension hypothesis specifically**: `math.abst.galois-theory` already verified $|\text{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,\sqrt3)/\mathbb{Q})|=4=[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,\sqrt3):\mathbb{Q}]$ on a GALOIS extension. This equality is NOT automatic for every field extension — it specifically requires $K/F$ to be Galois (normal AND separable); for a non-Galois extension of the same degree, the Galois group can be strictly SMALLER than the degree, since not every embedding of $K$ extends to an automorphism landing back inside $K$ itself.

**The Frobenius automorphism generates the entire cyclic Galois group of a finite field extension**: for $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p$, the map $\sigma(x)=x^p$ (the Frobenius automorphism) is a genuine field automorphism fixing $\mathbb{F}_p$ pointwise (by Fermat's Little Theorem, generalized: every element of $\mathbb{F}_p$ satisfies $x^p=x$). Its powers $\sigma,\sigma^2,\dots,\sigma^n=\text{id}$ generate the ENTIRE Galois group $\text{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p)\cong\mathbb{Z}_n$ — $\sigma$ is not one example automorphism among others; it IS the generator producing every element of the group.

**Not every Galois group is small and abelian**: the concrete examples studied so far ($\mathbb{Z}/2\times\mathbb{Z}/2$ for $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,\sqrt3)/\mathbb{Q}$; $\mathbb{Z}_n$ for $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p$) are both abelian and relatively small. For a GENERIC degree-5 polynomial, the Galois group of its splitting field is the FULL symmetric group $S_5$ (order $120$, genuinely non-abelian) — exactly the group whose non-solvability underlies Abel–Ruffini's theorem (already introduced in `math.abst.galois-theory`'s own orientation-level discussion). Galois groups can be considerably more complex than the small, well-behaved examples studied first.

## Component 4 — Worked Examples

**Example 1 (LO1 — the equality fails for a non-Galois extension, reusing already-established results, breaking MC-1)**: `math.abst.galois-theory`'s own example confirmed $|\text{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,\sqrt3)/\mathbb{Q})|=4=[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,\sqrt3):\mathbb{Q}]$ — a Galois extension, equality holds. Contrast: $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]2)/\mathbb{Q}$ has $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]2):\mathbb{Q}]=3$ (established via Eisenstein's criterion in `math.abst.algebraic-extension`'s own example) — but this extension is NOT Galois (it is not a splitting field: $x^3-2$'s minimal polynomial has two OTHER complex roots not contained in the entirely-real field $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]2)$). Any automorphism must send $\sqrt[3]2$ to another root of $x^3-2$ WITHIN $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]2)$ — but the only such root available inside this real field is $\sqrt[3]2$ itself (the other two roots are genuinely complex). So $\text{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]2)/\mathbb{Q})=\{\text{id}\}$, order $1\ne3=[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]2):\mathbb{Q}]$ — the equality genuinely FAILS here, confirming the Galois hypothesis is necessary, not automatic.

**Example 2 (LO2 — Frobenius as the group's generator, breaking MC-2)**: For $\mathbb{F}_8/\mathbb{F}_2$ ($p=2,n=3$): $\sigma(x)=x^2$ (Frobenius). Compute $\sigma^2(x)=x^4$, $\sigma^3(x)=x^8=x$ (since every element of $\mathbb{F}_8$ satisfies $x^8=x$, the finite-field analogue of Fermat's Little Theorem) — so $\sigma$ has order EXACTLY $3$, matching $[\mathbb{F}_8:\mathbb{F}_2]=3$ (finite field extensions are always Galois, so equality holds here too). The full Galois group is $\{\text{id},\sigma,\sigma^2\}$ — every single element is a POWER of $\sigma$; $\sigma$ is not merely "an example automorphism" but genuinely GENERATES the entire cyclic group.

**Example 3 (orientation-level — Galois groups can be much larger and non-abelian, breaking MC-3)**: The two Galois groups studied so far — $\mathbb{Z}/2\times\mathbb{Z}/2$ (order 4, abelian, from $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,\sqrt3)/\mathbb{Q}$) and $\mathbb{Z}_3$ (order 3, abelian, from $\mathbb{F}_8/\mathbb{F}_2$) — are both small and abelian. For a GENERIC degree-5 polynomial, the Galois group of its splitting field is $S_5$, the full symmetric group on 5 elements, order $120$ — genuinely NON-ABELIAN (e.g., two specific permutations composed in different orders give different results). This is exactly the group `math.abst.galois-theory`'s own orientation-level LO3 identified as non-solvable, underlying Abel–Ruffini's theorem — a far more complex object than either small example studied here.

## Component 5 — Teaching Actions

### Teaching Action A01 — The Galois Hypothesis Is Necessary, Not Automatic (Primitive P28: Conflict Evidence)

Present Example 1's direct evidence: $|\text{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,\sqrt3)/\mathbb{Q})|=4=[K:\mathbb{Q}]$ (Galois, equality holds) against $|\text{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]2)/\mathbb{Q})|=1\ne3=[K:\mathbb{Q}]$ (not Galois, equality fails). State: "$|\text{Gal}(K/F)|=[K:F]$ is a special property of GALOIS extensions — it can genuinely fail otherwise, as this real-cube-root case shows."

- **MC-1 hook**: ask "does $|\text{Gal}(K/F)|=[K:F]$ hold for every finite field extension, regardless of whether it's Galois?" — a "yes" answer reveals MC-1 (missing that this equality specifically requires the Galois-extension hypothesis).

### Teaching Action A02 — Frobenius Generates, Doesn't Just Exemplify (Primitive P11: Representation Shift)

State: "$\sigma(x)=x^p$ isn't just one convenient automorphism to check — its powers produce EVERY element of the Galois group, making it the group's generator." Work Example 2's explicit computation of $\sigma,\sigma^2,\sigma^3=\text{id}$ for $\mathbb{F}_8/\mathbb{F}_2$.

- **MC-2 hook**: ask "is the Frobenius automorphism just one example of an element in $\text{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p)$, among possibly many other unrelated automorphisms?" — a "yes" answer reveals MC-2 (missing that Frobenius specifically generates the entire cyclic group).

### Teaching Action A03 — Galois Groups Can Be Large and Non-Abelian (Primitive P06: Contrast Pair)

Contrast the two small, abelian groups studied ($\mathbb{Z}/2\times\mathbb{Z}/2$, $\mathbb{Z}_3$) against Example 3's $S_5$ (order 120, non-abelian). State: "don't let these small, well-behaved examples convince you every Galois group looks like this — the generic quintic's Galois group is genuinely large and non-abelian."

- **MC-3 hook**: ask "do all Galois groups resemble the small, abelian examples studied so far (Klein four-group, cyclic groups)?" — a "yes" answer reveals MC-3 (overgeneralizing from small examples, missing that Galois groups can be much larger and non-abelian).

### Teaching Action A04 — Mastery Gate (Primitive P91)

P91 = P77 → P55 → P76 → P55 → P75 → P55 → P74 → P55 → P78.

- **P77 (4-problem set)**:
  1. Explain why $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]2)/\mathbb{Q}$ fails to be Galois, referencing its missing complex roots.
  2. Compute the Frobenius automorphism's order for $\mathbb{F}_{27}/\mathbb{F}_3$ ($p=3,n=3$), verifying it matches $[\mathbb{F}_{27}:\mathbb{F}_3]$.
  3. Explain why $|\text{Gal}(K/F)|=[K:F]$ failing for $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]2)/\mathbb{Q}$ does not contradict the Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory verified on $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt2,\sqrt3)/\mathbb{Q}$.
  4. Explain why the fact that $S_5$ is non-abelian is relevant to understanding why the general quintic behaves differently from lower-degree polynomials.
- **P76 (Transfer Probe, mode = independence)**: "A cryptographer working with finite-field-based algorithms relies on the structure of $\text{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p)$ being cyclic, generated by Frobenius, to design an efficient computation. (a) Explain why the cryptographer can safely assume $|\text{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p)|=n$ without separately verifying the Galois property each time, for any finite field extension. (b) A colleague, working on a DIFFERENT problem involving the extension $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[5]2)/\mathbb{Q}$ (degree 5), assumes by analogy that its Galois group also has order 5, cyclic, generated by some natural automorphism. Explain specifically why this assumption is unjustified, using this lesson's non-Galois-extension reasoning. (c) The colleague then asks whether Galois groups arising from degree-5 extensions are generally simple, cyclic groups like the finite-field case. Explain why the generic degree-5 case (via $S_5$) suggests the opposite."
- **P55 (Score)** after each stage.
- **P75 (Mastery Assessment against MAMR = 3/5)**.
