## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-TEXT-CAN-ONLY-HAVE-ONE-PURPOSE-INFORM-OR-PERSUADE-OR-ENTERTAIN
- **Trigger signal:** Student forces every text into exactly one of the three classic purpose categories (inform, persuade, entertain) even when a text genuinely blends purposes — for example, an entertaining story that also subtly persuades the reader toward a moral viewpoint, or an informative article written with an entertaining, humorous style — refusing to recognize that purposes can combine or that one purpose can be primary while another is secondary.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present a short humorous personal essay that both entertains (funny anecdotes, playful language) AND persuades (arguing for a specific viewpoint about how to live a happier life, using the humor to make the argument land). Ask the student to name "the" single purpose, then ask whether the text is doing more than one job at once.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Purposes aren't always neatly separate — a text can entertain while also persuading, or inform while also entertaining through an engaging style. Instead of forcing one label, ask which purpose is PRIMARY (the main goal) and whether a secondary purpose is also genuinely present. Real texts often blend purposes; that's not a failure of classification, it's just how writing often works."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't force a text into exactly one purpose category. Ask: what's the primary goal here — informing, persuading, or entertaining? Then check whether a secondary purpose is also clearly present, and name both if so, rather than picking just one and ignoring the rest."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Single-purpose-only classification (every text has exactly one purpose, chosen from inform/persuade/entertain) vs. (b) Primary-plus-secondary-purpose recognition (a text can have one primary purpose and a genuine secondary purpose simultaneously, and both should be named when present).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1 re-check, then re-run TA-1's multi-purpose sort before continuing.

### MC-TONE-IS-JUST-WHATEVER-EMOTION-THE-READER-HAPPENS-TO-FEEL
- **Trigger signal:** Student confuses the author's tone (the attitude conveyed through the author's own word choice and style, which is a property of the TEXT) with the reader's personal emotional reaction (which is a property of the READER and can vary from person to person) — describing "tone" by reporting how the passage made them feel rather than analyzing what attitude the author's specific word choices actually convey.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present a passage describing a difficult historical event using clinical, detached, factual language (e.g., "The battle resulted in significant casualties on both sides.") Ask the student what the AUTHOR's tone is (likely: neutral, detached, factual/clinical) versus how the passage might make different readers FEEL (one reader might feel sad, another might feel nothing, another might feel angry) — demonstrating that tone (an author's crafted attitude) and reader emotional response (personal and variable) are different things, even though tone often influences reader response.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Tone is about the AUTHOR's attitude, shown through specific word choices, sentence structure, and style — not about how you personally happen to feel reading it. Two different readers can feel differently about the same passage, but the author's tone (detached, sarcastic, urgent, reverent, etc.) is something you can point to in the text itself, in specific word choices, regardless of the reader's personal reaction."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "To identify tone, look at the author's specific word choices and style — are they formal or casual, warm or clinical, serious or playful? Point to specific words or phrases as evidence. Don't just report your own emotional reaction; analyze what attitude the author's craft actually conveys."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Tone-as-reader-feeling (tone is whatever emotional reaction the reader personally experiences) vs. (b) Tone-as-authorial-attitude (tone is the attitude conveyed by the author's specific word choices and style, evidenced by the text itself, distinct from any individual reader's personal emotional response).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1, then re-present the Concrete Anchor's word-choice-evidence framing before continuing to TA-3.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

### PD-1: Inference Readiness
- **Tests:** Can the student already draw evidence-supported conclusions from textual clues combined with background knowledge — the exact skill purpose/tone identification depends on, since neither is usually stated directly and must be inferred from word-choice and structural evidence?
- **Probe:** Give a short passage and ask the student to infer something not explicitly stated, citing specific textual evidence.
- **Pass condition:** Produces a reasonable, evidence-cited inference.
- **Fail routing:** If inference-making fails, route to `eng.reading.inference-in-reading` review — purpose and tone identification are both specialized applications of general inference skill.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**"The Word-Choice Detective Board."** Present a short passage with several key words/phrases highlighted (e.g., "the crumbling old shack" vs. a neutral alternative "the older house"). Have the student physically pin evidence cards (specific highlighted words) onto a detective board next to a "verdict" card stating the inferred tone (e.g., "critical/negative attitude toward the building"). Emphasize: the verdict must be pinned to SPECIFIC WORD EVIDENCE from the text, not to "how I feel about old houses" — directly seeding MC-TONE-IS-JUST-WHATEVER-EMOTION-THE-READER-HAPPENS-TO-FEEL's fix. Separately, present a text with two verdict cards simultaneously pinnable (e.g., "primarily informs" AND "secondarily entertains") to show a text can legitimately support more than one purpose verdict at once — seeding MC-A-TEXT-CAN-ONLY-HAVE-ONE-PURPOSE-INFORM-OR-PERSUADE-OR-ENTERTAIN's fix.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1: Multi-Purpose Text Sorting
Present 4 texts, including at least one blended-purpose text (e.g., a humorously-written informative article), and have the student identify primary and, where present, secondary purposes rather than forcing a single label. Directly counters MC-A-TEXT-CAN-ONLY-HAVE-ONE-PURPOSE-INFORM-OR-PERSUADE-OR-ENTERTAIN.

### TA-2: Word-Choice Evidence for Tone
Present passages with contrasting word choices describing the same subject (e.g., "the crumbling old shack" vs. "the historic older home") and have the student identify the tone each conveys, citing the specific word choice as evidence — building the evidence-based tone-identification habit.

### TA-3: Tone vs. Reader Reaction
Using the discrimination pairs from MC-TONE-IS-JUST-WHATEVER-EMOTION-THE-READER-HAPPENS-TO-FEEL, present a passage and have two different (real or hypothetical) readers report different personal emotional reactions, while the student separately identifies the single, text-evidenced authorial tone that both readers are reacting to — demonstrating tone and reaction are distinct.

### TA-4: Author's Purpose Signals
Teach structural and stylistic signals of each purpose (informational: fact-heavy, neutral tone, statistics/data; persuasive: opinion language, calls to action, emotionally-loaded word choice; entertaining: narrative elements, humor, vivid sensory description) and have the student identify these signals in 4 new passages.

### TA-5: Analyzing Purpose and Tone Together in a Novel Text
Student analyzes a new, unscaffolded passage for both purpose (primary and any secondary) and tone (with specific word-choice evidence), producing a combined analysis — the generative capstone integrating both skills.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

### WE-1: Purpose Identification with Blend (Scaffolded)
**Text:** A blog post that humorously recounts a camping mishap while also subtly arguing that people should unplug from technology more often.
**Walkthrough:** Identify primary purpose as entertaining (the humorous anecdotal style) with a secondary persuasive purpose (the underlying argument about unplugging), confirming both can be named rather than forcing one label.

### WE-2: Tone from Word-Choice Evidence (Guided)
**Sentence pair:** "The committee's decision was, frankly, a reckless gamble with taxpayer money." vs. "The committee's decision involved allocating additional taxpayer funds."
**Walkthrough:** Identify the first as conveying a critical, alarmed tone (evidenced by "frankly," "reckless gamble") and the second as neutral/factual (evidenced by plain, unloaded language), pointing to specific words as evidence rather than reporting a personal reaction.

### WE-3: Full Purpose-and-Tone Analysis (Independent)
**Passage:** A persuasive editorial arguing for a new park, using vivid, warm language about community togetherness and a hopeful, encouraging tone throughout.
**Walkthrough:** Student identifies primary purpose (persuade), notes secondary quality (the vivid, almost entertaining descriptive style), and identifies tone as hopeful/encouraging, citing specific evidence (word choices like "vibrant," "come together," "brighter future").

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identify primary and secondary purpose**
Given a blended-purpose text, student identifies both a primary and secondary purpose rather than forcing a single label. *(Directly targets MC-A-TEXT-CAN-ONLY-HAVE-ONE-PURPOSE-INFORM-OR-PERSUADE-OR-ENTERTAIN.)*

**MP-2 [P36] — Distinguish tone from personal reaction**
Given a passage and a description of one reader's strong personal emotional reaction, student identifies the text's actual authorial tone using word-choice evidence, correctly distinguishing it from the reader's individual response. *(Directly targets MC-TONE-IS-JUST-WHATEVER-EMOTION-THE-READER-HAPPENS-TO-FEEL.)*

**MP-3 [P36] — Purpose-signal recognition**
Given 4 unlabeled passages, student identifies the primary purpose of each using structural/stylistic signals as evidence.

**MP-4 [P36] — Tone identification with evidence**
Given a passage, student identifies its tone and cites at least 2 specific word choices as supporting evidence.

**MP-5 [P36] — Combined novel-text analysis**
Given a novel passage, student produces a combined purpose-and-tone analysis, correctly identifying primary (and any secondary) purpose and tone with specific textual evidence.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** At session open (S0), confirm PD-1 passes — purpose and tone identification are both specialized inference tasks, requiring the student to draw evidence-supported conclusions rather than state literal facts. During instruction (S1), watch for both misconception patterns: forcing single-purpose classification (MC-A-TEXT-CAN-ONLY-HAVE-ONE-PURPOSE-INFORM-OR-PERSUADE-OR-ENTERTAIN) and conflating tone with personal reaction (MC-TONE-IS-JUST-WHATEVER-EMOTION-THE-READER-HAPPENS-TO-FEEL) both substitute an oversimplified judgment for genuine textual-evidence analysis — redirect both to the Word-Choice Detective Board anchor. If the student forces a single purpose label on a blended text or reports personal feelings instead of textual tone during TA-1/TA-3 (S6), drop back to the Concrete Anchor's dual-verdict and evidence-pinning demonstrations before returning to independent analysis. At mastery-probe stage (S9), do not certify mastery unless MP-1 and MP-2 both pass — a student who identifies purpose signals correctly (MP-3) but forces single-purpose labels or conflates tone with reaction has not yet learned the precise analytical judgment this concept requires.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student forces every text into a single purpose category, weight remediation toward MC-A-TEXT-CAN-ONLY-HAVE-ONE-PURPOSE-INFORM-OR-PERSUADE-OR-ENTERTAIN's bridge text and re-run TA-1's multi-purpose sort before returning to novel-text analysis.
- If the student reports personal emotional reaction instead of authorial tone, weight remediation toward MC-TONE-IS-JUST-WHATEVER-EMOTION-THE-READER-HAPPENS-TO-FEEL's bridge text and TA-2/TA-3's word-choice-evidence practice specifically.
- This concept feeds one direct downstream unlock (`eng.reading.close-reading`) — do not certify mastery without TA-5's combined novel-text analysis, since close reading requires simultaneously tracking multiple layers of textual evidence (purpose, tone, and more), building directly on this concept's evidence-based, multi-dimensional analytical habit.
- Reuse the "word-choice detective board" framing explicitly when introducing `eng.reading.close-reading` — that concept extends the same specific-word-choice-as-evidence discipline to a wider range of literary analysis questions.
