**Concept:** Genre Recognition — identifying a text's genre (fiction, nonfiction, poetry, drama, and
their subtypes) and the conventions associated with it, by reading for purpose and structural
convention rather than surface appearance alone.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Text Structure (prerequisite) taught recognizing how a
text is organized (chronological, cause-effect, compare-contrast, and so on). Genre Recognition
extends this by teaching students to use structural and purpose-based cues to identify what KIND of
text they are reading, and to recognize that some texts deliberately blend genre conventions rather
than fitting neatly into one box. This concept has no further downstream unlocks in the current
curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) identify a text's genre using its overall purpose
and structural conventions rather than superficial surface features alone, and (b) recognize
hybrid or blended texts that combine conventions from more than one genre, rather than assuming
every text fits into exactly one fixed genre box.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-A-TEXTS-GENRE-CAN-BE-IDENTIFIED-FROM-ONE-SURFACE-FEATURE-ALONE

**Trigger signal:** Student identifies genre by a single superficial surface feature (line breaks
mean poetry; dialogue in quotation marks means fiction; a serious tone means nonfiction) rather than
by the text's overall purpose and structural conventions, leading to genre misidentification when a
surface feature appears in an unexpected genre (a nonfiction book written with line breaks; a
fictionalized biography that reads "seriously").

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a nonfiction informational text about ocean animals
written with poetic line breaks for young readers, next to an actual poem. Ask: "Does having line
breaks automatically make a text a poem? What does the ocean-animals text's actual PURPOSE tell you
about its real genre, regardless of its line breaks?"

**Conflict evidence continued:** Also show a fictionalized biography (a novel based on a real
person's life, containing invented dialogue) next to a factual biography. Ask: "Does sounding
'serious' or being about a real person automatically make a text nonfiction?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Identifying genre from one surface feature is like assuming any building with
a pointed roof must be a church — plenty of houses have pointed roofs too. A single surface feature
can appear in many different genres; genre is really determined by the text's overall purpose (to
inform, to tell an invented story, to persuade) and its full set of structural conventions, not any
one visible trait."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't identify genre from one surface feature alone. Ask: what is this
text's overall PURPOSE — to inform with verified facts, to tell an invented story, to persuade, to
evoke feeling through condensed language? Check multiple structural conventions together, not just
one visible trait like line breaks or dialogue."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A nonfiction informational text about ocean animals written with poetic line breaks (surface
   feature suggests poetry, but purpose and content are informational nonfiction) vs. an actual poem
   using line breaks to shape rhythm and meaning (genuine poetry).
2. A student who calls any text with dialogue "fiction" (misconception — surface feature only) vs. a
   student who checks whether the dialogue is invented (fiction) or a verified quotation from a real
   source (nonfiction) before deciding (correct, purpose-based check).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to rely on single surface features after the bridge, give them
three texts each with one misleading surface feature (a nonfiction text with line breaks, a fiction
text with a serious tone, a play script with narrative-style stage directions) and have them identify
the actual genre using purpose and full structural convention instead.

### MC-B-EVERY-TEXT-BELONGS-TO-EXACTLY-ONE-FIXED-GENRE-WITH-NO-OVERLAP

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes genre categories are fixed, mutually exclusive boxes, failing to
recognize that some texts deliberately blend conventions from more than one genre (a narrative-
nonfiction memoir using story structure to tell true events; a graphic novel that can be fiction or
nonfiction depending on content) — forcing a hybrid text into a single ill-fitting category rather
than recognizing its blended nature.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a memoir that uses a clear narrative arc (rising
action, climax, resolution) to tell a true, factual story of the author's life. Ask: "Is this text
telling a true story (nonfiction) or does it use story-shaped structure (usually associated with
fiction)? Can a text be both factual AND structured like a story at the same time?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming every text fits exactly one genre box is like assuming every dish
must be purely 'sweet' or purely 'savory' — but plenty of dishes are deliberately both. Narrative
nonfiction deliberately borrows story structure to tell true events; some texts are genuinely
hybrids, blending conventions from more than one genre rather than belonging cleanly to just one."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't force every text into exactly one fixed genre box. Ask: does this
text combine conventions from more than one genre — for example, true content told with story
structure? If so, name it as a hybrid (like narrative nonfiction) rather than picking just one
category that doesn't fully fit."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A memoir using narrative arc structure to tell factual events (a genuine fiction-nonfiction
   hybrid: narrative nonfiction) vs. a purely informational nonfiction text with no story structure
   at all (straightforward nonfiction, no hybrid).
2. A student who forces a narrative-nonfiction memoir into either "pure fiction" or "pure nonfiction"
   (misconception — ignoring its blended nature) vs. a student who correctly names it as narrative
   nonfiction, a recognized hybrid genre (correct recognition).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to force hybrid texts into single categories after the bridge,
give them three genuinely hybrid texts (a graphic memoir, a narrative-nonfiction article, a
docudrama script) and have them identify specifically which conventions from which genres each one
combines.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.reading.text-structure`):** "Identify the organizational structure of this
passage (chronological, cause-effect, compare-contrast, or problem-solution)." A student who cannot
recognize basic text structure lacks the foundation for identifying genre-specific structural
conventions — route to `eng.reading.text-structure` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Pointed-Roof-House and Sweet-and-Savory-Dish Anchor.** A house with a pointed roof mistaken
for a church based on one surface feature alone — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (genre requires
checking overall purpose and multiple structural conventions, not one surface trait). Second, a dish
that is deliberately both sweet and savory at once, resisting a single fixed category — directly
seeding the fix for MC-B (some texts are genuine hybrids blending more than one genre's
conventions, not forced into one fixed box).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Purpose and Convention, Not One Surface Feature (MC-A)

Using the pointed-roof anchor, teach checking a text's overall purpose and multiple structural
conventions together. Student practice: given a text with one misleading surface feature, identify
its actual genre using purpose and full convention instead.

### TA-2 — The Major Genres and Their Core Conventions

Extend TA-1: teach the core purpose and structural conventions of fiction, nonfiction, poetry, and
drama. Student practice: given four short unlabeled texts, match each to its genre and state the
convention that confirms it.

### TA-3 — Recognizing Genre Subtypes

Extend TA-2 into subtypes within a genre (e.g., within nonfiction: biography, informational text,
persuasive essay). Student practice: given three nonfiction texts, identify each one's specific
subtype.

### TA-4 — Recognizing Hybrid and Blended Texts (MC-B)

Using the sweet-and-savory anchor, teach identifying texts that deliberately blend conventions from
more than one genre. Student practice: given a narrative-nonfiction text, identify which
conventions come from which genre rather than forcing it into one category.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Four short unlabeled texts (a folk tale, a science article, a poem about
autumn, a scene from a play), fully worked: each identified by purpose and structural convention
rather than surface feature; a fifth text (a narrative-nonfiction excerpt) explicitly identified as a
fiction-nonfiction hybrid, naming which conventions come from each genre. Annotation confirms both
the purpose-and-convention identification method (fixing MC-A) and the hybrid-recognition principle
(fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new set of unlabeled texts including one hybrid. Student is prompted with
guiding questions ("What is this text's overall purpose? Does it rely on one surface feature that
might be misleading? Does it combine conventions from more than one genre?") to complete the
identification.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new set of texts, unaided, including at least one hybrid. Student
identifies each genre or hybrid classification independently.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Avoiding the Single-Surface-Feature Trap**
Given a text with one misleading surface feature, identify its actual genre using purpose and full
structural convention.

**MP-2 [P36] — Matching Texts to Major Genres**
Given four short unlabeled texts, match each to fiction, nonfiction, poetry, or drama and state the
confirming convention.

**MP-3 [P36] — Identifying Genre Subtypes**
Given three texts within one genre, identify each one's specific subtype.

**MP-4 [P36] — Recognizing a Hybrid Text**
Given a narrative-nonfiction or other hybrid text, identify which conventions come from which
genre.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Genre Identification Across a New Text Set**
Given a new set of unlabeled texts including at least one hybrid, identify each one's genre or
hybrid classification.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite.
During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1/TA-2 (surface-

failures indicate residual MC-A; MP-4 failure indicates residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1
through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-set example before
re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly identifies genre on texts with obvious conventions (MP-2) but reverts to
  surface-feature judgments on a text with one misleading trait (MP-1), provide a mandatory
  purpose-first checklist step before checking any surface feature.
- If the student's major-genre identification is strong (MP-2) but subtype identification (MP-3)
  remains shaky, provide a reference list of common subtypes within each major genre as a scaffolding
  aid.
- If the student struggles to recognize hybrid texts at all, provide one fully-annotated hybrid
  example showing exactly which conventions come from which genre before asking them to analyze a
  new one independently.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.reading.text-structure`'s structural-pattern recognition
  should transfer readily to genre-convention recognition, since both require identifying underlying
  organizational patterns rather than surface details — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
