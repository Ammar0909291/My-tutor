**Concept:** Academic Vocabulary — the general, discipline-neutral words (analyze, significant,
factor, concept, method, evidence, interpret, context) that recur constantly across academic texts
in every subject, distinct from the technical, subject-specific jargon of any one field.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Register and Formality (prerequisite) taught
recognizing formal vs. informal register. Word Formation Processes (prerequisite) taught how words
are built from roots and affixes. Academic Vocabulary extends both by teaching a specific, bounded
word set: the general-purpose academic words that appear across biology, history, and literature
alike, and the precise academic senses those words carry — senses that often differ from their
everyday, informal meaning. This concept has no further downstream unlocks in the current
curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) distinguish general academic vocabulary (words
recurring across every academic discipline) from discipline-specific technical jargon (words
belonging to one field only), and (b) use common academic words in their precise academic sense
rather than their looser everyday sense, recognizing when a word's meaning shifts between casual and
academic contexts.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-ANY-UNFAMILIAR-OR-COMPLEX-SOUNDING-WORD-IN-AN-ACADEMIC-TEXT-COUNTS-AS-ACADEMIC-VOCABULARY

**Trigger signal:** Student treats any unfamiliar, complex, or subject-specific technical term
encountered in an academic text (e.g., "mitochondria," "photosynthesis," "iambic pentameter") as
"academic vocabulary," failing to distinguish general academic words that recur across every
discipline from technical jargon that belongs to one specific field only.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a biology passage containing both "mitochondria"
(technical, biology-only) and "significant" (general academic, appears in biology, history, and
literature alike). Ask: "If you read a history essay or a literature essay, would you expect to see
the word 'mitochondria'? Would you expect to see the word 'significant'?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Treating every unfamiliar academic-sounding word as 'academic vocabulary' is
like calling every specialized tool in a workshop a 'general-purpose tool' — a soldering iron only
belongs in an electronics workshop, but a screwdriver is used in nearly every workshop there is.
General academic vocabulary is the screwdriver: words that recur across every subject. Technical
jargon is the soldering iron: it belongs to one field."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before calling a word 'academic vocabulary,' ask: would this word
plausibly appear in an essay about a completely different subject too? If yes, it's general academic
vocabulary. If it only makes sense within one specific field, it's technical jargon for that field,
not general academic vocabulary."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Mitochondria" (technical, biology-only — would not appear in a history essay) vs. "significant"
   (general academic — appears naturally in biology, history, and literature essays alike).
2. A student who lists every complex word from a science passage as "academic vocabulary"
   (misconception) vs. a student who separates the passage's general academic words from its
   subject-specific technical terms (correct distinction).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to conflate technical jargon with academic vocabulary after
the bridge, give them passages from three different subjects and have them sort all unfamiliar words
into two columns — "appears across subjects" and "belongs to this subject only" — confirming the
distinction empirically.

### MC-B-ACADEMIC-WORDS-MEAN-THE-SAME-THING-IN-ACADEMIC-WRITING-AS-THEY-DO-IN-EVERYDAY-CASUAL-SPEECH

**Trigger signal:** Student applies a common word's everyday, casual meaning when the word appears
in academic context, failing to recognize that many academic words carry a more precise, technical
sense in formal writing than their everyday sense suggests (e.g., "argument" as a fight/disagreement
vs. "argument" as a reasoned case supported by evidence; "significant" as important/big vs.
"significant" as a specific measure of a non-random effect).

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student the sentence "They had an argument about the results"
in a casual context (a disagreement) next to "The essay's argument is supported by three pieces of
evidence" in an academic context (a reasoned case, not a fight). Ask: "Does 'argument' mean the same
thing in both sentences? What does it mean in each?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming an academic word means exactly what it means casually is like
assuming a 'trial' in a science class means the same thing as a courtroom 'trial' — related, but not
identical. Academic writing often narrows or sharpens a common word's meaning into a more precise
sense; using only the everyday sense in academic writing can create real confusion or imprecision."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When you meet a familiar word in an academic text, check whether it's
being used in its ordinary everyday sense or a more precise academic sense — 'argument' as a reasoned
case, not a fight; 'significant' as a specific measured effect, not just 'big' or 'important.' Use the
academic sense when writing academically."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "They had an argument about the results." (everyday sense: a disagreement) vs. "The essay's
   argument is supported by three pieces of evidence." (academic sense: a reasoned case).
2. A student who writes "the data showed a significant improvement" meaning only "a big one"
   (everyday sense, imprecise in an academic context) vs. a student who understands "significant" in
   a research context implies a specific, non-random measured effect (correct academic sense).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to apply only everyday senses after the bridge, give them five
common academic words (argument, significant, context, evidence, interpret) each in one everyday
sentence and one academic sentence, and have them explain how the meaning shifts between the two.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.vocab.register-and-formality`):** "Identify whether this sentence is formal or
informal, and explain how you know." A student who cannot distinguish formal from informal register
lacks the foundation to recognize when a word is being used in its academic sense — route to
`eng.vocab.register-and-formality` review first.

**PD-2 (checks `eng.vocab.word-formation-processes`):** "Identify the root and one affix in this
word, and explain what each contributes to the meaning." A student who cannot analyze word structure
lacks the foundation for recognizing academic word families — route to
`eng.vocab.word-formation-processes` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Screwdriver-vs-Soldering-Iron Workshop Anchor.** A screwdriver used across nearly every
workshop (electronics, carpentry, plumbing) vs. a soldering iron that belongs only in an electronics
workshop — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (general academic vocabulary recurs across every
subject; technical jargon belongs to one field only). Pair this with the courtroom-vs-classroom
"trial" contrast — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (a familiar word can carry a sharper, more
precise sense in academic use than in everyday use).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — General Academic Vocabulary vs. Technical Jargon (MC-A)

Using the screwdriver-vs-soldering-iron anchor, teach the cross-subject-recurrence test for
identifying general academic vocabulary. Student practice: given a passage, sort unfamiliar words
into "recurs across subjects" and "belongs to this subject only."

### TA-2 — The Academic Sense vs. the Everyday Sense (MC-B)

Using the trial anchor, teach that common words can carry a sharper academic sense. Student
practice: given five common words each in an everyday and an academic sentence, explain the meaning
shift.

### TA-3 — Recognizing Academic Vocabulary in Context

Extend TA-1/TA-2: give the student an unfamiliar academic passage and have them identify all general
academic vocabulary present, distinguishing it from any technical jargon and confirming each word's
academic sense.

### TA-4 — Producing Academic Vocabulary in Original Writing

Student writes two or three sentences of their own on a given topic, deliberately using at least
three general academic vocabulary words in their correct academic sense.

### TA-5 — Full Application: Reading and Writing with Academic Vocabulary

Give the student a new academic passage from an unfamiliar subject. They must identify its general
academic vocabulary (TA-1/TA-3), confirm each word's academic sense (TA-2), and then write a short
response using at least four of the identified words correctly (TA-4) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A short history passage, fully worked: general academic words (analyze,
context, evidence, significant) separated from history-specific technical terms (treaty, monarchy);
each academic word's academic sense confirmed against a matching everyday-sense contrast sentence.
Annotation confirms both the cross-subject-recurrence distinction (fixing MC-A) and the academic-
sense recognition (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new passage from a different subject. Student is prompted with guiding
questions ("Would this word appear in an essay about a totally different subject? Does this familiar
word mean something more precise here than it would in casual conversation?") to complete the
sorting and sense-confirmation.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new academic passage, unaided. Student identifies general academic
vocabulary, confirms academic senses, and writes a short original response using several of the
identified words correctly.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Sorting Academic Vocabulary from Technical Jargon**
Given a passage, sort unfamiliar words into "recurs across subjects" (academic vocabulary) and
"belongs to this subject only" (technical jargon).

**MP-2 [P36] — Identifying the Academic Sense of a Familiar Word**
Given a common word used in both an everyday and an academic sentence, explain how its meaning
shifts between the two.

**MP-3 [P36] — Recognizing Academic Vocabulary in an Unfamiliar Passage**
Given a new academic passage, identify all general academic vocabulary present.

**MP-4 [P36] — Confirming Correct Academic Sense in Use**
Given a sentence using an academic word, state whether it is used in its academic sense or its
everyday sense, and correct it if needed.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Reading-and-Writing Application**
Given a new academic passage, identify its academic vocabulary and write a short original response
using at least four of the words in their correct academic sense.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a gap in either routes back to the
relevant prerequisite. During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during
TA-1 (conflating jargon with academic vocabulary) and MC-B during TA-2 (applying only everyday
senses) — these are largely independent and often need separate, explicit correction. If either
fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route
by failure: MP-1/MP-3 failures indicate residual MC-A; MP-2/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B;
MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided
full passage-and-writing example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly sorts vocabulary on provided passages (MP-1) but reverts to listing
  technical jargon as "academic vocabulary" in a new subject (MP-3), provide a mandatory cross-
  subject-recurrence check for every candidate word until the habit is automatic.
- If the student's sorting is strong (MP-1, MP-3) but their own original writing (MP-5) misuses
  academic words in their everyday sense, require an explicit sense-check step ("is this the academic
  sense or the everyday sense?") before finalizing each sentence.
- If the student struggles to generate original sentences using academic vocabulary at all, provide a
  sentence-starter template using one academic word per starter as scaffolding before free writing.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.vocab.register-and-formality`'s formal/informal
  distinction should transfer readily to the everyday-vs-academic sense distinction, since both
  require recognizing that context shifts a word's appropriate meaning — draw this parallel
  explicitly if useful.
