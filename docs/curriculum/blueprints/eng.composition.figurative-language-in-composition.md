**Concept:** Figurative Language in Composition — using metaphor, imagery, and other non-literal
language purposefully in expository and persuasive writing, not just in creative fiction or poetry.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Rhetorical Devices (prerequisite) teaches structural
and stylistic patterns (repetition, parallelism, antithesis) used for persuasive effect. Figurative
Language in Composition teaches the complementary tool: non-literal comparisons and vivid imagery
that make abstract ideas more concrete, memorable, and emotionally resonant within otherwise
expository or persuasive writing. This concept unlocks `eng.composition.style-voice-and-tone` (a
writer's distinctive voice is built partly from their figurative-language choices, layered on top of
the more mechanical devices and appeals already covered).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) identify metaphor, simile, and imagery used
purposefully within an expository or persuasive passage (not just in poetry or fiction), (b) explain
the specific effect a given figurative comparison creates — what it makes the abstract idea feel
like, or what it emphasizes — and (c) deliberately compose original figurative language for their own
expository or persuasive writing that clarifies or strengthens an abstract point rather than
decorating it emptily.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-FIGURATIVE-LANGUAGE-ONLY-BELONGS-IN-FICTION-OR-POETRY

**Trigger signal:** Student believes metaphor, simile, and imagery are exclusively creative-writing
tools and have no place in expository or persuasive composition, producing flat, purely literal
writing even where a figurative comparison would clarify an abstract idea — or conversely, avoiding
figurative language in an essay because they believe it makes the writing seem "less serious" or
"less academic."

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two explanations of the same abstract concept
(inflation) — one purely literal and technical, one that uses a controlled metaphor ("Inflation is
like a slow leak in a tire: the car still runs, but every mile requires the engine to work slightly
harder to compensate for the air you're steadily losing.") in an otherwise expository passage from a
respected publication. Ask: "Does the metaphor make this feel like fiction, or does it make a
technical idea easier to understand while staying clearly expository?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Figurative language isn't a costume that only belongs at a fiction party —
it's a tool used across serious, respected expository and persuasive writing (economics columns,
scientific explainers, political speeches) specifically because abstract ideas are hard to grasp in
purely literal terms. A well-chosen metaphor doesn't make writing less serious — it makes a hard idea
easier to hold onto."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When explaining an abstract idea (a concept, a process, a relationship
between things), consider whether a metaphor or vivid image would make it more concrete and
memorable for your reader — this is a legitimate expository and persuasive tool, not a fictional
indulgence, as long as it clarifies rather than distracts."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A purely literal, technical explanation of a concept with no figurative language (valid, but
   potentially harder to grasp) vs. the same explanation with one purposeful metaphor added,
   remaining fully expository in tone and structure (figurative language used appropriately in a
   non-fiction context).
2. A student avoiding all figurative language in a science report because "metaphors are for stories"
   (the misconception) vs. a student using a controlled analogy to explain a scientific process,
   citing it as a legitimate explanatory tool used in published scientific writing (correct
   understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues avoiding figurative language in expository contexts after the
bridge, show them three published expository or persuasive texts (a science article, an editorial, a
speech) each containing at least one deliberate metaphor, and have them identify the specific
abstract idea each metaphor is clarifying.

### MC-B-ANY-FIGURATIVE-LANGUAGE-ADDED-IS-AUTOMATICALLY-GOOD-WRITING

**Trigger signal:** Student adds figurative language indiscriminately, believing that more metaphors
and vivid imagery automatically improve writing quality, without considering whether a given
comparison actually clarifies the abstract idea or is simply decorative, mixed, or confusing — e.g.,
piling up multiple unrelated metaphors for the same idea in one paragraph, or using a comparison that
doesn't actually map onto the concept being explained.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a paragraph explaining a concept with THREE different,
unrelated metaphors crammed in ("Inflation is a leaking tire, and also a runaway train, and also a
hungry beast") alongside a paragraph using ONE well-developed, consistent metaphor throughout. Ask:
"Does having three different comparisons for the same idea in one paragraph make it clearer, or does
switching comparisons partway through actually make it harder to follow?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Piling up multiple unrelated metaphors for one idea is like giving someone
three different sets of directions to the same house, each using a different landmark — instead of
helping them arrive, it leaves them lost switching between mental maps. One well-chosen, consistently
developed comparison usually clarifies far more than several competing ones crammed together."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before adding figurative language, ask: does this specific comparison
actually map clearly onto the idea I'm explaining, and am I sticking with ONE developed comparison
rather than switching between several? If a comparison doesn't fit cleanly or if you're mixing
metaphors, cut or revise rather than adding more."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A paragraph mixing three unrelated metaphors for the same concept (confusing, decorative
   overload) vs. a paragraph developing one metaphor consistently across several sentences
   (clarifying, purposeful).
2. A metaphor that doesn't actually map onto the concept's real structure (e.g., calling a gradual
   process "an explosion," which implies suddenness the concept doesn't have — a poor fit) vs. a
   metaphor whose structure genuinely matches the concept (a slow leak for a gradual, continuous
   process — a good fit).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to add indiscriminate or mixed figurative language after the
bridge, have them write out, in plain literal terms, exactly what their metaphor is supposed to map
onto (which part of the metaphor represents which part of the real concept) — if the mapping breaks
down or contradicts itself, the metaphor needs to be revised or cut.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.composition.rhetorical-devices`):** "Identify one rhetorical device in this
short passage." A student who cannot identify basic devices lacks the stylistic-analysis foundation
this concept extends — route to `eng.composition.rhetorical-devices` review first.

**PD-2 (checks basic metaphor/simile recognition, informal pre-check):** "Is this a literal or
figurative statement: 'Time is money'?" A student who cannot make this basic distinction needs a
brief primer before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Directions-to-One-House Anchor.** Present three different sets of directions to the same
house, each using a completely different landmark system (one uses street names, one uses
"turn left at the big oak tree," one uses "go past the blue mailbox") — mixed together in one
confusing set of instructions. Then present a single, consistent set of directions using ONE landmark
system throughout. Ask which actually gets someone to the house more reliably. This directly seeds
the fix for MC-B — one consistent, well-mapped comparison beats several competing ones. Extend the
anchor for MC-A by asking: "Does giving someone landmark-based directions (rather than pure GPS
coordinates) make the directions less serious or professional, or does it make them easier to
follow?" — showing that a well-chosen figurative aid is a legitimate clarifying tool, not a frivolous
one.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Recognizing Figurative Language in Expository/Persuasive Contexts (MC-A)

Using the inflation/leaking-tire example, teach students to recognize purposeful figurative language
in non-fiction writing. Student practice: given three expository/persuasive excerpts (one using
metaphor purposefully, two purely literal), identify the figurative one and explain what it
clarifies.

### TA-2 — Evaluating Whether a Comparison Actually Maps

Teach the mapping-test: does the structure of the comparison genuinely correspond to the structure of
the real concept? Student practice: given four candidate metaphors for one concept, evaluate which
ones have a genuine structural fit and which don't (e.g., "explosion" for a gradual process is a
poor fit).

### TA-3 — Developing One Consistent Comparison (MC-B)

Using the directions-to-one-house anchor, teach students to develop a single metaphor consistently
across several sentences rather than mixing multiple comparisons. Student practice: given a
paragraph with three mixed metaphors, revise it to develop just one consistently.

### TA-4 — Composing Original Figurative Language for an Abstract Idea

Give the student an abstract concept from another subject area (a science or social studies concept)
and have them compose an original, well-mapped, consistently-developed metaphor or extended image to
explain it within an expository paragraph.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Prompt: "Explain the concept of compound interest in an expository paragraph."
Fully worked: a single, well-mapped metaphor developed consistently ("Compound interest is like a
snowball rolling downhill: it starts small, but as it rolls, it picks up more snow, and that larger
snowball picks up even more snow with each turn — the growth accelerates because each addition builds
on everything already gathered.") Annotation confirms the metaphor is used within a clearly
expository (not fictional) context (fixing MC-A), is structurally well-mapped (growth building on
prior growth = compounding), and is developed consistently rather than mixed with other comparisons
(fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New concept ("Explain photosynthesis in an expository paragraph"). Student is
given a first-draft paragraph with two competing, undeveloped metaphors and prompted with guiding
questions ("Do these two comparisons actually fit together, or are they competing? Pick the one that
maps most accurately onto how photosynthesis actually works, and develop it consistently across the
whole paragraph.") to revise into one well-developed comparison.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new abstract concept, unaided. Student composes an original, well-mapped,
consistently-developed metaphor or extended image within a fully expository paragraph.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Recognizing Purposeful Figurative Language**
Given three expository/persuasive excerpts, identify which uses figurative language purposefully and
explain what abstract idea it clarifies.

**MP-2 [P36] — Evaluating Structural Fit**
Given four candidate metaphors for one concept, evaluate which have genuine structural fit and which
don't, explaining the mismatch for the poor fits.

**MP-3 [P36] — Detecting Mixed Metaphors**
Given a paragraph mixing multiple unrelated comparisons for the same idea, identify the mixing
problem and select which single comparison should be developed instead.

**MP-4 [P36] — Revising for Consistency**
Given a paragraph with a mixed-metaphor problem, revise it to develop one well-mapped comparison
consistently throughout.

**MP-5 [P36] — Original Composition**
Given a new abstract concept, compose an original expository paragraph using one well-mapped,
consistently-developed metaphor or extended image.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a devices gap routes back to that
prerequisite, a metaphor-recognition gap gets a brief primer. During Component 4 (S1, active
instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1 (initial hesitation to use figurative language at
all in expository contexts) and MC-B during TA-3 (once given permission, over-adding competing
comparisons) — these often occur in sequence as a student moves from under-using to over-using the
tool. If either fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe
stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure indicates residual MC-A; MP-2/MP-3 failures indicate
residual MC-B (mapping evaluation and mixing detection respectively); MP-4 failure alone (with MP-2/
MP-3 passing) indicates a revision-mechanics gap distinct from the recognition skill; MP-5 failure
with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates a production/transfer gap — provide one more guided
composition example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student can evaluate and revise others' figurative language (MP-1 through MP-4) but freezes
  or produces flat, purely literal writing when composing original figurative language (MP-5), treat
  this as a production-confidence gap — provide a partially-scaffolded composition task (given
  concept plus a starting comparison to develop) before fully independent composition.
- If the student's original metaphors are structurally clever but emotionally or tonally mismatched
  to a persuasive purpose (e.g., a whimsical metaphor in a serious policy argument), connect this to
  the Audience and Purpose concept — figurative language must fit not just the concept's structure
  but the piece's overall tone and audience.
- If the student over-corrects after learning MC-B and becomes reluctant to use ANY figurative
  language for fear of "mixing," clarify that the goal is one well-developed comparison, not zero
  comparisons — the lesson is consistency, not avoidance.
- Students who excel at generating original metaphors quickly but skip the structural mapping-check
  step (TA-2) should be required to explicitly write out the point-by-point mapping before finalizing
  any original figurative language, until the habit becomes automatic.
