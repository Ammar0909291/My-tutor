## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-VOWELS-HAVE-NO-ARTICULATION-PLACE
- **Trigger signal:** Student assumes vowels, unlike consonants, involve no specific physical positioning — treats vowel sounds as "just open mouth noises" with no locatable tongue position, unable to describe or feel any difference between vowel sounds beyond "they sound different."
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "Say 'ee' as in 'see', then 'ah' as in 'father'. Don't just listen — notice where your tongue is. Is it higher and further forward for one, and lower and further back for the other?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Vowels DO have a precise articulatory description, just like consonants — instead of place/manner/voicing, vowels are described by tongue HEIGHT (how high/low the tongue is), tongue BACKNESS (how front/back), and lip ROUNDING. There is no closure or blockage (that's what makes them vowels, not consonants), but there is still a specific, describable tongue position for each."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "For any vowel, check three things: how high is the tongue, how far forward/back is it, and are the lips rounded or spread."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "ee" /iː/ (high, front, unrounded) vs. "ah" /ɑː/ (low, back, unrounded) — a clear height+backness contrast.
  - "oo" /uː/ (high, back, rounded) vs. "ee" /iː/ (high, front, unrounded) — same height, different backness and rounding.
- **s6_path:** "It's easy to think vowels are just 'open sounds' with nothing specific happening, since there's no blockage to feel like with consonants — but your tongue really is moving to a distinct position for each one, and once you notice it, the pattern becomes clear."

### MC-MONOPHTHONGS-AND-DIPHTHONGS-ARE-THE-SAME
- **Trigger signal:** Student treats all vowel sounds as single, unchanging positions, not recognizing that some English vowels (diphthongs) involve the tongue GLIDING from one position to another within a single vowel sound (e.g., treats "boy" /bɔɪ/ or "time" /taɪm/ as if the vowel is one static sound rather than a glide).
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "Say 'time' very slowly, stretching out the vowel. Does your tongue/mouth position stay exactly the same the whole time, or does it move partway through, from one shape toward another?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Most vowels (monophthongs) hold one steady tongue position for their whole duration — like /iː/ in 'see'. But some vowels (diphthongs) are actually a GLIDE — the tongue starts at one position and moves toward another within the same syllable, like /aɪ/ in 'time' (starts low-central, glides toward high-front)."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Stretch out any vowel sound slowly and check whether the tongue position stays still (monophthong) or moves partway through (diphthong)."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "see" /siː/ (monophthong, steady /iː/) vs. "say" /seɪ/ (diphthong, glides from /e/ toward /ɪ/).
  - "boy" /bɔɪ/ (diphthong, glides from /ɔ/ toward /ɪ/) vs. "law" /lɔː/ (monophthong, steady /ɔː/).
- **s6_path:** "This distinction is subtle because both types just sound like 'one vowel sound' to an untrained ear — stretching the sound out slowly is the key trick that reveals whether it's actually gliding."

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 [P41] — Articulator awareness readiness**
Prompt: "Say 'ah' — can you feel roughly where your tongue is, high/low and front/back?"
- Pass: demonstrates basic tongue-position awareness (per `eng.phonetics.articulation-organs`).
- Fail → [P52]: brief articulation-organs refresher (tongue positioning awareness) before proceeding. Persistent failure → route to eng.phonetics.articulation-organs for reteaching.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The vowel space map**

> Introduce a simplified vowel-quadrilateral diagram (high-front, high-back, low-front, low-back corners). Student says a series of vowels moving corner to corner (/iː/ high-front → /ɑː/ low-back → /uː/ high-back → /æ/ low-front), feeling the tongue travel to each corner of the mouth-space. Mark each vowel's position on the diagram as it's produced. This anchors the height/backness system as a physical map of mouth-space, not an abstract chart.

---

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Tongue Height Classification [C]**

Present vowel pairs contrasting height only (e.g., /iː/ high vs. /æ/ low, both front); student identifies which is higher/lower by feel.

**TA-2 — Tongue Backness Classification [C]**

Present vowel pairs contrasting backness only (e.g., /iː/ front vs. /uː/ back, both high); student identifies which is more front/back by feel.

**TA-3 — Lip Rounding Classification [C→P]**

Present vowel pairs contrasting rounding (e.g., /iː/ unrounded vs. /uː/ rounded); student identifies rounded vs. spread lips by touch/mirror-check.

**TA-4 — Monophthong vs. Diphthong Discrimination [P]**

Using the slow-stretch technique from Component 1, student sorts a mixed set of vowel sounds (in words) into monophthongs (steady) vs. diphthongs (gliding), directly targeting MC-MONOPHTHONGS-AND-DIPHTHONGS-ARE-THE-SAME.

**TA-5 — Full Three-Dimension Vowel Classification [P]**

Student produces a complete classification (height + backness + rounding) for a monophthong, and a start-point/end-point description for a diphthong, without scaffolding.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — height)**

> "Which is higher, the tongue position for /iː/ (see) or /æ/ (cat)?"

Target response: /iː/ is higher; /æ/ is lower.

**WE-2 (Foundational — backness + rounding)**

> "Compare /iː/ (see) and /uː/ (too) — which is more back, and which has rounded lips?"

Target response: /uː/ is more back and has rounded lips; /iː/ is front and unrounded (spread lips).

**WE-3 (Intermediate — monophthong vs. diphthong)**

> "Stretch out the vowel in 'boat' slowly. Is it a monophthong or a diphthong?"

Target response: diphthong — /əʊ/ (or /oʊ/ depending on dialect), the tongue/lips glide from one position toward another during the sound.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Height, novel pair**
"Which is higher, /ʊ/ (as in 'book') or /ɒ/ (as in 'hot')?"
*Target:* /ʊ/ is higher; /ɒ/ is lower.

**MP-2 [P36] — Backness, novel pair**
"Which is further back, /æ/ (as in 'cat') or /ɑː/ (as in 'father')?"
*Target:* /ɑː/ is further back; /æ/ is more front.

**MP-3 [P36] — Rounding, novel vowel**
"Is /ɔː/ (as in 'law') rounded or unrounded?"
*Target:* rounded.

**MP-4 [P36] — Monophthong vs. diphthong, novel word**
"Stretch out the vowel in 'house'. Monophthong or diphthong?"
*Target:* diphthong — /aʊ/, gliding from a low-central/front position toward a high-back rounded position.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full classification, novel monophthong**
"Give the full classification (height, backness, rounding) for /ɛ/ (as in 'bed')."
*Target:* mid-height, front, unrounded.

**Protocol routing:** S0 (secure on articulation-organs basics, new to vowel-specific classification): move through TA-1/TA-2/TA-3 one dimension at a time, using clear corner-vowel contrasts before subtler mid-vowel distinctions. S1 (already aware vowels differ, lacks systematic vocabulary): compress TA-1-3, focus on TA-4/TA-5's integration and diphthong work. S6 (frustrated that vowel differences feel subtle/subjective compared to consonants): validate explicitly that vowel distinctions ARE more continuous/gradient than consonants' more categorical contrasts, and that the vowel-space map is a genuine simplification tool, not oversimplification. S9 (L1 vowel inventory differs substantially, e.g. 5 pure vowels with no diphthongs, or a very large vowel inventory with additional height/backness/rounding distinctions not in English): use TA-5 to have the student classify a genuine L1 vowel using the same three-dimension framework — reinforcing the system's generality and treating the L1 vowel system as a valid comparison point, never as a deficit.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Vowels are genuinely describable, just differently from consonants**: this concept must establish upfront that "no closure" does not mean "no describable position" — directly countering MC-VOWELS-HAVE-NO-ARTICULATION-PLACE as the likely default assumption.
- **The slow-stretch technique is the core diagnostic tool**: teach and reuse this technique (Component 1's diphthong conflict evidence) as the standing method for monophthong/diphthong discrimination throughout TA-4/TA-5 and beyond.
- **Corner vowels before subtle contrasts**: sequence teaching from the four extreme "corner" vowels (high-front /iː/, low-front /æ/, high-back /uː/, low-back /ɑː/) — which are easiest to feel distinctly — before introducing closer mid-vowel contrasts (e.g., /ɛ/ vs. /æ/) that require finer discrimination.
- **L1 vowel-system comparison as an asset**: for S9 learners, deliberately incorporate a genuine L1 vowel into classification practice, paralleling the consonant-sounds concept's approach — this validates existing phonological knowledge and tests the framework's generality.
