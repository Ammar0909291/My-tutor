**Concept:** Presentation Design — designing visual and structural support materials (slides,
handouts, diagrams) that clarify and reinforce a spoken presentation, rather than duplicating or
competing with it.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Presentation Skills (prerequisite) taught delivering a
spoken presentation effectively. Technical Writing (prerequisite) taught producing clear written
support material. Presentation Design extends both by teaching the specific discipline of designing
visual materials meant to be seen WHILE someone is speaking — a fundamentally different design
problem than a standalone document, because slides that try to say everything compete with the
speaker instead of supporting them. This concept has no further downstream unlocks in the current
curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) design slides that support spoken content rather
than duplicating it word-for-word, (b) apply a one-main-idea-per-slide principle so each visual
reinforces a single point instead of overwhelming the audience with simultaneous competing
information, and (c) choose visual elements (a diagram, a single statistic, an image) that clarify
the spoken point rather than decorate the slide without adding meaning.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-A-GOOD-SLIDE-CONTAINS-THE-FULL-TEXT-OF-WHAT-THE-SPEAKER-WILL-SAY

**Trigger signal:** Student designs slides packed with full sentences or paragraphs duplicating
everything the speaker plans to say, rather than a small number of key words, phrases, or visuals
that support the spoken content — creating a slide that competes with the speaker for the audience's
attention rather than reinforcing what is being said aloud.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two slides on the same topic: one dense with full
sentences duplicating the entire spoken script, and one with a single short phrase and one supporting
image. Ask: "If you are reading the dense slide, are you also listening to the speaker at the same
time? Which slide lets the audience do both — read and listen — without choosing one over the
other?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Putting your full script on a slide is like handing someone a book to read
at the exact moment you're trying to tell them a story out loud — they have to choose between reading
your slide and listening to you, and usually the slide wins, so your spoken words go unheard. A
strong slide supports the spoken word instead of duplicating it, so the audience can do both at
once."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Never put your full spoken script on a slide. Reduce each slide to the
few key words, phrases, or visuals that support what you'll say aloud — the slide's job is to
reinforce your speech, not replace the audience's need to listen to it."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A slide containing three full sentences duplicating the entire spoken explanation (competes with
   the speaker, forces a reading-vs-listening choice) vs. a slide with one short phrase and one
   supporting diagram (reinforces the spoken explanation without competing for attention).
2. A student whose slides could be read silently and fully understood with the speaker absent
   (misconception — the slide has become the presentation) vs. a student whose slides only make full
   sense alongside the spoken explanation (correct — the slide supports, the speech carries the
   content).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to write full sentences on slides after the bridge, have them
take one of their own text-heavy slides and reduce it to no more than five words or one short phrase
plus a visual, then explain aloud, unaided by the slide, everything the original sentences said.

### MC-B-EVERY-SLIDE-NEEDS-DECORATIVE-VISUAL-ELEMENTS-TO-LOOK-PROFESSIONAL

**Trigger signal:** Student adds visual elements (clip art, unrelated stock images, decorative
borders, animations) chosen for visual appeal or a sense of "professionalism" rather than because
they clarify or reinforce the specific spoken point on that slide, cluttering slides with visuals
that add no meaning and can distract from the actual content.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two slides making the same single point: one with a
decorative, thematically unrelated stock image chosen only "to look professional," and one with a
simple diagram that directly illustrates the relationship being described aloud. Ask: "Does the
stock image help the audience understand the specific point being made? Does the diagram?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Adding a decorative image because a slide 'needs something' is like hanging
an unrelated painting on a whiteboard you're using to explain a math problem — it might look nicer,
but it does nothing to help anyone understand the problem, and it can even distract from the diagram
that actually would help. Every visual element on a slide should be chosen because it clarifies the
specific point being made, not merely to fill space or look polished."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before adding any visual to a slide, ask: does this specific image,
chart, or diagram help the audience understand the point I'm making right now, or is it just there to
fill space or look nice? Only keep visuals that clarify — cut anything purely decorative."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. An unrelated stock photo added "to look professional" that has no connection to the point being
   made (decorative, adds no meaning) vs. a simple diagram directly illustrating the relationship
   described aloud (clarifying, directly supports understanding).
2. A student who selects visuals based on appearance or a sense of obligation to fill the slide
   (misconception) vs. a student who selects visuals based on whether they clarify the specific
   spoken point (correct discipline).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to add purely decorative visuals after the bridge, have them
review a slide deck (their own or a provided one) and, for each visual element, state specifically
what point it clarifies — removing any visual for which they cannot state a clear answer.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.speaking.presentation-skills`):** "Describe two techniques you would use to
deliver this passage aloud effectively." A student who cannot describe basic spoken delivery
technique lacks the foundation this concept designs visual support around — route to
`eng.speaking.presentation-skills` review first.

**PD-2 (checks `eng.communication.technical-writing`):** "Summarize this technical passage in one
clear, precise sentence." A student who cannot produce clear, precise written summary lacks the
foundation for distilling a spoken point into minimal slide text — route to
`eng.communication.technical-writing` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Book-While-Storytelling and Unrelated-Painting Anchor.** Combine two images: someone handing a
listener a book to read at the exact moment they're telling a story aloud, forcing a choice between
reading and listening — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (slides must support speech, not duplicate
and compete with it). Second, an unrelated decorative painting hung on a whiteboard being used to
explain a math problem — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (visuals must be chosen to clarify the
specific point, not merely to look professional or fill space).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Slides Support Speech, They Don't Duplicate It (MC-A)

Using the book-while-storytelling anchor, teach that a slide's job is to reinforce, not replace,
spoken content. Student practice: given a text-heavy slide duplicating a full spoken script, reduce
it to a short phrase plus a supporting visual.

### TA-2 — One Main Idea Per Slide

Extend TA-1: teach that each slide should carry exactly one main idea, since multiple competing ideas
on one slide re-creates the same divided-attention problem as full-sentence text. Student practice:
given a slide crowded with three unrelated points, split it into three single-idea slides.

### TA-3 — Choosing Visuals That Clarify, Not Decorate (MC-B)

Using the unrelated-painting anchor, teach evaluating a candidate visual by whether it clarifies the
specific spoken point. Student practice: given a slide with a decorative, unrelated image, replace it
with a visual (or no visual) that actually clarifies the point.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Designing a Short Slide Set

Give the student a short spoken presentation script (3-4 points). They must design one slide per
main idea (TA-2), reducing text to key phrases that support rather than duplicate the speech (TA-1),
and select or reject visual elements based on whether each one clarifies its point (TA-3) — the full
synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A spoken script about a company's quarterly results, fully worked: the
text-heavy draft slide (full sentences duplicating the script) reduced to three single-idea slides,
each with a short phrase and one clarifying visual (a simple bar chart for the sales figure, not a
decorative stock photo); one candidate decorative image explicitly rejected with a stated reason.
Annotation confirms both the support-not-duplicate principle (fixing MC-A) and the clarify-not-
decorate visual selection (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new spoken script on a different topic. Student is prompted with guiding
questions ("If someone read only this slide with no speaker present, would they get everything, or
does it need the speech to make full sense? Does this visual element help explain the specific point
on this slide, or is it just there to fill space?") to complete the slide design.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new spoken script, unaided. Student designs a one-idea-per-slide set with
minimal supporting text and only clarifying visuals.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Reducing a Text-Heavy Slide to Supporting Text**
Given a slide duplicating a full spoken script, reduce it to a short phrase or key words that
support rather than replace the speech.

**MP-2 [P36] — Splitting a Multi-Idea Slide**
Given a slide crowded with three unrelated points, split it into three single-idea slides.

**MP-3 [P36] — Identifying a Decorative vs. Clarifying Visual**
Given two candidate visuals for the same slide, identify which is purely decorative and which
clarifies the specific point.

**MP-4 [P36] — Justifying or Rejecting a Visual Element**
Given a slide with one visual element, state specifically what point it clarifies, or explain why it
should be removed.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Slide Set Design**
Given a new short spoken script (3-4 points), design a one-idea-per-slide set with minimal
supporting text and only clarifying visuals.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a gap in either routes back to the
relevant prerequisite. During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during
TA-1/TA-2 (text-heavy, multi-idea slides) and MC-B during TA-3 (decorative visual choices) — these
are largely independent and often need separate, explicit correction. If either fires, branch to the
relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1/MP-2
failures indicate residual MC-A; MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1
through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-slide-set example
before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly reduces text on provided slides (MP-1) but reverts to dense, full-sentence
  slides when designing their own original set (MP-5), provide a mandatory word-count cap per slide
  as scaffolding until the habit is automatic.
- If the student's text reduction is strong (MP-1, MP-2) but their visual choices (MP-3, MP-4) remain
  decorative rather than clarifying, require an explicit written justification ("this visual clarifies
  ___") for every visual element before it's allowed onto a slide.
- If the student struggles to split multi-idea slides at all, provide a starter template (one slide
  per bullet point in their spoken outline) as scaffolding before free slide design.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.communication.technical-writing`'s concision work should
  transfer readily to slide text reduction, since both require distilling content to its essential
  meaning — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
