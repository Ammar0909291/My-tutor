**Concept:** Oral Storytelling — recounting a narrative aloud with clear sequencing, expression, and
audience engagement, distinct from reciting every detail flatly in order.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Conversation Skills (prerequisite) taught managing
spoken exchange. Oral Storytelling extends this into sustained spoken narrative: selecting and
emphasizing the events that matter for the listener, and using vocal expression as an integral part
of how the story lands, not an optional decoration on top of accurate content. This concept has no
further downstream unlocks in the current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) select and emphasize the key events of a story for
audience engagement rather than reciting every detail in flat, undifferentiated sequence, and (b) use
vocal expression (tone, pacing, emphasis) as an integral part of storytelling rather than treating it
as optional or separate from the story's actual content.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-A-GOOD-ORAL-STORY-INCLUDES-EVERY-DETAIL-IN-EXACT-ORDER

**Trigger signal:** Student recounts a narrative by including every detail, in strict chronological
order, with equal weight given to minor and major events alike, rather than selecting and emphasizing
the events that actually matter for audience engagement — producing a flat, exhaustive info-dump
rather than a shaped story.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present two retellings of the same simple event (a student getting lost
on the way to school): one listing every single detail in order (what time they woke up, what they
ate, every street they walked down, minor observations) with no emphasis, and one selecting only the
key turning points (realizing they were lost, the moment of panic, how they found their way) with
clear emphasis on those moments. Ask: "Which retelling would hold a listener's attention? Does
including every single detail make a story better?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Including every detail in exact order is like a tour guide reading every line
of a building's blueprint aloud instead of pointing out what's actually interesting to see — technically
complete, but nobody stays engaged. A good oral story selects and emphasizes the events that matter,
leaving out or compressing the parts that don't add to the story's impact."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't try to include every detail in exact order. Select the events that
actually matter for your story's impact — a turning point, a moment of tension, a surprise — and
emphasize those, compressing or cutting minor details that don't add anything."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A retelling listing every minor detail in exact chronological order with no emphasis (an
   info-dump, hard to follow or care about) vs. a retelling selecting and emphasizing only the key
   turning points (engaging, shaped narrative).
2. A student who treats every event as equally important in their retelling (misconception) vs. a
   student who identifies which 2-3 events matter most and gives them more time and emphasis
   (correct selective structuring).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to recite every detail flatly after the bridge, have them take
their own exhaustive retelling and cut it down to only the three most important moments, explaining
why each one matters to the story.

### MC-B-VOCAL-EXPRESSION-IS-AN-OPTIONAL-EXTRA-SEPARATE-FROM-THE-REAL-CONTENT-OF-THE-STORY

**Trigger signal:** Student treats tone, pacing, and vocal emphasis as a decorative extra separate
from the "real" content of a story (the words and events), delivering even exciting or tense events in
a flat, unvarying monotone, failing to recognize that vocal expression is integral to how a story is
actually received by a listener.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present a description of an exciting chase scene told in a flat,
unvarying monotone, next to the same content told with vocal pacing changes (a quickened pace during
the chase, a pause before the resolution, emphasis on the key moment). Ask: "Do both versions contain
the same events? Does the flat version actually feel exciting to the listener, even though the
content is identical?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Treating vocal expression as separate from the real content is like assuming
a song's melody doesn't matter as long as the lyrics are correct — but a flat, monotone delivery of
exciting lyrics simply doesn't land as a song, and a flat, monotone retelling of an exciting event
doesn't land as an exciting story. How you say something is part of what you're actually
communicating, not an optional add-on."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't treat your voice as separate from your story's content. Match
your pacing, tone, and emphasis to what's happening — speed up for tension, pause before a big
moment, raise or lower your voice for emphasis — because how you say the story is part of what
actually makes it land."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. An exciting chase scene told in a flat, unvarying monotone (identical content, but the delivery
   fails to convey the excitement) vs. the same content told with quickened pacing during the chase
   and a pause before the resolution (delivery reinforces and conveys the excitement).
2. A student who assumes accurate word choice alone will make their story land (misconception) vs. a
   student who deliberately matches vocal pacing and emphasis to the story's emotional shape (correct
   integration of expression and content).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to deliver stories in flat monotone after the bridge, have them
retell the same short story twice — once flatly, once with deliberate pacing and emphasis changes —
and describe, from a listener's perspective, how differently the two versions actually land.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.speaking.conversation-skills`):** "Respond appropriately to this conversational
prompt, maintaining the exchange for at least two turns." A student who cannot manage basic spoken
exchange lacks the foundation for sustained oral narrative — route to `eng.speaking.conversation-skills`
review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Blueprint-Reading-Tour-Guide and Flat-Song-Lyrics Anchor.** A tour guide reading every line of
a blueprint aloud instead of pointing out what's actually interesting — directly seeding the fix for
MC-A (a good story selects and emphasizes what matters, not every detail in order). Second, a song
whose melody doesn't matter as long as the lyrics are correct, delivered in a flat monotone — directly
seeding the fix for MC-B (vocal expression is integral to how a story is received, not a separate
decoration).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Selecting Key Events for a Story (MC-A)

Using the blueprint-tour-guide anchor, teach identifying which events matter most for a story's
impact. Student practice: given an exhaustive retelling, cut it down to the 2-3 most important
moments.

### TA-2 — Sequencing Selected Events for Engagement

Extend TA-1: teach ordering and pacing the selected key events (buildup, turning point, resolution)
for maximum engagement. Student practice: given three selected key events out of order, sequence them
for the strongest narrative effect.

### TA-3 — Matching Vocal Expression to Story Content (MC-B)

Using the flat-song-lyrics anchor, teach matching pacing, tone, and emphasis to the story's emotional
shape. Student practice: given a short story passage, mark where pacing should quicken, where a pause
belongs, and where emphasis should land.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Telling a Complete Oral Story

Give the student a new event or prompt to recount. They must select and sequence key events (TA-1/
TA-2) and deliver the retelling with matched vocal expression (TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** An account of a student's first day at a new school, fully worked: an
exhaustive draft trimmed to three key moments (the nervous arrival, an unexpected friendly gesture,
the relief by day's end); the three moments sequenced for buildup and resolution; delivery notes
added (slower, quieter pacing for the nervous arrival, a brighter, quicker tone for the friendly
gesture, a warm, settled tone for the resolution). Annotation confirms both the selective-emphasis
principle (fixing MC-A) and the vocal-expression-as-integral principle (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new event to recount. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("Which 2-3
moments actually matter here? How should your pacing and tone change across these moments to match
what's happening?") to complete the retelling.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new event, unaided. Student selects key moments, sequences them, and
delivers the story with matched vocal expression.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Selecting Key Events from an Exhaustive Account**
Given an exhaustive retelling, cut it down to the 2-3 most important moments.

**MP-2 [P36] — Sequencing Selected Events for Engagement**
Given three selected key events out of order, sequence them for the strongest narrative effect.

**MP-3 [P36] — Marking Vocal Expression Cues**
Given a short story passage, mark where pacing should quicken, where a pause belongs, and where
emphasis should land.

**MP-4 [P36] — Explaining Why Flat Delivery Undermines a Story**
Given a description of an exciting event told in monotone, explain specifically why the flat
delivery fails to convey the excitement despite accurate content.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Oral Storytelling Application**
Given a new event or prompt, select and sequence key events and deliver the story with matched
vocal expression.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite.
During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1/TA-2 (exhaustive,
undifferentiated retelling) and MC-B during TA-3 (flat delivery) — these are largely independent and
often need separate, explicit correction. If either fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill
before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1/MP-2 failures indicate
residual MC-A; MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing
indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-story example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly trims events on provided exhaustive drafts (MP-1) but reverts to
  exhaustive retelling with a new original prompt (MP-5), provide a mandatory "pick your 3 moments
  first, before telling anything" planning step.
- If the student's event selection is strong (MP-1, MP-2) but their delivery (MP-3) stays flat
  regardless of content, require an explicit pacing/pause/emphasis annotation on their story draft
  before speaking it aloud.
- If the student struggles to identify which events matter at all, provide a guiding question set
  ("what surprised you? what changed? how did it end?") as a scaffolding aid for event selection.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.speaking.conversation-skills`'s turn-taking and responding
  should transfer readily to audience-engagement awareness, since both require attending to how a
  listener is receiving what's being said — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
