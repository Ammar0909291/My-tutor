## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-FORMAL-ALWAYS-MEANS-BETTER-OR-MORE-CORRECT
- **Trigger signal:** Student rewrites every sentence into the most formal/elevated phrasing possible regardless of audience or context — e.g., insisting a text message to a friend should read "I would be delighted to accompany you" instead of "sure, I'm in" — treating formality as a quality scale (more formal = more correct English) rather than a fit-to-context scale.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two versions of the same message sent to a best friend: one casual ("hey, wanna grab pizza later?") and one maximally formal ("I am writing to inquire whether you would be amenable to sharing a meal consisting of pizza at a mutually convenient time"). Ask which one the friend would find normal, and which one would make the friend worried something was wrong or being made fun of.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Formal English isn't 'better' English — it's a different *tool* for a different *job*. Using a hyper-formal register with a close friend doesn't sound more correct, it sounds strange or even sarcastic, the same way showing up to a beach party in a tuxedo isn't 'more correct' than swim trunks — it's the wrong outfit for the occasion."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Ask first: who is this for, and what's the relationship? Register should match the audience and situation, not maximize formality. The 'best' register is the one that fits — sometimes that's casual, sometimes formal, and neither is inherently superior English."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Formality as inherent correctness (a single "proper English" scale where more formal always scores higher) vs. (b) Formality as context-fit (a register is correct when it matches audience/purpose/relationship, and a mismatched register — in either direction — is the actual error).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1 re-check, then re-run TA-1 (audience-sorting task) with exaggerated mismatched examples before returning to probes.

### MC-INFORMAL-REGISTER-MEANS-BAD-GRAMMAR-OR-SLOPPY-WRITING
- **Trigger signal:** Student treats casual/informal register choices (contractions, sentence fragments used for effect, colloquial vocabulary, starting sentences with "and"/"but") as grammar errors to be corrected, rather than as deliberate, appropriate register choices for informal contexts.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present a well-written informal text message ("omw, running like 5 min late, save me a seat!") and ask the student to "fix" what's wrong with it. Then ask: would fixing it into "I am on my way; I will be arriving approximately five minutes late. Please reserve a seat for me" actually improve the message for its real purpose (a quick heads-up to a friend)?
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Informal writing follows its own real rules — it isn't 'grammar gone wrong,' it's a different, equally legitimate set of conventions suited to speed, closeness, and everyday contexts. A text message that reads like a legal letter has failed at its job just as much as a job application that reads like a text message."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Before calling something an error, check which register it's written in and whether it fits that register's own conventions. Contractions, fragments, and casual word choices are correct informal-register choices, not mistakes — the real error is only when the register doesn't match the situation."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Informal = incorrect/sloppy (any deviation from formal conventions is a grammar mistake needing correction) vs. (b) Informal = a distinct, rule-governed register (has its own legitimate conventions suited to casual/close/fast-paced contexts, judged by fit rather than by formal-register rules).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1, then re-present the Concrete Anchor's outfit-matching framing before continuing to TA-2.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

### PD-1: Connotation-Denotation Readiness
- **Tests:** Can the student distinguish a word's neutral dictionary meaning from its emotional/associative shading — the foundation register judgments are built on, since register is largely a pattern of connotation-consistent word choices?
- **Probe:** Give three near-synonym triples (e.g., "cheap / affordable / inexpensive"; "skinny / slim / thin"; "kid / child / minor") and ask the student to identify which word in each triple carries a negative shade, which is neutral, and which is more formal/clinical.
- **Pass condition:** Correctly sorts at least 2 of 3 triples by connotation and formality shading.
- **Fail routing:** If connotation sorting fails, route to `eng.vocab.connotation-denotation` review — register judgments require reliably detecting word-choice shading first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**"The Outfit Closet."** Present the student with a physical or drawn "closet" containing five outfit cards (pajamas, gym clothes, jeans-and-t-shirt, business-casual, tuxedo/formal gown) and five event cards (sleeping, a run, hanging out with friends, a job interview, a wedding). Ask the student to match each outfit to its event. Highlight two failure cases explicitly: wearing a tuxedo to hang out with friends (over-formal for the context — seeds MC-FORMAL-ALWAYS-MEANS-BETTER-OR-MORE-CORRECT) and wearing pajamas to a job interview (under-formal for the context — the mirror-image error). Then swap the metaphor: "Language has outfits too — we call them registers. A text to a friend, a school essay, and a wedding speech each need a different 'outfit' of words. No outfit is the best one overall — only the one that fits the event." Explicitly connect: mismatched register in either direction (too formal OR too casual) is the real error, not informality itself.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1: Audience and Purpose Sorting
Present 6 short language samples (a text to a friend, a cover letter opening, a diary entry, a scientific abstract sentence, a birthday card message, a courtroom statement) without labels. Student sorts each into "very informal / neutral / very formal" and names the likely audience and purpose for each, reinforcing that register is read off audience+purpose, not off some inherent word quality.

### TA-2: Register Markers — What Signals Formality
Teach the concrete linguistic markers of register: contractions vs. full forms ("can't" vs. "cannot"), first/second person vs. passive/impersonal constructions, slang/idiom vs. precise/technical vocabulary, sentence fragments vs. complete complex sentences, direct address vs. formal titles. Student highlights these markers in the TA-1 samples.

### TA-3: Register Mismatch Detection (Both Directions)
Present paired scenarios where register is deliberately mismatched in BOTH directions: an overly formal text to a sibling, and an overly casual email to a school principal requesting a meeting. Student identifies what's wrong in each and explains that the direction of mismatch differs but both are genuine errors — directly targeting MC-FORMAL-ALWAYS-MEANS-BETTER-OR-MORE-CORRECT (over-formal isn't automatically "better") and MC-INFORMAL-REGISTER-MEANS-BAD-GRAMMAR-OR-SLOPPY-WRITING (under-formal isn't automatically "worse," only mismatched).

### TA-4: Register Conversion Practice
Give the student one message in one register and ask them to rewrite it into two other registers (e.g., take a casual text "can u send the notes? thx" and convert it to a formal email to a professor, and to a mid-register message to a classmate group chat), preserving the same core content/request across all three versions.

### TA-5: Choosing Register for a Novel Situation
Present a new, unscripted scenario (e.g., "you need to ask your new part-time boss for next Tuesday off") and have the student independently decide the appropriate register, draft the message, and justify their register choice by naming the audience/relationship/purpose factors that drove it — the generative capstone linking directly to `eng.communication.digital-communication`'s real-world register decisions.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

### WE-1: Sorting by Register (Scaffolded)
**Samples:** "yo, u coming tonight??" / "Dear Hiring Manager, I am writing to apply for the position of..." / "Once upon a time, in a village by the sea..."
**Walkthrough:** Sample 1 has slang ("yo"), no capitalization/punctuation norms, abbreviation ("u") — informal, peer audience. Sample 2 has a formal salutation, full sentences, professional vocabulary — formal, employer audience. Sample 3 is narrative/neutral register, aimed at a general reader — neither casual slang nor institutional formality.

### WE-2: Detecting Mismatch (Guided)
**Scenario:** A student texts their teacher: "Yo teach, wuts the hw for 2nite lol." Ask what's mismatched (slang/abbreviations to an authority-relationship audience in a request context) and have the student rewrite it appropriately ("Hi Ms. Rivera, could you let me know tonight's homework? Thank you!"), preserving the same request.

### WE-3: Full Register Conversion (Independent)
**Base message:** "Can't make it Friday, something came up, sorry!" Student converts this into: (a) a formal email to a professor explaining an absence, (b) a mid-register message to a study group chat, (c) confirms the original casual version is appropriate for a close friend — demonstrating that all three registers are legitimate for their respective contexts, none inherently superior.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Over-formal mismatch**
Given a hyper-formal message sent in a casual peer context, student identifies the register mismatch and explains why "more formal" was not "more correct" here. *(Directly targets MC-FORMAL-ALWAYS-MEANS-BETTER-OR-MORE-CORRECT.)*

**MP-2 [P36] — Informal is not an error**
Given a casual, contraction-heavy, fragment-using text message, student confirms it is NOT a grammar error and explains why it correctly fits its informal context. *(Directly targets MC-INFORMAL-REGISTER-MEANS-BAD-GRAMMAR-OR-SLOPPY-WRITING.)*

**MP-3 [P36] — Register spectrum ranking**
Given 4 unlabeled short samples spanning the register spectrum, student ranks them from most informal to most formal and names the audience/purpose evidence used for each ranking.

**MP-4 [P36] — Register-shift rewrite**
Given one message and a target audience change (e.g., "rewrite this restaurant complaint text for a formal written complaint to management"), student produces an appropriately-registered rewrite preserving the original content.

**MP-5 [P36] — Novel-situation register choice**
Given a novel real-world scenario with no register specified, student chooses and justifies an appropriate register from scratch, explicitly naming the audience/relationship/purpose factors driving the choice.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** At session open (S0), confirm PD-1 passes — a student who cannot detect connotation/formality shading in near-synonyms will not reliably judge register either, since register is largely built from connotation-consistent word patterns. During instruction (S1), watch closely for the direction of any register-judgment error: correcting only "sloppy" informal writing (MC-INFORMAL-REGISTER-MEANS-BAD-GRAMMAR-OR-SLOPPY-WRITING) while praising over-formal writing (MC-FORMAL-ALWAYS-MEANS-BETTER-OR-MORE-CORRECT) as automatically better are mirror-image failures of the same underlying misconception (formality-as-correctness-scale) and both must be addressed via TA-3's paired both-directions examples, not just one direction. If the student defaults to rating everything by a single "more formal = better" axis during probes (S6), drop to the Concrete Anchor's outfit-matching framing and re-run TA-3 before continuing. At mastery-probe stage (S9), do not certify mastery unless both MP-1 (over-formal mismatch) and MP-2 (informal-is-not-an-error) are passed — passing only one of the two paired misconception probes indicates the student has only learned half of the symmetric lesson.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student shows a one-directional bias (always over-correcting toward formality), weight remediation toward MC-INFORMAL-REGISTER-MEANS-BAD-GRAMMAR-OR-SLOPPY-WRITING's bridge text and TA-3's informal-is-legitimate examples specifically.
- If the student shows the opposite bias (treating all formal writing as stuffy/wrong), weight remediation toward MC-FORMAL-ALWAYS-MEANS-BETTER-OR-MORE-CORRECT's bridge text and TA-3's over-casual-to-authority examples.
- This concept directly feeds `eng.vocab.academic-vocabulary` (which assumes the student already accepts formal register as situationally appropriate, not "try-hard") and `eng.communication.digital-communication` (which requires real-time register judgment across platforms) — do not certify mastery without TA-5's novel-situation generative task, since both downstream concepts require independent register choice, not just recognition.
- Reuse the "outfit fits the event" framing explicitly when introducing `eng.communication.digital-communication` — that concept's platform-switching register decisions (text vs. email vs. formal document) are the same skill applied across communication channels rather than just written genres.
