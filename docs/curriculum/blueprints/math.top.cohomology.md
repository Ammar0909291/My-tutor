## Component 1 — Learning Objectives

- LO1: Define **cohomology groups** $H^n(X)=\mathrm{Hom}(H_n(X),\mathbb{Z})$ (via the universal coefficient theorem, in the simplest case) as the DUAL construction to `math.top.homology`'s own $H_n(X)$ — homomorphisms FROM the homology groups TO $\mathbb{Z}$, rather than the homology groups themselves.
- LO2: Recognize the **cup product** as a genuinely NEW structure cohomology has that homology (as bare groups) lacks: $H^*(X)=\bigoplus_nH^n(X)$ assembles into a RING, with multiplication given by the cup product $\smile:H^p(X)\times H^q(X)\to H^{p+q}(X)$ — extra algebraic structure not visible in homology alone.
- LO3 (orientation level — full de Rham theory deferred): recognize, without full derivation, that **de Rham cohomology** (built from closed/exact differential forms on a smooth manifold) is a DIFFERENT-LOOKING construction that de Rham's theorem shows is ISOMORPHIC to singular cohomology with real coefficients — the same invariant, computed by a genuinely different (analytic, not combinatorial) route.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Check

Assumes mastery of `math.top.homology` (the homology groups $H_n(X)$, their functoriality and homotopy invariance, and computability via chain complexes).

## Component 3 — Core Explanation

**Cohomology is homology's dual — homomorphisms OUT of $H_n(X)$, not a redefinition of it**: `math.top.homology` already established $H_n(X)$ as abelian groups capturing $n$-dimensional holes. The universal coefficient theorem (in its simplest, torsion-free case) identifies $H^n(X)=\mathrm{Hom}(H_n(X),\mathbb{Z})$ — the group of homomorphisms FROM $H_n(X)$ TO the integers $\mathbb{Z}$. This is a genuinely DIFFERENT (though closely related) object from $H_n(X)$ itself: dualizing a group doesn't just relabel it, it produces the group of ways to "measure" it linearly against $\mathbb{Z}$.

**The cup product gives cohomology a ring structure homology lacks**: individually, each $H^n(X)$ is just an abelian group, same as each $H_n(X)$. But cohomology classes from DIFFERENT degrees can be MULTIPLIED via the cup product $\smile:H^p(X)\times H^q(X)\times\to H^{p+q}(X)$, making the direct sum $H^*(X)=\bigoplus_nH^n(X)$ into a genuine RING (with addition and multiplication, satisfying ring axioms) — additional algebraic structure that has NO natural analogue on the homology side, where there is no comparable way to multiply classes from different degrees.

**De Rham cohomology reaches the same invariant by an entirely different route (orientation level)**: on a smooth manifold, de Rham cohomology is built from DIFFERENTIAL FORMS — closed forms (satisfying $d\omega=0$) modulo exact forms (those of the form $d\eta$) — an entirely ANALYTIC construction, using calculus on manifolds, with no obvious connection to `math.top.homology`'s combinatorial chain complexes. De Rham's theorem asserts these give an ISOMORPHIC answer to singular cohomology with real coefficients: $H^n_{\mathrm{dR}}(X)\cong H^n(X;\mathbb{R})$ — two utterly different-looking constructions computing the identical invariant. Full proof of this isomorphism is deferred beyond this concept's core scope.

## Component 4 — Worked Examples

**Example 1 (LO1 — cohomology as the dual of homology, breaking MC-1)**: for $X=S^1$ (the circle), `math.top.homology` gives $H_0(S^1)=\mathbb{Z}$ (one connected component) and $H_1(S^1)=\mathbb{Z}$ (one loop not bounding a disc). Since both are torsion-free (isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}$, not $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ for any $n$), the universal coefficient theorem gives $H^0(S^1)=\mathrm{Hom}(\mathbb{Z},\mathbb{Z})=\mathbb{Z}$ and $H^1(S^1)=\mathrm{Hom}(\mathbb{Z},\mathbb{Z})=\mathbb{Z}$ — numerically the SAME groups as homology in this torsion-free case, but conceptually DIFFERENT objects: $H^1(S^1)$ consists of homomorphisms from $H_1(S^1)$ to $\mathbb{Z}$ (ways of "measuring" the loop), not the loop-classes themselves.

**Example 2 (LO2 — the cup product giving a ring structure, breaking MC-2)**: for $X=T^2$ (the torus), $H^1(T^2)\cong\mathbb{Z}^2$, generated by two classes $\alpha,\beta$ dual to the torus's two independent loops. The cup product $\alpha\smile\beta\in H^2(T^2)\cong\mathbb{Z}$ is NONZERO (in fact a generator), while $\alpha\smile\alpha=0$ and $\beta\smile\beta=0$ (by the anticommutativity of the cup product on odd-degree classes: $\alpha\smile\beta=-\beta\smile\alpha$, forcing $\alpha\smile\alpha=-\alpha\smile\alpha$, so $2(\alpha\smile\alpha)=0$, and since $H^2$ is torsion-free here, $\alpha\smile\alpha=0$). This multiplicative structure — DISTINGUISHING $\alpha\smile\beta\ne0$ from $\alpha\smile\alpha=0$ — has NO counterpart when $H_1(T^2)$ is viewed merely as an abelian group; the cup product is genuinely additional information.

**Example 3 (LO3, orientation level — de Rham cohomology reaching the same answer analytically, breaking MC-3)**: on the circle $S^1$ (viewed as a smooth manifold), the $1$-form $d\theta$ (the standard angle form) is CLOSED ($d(d\theta)=0$, trivially, since it's already a top-degree form on a $1$-manifold) but NOT EXACT (there is no globally-defined smooth function $\theta$ on $S^1$ itself — going around the circle, $\theta$ increases by $2\pi$ rather than returning to its starting value) — so $[d\theta]$ represents a NONZERO class in $H^1_{\mathrm{dR}}(S^1)$, matching (via de Rham's theorem) the nonzero singular cohomology class $H^1(S^1;\mathbb{R})\cong\mathbb{R}$ found combinatorially in Example 1. Two utterly different techniques — one built from calculus on the circle, one from `math.top.homology`'s combinatorial chain complexes — arrive at the SAME underlying invariant.

## Component 5 — Teaching Actions

### Teaching Action A01 — Cohomology Dualizes Homology, It Doesn't Redefine It (Primitive P11: Representation Shift)

State: "cohomology isn't a rebranding of homology — $H^n(X)$ is the group of homomorphisms FROM $H_n(X)$ TO $\mathbb{Z}$, a genuinely dual construction, even when the resulting groups happen to look numerically identical." Walk Example 1's dualization of $S^1$'s homology.

- **MC-1 hook**: ask "is $H^n(X)$ just another name or notation for $H_n(X)$, computed the same way?" — a "yes" answer reveals MC-1 (missing that $H^n(X)$ is defined as the dual group $\mathrm{Hom}(H_n(X),\mathbb{Z})$, a conceptually different construction).

### Teaching Action A02 — The Cup Product Is Genuinely New Structure, Not Automatic (Primitive P28: Conflict Evidence)

Present Example 2's direct evidence: $\alpha\smile\beta\ne0$ while $\alpha\smile\alpha=0$ — a genuine multiplicative distinction with no counterpart in bare homology groups. State: "if cohomology were just 'homology with a different name,' there'd be nothing new to compute here — but the cup product reveals structure (which classes multiply to zero, which don't) that homology alone simply cannot see."

- **MC-2 hook**: ask "does cohomology, as a collection of groups $H^n(X)$, carry exactly the same information as homology, just presented differently?" — a "yes" answer reveals MC-2 (missing that the cup product gives $H^*(X)$ a ring structure — genuinely additional algebraic information beyond the groups themselves).

### Teaching Action A03 — De Rham Cohomology Reaches the Same Answer By a Completely Different Method (Primitive P06: Contrast Pair)

Contrast Example 1's COMBINATORIAL computation of $H^1(S^1)$ (via chain complexes, from `math.top.homology`) against Example 3's ANALYTIC computation via closed/exact differential forms — utterly different-looking methods, provably the same answer by de Rham's theorem. State: "these aren't two competing definitions where you have to pick the 'right' one — de Rham's theorem GUARANTEES they compute the identical invariant, despite using completely different mathematical machinery."

- **MC-3 hook**: ask "are de Rham cohomology (differential forms) and singular cohomology (from chain complexes) fundamentally different invariants that merely happen to have similar names?" — a "yes" answer reveals MC-3 (missing that de Rham's theorem proves these ARE isomorphic — the same invariant, computed by different methods).

### Teaching Action A04 — Mastery Gate (Primitive P91)

P91 = P77 → P55 → P76 → P55 → P75 → P55 → P74 → P55 → P78.

- **P77 (4-problem set)**:
  1. State the universal coefficient theorem's simplest (torsion-free) form relating $H^n(X)$ to $H_n(X)$.
  2. For $X=S^2$ (the sphere), given $H_0(S^2)=\mathbb{Z}$, $H_1(S^2)=0$, $H_2(S^2)=\mathbb{Z}$, compute $H^0(S^2)$, $H^1(S^2)$, $H^2(S^2)$ via the torsion-free universal coefficient theorem.
  3. Explain, citing the cup product, why cohomology carries strictly more information than the bare list of cohomology GROUPS alone.
  4. Explain, without full proof, what de Rham's theorem asserts and why it is a nontrivial, noteworthy result (rather than an obvious restatement).
- **P76 (Transfer Probe, mode = independence)**: "Consider $X=T^2\times S^1$ (a 3-torus-like space), whose cohomology ring $H^*(X)$ has generators $\alpha,\beta$ (from the $T^2$ factor, degree 1) and $\gamma$ (from the $S^1$ factor, degree 1), with $\alpha\smile\beta\ne0$ but $\alpha\smile\alpha=\beta\smile\beta=\gamma\smile\gamma=0$. (a) Explain why simply knowing the cohomology GROUPS $H^0,H^1,H^2,H^3$ (their ranks) would not distinguish this space's cup-product structure from a space with the same group ranks but a trivial (all-zero) cup product. (b) Explain, conceptually, why $H^1(X)$ being defined as $\mathrm{Hom}(H_1(X),\mathbb{Z})$ rather than simply 'being' $H_1(X)$ matters for setting up this dual pairing correctly. (c) If someone proposed computing this space's cohomology via de Rham's differential-forms approach instead of singular cohomology, explain why de Rham's theorem guarantees this alternative route would be legitimate and would agree with the singular computation."
