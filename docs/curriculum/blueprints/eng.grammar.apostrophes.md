**Concept:** Apostrophes — using apostrophes correctly to show possession (whose something is) and to
mark omitted letters in contractions (two words shortened into one), two distinct jobs performed by
the same punctuation mark.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Comma Usage (prerequisite) established careful attention
to punctuation's precise rules and functions. Apostrophes extends that precision to a mark with TWO
genuinely different jobs — showing possession and marking contractions — that are easily confused with
each other and with the unrelated case of simple plurals, which never take an apostrophe. This concept
has no further downstream unlocks in the current curriculum but is a foundational mechanical skill
underlying clear written English throughout.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) correctly use an apostrophe to show possession
(singular and plural possessives), (b) correctly use an apostrophe to mark omitted letters in a
contraction, and (c) correctly identify that a simple plural (more than one of something, with no
possession or contraction involved) never takes an apostrophe.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-ADD-AN-APOSTROPHE-BEFORE-ANY-S-AT-THE-END-OF-A-WORD-INCLUDING-SIMPLE-PLURALS

**Trigger signal:** Student adds an apostrophe before the "s" whenever a word ends in "s," including
simple plurals with no possession or contraction involved (e.g., writing "I have three dog's" instead
of "I have three dogs"), treating the apostrophe as a generic marker for "s" rather than a mark with
two SPECIFIC jobs.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two sentences: "I have three dog's." (an
apostrophe incorrectly inserted before a simple plural "s") and "The dog's bone is missing." (an
apostrophe correctly showing the bone belongs to the dog — possession). Ask: "Does the first sentence
show that something belongs to the dog, or is it just saying there are three dogs? Does it need an
apostrophe at all?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Adding an apostrophe to any word ending in 's' is like putting a 'FRAGILE'
sticker on every box you ship, regardless of whether it actually contains something fragile — the
sticker (apostrophe) has a SPECIFIC job (marking possession or a contraction), and slapping it on
everything that happens to end in 's' misuses it. A simple plural (more than one of something) never
needs an apostrophe at all."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before adding an apostrophe, ask: am I showing that something BELONGS to
someone/something (possession), or am I SHORTENING two words into one (a contraction)? If neither is
true — if I'm just saying there's more than one of something — no apostrophe is needed."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "I have three dog's." (incorrect — no possession or contraction, just a simple plural, no
   apostrophe needed) vs. "I have three dogs." (correct simple plural, no apostrophe).
2. "The dog's bone is missing." (correct — apostrophe shows the bone belongs to the dog, a genuine
   possessive) vs. "The dogs ran in the yard." (correct simple plural, no possession, no apostrophe).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to add apostrophes to simple plurals after the bridge, have
them apply the "does this show belonging, or shortening two words?" test directly to five sentences
containing words ending in "s," inserting an apostrophe ONLY when the test confirms one of those two
specific jobs.

### MC-B-POSSESSIVE-APOSTROPHES-ALWAYS-GO-IN-THE-SAME-PLACE-REGARDLESS-OF-WHETHER-THE-NOUN-IS-SINGULAR-OR-PLURAL

**Trigger signal:** Student applies one fixed rule for possessive apostrophe placement regardless of
whether the possessing noun is singular or plural, failing to distinguish singular possessive ("the
dog's bone" — one dog, apostrophe before the s) from plural possessive ("the dogs' bones" — multiple
dogs, apostrophe after the s), producing errors like "the dogs's bones" or using the singular pattern
for a plural possessor.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two sentences: "The dog's bone is missing." (one dog
possesses the bone) and "The dogs' bones are missing." (multiple dogs each possess a bone). Ask: "Does
the apostrophe go in the same place in both sentences? What's different about how many dogs are being
discussed, and does that change the correct placement?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Applying one fixed apostrophe-placement rule regardless of singular versus
plural is like using the same size lock for a single door and a set of double doors — the basic
principle (secure the door) is the same, but the mechanism needs to adapt to how many doors there
actually are. For a SINGULAR possessor, the apostrophe goes before the 's' ('the dog's bone'). For a
PLURAL possessor already ending in 's,' the apostrophe goes AFTER the 's' ('the dogs' bones') — the
placement depends on whether one or many are doing the possessing."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before placing a possessive apostrophe, ask: is the possessor SINGULAR
(one dog) or PLURAL (multiple dogs, already ending in 's')? For singular, add apostrophe-then-s. For
plural nouns already ending in 's,' add the apostrophe AFTER the existing 's,' with no additional s."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "The dog's bone is missing." (singular possessor — one dog — apostrophe before the s) vs. "The
   dogs' bones are missing." (plural possessor — multiple dogs, the noun already ends in 's' —
   apostrophe after the s, no additional s added).
2. A student writing "the dogs's bones" (incorrectly adding both the plural 's' and a redundant
   possessive 's' after the apostrophe) vs. correctly writing "the dogs' bones" (apostrophe placed
   directly after the existing plural 's,' nothing added).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to misplace possessive apostrophes for plural nouns after the
bridge, have them convert five singular possessive phrases into their plural possessive equivalents
(e.g., "the cat's toy" → "the cats' toys"), explicitly tracking where the apostrophe moves as the
noun shifts from singular to plural.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.grammar.comma-usage`):** "Correctly punctuate this sentence containing a
list of three items." A student who cannot apply basic comma rules lacks the punctuation-precision
foundation this concept builds on — route to `eng.grammar.comma-usage` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Fragile-Sticker-and-Adjustable-Lock Anchor.** Combine two images: a "FRAGILE" sticker applied
correctly only to boxes that actually contain fragile items, never applied automatically to every box —
directly seeding the fix for MC-A (apostrophes have a specific job, not a generic "any word ending in
s" rule). Second, a single door lock versus a double-door locking mechanism, each suited to how many
doors need securing — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (singular versus plural possessive placement
depends on how many possessors there are).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Simple Plurals Never Take an Apostrophe (MC-A)

Using the fragile-sticker anchor, teach the "belonging or shortening?" test to rule out apostrophes on
simple plurals. Student practice: given six sentences with words ending in "s," identify which are
simple plurals needing no apostrophe.

### TA-2 — Singular Possessive: Apostrophe Before the S

Teach the singular possessive rule (add apostrophe-then-s to a singular noun to show possession).
Student practice: given four sentences describing one person/thing possessing something, correctly
form the singular possessive.

### TA-3 — Plural Possessive: Apostrophe After the S (MC-B)

Using the adjustable-lock anchor, teach the plural possessive rule (add the apostrophe after an
existing plural "s," with no additional s). Student practice: given four sentences describing
multiple people/things possessing something, correctly form the plural possessive.

### TA-4 — Contractions: Apostrophes Marking Omitted Letters

Teach the contraction use of apostrophes (marking where letters have been omitted when two words are
shortened into one, e.g., "do not" → "don't"). Student practice: given six pairs of words, form the
correct contraction with the apostrophe placed exactly where letters are omitted.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Sentence set: "Three cat's played in the yard. The cat's toy was chewed. The
cats' toys were chewed. Its raining outside." Fully worked: "Three cats played" (simple plural,
apostrophe removed — fixing MC-A); "The cat's toy" (singular possessive, one cat, apostrophe before s
— correct); "The cats' toys" (plural possessive, multiple cats already ending in s, apostrophe after s
— fixing MC-B); "It's raining outside" (contraction of "it is," apostrophe added where letter is
omitted). Annotation confirms all four apostrophe rules applied correctly and distinctly.

**WE-2 (Guided):** New sentence set with similar errors (a simple plural incorrectly apostrophized, a
singular possessive, a plural possessive, and a missing contraction apostrophe). Student is prompted
with guiding questions ("Is this word just showing more than one, or does it show belonging or a
shortened word? If it's possession, is the possessor singular or plural — where does the apostrophe
go?") to correct each instance.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new sentence set containing all four apostrophe scenarios (simple plural,
singular possessive, plural possessive, contraction), unaided. Student corrects and applies apostrophes
appropriately throughout.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying Simple Plurals**
Given four sentences with words ending in "s," identify which are simple plurals requiring no
apostrophe.

**MP-2 [P36] — Forming Singular Possessives**
Given three sentences describing one person/thing possessing something, correctly form the singular
possessive.

**MP-3 [P36] — Forming Plural Possessives**
Given three sentences describing multiple people/things possessing something, correctly form the
plural possessive.

**MP-4 [P36] — Forming Contractions**
Given four word pairs, form the correct contraction with the apostrophe placed exactly where letters
are omitted.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Mixed Application**
Given a new sentence set containing simple plurals, singular possessives, plural possessives, and
contractions mixed together, correctly apply (or correctly omit) apostrophes throughout.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite. During
Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1 (before the simple-plural

into possessive formation as well. If either fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill before
resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure indicates residual MC-A;
MP-2 failure alone indicates the singular-possessive mechanics (TA-2) need review, distinct from
either misconception; MP-3 failure alone indicates residual MC-B; MP-4 failure alone indicates the
contraction mechanics (TA-4) specifically; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an
integration gap across all four rules simultaneously — provide one more guided mixed-application
example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters each apostrophe rule in isolation (MP-1 through MP-4) but reverts to errors
  when all four scenarios are mixed together (MP-5), provide a structured decision-tree checklist
  (plural, no apostrophe → possession? → singular or plural possessor? → contraction?) as scaffolding
  during mixed practice before removing it.
- If the student's plural possessive errors (MP-3) specifically involve irregular plurals that don't
  end in "s" (e.g., "children," "people"), note that these follow the SINGULAR possessive pattern
  (apostrophe-then-s: "the children's toys") since they don't already end in "s" — flag this as a
  distinct sub-case requiring separate practice.
- If the student confuses "its" (possessive, no apostrophe) with "it's" (contraction of "it is,"
  apostrophe required) — one of the most persistent errors even among skilled writers — give this
  specific pair dedicated, repeated practice beyond the general rules.
- Younger or less experienced writers may benefit from reading their own sentences aloud and
  mentally expanding any contraction to its full two-word form as a check that the apostrophe is
  correctly placed where letters were genuinely omitted.
