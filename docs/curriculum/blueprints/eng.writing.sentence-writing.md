## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-SENTENCE-IS-COMPLETE-AS-LONG-AS-IT-HAS-A-CAPITAL-LETTER-AND-A-PERIOD
- **Trigger signal:** Student judges sentence completeness purely by surface formatting (starts with a capital letter, ends with a period) rather than by checking for an actual complete subject-verb pair expressing a full thought — producing fragments punctuated as if they were complete sentences (e.g., "Running through the park." or "Because I was tired.").
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present "Running through the park." (capitalized, period-ended, but no subject-verb pair — a fragment) alongside "She ran through the park." (a genuine complete sentence). Ask the student what the difference is if both "look" like sentences by capitalization and punctuation.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Capital letters and periods are just the packaging — they don't make something a complete sentence any more than gift wrap makes an empty box a real present. A true sentence needs a genuine subject doing a genuine verb, expressing a complete thought that could stand alone and make sense by itself."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Before trusting the capital-letter-and-period formatting, check inside: is there an actual subject and verb pair? Does it express a complete thought that makes sense on its own, without needing anything before or after it? If not, it's a fragment wearing sentence clothing, not a real sentence."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Formatting-based completeness (capital letter + period = complete sentence) vs. (b) Structure-based completeness (a genuine subject-verb pair expressing a standalone complete thought = complete sentence, regardless of formatting, which fragments can also wear).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1 re-check, then re-run TA-1's subject-verb hunt before continuing.

### MC-LONGER-SENTENCES-ARE-ALWAYS-BETTER-WRITING
- **Trigger signal:** Student equates sentence quality with sentence length, deliberately padding sentences with unnecessary words, redundant phrases, or run-together ideas in an attempt to seem more sophisticated, rather than valuing clarity and precision — sometimes producing a run-on sentence or comma splice in the process of over-lengthening.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present two sentences describing the same event: a padded, wordy version ("The thing that happened was that the dog, which was a very big and also quite furry dog, ran extremely fast in the direction of the ball that had been thrown by the boy.") and a clear, appropriately-sized version ("The big, furry dog raced toward the ball the boy had thrown."). Ask which communicates the same information more clearly and which sounds more like skilled writing.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "A longer sentence isn't automatically better writing — it's often just less disciplined writing. Skilled writers choose the sentence length that best fits the idea: sometimes short and punchy, sometimes longer for a complex idea, but always as clear and precise as the content allows. Padding for length usually makes writing worse, not more impressive."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Write the sentence that most clearly and precisely expresses your idea — don't add extra words just to make it longer. If a shorter sentence says the same thing just as well, use the shorter one. Save longer sentences for ideas that genuinely need the extra structure to connect related information."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Length-as-quality (longer sentences signal more sophisticated or better writing) vs. (b) Clarity-as-quality (sentence quality is measured by how clearly and precisely it expresses the intended idea, independent of length; unnecessary padding reduces quality).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1, then re-present the Concrete Anchor's clear-window framing before continuing to TA-4.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

### PD-1: Simple-Sentence and Spelling Readiness
- **Tests:** Can the student already identify a complete independent clause (subject-verb pair standing alone) and spell common words with reasonable accuracy — the two component skills sentence-writing combines into original composition?
- **Probe:** Give 2 word groups, one a complete simple sentence and one a fragment, and ask the student to identify which is complete and why; separately, ask the student to spell 3 grade-appropriate words.
- **Pass condition:** Correctly identifies the complete sentence with a structural reason, and spells at least 2 of 3 words correctly.
- **Fail routing:** If clause-completeness judgment fails, route to `eng.grammar.simple-sentences` review; if spelling accuracy is very low, route to `eng.writing.spelling-strategies` review — sentence writing depends on both being reasonably solid.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**"The Clear Window vs. the Foggy Window."** Present two picture frames: one with a clear pane of glass showing a sharp, easily-seen picture behind it (representing a clear, well-constructed sentence — the reader sees the idea instantly), and one with a foggy, smudged pane where the same picture is barely visible through clutter (representing a padded, wordy, or fragmented sentence — the idea is obscured by unnecessary mess). Read a padded sentence aloud while pointing to the foggy window, then read its clear, appropriately-sized rewrite while pointing to the clear window. Also use the frame itself as a completeness check: a picture frame with no picture inside (an empty frame, formatted nicely) represents a fragment dressed up with capital-letter-and-period formatting but containing no genuine complete thought — directly seeding MC-A-SENTENCE-IS-COMPLETE-AS-LONG-AS-IT-HAS-A-CAPITAL-LETTER-AND-A-PERIOD's fix alongside MC-LONGER-SENTENCES-ARE-ALWAYS-BETTER-WRITING's clarity lesson.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1: The Subject-Verb Hunt for Completeness
Present a mixed batch of complete sentences and fragments, all correctly capitalized and punctuated, and have the student hunt for an actual subject-verb pair in each before judging completeness — directly countering MC-A-SENTENCE-IS-COMPLETE-AS-LONG-AS-IT-HAS-A-CAPITAL-LETTER-AND-A-PERIOD.

### TA-2: Building Sentences from a Word Bank
Given a small word bank (a subject, a verb, and optional details), student constructs multiple different complete sentences, checking each for genuine subject-verb completeness using the TA-1 test.

### TA-3: Precision Over Padding
Present a padded, wordy sentence and have the student identify and remove unnecessary filler words/phrases while preserving the core meaning, producing a clearer, more precise version.

### TA-4: Choosing Appropriate Sentence Length for the Idea
Using the discrimination pairs from MC-LONGER-SENTENCES-ARE-ALWAYS-BETTER-WRITING, present a set of ideas of varying complexity and have the student write an appropriately-sized sentence for each — a short punchy sentence for a simple idea, a longer well-structured sentence for a genuinely complex idea — rather than defaulting to maximal length for all.

### TA-5: Composing Original Sentences on a Topic
Student writes 5 original sentences on an assigned topic, varying in length and structure but each independently verified as complete (subject-verb hunt) and clear (no unnecessary padding) — the generative capstone.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

### WE-1: Fragment vs. Complete Sentence Detection (Scaffolded)
**Examples:** "Walking to school every morning." (fragment — no subject-verb pair, just a participial phrase) / "I walk to school every morning." (complete — clear subject "I," verb "walk").
**Walkthrough:** Apply the subject-verb hunt to both; confirm the first has no genuine subject performing an action as the main clause, despite proper capitalization and punctuation.

### WE-2: Removing Padding (Guided)
**Padded sentence:** "It was the case that the weather that day was something that could be described as extremely rainy and wet."
**Walkthrough:** Identify unnecessary filler ("it was the case that," "something that could be described as") and rewrite: "The weather that day was extremely rainy." Confirm the meaning is preserved with far greater clarity.

### WE-3: Appropriate Length for the Idea (Independent)
**Task:** Write a sentence about (a) a simple fact (the sun rose) and (b) a more complex situation (a student who studied hard for an exam but still felt nervous walking in).
**Walkthrough:** Student produces a short sentence for (a) ("The sun rose over the hills.") and an appropriately longer, well-structured sentence for (b) ("Even though she had studied hard for weeks, she still felt a flutter of nerves as she walked into the exam room."), confirming length matched the idea's actual complexity rather than being padded or truncated arbitrarily.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Detect a formatted fragment**
Given a properly capitalized and punctuated fragment, student correctly identifies it as incomplete using the subject-verb hunt, rejecting the formatting-based shortcut. *(Directly targets MC-A-SENTENCE-IS-COMPLETE-AS-LONG-AS-IT-HAS-A-CAPITAL-LETTER-AND-A-PERIOD.)*

**MP-2 [P36] — Remove unnecessary padding**
Given a padded, wordy sentence, student rewrites it more concisely while preserving the original meaning, explaining why the shorter version is better writing, not worse. *(Directly targets MC-LONGER-SENTENCES-ARE-ALWAYS-BETTER-WRITING.)*

**MP-3 [P36] — Word bank sentence construction**
Given a small word bank, student constructs 2 different complete, correct sentences.

**MP-4 [P36] — Length-appropriate composition**
Given one simple idea and one complex idea, student writes an appropriately-sized sentence for each.

**MP-5 [P36] — Original topic composition**
Student writes 4 original sentences on an assigned topic, each verified complete and free of unnecessary padding.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** At session open (S0), confirm PD-1 passes — sentence writing combines clause-completeness judgment and basic spelling accuracy into original composition, so weakness in either undermines the writing task itself. During instruction (S1), watch for both misconception patterns: trusting surface formatting over genuine structure (MC-A-SENTENCE-IS-COMPLETE-AS-LONG-AS-IT-HAS-A-CAPITAL-LETTER-AND-A-PERIOD) and equating length with quality (MC-LONGER-SENTENCES-ARE-ALWAYS-BETTER-WRITING) both substitute a surface signal for the real underlying judgment — redirect both to the Clear/Foggy Window anchor. If the student produces a formatted fragment or a padded sentence during TA-3/TA-4 (S6), drop back to the Concrete Anchor's subject-verb hunt and clarity demonstration before returning to composition practice. At mastery-probe stage (S9), do not certify mastery unless MP-1 and MP-2 both pass — a student who constructs correct sentences from a provided word bank (MP-3) but still produces fragments or padding in free composition has not yet internalized the completeness and clarity judgments this concept requires.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student produces properly formatted fragments, weight remediation toward MC-A-SENTENCE-IS-COMPLETE-AS-LONG-AS-IT-HAS-A-CAPITAL-LETTER-AND-A-PERIOD's bridge text and re-run TA-1's subject-verb hunt before returning to free composition.
- If the student pads sentences unnecessarily, weight remediation toward MC-LONGER-SENTENCES-ARE-ALWAYS-BETTER-WRITING's bridge text and TA-3's precision-editing practice specifically.
- This concept feeds one direct downstream unlock (`eng.writing.paragraph-structure`) — do not certify mastery without TA-5's original topic composition, since paragraph structure assumes the student can already reliably produce multiple clear, complete, appropriately-varied sentences before combining them into a coherent paragraph.
- Reuse the "clear window, not foggy window" framing explicitly when introducing `eng.writing.paragraph-structure` — that concept extends the same clarity-over-padding principle from the sentence level to the paragraph level.
