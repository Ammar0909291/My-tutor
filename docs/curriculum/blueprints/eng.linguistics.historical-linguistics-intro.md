**Concept:** Introduction to Historical Linguistics — a survey of how languages change over time in
sound, structure, and meaning, and how linguists systematically document and explain that change
rather than treating it as random drift or decay.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Etymology (prerequisite) teaches tracing individual
words' historical origins. Phonology (prerequisite) teaches the abstract sound system of a language
at a single point in time. Historical Linguistics teaches the DYNAMIC dimension: how entire sound
systems, grammatical structures, and word meanings change systematically over time, following
observable patterns — not randomly, and not as a decline from some "purer" earlier form. This concept
unlocks `eng.linguistics.language-families` (grouping languages into families based on shared
historical origins requires first understanding the mechanisms of systematic language change).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) explain that language change is systematic and
regular (following observable patterns across many words), not random or arbitrary, (b) identify at
least one type of systematic sound change, one type of grammatical change, and one type of meaning
change using real historical examples, and (c) explain why linguists treat language change as a
natural, ongoing process rather than a decline or corruption of some earlier, "purer" form of the
language.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-LANGUAGE-CHANGE-IS-RANDOM-DECAY-OR-CORRUPTION-OF-A-PURER-EARLIER-FORM

**Trigger signal:** Student describes historical language change as "language getting worse" or
"corruption" of some earlier, more correct or pure form — e.g., viewing modern English as a degraded
version of Old English or Shakespearean English, rather than recognizing that EVERY stage of a
language's history was itself a fully functional, complete linguistic system, and Old English was
just as "changed" from its own ancestor language as Modern English is from Old English.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a systematic historical sound change (e.g., the Great
Vowel Shift, which changed the pronunciation of English long vowels in a regular, predictable pattern
across many words over a couple of centuries) and ask: "Did this happen randomly and haphazardly, or
does it follow a consistent, predictable pattern? Was Middle English itself unchanged from Old
English, or had it also changed systematically from an even earlier stage?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Treating modern language as a 'corrupted' version of an earlier, purer form is
like assuming a river's current path is a 'corruption' of where it used to flow, rather than
recognizing that rivers ALWAYS change course over time through the same natural erosion processes —
there was never a moment where the river was in its permanently 'correct,' unchanging state. Every
stage of a language, at every point in history, is a complete, fully functional system that has
already changed from its own ancestor and will keep changing — there's no privileged 'pure' starting
point to measure decline from."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Instead of calling language change 'decay' or 'corruption,' recognize it
as an ongoing, natural process every language undergoes constantly — the same kind of process that
produced whatever 'earlier' stage you might be tempted to call 'purer.' Every historical stage of
English was itself the product of prior systematic change."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Modern English is a corrupted, lazy version of proper Old English." (treats one stage as
   privileged and pure, later change as decline) vs. "Modern English changed systematically from
   Middle English, which itself changed systematically from Old English — each stage is a complete
   system, not a corruption of the one before." (recognizes continuous, non-judgmental change).
2. A student assuming a language "used to be correct" and has since degraded (misconception) vs. a
   student recognizing that the specific features considered "correct" today will themselves seem
   old-fashioned or different in a few centuries, following the same ongoing process (correct
   understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues framing change as decay after the bridge, show them a specific
feature of Old English that was ITSELF a change from an earlier ancestor language (e.g., an
inflectional ending that developed from an earlier, different form), demonstrating that "the
original, pure form" recedes infinitely — there's no fixed starting point.

### MC-B-LANGUAGE-CHANGE-HAPPENS-RANDOMLY-WORD-BY-WORD-WITH-NO-SYSTEMATIC-PATTERN

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes that historical sound or grammatical changes happen
unpredictably, word by word, with no underlying regular pattern — failing to recognize that
historical linguists can identify systematic RULES governing change (e.g., a specific sound
changing the same way across dozens or hundreds of words that share a particular phonetic
environment), which is precisely what allows linguists to reconstruct language history and group
related languages into families.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a set of several English words that all underwent the
same historical sound change in a predictable, patterned way (e.g., several words where a specific
vowel sound shifted the same way across all of them, following the Great Vowel Shift), alongside the
observation that this same shift did NOT affect words with a different original vowel sound. Ask:
"Does this look like random, word-by-word chaos, or does it look like a systematic rule being applied
consistently across many words that share a specific feature?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming language change happens randomly, word by word, is like assuming a
city's traffic light timing changes happen by pure chance rather than following a systematic engineered
pattern — but if you actually study multiple intersections, you'd find a consistent, predictable
system underlying what looks chaotic at first glance. Historical linguists study language change the
same way: what looks like scattered individual word changes usually reveals a systematic sound or
grammar rule, once enough examples are compared."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When you encounter a historical language change, don't assume it's an
isolated, random event for that one word. Ask: does this same kind of change appear in OTHER words
sharing a similar sound or structure? Systematic patterns, not random word-by-word chaos, are the norm
in language change — that's exactly what lets linguists reconstruct language history."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Assuming a specific historical vowel change in one word was a one-off, isolated event
   (misconception) vs. recognizing that the same vowel change appears across dozens of words sharing
   the same original sound, revealing a systematic pattern (correct understanding).
2. A student unable to predict how an unfamiliar Old English word would likely be pronounced today,
   given a known systematic pattern (missing the systematicity) vs. a student successfully applying a
   known sound-change pattern to predict an unfamiliar word's likely modern form (correctly grasping
   systematicity).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to view changes as isolated after the bridge, give them three
new words that underwent the same historical sound change pattern already studied, and have them
predict the modern pronunciation of a fourth, previously unseen word sharing the same original sound,
testing whether they can apply the pattern predictively.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.vocab.etymology`):** "Trace the historical origin of this word back through at
least one earlier form." A student who cannot perform basic etymological tracing lacks the foundation
this concept builds on — route to `eng.vocab.etymology` review first.

**PD-2 (checks `eng.linguistics.phonology-intro`):** "Is this a phonetic or phonological question:
'does swapping these two sounds change a word's meaning in English?'" A student who cannot make this
basic distinction needs `eng.linguistics.phonology-intro` reviewed before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The River-Erosion-and-Traffic-Pattern Anchor.** Combine two images: a river's course changing
gradually over time through the same ongoing natural process at every stage, with no single "correct,
unchanging" original path — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (no privileged pure form exists; change
is continuous and normal). Second, a city's traffic light system, which looks chaotic at any single
intersection but reveals a systematic, engineered pattern once multiple intersections are compared —
directly seeding the fix for MC-B (apparent word-by-word chaos usually reveals a systematic underlying
rule once enough examples are compared).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Language Change as Continuous and Non-Judgmental (MC-A)

Using the river-erosion anchor, teach that every stage of a language is a complete system that has
already changed from its ancestor, with no privileged "pure" starting point. Student practice: given
three historical stages of English with a specific feature at each stage, explain that each stage
represents change from the one before, not decline from an original correct form.

### TA-2 — Systematic Sound Change

Using the traffic-pattern anchor, teach that historical sound changes follow systematic, predictable
rules across many words sharing a feature (e.g., the Great Vowel Shift). Student practice: given five
words that underwent the same historical sound change, identify the shared original feature that
triggered the systematic change.

### TA-3 — Systematic Grammatical Change

Introduce an example of systematic grammatical change over time (e.g., the loss of most inflectional
endings in the shift from Old English to Modern English, following a broad systematic trend rather
than random loss). Student practice: given examples of an inflectional feature present in an earlier
stage and absent in a later stage, identify the systematic pattern of loss.

### TA-4 — Systematic Meaning Change

Introduce common patterns of meaning change (broadening, narrowing, amelioration, pejoration) with
real historical examples (e.g., "meat" narrowing from "food in general" to specifically animal flesh).
Student practice: given four words with documented meaning shifts, classify each shift as broadening,
narrowing, amelioration, or pejoration.

### TA-5 — Full Application: Predicting and Explaining Systematic Change

Give the student a documented sound-change pattern and three words that underwent it, then ask them to
predict how a fourth, previously unseen word sharing the same original feature likely changed (TA-2),
while also identifying an example of grammatical or meaning change in the same data set (TA-3, TA-4)
and articulating that none of this represents decay or corruption (TA-1) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Sound change: the Great Vowel Shift's effect on a set of words with an
original long "i" sound (shifting toward a diphthong over time). Fully worked: three words identified
sharing the original sound; the systematic shift pattern described; a fourth, unfamiliar word with the
same original sound predicted to have undergone the same shift; explicit framing that this represents
ongoing systematic change, not corruption, since Middle English's pronunciation was itself already
changed from Old English. Annotation confirms both the systematic-pattern recognition (fixing MC-B)
and the non-judgmental, continuous-change framing (fixing MC-A).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new sound-change pattern with three example words and one word showing a meaning
shift. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("What do these three words have in common in their
original form? Can you predict how a fourth similar word likely changed? This word's meaning appears
to have shifted — has it broadened, narrowed, or shifted in a positive/negative direction? Is any of
this a sign of the language 'declining,' or is it the same kind of systematic change every stage of
every language undergoes?") to complete the analysis.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new data set with a sound-change pattern, a grammatical change example, and a
meaning-change example, unaided. Student identifies the systematic pattern, predicts an unseen case,
classifies the meaning change type, and explicitly frames all of it as ongoing, non-judgmental
language change.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Non-Judgmental Framing of Change**
Given a description of a historical language change, explain why it represents ongoing systematic
change rather than decay or corruption of a "purer" earlier form.

**MP-2 [P36] — Identifying Systematic Sound Change**
Given four words that underwent the same historical sound change, identify the shared original
feature that triggered the systematic pattern.

**MP-3 [P36] — Predicting an Unseen Case**
Given a documented sound-change pattern and three example words, predict how a fourth, previously
unseen word sharing the same original feature likely changed.

**MP-4 [P36] — Classifying Meaning Change**
Given three words with documented historical meaning shifts, classify each as broadening, narrowing,
amelioration, or pejoration.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Historical Analysis**
Given a new data set with a sound-change pattern and a meaning-change example, identify the
systematic sound pattern, predict an unseen case, classify the meaning change, and explain why none of
it represents decay.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; a gap in either prerequisite routes
back before Component 3. During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during
TA-1 (framing change as decline) and MC-B during TA-2 (viewing changes as isolated) — MC-A generally
needs resolving first since a student who views the current changes as "decay" often resists engaging
with the systematic-pattern analysis in TA-2 as genuinely legitimate. If either fires, branch to the
relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1
failure indicates residual MC-A; MP-2/MP-3 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-4 failure alone
indicates the meaning-change-classification step (TA-4) needs review, distinct from either
misconception; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one
more guided full-analysis example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student holds strong prior beliefs about "proper" or "correct" language (common among
  students who have internalized prescriptive grammar instruction), expect more resistance to MC-A's
  reframe — connect explicitly back to `eng.linguistics.what-is-linguistics`'s descriptive/
  prescriptive distinction if that concept has been completed, since this is the same principle
  applied historically rather than synchronically.
- If the student can identify systematic sound-change patterns (MP-2) but struggles specifically with
  predicting unseen cases (MP-3), this indicates the pattern was recognized but not yet internalized
  as a productive rule — provide additional practice with immediate feedback on predictions before
  re-attempting.
- If the student conflates meaning-change categories (broadening/narrowing/amelioration/pejoration),
  provide a clear two-axis sorting framework (scope: wider or narrower; valence: more positive or more
  negative) to disambiguate the four categories explicitly.
- Given this concept's expert-level difficulty and reliance on the etymology prerequisite, students
  with only shallow etymology skills may need brief re-grounding in specific word histories before
  the systematic-pattern work of TA-2 through TA-4 will land meaningfully.
