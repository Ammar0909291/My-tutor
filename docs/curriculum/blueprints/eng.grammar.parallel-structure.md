**Concept:** Parallel Structure — using matching grammatical forms for items joined in a series or
comparison (e.g., all gerunds, all infinitives, all nouns), avoiding mismatched structures that
create an unbalanced, awkward, or grammatically unclear sentence.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Compound Sentences (prerequisite) teaches joining
independent clauses with coordinating conjunctions. Parallel Structure teaches a related but distinct
skill: ensuring that items joined together in a SERIES (a list of three or more) or a COMPARISON share
the same grammatical form, so the sentence reads as balanced and clear rather than lopsided. This
concept has no downstream unlocks in the current curriculum but is cross-linked to
`eng.composition.style-voice-and-tone`, since parallel structure is one of the specific grammatical
patterns that contributes to a writer's recognizable stylistic signature.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) identify a parallel structure violation in a sentence
containing a series or comparison with mismatched grammatical forms, (b) correctly revise a
non-parallel sentence by making all items in the series or comparison match in grammatical form, and
(c) explain why parallel structure matters for readability and balance, not merely as an arbitrary
grammar rule.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-PARALLEL-STRUCTURE-ONLY-APPLIES-TO-SIMPLE-LISTS-OF-SINGLE-WORDS

**Trigger signal:** Student correctly applies parallel structure to simple word lists (e.g., "I like
apples, oranges, and bananas") but fails to recognize that the SAME principle applies to lists of
PHRASES or CLAUSES (e.g., gerund phrases, infinitive phrases, or full clauses joined in a series),
missing violations like "She likes swimming, to run, and biking" where the grammatical forms of the
three items don't match.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a correctly parallel list of single words ("I like
apples, oranges, and bananas" — all nouns) alongside a non-parallel list of PHRASES ("She likes
swimming, to run, and biking" — a gerund, an infinitive, and another gerund, mismatched). Ask: "Does
the same 'matching forms' principle that makes the first sentence work also apply to the second, even
though the items are phrases rather than single words?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Parallel structure isn't just a rule for simple word lists — it's a general
principle that applies any time you join items in a series or comparison, whether those items are
single words, phrases, or entire clauses. Think of it like matching place settings at a dinner table:
whether you're setting out spoons, forks, and knives (single items) or entire place-setting bundles
(matched combinations), the principle is the same — everything in the set should follow the same
pattern."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When checking for parallel structure, don't just check single-word
lists — check ANY series or comparison, including ones made of phrases (gerund phrases like
'swimming,' infinitive phrases like 'to swim') or full clauses. Every item in the series needs to
share the same grammatical form, no matter how long or complex each item is."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "She likes swimming, to run, and biking." (a gerund, an infinitive, and another gerund — mismatched
   phrase forms) vs. "She likes swimming, running, and biking." (all gerunds — matched phrase forms).
2. A student who catches "I like apples, oranges, and running" (an obvious single-word/phrase mismatch)
   but misses "She enjoys reading books, to write essays, and studying grammar" (a more subtle
   phrase-level mismatch) — showing the principle wasn't generalized beyond simple cases.

**s6_path:** If the student continues to miss phrase-level violations after the bridge, have them
underline each item in a series (regardless of length) and label its grammatical form (noun, gerund
phrase, infinitive phrase, clause) directly above it, making any mismatch visually obvious before
attempting a fix.

### MC-B-FIXING-A-PARALLEL-STRUCTURE-ERROR-JUST-MEANS-MAKING-THE-ITEMS-SOUND-SIMILAR

**Trigger signal:** Student attempts to fix a parallel structure violation by making the items sound
more similar in a surface, informal way (e.g., adding similar-sounding words) without actually
matching their underlying GRAMMATICAL FORM (part of speech, verb form, phrase type) — producing a
revision that still contains a genuine structural mismatch despite superficially "sounding better."

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student a non-parallel sentence ("The coach asked the team to
practice harder, to focus more, and improving their communication.") alongside two "fixes": one that
only superficially adjusts wording without fixing the actual grammatical mismatch ("The coach asked
the team to practice harder, to focus more, and to try improving their communication." — still mixing
an infinitive-plus-gerund construction with two plain infinitives), and one that correctly matches all
three items to the same infinitive form ("...to practice harder, to focus more, and to improve their
communication."). Ask: "Does the first 'fix' actually match the grammatical FORM of all three items,
or does it just add some similar-sounding words without truly fixing the structural mismatch?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Fixing parallel structure by just making items 'sound similar' without
checking the actual grammatical form is like tuning three instruments to roughly the same volume
without checking they're actually in the same musical key — it might sound closer at a glance, but
the underlying structure is still mismatched. A true fix requires identifying the EXACT grammatical
form (infinitive, gerund, specific verb tense) and making every item in the series match that exact
form precisely."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When fixing a parallel structure error, don't just adjust wording until
it 'sounds better' — identify the specific grammatical form of the items that DO match, and revise
the mismatched item(s) to that exact same form (same part of speech, same phrase type, same verb
form)."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "...to practice harder, to focus more, and to try improving their communication." (superficially
   adjusted, but still structurally mismatched — the third item isn't a simple infinitive like the
   first two) vs. "...to practice harder, to focus more, and to improve their communication." (all
   three items are now simple infinitives — genuinely matched).
2. A student who revises a non-parallel sentence by adding filler words until it "reads more smoothly"
   without checking actual grammatical form (surface fix) vs. a student who identifies the exact
   matching form needed and revises precisely to that form (structural fix).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to apply surface-level fixes after the bridge, have them label
the exact grammatical form of the two items that DO match in a non-parallel sentence, then revise ONLY
the mismatched item to that exact labeled form, with no other wording changes permitted — isolating
the structural-matching skill from general sentence polishing.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.grammar.compound-sentences`):** "Combine these two independent clauses using an
appropriate coordinating conjunction." A student who cannot form basic compound sentences lacks a
related joining skill this concept builds on — route to `eng.grammar.compound-sentences` review
first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Matched-Place-Settings-and-Instrument-Tuning Anchor.** Combine two images: a dinner table with
matched place settings (each setting following the identical pattern, whether simple or elaborate) —
directly seeding the fix for MC-A (the matching principle applies at any level of complexity, not
just single items). Second, three musical instruments being tuned to the exact same pitch (checked
precisely, not just adjusted until they sound roughly similar) — directly seeding the fix for MC-B
(true parallel structure requires matching the EXACT grammatical form, not just superficial
similarity).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Parallel Structure in Simple Word Lists

Introduce the basic principle with simple word-list examples (matching nouns, matching adjectives).
Student practice: given four simple word-list sentences, identify which are parallel and which are
not.

### TA-2 — Parallel Structure Extends to Phrases and Clauses (MC-A)

Using the place-settings anchor, teach that the same principle applies to gerund phrases, infinitive
phrases, and full clauses in a series. Student practice: given four phrase-level or clause-level
series, label each item's grammatical form and identify any mismatches.

### TA-3 — Precise Grammatical Matching, Not Surface Similarity (MC-B)

Using the instrument-tuning anchor, teach students to identify the exact matching form needed and
revise precisely to it. Student practice: given three non-parallel sentences with an attempted but
incomplete "surface" fix, identify why the fix still fails and correct it to genuinely match the form.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Identifying and Correcting Parallel Structure Violations

Give the student five sentences (a mix of word-list, phrase-level, and clause-level series, some
parallel and some not). They must identify any violations at any level of complexity (TA-1, TA-2) and
correct them by matching the exact grammatical form (TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Sentence: "The report should be clear, concise, and persuading readers."
Fully worked: items labeled ("clear" — adjective; "concise" — adjective; "persuading readers" — a
gerund phrase, mismatched with the two adjectives); revised to "The report should be clear, concise,
and persuasive" (all three now adjectives, matching exactly). Annotation confirms both that the
violation was correctly identified despite involving a phrase, not just single words (fixing MC-A),
and that the fix matches the EXACT grammatical form (adjective), not just a superficial adjustment
(fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New sentence with a phrase-level parallel structure violation and an incomplete
attempted fix already provided. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("Label the grammatical
form of each item in this series — do they all match, even though some are phrases rather than single
words? Does the provided 'fix' actually match the exact form of the other items, or does it just sound
somewhat better without truly fixing the mismatch?") to identify the remaining issue and complete a
genuine fix.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new sentence containing a parallel structure violation at the phrase or
clause level, unaided. Student identifies the violation and revises it to genuinely matching
grammatical form.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying Simple Word-List Violations**
Given three simple word-list sentences, identify which contain a parallel structure violation.

**MP-2 [P36] — Identifying Phrase-Level Violations**
Given three sentences containing series of phrases (gerund, infinitive, or clause-level), identify
which contain a parallel structure violation, labeling each item's grammatical form.

**MP-3 [P36] — Detecting Incomplete Surface Fixes**
Given a non-parallel sentence with an attempted but still-mismatched "fix," identify why the fix does
not actually achieve grammatical matching.

**MP-4 [P36] — Precise Correction**
Given a non-parallel sentence, revise it by matching all items to one exact, identified grammatical
form.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Identification and Correction**
Given a new sentence containing a parallel structure violation at any level of complexity (word,
phrase, or clause), identify the violation and correct it to genuinely matching grammatical form.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite.
During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-2 (missing phrase-level
violations after mastering simple word lists) and MC-B during TA-3 (surface-only fixes) — MC-A
generally needs resolving first since a student who cannot even detect phrase-level violations has
nothing yet to practice fixing precisely. If either fires, branch to the relevant s6_path drill before
resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure indicates the basic
principle (TA-1) needs review, distinct from either misconception; MP-2 failure alone indicates
residual MC-A; MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing
indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-identification-and-correction example
before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters simple word-list parallel structure (MP-1) but consistently misses phrase-
  level and clause-level violations (MP-2), continue extending practice specifically at those higher
  complexity levels rather than assuming the underlying principle has generalized.
- If the student's corrections (MP-4) are grammatically parallel but awkward or unnatural-sounding,
  clarify that correct parallel structure sometimes requires choosing a DIFFERENT but still-matching
  form for all items (not necessarily forcing the mismatched item into the other items' exact form) —
  connect this flexibility to `eng.composition.style-voice-and-tone`'s emphasis on natural, effective
  phrasing.
- If the student over-applies parallel structure to items that are NOT actually part of the same
  series or comparison (forcing unrelated sentence elements into artificial matching), clarify that
  the principle only applies to genuine series/comparison structures, not to every pair of nearby
  phrases.
- Students who struggle with abstract grammatical labeling (identifying "gerund phrase" vs.
  "infinitive phrase" by name) may still successfully identify mismatches by ear/intuition — allow
  this as a valid entry point, then connect it to the formal labels for the more precise correction
  work in TA-3/TA-4.
