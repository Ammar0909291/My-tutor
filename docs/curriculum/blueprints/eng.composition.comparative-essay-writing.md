**Concept:** Comparative Essay Writing — writing an essay that systematically analyzes similarities
and differences between two texts, ideas, or works, organized around genuine analytical POINTS of
comparison rather than describing each item fully in isolation, one after the other.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Rhetorical Analysis (prerequisite) taught analyzing how a
SINGLE text's rhetorical choices work together. Comparative Essay Writing extends this to TWO texts
simultaneously, teaching the specific organizational discipline a genuine comparison requires: point-
by-point structure organized around shared categories of comparison, rather than a "block" structure
that fully describes one item, then fully describes the other, leaving the actual comparing work
implicit or absent. This concept has no further downstream unlocks in the current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) organize a comparative essay by POINT (each paragraph
addressing one specific category of comparison across both items) rather than by BLOCK (fully
describing one item, then the other), (b) explicitly state the comparative relationship (similarity
or difference) within each point-based paragraph rather than leaving the reader to infer the
comparison from separately-described facts, and (c) select genuinely meaningful, analytically
significant points of comparison rather than superficial or trivial ones.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-A-COMPARATIVE-ESSAY-MEANS-FULLY-DESCRIBING-ONE-ITEM-THEN-FULLY-DESCRIBING-THE-OTHER

**Trigger signal:** Student organizes a comparative essay in "block" structure — writing several
paragraphs fully describing the first text or work, followed by several paragraphs fully describing
the second — leaving the actual COMPARISON implicit or entirely absent, rather than organizing by
POINT (a shared category addressed across both items within each paragraph), which is what allows a
reader to see the comparison actually being made, paragraph by paragraph.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student an outline organized in block structure (three
paragraphs fully describing Text A, then three paragraphs fully describing Text B, with no explicit
comparison paragraph) alongside an outline organized by point (three paragraphs, each addressing one
shared category — theme, structure, tone — comparing both texts within each paragraph). Ask: "Does
the block structure ever explicitly state how Text A and Text B are similar or different on any
specific point? Does the point structure make the actual comparison visible, paragraph by paragraph?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Organizing a comparative essay in block structure is like writing two
completely separate restaurant reviews and expecting the reader to somehow deduce which restaurant is
better on any specific dimension (service, food quality, price) by comparing the two reviews
themselves — the actual COMPARING work never happens on the page. A genuine comparative essay does
that comparing work explicitly, point by point, rather than leaving it as an exercise for the reader."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Organize your comparative essay by POINT, not by block. For each
paragraph, choose ONE specific category of comparison (a theme, a technique, an effect) and explicitly
address BOTH texts within that same paragraph, stating the similarity or difference directly — don't
describe each text fully in its own separate section."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Three paragraphs fully describing Text A, followed by three paragraphs fully describing Text B,
   with no paragraph explicitly comparing them (block structure, comparison left implicit) vs. three
   paragraphs, each addressing one shared category across both texts within the same paragraph (point
   structure, comparison made explicit).
2. A student whose "comparative" essay reads as two separate essays stapled together (block
   structure, missing the actual comparative work) vs. a student whose essay explicitly states
   "Text A does X, while Text B does Y" within each point-based paragraph (correct comparative
   structure).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to write in block structure after the bridge, have them take
their own block-structured draft and identify three genuine shared categories of comparison present
across both blocks, then reorganize the content into point-based paragraphs addressing each category
explicitly.

### MC-B-ANY-SUPERFICIAL-SIMILARITY-OR-DIFFERENCE-IS-A-MEANINGFUL-POINT-OF-COMPARISON-WORTH-A-PARAGRAPH

**Trigger signal:** Student selects trivial, superficial, or analytically insignificant similarities
or differences as points of comparison (e.g., "both texts have chapters," "one text is longer than
the other") rather than genuinely meaningful points that reveal something significant about the two
works' themes, techniques, or effects, failing to distinguish superficial comparison points from
analytically substantive ones.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two candidate comparison points for the same pair of
texts: a trivial one ("both texts are divided into chapters") and a substantive one ("both texts
explore the theme of isolation, but Text A frames it as a source of strength while Text B frames it as
a source of despair"). Ask: "Does the first point reveal anything meaningful about what these texts
are actually doing or mean? Does the second?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Choosing a trivial comparison point is like writing a movie review that
spends a paragraph comparing two films' RUNTIMES in minutes, while never discussing what the films
actually mean or how they achieve their effects — technically a true comparison, but analytically
empty. A genuine comparative essay selects points that reveal something meaningful about the works'
themes, techniques, or effects, not merely any true similarity or difference that happens to exist."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before choosing a comparison point, ask: does this point reveal something
meaningful about the works' themes, techniques, or effects, or is it a superficial fact true of both
texts that doesn't actually illuminate anything significant? Select points with real analytical
substance."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Both texts are divided into chapters." (a trivial, analytically empty comparison point) vs. "Both
   texts explore isolation, but frame it with opposite emotional valence." (a substantive point
   revealing something meaningful about each work's treatment of a shared theme).
2. A student who fills a comparative essay with easily-observed but analytically insignificant
   similarities (misconception) vs. a student who selects fewer but genuinely meaningful points of
   comparison, each revealing something significant (correct, substantive approach).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to select trivial comparison points after the bridge, have them
generate five candidate comparison points for a given text pair, then rank each by analytical
significance, explaining what (if anything) each point reveals about the works' themes, techniques, or
effects, discarding the ones that reveal nothing substantive.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.composition.rhetorical-analysis`):** "Analyze how two specific rhetorical choices
in this passage work together to create one cumulative effect." A student who cannot perform basic
single-text rhetorical synthesis lacks the foundation this concept extends to two texts — route to
`eng.composition.rhetorical-analysis` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Two-Separate-Restaurant-Reviews and Movie-Runtime-Comparison Anchor.** Combine two images: two
completely separate restaurant reviews with no paragraph actually comparing them on any specific
dimension — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (comparative work must be done explicitly, point by
point, not left implicit). Second, a movie review spending a paragraph comparing two films' runtimes
in minutes while never discussing meaning or effect — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (comparison
points must have real analytical substance, not just be any true similarity or difference).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Point Structure vs. Block Structure (MC-A)

Using the two-restaurant-reviews anchor, teach the point-structure organizational principle. Student
practice: given a block-structured outline for two texts, identify shared categories present across
both blocks and reorganize into a point-based outline.

### TA-2 — Explicitly Stating the Comparative Relationship

Extend TA-1: teach students to explicitly state the similarity or difference within each point-based
paragraph, not just describe both texts side by side without connecting them. Student practice: given
a point-based paragraph that describes both texts but never explicitly states how they compare,
revise it to state the comparative relationship directly.

### TA-3 — Selecting Substantive Points of Comparison (MC-B)

Using the movie-runtime anchor, teach students to evaluate candidate comparison points for analytical
substance. Student practice: given five candidate comparison points for a text pair, rank them by
significance and discard the trivial ones.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Writing a Comparative Essay Outline

Give the student two new texts (or text summaries) to compare. They must select genuinely substantive
comparison points (TA-3), organize them into a point-based structure (TA-1), and draft at least one
paragraph that explicitly states the comparative relationship (TA-2) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Two short story summaries sharing a theme of ambition. Fully worked: five
candidate comparison points generated, three identified as trivial (both have a protagonist, both are
short stories, both have dialogue) and two as substantive (both explore ambition, but one frames it as
morally corrupting while the other frames it as redemptive; both use a similar narrative device — an
unreliable narrator — but for different thematic purposes); the two substantive points organized into
point-based paragraphs, each explicitly stating the comparative relationship. Annotation confirms both
the point-structure organization (fixing MC-A) and the substantive-point selection (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New pair of text summaries with a different shared theme. Student is prompted with
guiding questions ("What candidate comparison points can you generate? Which reveal something
meaningful about these texts' themes or techniques, and which are trivial? Once you've selected
substantive points, does your paragraph for each one explicitly state how the two texts compare, or
does it just describe them side by side?") to complete the outline and drafting.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new pair of texts to compare, unaided. Student selects substantive
comparison points, organizes them by point structure, and drafts paragraphs explicitly stating the
comparative relationship.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Converting Block Structure to Point Structure**
Given a block-structured outline for two texts, identify shared categories and reorganize into a
point-based outline.

**MP-2 [P36] — Stating the Comparative Relationship Explicitly**
Given a paragraph describing both texts on one point but not explicitly comparing them, revise it to
state the similarity or difference directly.

**MP-3 [P36] — Identifying Trivial vs. Substantive Comparison Points**
Given four candidate comparison points, identify which are trivial and which are analytically
substantive.

**MP-4 [P36] — Ranking Comparison Points by Significance**
Given five candidate comparison points for a text pair, rank them by analytical significance,
explaining what each reveals (or fails to reveal).

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Comparative Essay Outline**
Given a new pair of texts to compare, select substantive comparison points, organize them by point
structure, and draft at least one paragraph explicitly stating the comparative relationship.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite. During

indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-outline example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student masters point-structure organization on provided outlines (MP-1) but reverts to
  block structure when generating their own original comparison (MP-5), provide a mandatory point-
  based planning template (list categories first, then draft) as scaffolding until the habit is
  automatic.
- If the student's substantive comparison points (MP-3, MP-4) are correctly significant but their
  explicit-comparison paragraphs (MP-2) still read as two separate descriptions loosely placed near
  each other, push for an explicit "while/whereas/similarly" sentence structure connecting the two
  texts directly within the same sentence.
- If the student struggles to generate candidate comparison points at all, provide a starter list of
  common comparison categories (theme, structure, tone, technique, effect on the reader) as a
  brainstorming aid before the significance-ranking step.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.composition.rhetorical-analysis`'s synthesis work should
  transfer readily to the point-structure organizational skill, since both require weaving multiple
  threads together rather than treating them separately — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
