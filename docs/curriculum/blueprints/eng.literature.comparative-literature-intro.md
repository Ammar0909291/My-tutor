**Concept:** Introduction to Comparative Literature — a survey introduction to studying literary works
across LANGUAGES, CULTURES, and TRADITIONS in relation to one another, examining both genuine shared
patterns (arising from cross-cultural influence or universal human concerns) and genuine, meaningful
differences shaped by distinct cultural and historical contexts, without assuming either that all
literature is fundamentally the same everywhere or that works from different traditions are too
different to meaningfully compare at all.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Introduction to Literary Criticism (prerequisite) taught
multiple interpretive lenses for a single text within one (implicitly English-language) tradition.
Comparative Literature extends this outward, teaching the student to compare works ACROSS languages,
cultures, and literary traditions — a genuinely different analytical task that requires holding two
things in mind simultaneously: real, meaningful commonalities across traditions, and real, meaningful
differences shaped by distinct cultural contexts, neither flattening all literature into one universal
sameness nor treating different traditions as incomparable. This concept has no further downstream
unlocks in the current curriculum, completing the literature sequence's cross-cultural branch.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) identify a genuine point of comparison between works
from two different literary traditions (a shared theme, device, or structural pattern), explaining
whether the similarity likely arises from direct cultural influence/contact or from independent,
convergent development around a universal human concern, (b) identify a genuine, meaningful
DIFFERENCE between two compared works that reflects their distinct cultural or historical contexts,
rather than dismissing the difference as irrelevant, and (c) explain the risk of assuming a work in
translation reads identically to how it reads in its original language, recognizing translation as an
interpretive act that can shape comparative analysis.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-ANY-SIMILARITY-BETWEEN-WORKS-FROM-DIFFERENT-TRADITIONS-MEANS-ONE-DIRECTLY-INFLUENCED-THE-OTHER

**Trigger signal:** Student, upon finding a genuine similarity (a shared theme, motif, or structural
pattern) between works from two different literary traditions, automatically assumes this must reflect
DIRECT cultural influence or borrowing from one tradition to the other, failing to recognize that
similar literary patterns can also arise INDEPENDENTLY through convergent development — different
cultures, with no direct contact, sometimes arrive at similar literary solutions to universal human
concerns (loss, coming of age, conflict between duty and desire) simply because these are shared
aspects of the human experience, not because one tradition borrowed from the other.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present the student with two literary traditions from historical periods
and regions with NO documented direct contact, both containing a similar narrative pattern (e.g., a
hero's journey structure, or a coming-of-age motif). Ask: "Given that these two traditions had no
documented historical contact, could this shared pattern have arisen through direct one-tradition-
influencing-the-other borrowing? What ELSE might explain the similarity?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming any similarity must mean direct influence is like assuming that
because two isolated island populations both independently developed similar tools for catching fish
in shallow water, one population must have taught the other — but similar environmental challenges
(shallow water fishing) can produce similar, independently-developed solutions with no contact at all.
Similarly, shared human concerns (loss, growing up, duty versus desire) can produce similar literary
patterns across traditions with no documented contact, through independent convergent development, not
necessarily direct borrowing."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When you find a similarity between works from different traditions, ask:
is there documented historical contact or evidence of direct influence between these traditions? If
not, consider that the similarity might reflect independent, convergent development around a shared
human concern, rather than assuming direct borrowing by default."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A shared narrative pattern between two traditions with documented trade, colonization, or
   translation contact (plausible direct influence) vs. the same pattern appearing in two traditions
   with no documented contact at all (more likely independent, convergent development around a shared
   human concern).
2. A student who assumes any cross-cultural literary similarity must mean one culture copied the
   other (misconception) vs. a student who considers documented historical contact as evidence before
   concluding direct influence, and considers convergent development as a genuine alternative
   explanation (correct, more careful reasoning).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to assume direct influence by default after the bridge, give
them two more pairs of traditions (one with documented contact, one without) sharing a similar literary
pattern, and have them research or reason through which explanation (direct influence or convergent
development) is better supported by the available historical evidence in each case.

### MC-B-COMPARING-LITERATURE-ACROSS-CULTURES-MEANS-IGNORING-OR-FLATTENING-OUT-THE-REAL-DIFFERENCES-BETWEEN-THEM

**Trigger signal:** Student, in the process of identifying genuine similarities between works from
different traditions for comparative purposes, downplays, ignores, or flattens out genuine, meaningful
DIFFERENCES shaped by each work's distinct cultural and historical context, treating comparison as a
search for sameness rather than recognizing that meaningful comparison requires holding BOTH genuine
similarities AND genuine, culturally-rooted differences in view simultaneously.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two works sharing a genuine common theme (e.g., family
duty) but treating that duty very differently based on their distinct cultural contexts (one framing
duty as an individual moral choice, one framing it as an inseparable extension of collective family
identity with different social and religious underpinnings). Ask: "Is it accurate to say these two
works 'have the same theme,' full stop? What would be lost by ignoring how differently each culture's
specific context shapes what that shared theme actually MEANS in each work?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Flattening out real cultural differences in the search for similarity is like
noting that two dishes from different culinary traditions both 'use spice' and concluding they're
'basically the same food' — technically true at a very general level, but this ignores everything that
actually makes each dish distinct and meaningful within its own culinary tradition. Genuine comparative
literature holds BOTH the real similarity (both works address family duty) AND the real, culturally-
specific differences (what duty means, how it's enacted, what tensions it creates) in view at once."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "When comparing works across traditions, don't stop at identifying a
shared theme or pattern. Always also ask: how does each work's distinct cultural and historical
context shape what that shared element actually MEANS or how it FUNCTIONS differently in each case?
Both the similarity and the difference are part of a genuine comparison."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Both works are about family duty, so they're essentially the same." (flattening real cultural
   differences in the pursuit of similarity) vs. "Both works explore family duty, but Work A frames it
   as an individual moral choice while Work B frames it as an inseparable extension of collective
   identity, reflecting their distinct cultural contexts." (holding similarity and difference together).
2. A student whose comparative analysis stops at "they're similar because..." with no attention to
   meaningful difference (incomplete, flattened comparison) vs. a student whose analysis addresses
   both what's shared and what's genuinely different and why (complete, genuine comparison).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to flatten differences after the bridge, have them take a
comparison they've already identified a similarity for and explicitly articulate at least one specific,
culturally-rooted difference in how each work treats that shared element, before considering the
comparison complete.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.literature.literary-criticism-intro`):** "Explain why different critical
approaches (historical, formalist, reader-response) can be complementary rather than competing for one
correct interpretation." A student who cannot reason about this correctly lacks the interpretive-
plurality foundation this concept extends across traditions — route to
`eng.literature.literary-criticism-intro` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Isolated-Island-Fishing-Tools and Spice-Flattening Anchor.** Combine two images: two isolated
island populations independently developing similar fishing tools with no contact, due to shared
environmental challenges rather than one teaching the other — directly seeding the fix for MC-A
(similarity can arise from independent, convergent development, not only direct influence). Second, two
very different dishes both described merely as "using spice," flattening out everything that actually
makes each dish distinct within its own tradition — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (genuine
comparison requires holding both similarity and meaningful cultural difference in view).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Direct Influence vs. Independent Convergent Development (MC-A)

Using the isolated-island anchor, teach students to check for documented historical contact before
assuming direct influence explains a cross-traditional similarity. Student practice: given three
pairs of traditions sharing a literary pattern, evaluate the likelihood of documented contact versus
independent development for each.

### TA-2 — Holding Similarity and Difference Together (MC-B)

Using the spice-flattening anchor, teach students to identify genuine cultural differences alongside
identified similarities. Student practice: given two works sharing a theme, identify at least one
genuine, culturally-rooted difference in how each work treats that shared theme.

### TA-3 — Translation as an Interpretive Act

Introduce the risk of assuming a translated work reads identically to its original — translation
involves interpretive choices (word connotation, rhythm, cultural reference) that can shape what a
comparative reader experiences. Student practice: given two different translations of the same
original passage, identify at least one meaningful difference in interpretive choice between them.

### TA-4 — Full Application: A Genuine Cross-Traditional Comparison

Give the student descriptions of two works from different traditions sharing an apparent similarity.
They must evaluate whether the similarity likely reflects direct influence or convergent development
(TA-1), identify a genuine, culturally-rooted difference (TA-2), and note any translation-related
caveat if relevant (TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Two works from traditions with no documented historical contact, both featuring
a "return home after a long journey" narrative pattern, but treating the returning figure's
relationship to home very differently (one framing return as restoration of belonging, one framing it
as unsettling and irrevocably changed). Fully worked: the shared pattern's likely origin evaluated
(no documented contact — more likely independent, convergent development around the universal
experience of journeying and returning); the genuine cultural difference identified and explained
(differing conceptions of home and identity shaped by each tradition's distinct context); a brief note
on translation risk if either work is being read in translation. Annotation confirms both the
influence-versus-convergence reasoning (fixing MC-A) and the similarity-plus-difference analysis
(fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New pair of works from traditions WITH documented historical contact (trade,
colonization, or translation history), sharing a different literary pattern. Student is prompted with
guiding questions ("Is there documented contact between these traditions — does that make direct
influence more plausible here than in the previous example? What's a genuine, culturally-specific
difference in how each work treats the shared pattern, beyond just noting the similarity?") to
complete the comparison.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new pair of works from different traditions, unaided. Student evaluates the
likely origin of any shared pattern (influence vs. convergence), identifies a genuine cultural
difference, and notes translation considerations if relevant.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Evaluating Influence vs. Convergent Development**
Given two traditions sharing a literary pattern, with information about their historical contact (or
lack thereof), evaluate whether direct influence or independent convergent development is more
plausible.

**MP-2 [P36] — Identifying a Genuine Cultural Difference**
Given two works sharing a theme, identify at least one genuine, culturally-rooted difference in how
each treats that shared theme.

**MP-3 [P36] — Recognizing Translation as Interpretive**
Given two different translations of the same original passage, identify at least one meaningful
difference in interpretive choice between them.

**MP-4 [P36] — Avoiding the Flattening Trap**
Given a comparative analysis that stops at "these works are similar because...", identify what genuine
cultural difference is missing from the analysis and add it.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Cross-Traditional Comparison**
Given descriptions of two new works from different traditions sharing an apparent similarity, evaluate
the likely origin of the similarity, identify a genuine cultural difference, and note translation
considerations if relevant.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite. During
Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1 (assuming direct influence
by default) and MC-B during TA-2 (flattening differences) — these are largely independent and often
need separate, explicit correction, since a student can correctly reason about influence-versus-
convergence while still flattening cultural differences, or vice versa. If either fires, branch to the
relevant s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure
indicates residual MC-A; MP-2/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-3 failure alone indicates the
translation-awareness extension (TA-3) needs review, distinct from either misconception; MP-5 failure
with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-comparison
example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student has personal, lived experience with more than one cultural or literary tradition,
  invite them to bring this experience directly into the comparative work — personal, culturally
  grounded insight can substantially deepen the analysis beyond what abstract examples alone provide.
- If the student masters influence-versus-convergence reasoning (MP-1) but their cultural-difference
  identification (MP-2, MP-4) remains superficial (naming a difference without explaining its cultural
  roots), push for the "why" — what specific aspect of each tradition's history, values, or context
  produces this particular difference.
- If the student becomes overly cautious about ever claiming a genuine cross-traditional similarity
  (fearing any comparison risks "flattening" or cultural insensitivity), clarify that genuine,
  well-reasoned comparison — holding both similarity and difference — is the goal, not avoidance of
  comparison altogether.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.reading.reading-across-genres` should transfer well to this
  concept's comparative reasoning skills, since both require holding multiple texts' distinct
  characteristics in view simultaneously rather than collapsing them into one undifferentiated
  category.
