**Concept:** Skimming and Scanning — rapidly surveying a text for its general idea (skimming) or
locating one specific piece of information (scanning), two distinct rapid-reading techniques chosen
according to the reader's actual purpose, not a substitute for careful reading when deep
comprehension is genuinely needed.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Reading Fluency (prerequisite) taught reading smoothly
and automatically at a normal pace. Skimming and Scanning extends this by teaching two deliberately
NON-complete reading strategies used for specific purposes: skimming samples a text's structure to
grasp its general idea quickly; scanning hunts for one specific target (a name, date, term) while
ignoring everything else. Both are chosen when the task doesn't require full comprehension, and both
are misapplied when they replace careful reading for tasks that actually need it. This concept has
no further downstream unlocks in the current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) select the correct rapid-reading technique for a
given purpose — skimming for general idea, scanning for one specific piece of information — rather
than confusing the two or applying the wrong one, and (b) recognize when a task actually requires
full careful reading rather than skimming or scanning, rather than treating rapid techniques as
adequate for every reading task.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-SKIMMING-AND-SCANNING-ARE-THE-SAME-TECHNIQUE-WITH-DIFFERENT-NAMES

**Trigger signal:** Student uses skimming and scanning interchangeably, applying the wrong technique
for the task at hand — for example, scanning (hunting for one specific word or number) when the task
actually requires understanding the text's overall gist (skimming), or skimming broadly for general
sense when the task requires locating one specific fact (scanning) — failing to recognize these are
two distinct techniques suited to two distinct purposes.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Give the student a task: "What is this article generally about?" and a
task: "What year did this event happen?" using the same article. Ask: "For the first question, do
you need to find one specific word, or get a general sense of the whole piece? For the second
question, do you need the general sense, or one specific piece of information?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Treating skimming and scanning as the same technique is like using a
telescope and a magnifying glass interchangeably — a telescope helps you see the big picture from far
away (skimming for general idea), while a magnifying glass helps you zoom in on one tiny specific
detail (scanning for a specific fact). Using the wrong tool for the task wastes time and gives the
wrong kind of result."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before rapidly reading a text, identify your actual purpose: do you
need the text's general idea (skim — read headings, topic sentences, first/last lines), or do you
need one specific piece of information (scan — search only for that specific word, number, or name,
ignoring everything else)?"

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "What is this article generally about?" (requires skimming — general idea) vs. "What year did
   this event happen?" (requires scanning — one specific fact).
2. A student who scans word-by-word for a general-idea question, missing the overall point
   (misconception — wrong technique for the task) vs. a student who correctly skims headings and
   topic sentences for a general-idea question (correct technique match).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to confuse the two techniques after the bridge, give them four
reading tasks, alternating between general-idea and specific-fact purposes, and have them state which
technique (skim or scan) fits each task before beginning to read.

### MC-B-SKIMMING-OR-SCANNING-GIVES-THE-SAME-DEPTH-OF-UNDERSTANDING-AS-CAREFUL-READING

**Trigger signal:** Student treats a quick skim or scan as sufficient for any reading task, including
tasks that genuinely require full, careful comprehension (understanding an author's nuanced argument,
following a complex multi-step explanation) — using surface-level rapid techniques where they don't
belong and missing understanding that only careful, complete reading would provide.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Give the student a task: "Explain the three reasons the author gives for
their position, and how they relate to each other." Ask: "If you only skim the headings and topic
sentences, will you actually understand all three reasons and how they connect, or will you need to
read the whole passage carefully?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming a skim or scan gives the same understanding as careful reading is
like assuming glancing at a building's outline from across the street tells you everything about its
interior layout — you get a rough shape, not the actual detail. Skimming and scanning are useful for
locating or previewing, but genuine comprehension of nuanced or complex content requires actually
reading it carefully."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before deciding to skim or scan, ask: does this task need me to
genuinely understand nuance, connections, or complex reasoning? If so, skimming or scanning is not
enough — switch to full, careful reading. Save skimming and scanning for previewing or locating
specific facts, not for deep comprehension tasks."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "What is this article generally about?" (skimming is genuinely sufficient) vs. "Explain the three
   reasons the author gives and how they connect." (requires full careful reading; skimming alone
   would miss the connections).
2. A student who skims a complex argument and then answers a nuanced comprehension question
   incorrectly, missing the actual reasoning (misconception — used the wrong-depth technique) vs. a
   student who recognizes the task requires careful reading and switches technique accordingly
   (correct judgment).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to rely on skim/scan for deep-comprehension tasks after the
bridge, give them a task explicitly requiring nuanced understanding and have them first predict, from
a skim alone, what the answer might be — then read carefully and compare, confirming what the skim
missed.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.reading.reading-fluency`):** "Read this short passage aloud (or silently within a
timed window) at a normal, smooth pace." A student who cannot read fluently at a normal pace lacks
the foundation for the accelerated, purposeful reading skimming and scanning require — route to
`eng.reading.reading-fluency` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Telescope-vs-Magnifying-Glass and Building-Outline-from-Across-the-Street Anchor.** A telescope
for seeing the big picture from far away vs. a magnifying glass for zooming in on one tiny detail —
directly seeding the fix for MC-A (skimming gets the general idea; scanning locates one specific
fact — different tools for different purposes). Second, glancing at a building's outline from across
the street versus actually walking through its interior — directly seeding the fix for MC-B
(skimming/scanning give a rough shape, not the detailed understanding that careful reading provides).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Choosing Skim vs. Scan for the Task (MC-A)

Using the telescope-vs-magnifying-glass anchor, teach matching the technique to the purpose. Student
practice: given four reading tasks, state whether each calls for skimming or scanning.

### TA-2 — Executing a Skim (Structural Sampling)

Teach the mechanics of skimming: reading headings, topic sentences, first and last sentences of
paragraphs, without reading every word. Student practice: given a passage, skim it and state its
general idea using only structural cues.

### TA-3 — Executing a Scan (Targeted Search)

Teach the mechanics of scanning: searching for a specific word, number, or name while deliberately
ignoring surrounding text. Student practice: given a passage and a specific fact to locate, scan for
it without reading every word.

### TA-4 — Recognizing When Careful Reading Is Required (MC-B)

Using the building-outline anchor, teach identifying tasks that require full comprehension rather
than skim/scan. Student practice: given a task requiring nuanced understanding, explain why skimming
or scanning alone would be insufficient, then read the passage carefully to answer it.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A news article, fully worked: task 1 ("what is this article about?") answered
correctly via skimming headings and topic sentences; task 2 ("what date is mentioned?") answered
correctly via scanning for the specific date; task 3 ("why does the author believe the policy will
fail, and how does that connect to their earlier point?") explicitly identified as requiring full
careful reading, with the skim/scan attempt shown to miss the connection. Annotation confirms both
the skim-vs-scan technique match (fixing MC-A) and the recognizing-when-careful-reading-is-needed
judgment (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new passage with three new tasks of varying depth. Student is prompted with
guiding questions ("Does this task need the general idea, one specific fact, or full understanding of
how ideas connect? Which technique fits?") to complete each task with the appropriate technique.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new passage with tasks of varying depth, unaided. Student selects and
executes the correct technique (skim, scan, or full careful reading) for each task.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Matching Technique to Task Purpose**
Given four reading tasks, state whether each calls for skimming, scanning, or full careful reading.

**MP-2 [P36] — Executing a Skim**
Given a passage, skim it and state its general idea using only structural cues.

**MP-3 [P36] — Executing a Scan**
Given a passage and a specific fact to locate, scan for it without reading every word.

**MP-4 [P36] — Recognizing a Task That Requires Careful Reading**
Given a nuanced comprehension task, explain why skimming or scanning alone would be insufficient.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Application Across Mixed Tasks**
Given a new passage with tasks of varying depth, select and execute the correct technique for each.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite.
During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1/TA-2/TA-3

s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1/MP-2/MP-3
failures indicate residual MC-A; MP-4 failure indicates residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1
through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided mixed-task example
before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly matches technique to task in isolation (MP-1) but defaults to scanning
  for every task type on a new mixed set (MP-5), provide a mandatory "what is my actual purpose
  here?" question before beginning any rapid-reading task.
- If the student's skim/scan mechanics are strong (MP-2, MP-3) but they still attempt to answer
  nuanced questions from a skim alone (MP-4), require an explicit "does this need connections or
  reasoning, not just facts or gist?" check before finalizing an answer.
- If the student struggles to execute a skim without reading every word, provide a structural
  checklist (title, headings, first sentence of each paragraph) as a scaffolding guide.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.reading.reading-fluency`'s automatic word recognition
  should transfer readily to rapid scanning, since both rely on quick visual processing rather than
  effortful decoding — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
