**Concept:** Following Spoken Instructions — understanding and correctly executing a sequence of
spoken directions, including sequences where the stated order of mention does not match the actual
required order of execution.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Listening for Detail (prerequisite) taught catching
specific pieces of information within spoken language. Following Spoken Instructions extends this by
teaching students to hold an entire sequence of steps in mind before acting, and to track sequence
markers ("first," "before you," "after") that reveal the true execution order — which is not always
the same as the order in which the steps were mentioned. This concept has no further downstream
unlocks in the current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) hold a full multi-step spoken instruction in mind
before beginning to act, rather than acting immediately on the first step heard and losing track of
the rest, and (b) correctly determine the true execution order of steps using sequence markers, even
when steps are not mentioned in their actual required order.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-ACTING-IMMEDIATELY-ON-THE-FIRST-INSTRUCTION-HEARD-IS-THE-RIGHT-APPROACH

**Trigger signal:** Student begins acting on the first spoken instruction the moment it is heard,
rather than listening to the full multi-step sequence first — causing later steps to be forgotten or
missed because attention shifted to executing the first step before the rest of the instruction was
even given.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Give the student a three-step spoken instruction ("Open the drawer,
take out the blue folder, and put it on the desk.") but stop the student partway through to ask what
they are already doing. Ask: "If you started opening the drawer the instant you heard 'open the
drawer,' were you still listening carefully to the rest of the instruction, or did your attention
move to the action?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Acting on the first instruction before hearing the rest is like starting to
dial a phone number after hearing only the first two digits — you're acting on incomplete
information, and you'll likely get it wrong or have to start over. A full instruction needs to be
held in mind, complete, before any part of it is executed."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Listen to the entire instruction before beginning any action. Hold all
the steps in mind first, then execute them in the correct order — don't let hearing the first step
trigger immediate action before you've heard the rest."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A listener who starts opening the drawer the instant "open the drawer" is heard, missing the
   remaining two steps (acting on incomplete information) vs. a listener who waits to hear the full
   three-step instruction before beginning to act (complete information, correct approach).
2. A student who can only recall the first step of a three-step instruction because they started
   acting immediately (misconception) vs. a student who recalls all three steps because they listened
   to the whole instruction first (correct discipline).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to act on partial instructions after the bridge, give them
three multi-step instructions in a row, explicitly instructing them to repeat back the full sequence
before performing any action, to build the habit of complete listening before execution.

### MC-B-INSTRUCTIONS-ARE-ALWAYS-EXECUTED-IN-THE-SAME-ORDER-THEY-ARE-MENTIONED

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes the order in which steps are mentioned in a spoken instruction
always matches the order in which they must be executed, failing to track sequence markers ("before
you," "after," "once you have") that can reorder steps relative to their mention order — for example,
"Before you close the window, turn off the light" requires turning off the light FIRST, despite
"close the window" being mentioned first.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Give the student the instruction "Before you close the window, turn off
the light." Ask: "Which action was mentioned first in the sentence? Which action should actually
happen first?" Confirm that "turn off the light" is mentioned second but must happen first, because
of the word "before."

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming mention order always equals execution order is like assuming the
credits at the end of a movie tell you what happened first in the story — the order something is
presented in doesn't always match the order it actually occurs in. Words like 'before,' 'after,' and
'once you have' are signals that can reverse or reorder steps relative to how they were mentioned —
track these markers, not just the mention order."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't assume steps happen in the order they're mentioned. Listen for
sequence markers like 'before,' 'after,' and 'once you have' — these words tell you the TRUE order of
execution, which can be different from the order the steps were spoken in."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Before you close the window, turn off the light." (light-off must happen first, despite being
   mentioned second, because of "before") vs. "Turn off the light, then close the window." (mention
   order and execution order match here).
2. A student who closes the window first because it was mentioned first (misconception, ignoring the
   "before" marker) vs. a student who identifies "before" and correctly turns off the light first
   (correct sequence-marker tracking).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to default to mention order after the bridge, give them three
instructions using "before," "after," and "once you have" respectively, each reordering steps
relative to mention order, and have them state the correct execution order for each while explicitly
naming the marker that determined it.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.listening.listening-for-detail`):** "Listen to this short spoken passage and
identify one specific detail (a name, a number, a time)." A student who cannot catch specific spoken
details lacks the foundation for tracking multiple steps and their sequence markers — route to
`eng.listening.listening-for-detail` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Incomplete-Phone-Number and Movie-Credits-Order Anchor.** Dialing a phone number after hearing
only the first two digits — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (a full instruction must be held in
mind before any action begins). Second, movie credits presented at the end even though the events
they represent happened throughout the film — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (mention order and
execution order are not always the same; sequence markers reveal the true order).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Listening to the Full Instruction Before Acting (MC-A)

Using the incomplete-phone-number anchor, teach holding a complete multi-step instruction in mind
before beginning any action. Student practice: given a three-step spoken instruction, repeat back
all three steps before performing any of them.

### TA-2 — Recognizing Sequence Markers (MC-B)

Using the movie-credits anchor, teach identifying "before," "after," and "once you have" as markers
that can reorder steps relative to their mention order. Student practice: given an instruction using
one of these markers, identify which step actually happens first.

### TA-3 — Determining True Execution Order

Extend TA-2: given a multi-step instruction mixing markers, write out the full correct execution
order (not the mention order). Student practice: given a four-step instruction with one reordering
marker, list the steps in true execution order.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Listening and Executing a Complete Instruction

Give the student a new multi-step spoken instruction including at least one reordering marker. They
must hold the full instruction in mind (TA-1), identify the marker and determine true execution
order (TA-2/TA-3), and correctly execute (or state) the steps in that order — the full synthesis
task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A four-step instruction ("Before you turn off the computer, save your file.
Then close the browser and shut the lid."), fully worked: full instruction repeated back before
acting; "before" identified as reordering "save your file" ahead of "turn off the computer"; final
correct execution order stated as save file → turn off computer → close browser → shut lid.
Annotation confirms both the complete-listening-before-acting discipline (fixing MC-A) and the
sequence-marker tracking (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new multi-step instruction with a different reordering marker. Student is
prompted with guiding questions ("Have you heard the whole instruction before starting to act? Is
there a word here — 'before,' 'after,' 'once you have' — that changes the order from how the steps
were mentioned?") to determine the correct execution order.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new multi-step instruction, unaided. Student holds the full instruction in
mind, identifies any reordering markers, and states or executes the steps in true order.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Repeating Back a Full Instruction Before Acting**
Given a three-step spoken instruction, repeat back all three steps before indicating any action.

**MP-2 [P36] — Identifying a Reordering Marker**
Given an instruction using "before," "after," or "once you have," identify which step actually
happens first.

**MP-3 [P36] — Writing the True Execution Order**
Given a four-step instruction with one reordering marker, list the steps in true execution order.

**MP-4 [P36] — Detecting a Mention-Order Error**
Given an incorrect execution sequence based on mention order alone, identify the error and state the
correct order.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Instruction Comprehension and Execution**
Given a new multi-step instruction including at least one reordering marker, state the complete
correct execution order.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1; a gap routes back to the prerequisite.
During Component 4 (S1, active instruction), MC-A typically surfaces during TA-1 (acting before the
instruction finishes) and MC-B during TA-2/TA-3 (defaulting to mention order) — these are largely
independent and often need separate, explicit correction. If either fires, branch to the relevant
s6_path drill before resuming. At the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure
indicates residual MC-A; MP-2/MP-3/MP-4 failures indicate residual MC-B; MP-5 failure with MP-1
through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide one more guided full-instruction example
before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly repeats back instructions on short two-step sequences (MP-1) but loses
  track on longer four-step sequences (MP-5), provide a mandatory pause-and-repeat-back checkpoint
  after every instruction before allowing execution.
- If the student correctly identifies markers in isolation (MP-2) but reverts to mention order when
  determining a full sequence (MP-3), require writing out each step on a separate line in mention
  order first, then explicitly renumbering based on markers.
- If the student struggles to notice sequence markers at all, provide a reference list of common
  reordering words ("before," "after," "once you have," "prior to") as a listening aid.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.listening.listening-for-detail`'s specific-detail
  catching should transfer readily to marker detection, since both require attending to small but
  meaning-changing words within a longer spoken passage — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
