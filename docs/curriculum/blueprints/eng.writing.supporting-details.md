## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-ANY-TRUE-STATEMENT-ABOUT-THE-TOPIC-COUNTS-AS-A-SUPPORTING-DETAIL
- **Trigger signal:** Student includes any factually true statement related to the general topic as a "supporting detail," without checking whether that detail actually supports the SPECIFIC claim made in the topic sentence — confusing topical relevance (true and about the same subject) with genuine support (directly develops or proves the specific claim).
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present the topic sentence "Golden retrievers make excellent family pets because of their gentle temperament." alongside two candidate details: "Golden retrievers were originally bred in Scotland for retrieving game." (true, about golden retrievers, but doesn't support the temperament/family-pet claim) versus "Golden retrievers are known for patiently tolerating rough play from young children." (directly supports the specific claim). Ask which detail actually develops the topic sentence's specific claim.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Being true and about the same topic isn't enough — a genuine supporting detail must directly develop or prove the SPECIFIC claim your topic sentence made, not just any fact about the general subject. A historical fact about dog breeding doesn't support a claim about temperament, even though it's true and about the same dog breed."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Before including a detail, check it against the topic sentence's specific claim, not just its general subject: does this detail directly develop or provide evidence for what the topic sentence actually asserted? If it's just a true fact about the topic that doesn't connect to the specific claim, leave it out."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Topical-truth sufficiency (any true statement about the general subject counts as support) vs. (b) Claim-specific support requirement (a genuine supporting detail must directly develop or evidence the topic sentence's specific claim, not merely share its general subject).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1 re-check, then re-run TA-1's claim-match test before continuing.

### MC-MORE-SUPPORTING-DETAILS-ALWAYS-MAKES-A-PARAGRAPH-STRONGER
- **Trigger signal:** Student believes piling on as many supporting details as possible automatically strengthens a paragraph, without considering that too many similar or repetitive details can dilute focus, that some details are stronger evidence than others, or that a smaller number of well-chosen, well-developed details often makes a more convincing paragraph than a large number of thin, underdeveloped ones.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present two paragraphs supporting the same topic sentence: one with 6 brief, underdeveloped details listed with minimal elaboration, and one with 3 details each explained with an example or explanation of why it matters. Ask which paragraph is actually more convincing and easier to follow.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Quantity isn't the goal — quality and development are. A paragraph with 3 well-explained, well-chosen details is usually stronger than one with 6 thin, underdeveloped ones. Each detail needs enough elaboration to actually do its job; cramming in more details often means each one gets less development, weakening the paragraph overall."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Instead of maximizing the number of details, choose your strongest 2-4 details and develop each one with enough explanation or example to make it convincing. A focused paragraph with well-developed details beats a crowded paragraph with thin ones."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Quantity-as-strength (more supporting details always makes a paragraph stronger) vs. (b) Quality-and-development-as-strength (a smaller number of well-chosen, well-developed details is usually more convincing than many underdeveloped ones).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1, then re-present the Concrete Anchor's fewer-strong-pillars framing before continuing to TA-3.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

### PD-1: Topic-Sentence Readiness
- **Tests:** Can the student already write a specific, focused topic sentence (a genuine claim, not a vague announcement) — the exact target supporting details must be checked against for genuine relevance?
- **Probe:** Ask the student to identify whether a given sentence is a specific claim or a vague announcement.
- **Pass condition:** Correctly identifies the difference.
- **Fail routing:** If topic-sentence judgment is weak, route to `eng.writing.topic-sentences` review — supporting-detail selection depends on having a specific claim to check details against.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**"The Three Strong Pillars, Not Six Weak Sticks."** Present a physical model: a roof supported by 3 sturdy, well-built pillars versus the same roof "supported" by 6 thin, flimsy sticks. Demonstrate that the 3 pillars actually hold the roof up firmly, while the 6 sticks, despite being more numerous, wobble and barely support anything — directly seeding MC-MORE-SUPPORTING-DETAILS-ALWAYS-MAKES-A-PARAGRAPH-STRONGER's fix (quality/development over raw quantity). Separately, present a topic-sentence "roof label" (e.g., "This roof is for a temperature-controlled greenhouse") and have the student check whether a candidate pillar ("this pillar is made of true, sturdy wood") genuinely relates to and supports THAT specific roof, or whether it's just generically "wood-related" without actually connecting to the greenhouse-temperature claim — seeding MC-ANY-TRUE-STATEMENT-ABOUT-THE-TOPIC-COUNTS-AS-A-SUPPORTING-DETAIL's fix.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1: The Claim-Match Test
Present a topic sentence and a batch of 6 candidate details, some genuinely supporting the specific claim and some merely topically-true but unconnected, and have the student sort them using the claim-match test. Directly counters MC-ANY-TRUE-STATEMENT-ABOUT-THE-TOPIC-COUNTS-AS-A-SUPPORTING-DETAIL.

### TA-2: Elaborating a Detail with Explanation or Example
Teach the student to develop a bare supporting detail with an explanation of why it matters or a specific example illustrating it, rather than leaving it as an underdeveloped one-liner.

### TA-3: Choosing Strongest Details Over Maximum Quantity
Using the discrimination pairs from MC-MORE-SUPPORTING-DETAILS-ALWAYS-MAKES-A-PARAGRAPH-STRONGER, present a set of 6 candidate details (varying in strength/relevance) and have the student select and develop only the 3 strongest rather than including all 6 thinly.

### TA-4: Ordering Supporting Details Effectively
Teach basic ordering strategies (strongest detail first or last for emphasis, logical/chronological order when relevant) and have the student arrange a set of details in an effective order.

### TA-5: Composing a Fully Developed Paragraph
Student writes an original paragraph: a specific topic sentence, 3 well-chosen and well-developed supporting details (each with explanation or example), in an effective order — the generative capstone integrating all prior skills.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

### WE-1: Claim-Match Sorting (Scaffolded)
**Topic sentence:** "Reading before bed helps children fall asleep more easily."
**Candidates:** "Reading involves recognizing letters and words." (true about reading, doesn't support the sleep claim) / "The calm, quiet activity of reading helps slow a child's mind down before sleep." (directly supports the claim).
**Walkthrough:** Confirm the second detail passes the claim-match test; the first, while true, doesn't develop the specific sleep-related claim.

### WE-2: Elaborating a Bare Detail (Guided)
**Bare detail:** "Reading is calming."
**Walkthrough:** Develop with explanation: "Reading is calming because it shifts a child's focus away from the stimulation of screens and toward a slower, more relaxed pace of attention, which naturally prepares the body for rest."

### WE-3: Quality-Over-Quantity Selection (Independent)
**Task:** Given 6 candidate details for a paragraph about exercise benefits (some strong and specific, some vague/repetitive), student selects the 3 strongest, develops each with an example, and explains why the other 3 were excluded (redundant, too vague, or weakly connected to the specific claim).
**Walkthrough:** Confirm the resulting 3-detail paragraph is more convincing and focused than a 6-detail version would have been.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Reject a topically-true but unconnected detail**
Given a topic sentence and a detail that is true about the topic but doesn't support the specific claim, student correctly rejects it and explains why. *(Directly targets MC-ANY-TRUE-STATEMENT-ABOUT-THE-TOPIC-COUNTS-AS-A-SUPPORTING-DETAIL.)*

**MP-2 [P36] — Choose fewer, stronger details over more, weaker ones**
Given 6 candidate details of varying strength, student selects and develops the 3 strongest rather than including all 6 thinly. *(Directly targets MC-MORE-SUPPORTING-DETAILS-ALWAYS-MAKES-A-PARAGRAPH-STRONGER.)*

**MP-3 [P36] — Elaborate a bare detail**
Given an underdeveloped one-line detail, student expands it with a specific explanation or example.

**MP-4 [P36] — Order details effectively**
Given 3 supporting details of varying strength, student arranges them in an effective order with justification.

**MP-5 [P36] — Compose a full developed paragraph**
Student writes an original paragraph with a specific topic sentence and 3 well-chosen, well-developed, effectively-ordered supporting details.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** At session open (S0), confirm PD-1 passes — supporting-detail selection is entirely checked against the topic sentence's specific claim, so a student who cannot distinguish a claim from a vague announcement will not know what a detail must support. During instruction (S1), watch for both misconception patterns: accepting topically-true-but-unconnected details (MC-ANY-TRUE-STATEMENT-ABOUT-THE-TOPIC-COUNTS-AS-A-SUPPORTING-DETAIL) and maximizing quantity over quality (MC-MORE-SUPPORTING-DETAILS-ALWAYS-MAKES-A-PARAGRAPH-STRONGER) both substitute a surface heuristic for genuine relevance/strength judgment — redirect both to the Three Strong Pillars anchor. If the student includes off-claim details or piles on thin details during TA-1/TA-3 (S6), drop back to the Concrete Anchor's pillar-demonstration before returning to composition. At mastery-probe stage (S9), do not certify mastery unless MP-1 and MP-2 both pass — a student who elaborates and orders details well (MP-3/MP-4) but still includes off-claim or excessive thin details has not learned the core selection judgment this concept requires.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student includes topically-true but unconnected details, weight remediation toward MC-ANY-TRUE-STATEMENT-ABOUT-THE-TOPIC-COUNTS-AS-A-SUPPORTING-DETAIL's bridge text and re-run TA-1's claim-match test before returning to composition.
- If the student piles on many thin details instead of fewer developed ones, weight remediation toward MC-MORE-SUPPORTING-DETAILS-ALWAYS-MAKES-A-PARAGRAPH-STRONGER's bridge text and TA-3's quality-selection practice specifically.
- This concept feeds one direct downstream unlock (`eng.writing.transitions-and-cohesion`) — do not certify mastery without TA-5's fully developed paragraph composition, since transitions/cohesion work assumes the student already has well-selected, well-ordered details needing smooth connective language between them.
- Reuse the "three strong pillars" framing explicitly when introducing `eng.writing.transitions-and-cohesion` — that concept teaches the connective "mortar" between the pillars established here.
