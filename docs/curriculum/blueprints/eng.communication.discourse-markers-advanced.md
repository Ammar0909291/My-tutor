**Concept:** Advanced Discourse Markers — sophisticated cohesive devices that signal logical
relationships between ideas across extended spoken and written discourse, chosen to match the actual
logical relationship intended and used only where they fit naturally, not layered in merely for the
appearance of sophistication.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Discourse Analysis Intro (prerequisite) taught
recognizing how ideas connect across an extended text or conversation. Advanced Discourse Markers
extends this by teaching the specific vocabulary of cohesive devices ("however," "consequently,"
"notwithstanding," "furthermore") and the discipline of selecting the marker that actually matches
the logical relationship intended, rather than treating markers as interchangeable or reaching for
the most sophisticated-sounding option regardless of fit. This concept has no further downstream
unlocks in the current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) select a discourse marker that accurately matches
the actual logical relationship between ideas (contrast, cause, addition) rather than treating
markers as interchangeable connectors, and (b) use discourse markers naturally and only where they
genuinely fit, rather than inserting sophisticated-sounding markers regardless of whether they
actually apply.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-DISCOURSE-MARKERS-ARE-INTERCHANGEABLE-CONNECTORS-REGARDLESS-OF-THE-ACTUAL-LOGICAL-RELATIONSHIP

**Trigger signal:** Student treats discourse markers as interchangeable transition words, using
"however" where the relationship between ideas is actually additive (not contrastive), or
"therefore" where there is no genuine causal link between the two ideas — picking any marker that
sounds like a transition rather than the one that accurately signals the actual logical relationship.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student the sentence "The report was thorough. However, it
included a detailed budget breakdown." Ask: "Does the second sentence actually contrast with the
first, or does it add to it? Is 'however' the right word here, or does it falsely signal a contrast
that isn't there?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Treating discourse markers as interchangeable is like using a stop sign and a
yield sign at the same intersection depending on which one you feel like putting up — both are
traffic signs, but they signal genuinely different instructions, and using the wrong one gives
drivers false information. A discourse marker signals a SPECIFIC logical relationship (contrast,
cause, addition); using the wrong one misleads the reader about how the ideas actually connect."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before choosing a discourse marker, identify the actual logical
relationship between the two ideas: is one contrasting the other, causing the other, or simply
adding to it? Choose the marker that accurately signals that specific relationship, not just any
transition word."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "The report was thorough. However, it included a detailed budget breakdown." (a false contrast —
   the second idea actually adds to the first, so "however" is the wrong marker) vs. "The report was
   thorough. Furthermore, it included a detailed budget breakdown." (correctly signals addition).
2. A student who uses "therefore" between two ideas with no genuine causal link (misconception —
   marker doesn't match the actual relationship) vs. a student who checks whether a genuine causal
   link exists before using "therefore" (correct discipline).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to use markers indiscriminately after the bridge, give them
five sentence pairs with a discourse marker inserted, some correctly matching the actual relationship
and some not, and have them identify and correct the mismatches.

### MC-B-MORE-SOPHISTICATED-SOUNDING-DISCOURSE-MARKERS-ARE-ALWAYS-BETTER-REGARDLESS-OF-NATURAL-FIT

**Trigger signal:** Student assumes that more elaborate, academic-sounding discourse markers
("notwithstanding," "furthermore," "consequently") are inherently superior to simpler ones
regardless of whether they fit naturally in context, leading to overstuffed, unnatural-sounding
writing or speech that sacrifices clarity for the mere appearance of sophistication.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two versions of the same short paragraph: one using
simple, clear connectors appropriately ("but," "so," "also") and one replacing every connector with
an elaborate alternative ("notwithstanding," "consequently," "furthermore") regardless of whether
each fits the sentence's actual register and flow. Ask: "Does the second version communicate more
clearly, or does it sound forced and harder to follow? Does using a more elaborate word automatically
make writing better?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Assuming elaborate discourse markers are always better is like assuming
wearing a tuxedo to a casual backyard barbecue makes you more impressive — instead, it just looks out
of place and draws attention away from what you're actually saying. A discourse marker's job is to
make the logical connection between ideas clear; an elaborate marker that doesn't fit the context
draws attention to itself instead of clarifying the relationship."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't assume a more elaborate-sounding discourse marker is automatically
better. Choose the marker — simple or sophisticated — that most naturally and clearly signals the
actual relationship in this specific context. Clarity matters more than the appearance of
sophistication."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. A paragraph where every connector is replaced with an elaborate alternative regardless of fit
   (forced, harder to follow) vs. the same paragraph using simple, natural connectors that clearly
   signal each relationship (clear, appropriately fitted).
2. A student who reflexively swaps "but" for "notwithstanding" without checking whether it fits the
   sentence's register and flow (misconception) vs. a student who selects a marker based on clarity
   and natural fit, whether simple or elaborate (correct discipline).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to over-elaborate markers after the bridge, have them take an
over-elaborated paragraph and revise it by replacing each marker with the simplest word that still
accurately signals the actual relationship, then compare clarity between the two versions.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.linguistics.discourse-analysis-intro`):** "Identify how these two sentences are
logically connected (contrast, cause, addition)." A student who cannot recognize basic logical
connections between ideas lacks the foundation for selecting an accurate discourse marker — route to
`eng.linguistics.discourse-analysis-intro` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Stop-Sign-vs-Yield-Sign and Tuxedo-at-a-Backyard-Barbecue Anchor.** A stop sign and a yield
sign used interchangeably at the same intersection despite signaling genuinely different
instructions — directly seeding the fix for MC-A (a discourse marker must accurately signal the
specific logical relationship, not just any transition). Second, wearing a tuxedo to a casual
backyard barbecue, drawing attention to itself rather than fitting the context — directly seeding the
fix for MC-B (an elaborate marker that doesn't naturally fit draws attention away from clarity, not
toward sophistication).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Identifying the Actual Logical Relationship (MC-A)

Using the stop-sign-vs-yield-sign anchor, teach identifying whether two ideas are related by
contrast, cause, or addition before selecting a marker. Student practice: given five sentence pairs,
identify the actual logical relationship in each.

### TA-2 — Matching the Marker to the Relationship (MC-A)

Extend TA-1: teach selecting the discourse marker that accurately signals the identified
relationship. Student practice: given sentence pairs with a mismatched marker, identify and correct
the mismatch.

### TA-3 — Choosing Natural Fit Over Elaborate Sound (MC-B)

Using the tuxedo-at-a-barbecue anchor, teach selecting markers for clarity and natural fit rather
than elaborate sound. Student practice: given an over-elaborated paragraph, revise it by replacing
each marker with the simplest word that still accurately signals the relationship.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Using Discourse Markers Across Extended Discourse

Give the student a new multi-paragraph passage with several logical connections needing markers.
They must identify each actual relationship (TA-1), select an accurately matching marker (TA-2), and
ensure natural fit rather than forced elaboration (TA-3) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A short passage with three connection points, fully worked: each relationship
identified (one contrast, one cause, one addition); an appropriately fitting marker selected for each
("but" for the genuine contrast, "so" for the genuine cause, "also" for the genuine addition); one
originally-elaborate substitution ("notwithstanding") checked and rejected as unnatural for this
context, replaced with a simpler, clearer alternative. Annotation confirms both the relationship-
matching principle (fixing MC-A) and the natural-fit-over-elaboration principle (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new passage with different connection points. Student is prompted with guiding
questions ("Is this relationship actually a contrast, a cause, or an addition? Does this marker
naturally fit here, or does it sound forced and out of place?") to complete the marker selection.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new multi-paragraph passage, unaided. Student identifies each relationship
and selects naturally-fitting, accurately-matching discourse markers throughout.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying the Actual Logical Relationship**
Given five sentence pairs, identify the actual logical relationship (contrast, cause, addition) in
each.

**MP-2 [P36] — Correcting a Mismatched Discourse Marker**
Given a sentence pair with a mismatched marker, identify and correct the mismatch.

**MP-3 [P36] — Revising an Over-Elaborated Passage**
Given an over-elaborated paragraph, replace each marker with the simplest word that still accurately
signals the relationship.

**MP-4 [P36] — Explaining Why an Elaborate Marker Fails**
Given a sentence with an elaborate marker that doesn't fit naturally, explain why it draws attention
away from clarity.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Discourse Marker Application**
Given a new multi-paragraph passage, identify each logical relationship and select naturally-fitting,
accurately-matching discourse markers throughout.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly identifies relationships on provided sentence pairs (MP-1) but selects
  mismatched markers when writing an original multi-paragraph passage (MP-5), provide a mandatory
  "what is the ACTUAL relationship here?" check before choosing any marker.
- If the student's relationship-matching is strong (MP-1, MP-2) but they still over-elaborate markers
  in original writing (MP-5), require an explicit "does this sound natural here, or forced?" read-
  aloud check before finalizing.
- If the student struggles to distinguish contrast, cause, and addition at all, provide a reference
  list of common markers sorted by relationship type as a scaffolding aid.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.linguistics.discourse-analysis-intro`'s connection-
  recognition work should transfer readily to marker selection, since both require identifying how
  ideas actually relate before labeling that relationship — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
