**Concept:** Foreshadowing and Suspense — narrative techniques that hint at future events (usually
recognized only in retrospect) and generate reader anticipation or tension, distinct from explicit
spoilers and distinct from dramatic irony's information structure.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Plot Structure (prerequisite) taught recognizing a
story's overall shape (exposition, rising action, climax, resolution). Foreshadowing and Suspense
extends this by teaching two specific techniques writers use to shape a reader's experience of that
plot over time: subtle hints planted early that only become meaningful once later events occur
(foreshadowing), and the tension created specifically by the reader NOT knowing what happens next
(suspense) — as distinct from dramatic irony's opposite information structure. This concept has no
further downstream unlocks in the current curriculum.

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) distinguish foreshadowing (a subtle hint that gains
significance only in retrospect) from an explicit flash-forward or spoiler (a direct, unambiguous
statement of a future event), and (b) distinguish suspense (tension from not knowing what will
happen) from dramatic irony (tension from knowing something a character does not), recognizing they
operate through opposite information structures.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-ANY-EARLY-MENTION-OF-A-LATER-EVENT-COUNTS-AS-FORESHADOWING

**Trigger signal:** Student treats any early reference to a later plot event — including a direct,
explicit, unambiguous statement of what will happen — as "foreshadowing," failing to recognize that
foreshadowing works specifically through subtle, often ambiguous hints that only become clearly
significant in retrospect, once the later event has actually occurred.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two early passages from different stories: one stating
directly, "Little did she know, this would be the last time she saw her hometown" (an explicit
flash-forward, unambiguous), and one describing a character pausing oddly at a doorway with no
explanation given at the time (a subtle detail that only makes sense once, much later, the reader
learns the doorway leads somewhere significant). Ask: "Which passage tells you directly and clearly
what's going to happen? Which one only makes sense in hindsight, after you've read further?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Calling any early mention of a later event 'foreshadowing' is like calling
any road sign a 'coded message' — a road sign that says 'Bridge Ahead' directly and clearly tells you
what's coming; a coded message only reveals its meaning once you have the key. Foreshadowing works
like the coded message: it's a subtle detail whose significance only becomes clear in retrospect, not
a direct, explicit statement of what will happen."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't call every early reference to a later event 'foreshadowing.' Ask:
is this a direct, clear statement of what will happen (a flash-forward or spoiler), or a subtle
detail that only reveals its significance once you've read further and the later event has actually
occurred? Only the second is true foreshadowing."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Little did she know, this would be the last time she saw her hometown." (an explicit
   flash-forward, unambiguous, spells out the future directly) vs. a character pausing oddly at a
   doorway with no explanation, which only makes sense once the doorway's significance is revealed
   much later (genuine foreshadowing — subtle, retrospective).
2. A student who labels an author's direct statement "years later, war would break out" as
   foreshadowing (misconception — this is an explicit flash-forward) vs. a student who identifies a
   character's unexplained unease near a specific object as foreshadowing once its later significance
   is revealed (correct recognition).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to conflate explicit flash-forwards with foreshadowing after
the bridge, give them three passages — one explicit flash-forward, one genuine subtle foreshadowing,
and one passage with no forward-looking content — and have them sort all three correctly.

### MC-B-SUSPENSE-AND-DRAMATIC-IRONY-ARE-THE-SAME-TECHNIQUE-BOTH-CREATE-TENSION-BY-TELLING-THE-READER-SOMETHING

**Trigger signal:** Student conflates suspense (tension generated by the reader NOT knowing what
will happen next) with dramatic irony (tension generated by the reader KNOWING something a character
does not), treating both as the same technique because both create tension, rather than recognizing
they operate through opposite information structures — one withholds information from the reader, the
other gives the reader information the character lacks.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present two scenarios: in one, neither the reader nor the character
knows whether a rescue will arrive in time (suspense — information withheld from both); in the
other, the reader has already seen the villain hiding in the closet, but the character walking toward
that closet has no idea (dramatic irony — the reader has information the character lacks). Ask: "In
which scenario does the reader know something the character doesn't? In which scenario is the reader
just as uncertain as the character?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Treating suspense and dramatic irony as the same technique is like confusing
the tension of not knowing if your team will win a game you're watching live with the tension of
watching a recording where you already know the villain is about to jump out, but the character on
screen doesn't. Both are tense, but the information you have is completely opposite in each case —
suspense withholds information from the reader; dramatic irony gives the reader information the
character lacks."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Don't treat suspense and dramatic irony as the same thing just because
both create tension. Ask: does the reader know something the character doesn't (dramatic irony), or
is the reader just as uncertain about what happens next as the character is (suspense)? The
information structure is the opposite in each case."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Neither the reader nor the character knows whether the rescue will arrive in time (suspense —
   shared uncertainty) vs. the reader has already seen the villain hiding, but the character does not
   know (dramatic irony — reader has information the character lacks).
2. A student who calls a scene "suspenseful" when the reader actually already knows the outcome the
   character doesn't (misconception — this is dramatic irony, not suspense) vs. a student who
   correctly identifies that both the reader and character share the same uncertainty in a genuinely
   suspenseful scene (correct distinction).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to conflate suspense with dramatic irony after the bridge, give
them three tense scenes and have them determine, for each, whether the reader knows something the
character does not (dramatic irony) or whether the reader is equally uncertain (suspense).

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.literature.plot-structure`):** "Identify the climax of this short plot summary
and explain how you know." A student who cannot recognize a story's basic plot shape lacks the
foundation for recognizing how foreshadowing and suspense function across that shape over time —
route to `eng.literature.plot-structure` review first.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Coded-Message-vs-Road-Sign and Live-Game-vs-Rewatched-Recording Anchor.** A road sign that
directly says "Bridge Ahead" vs. a coded message that only reveals its meaning once you have the key
— directly seeding the fix for MC-A (foreshadowing is subtle and retrospective, not a direct,
explicit statement). Second, watching a game live (not knowing the outcome, like the players) vs.
rewatching a recording where you already know the villain is about to appear but the character
doesn't — directly seeding the fix for MC-B (suspense withholds information from the reader;
dramatic irony gives the reader information the character lacks).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Foreshadowing vs. Explicit Flash-Forward (MC-A)

Using the coded-message-vs-road-sign anchor, teach that foreshadowing is subtle and only makes sense
in retrospect, unlike a direct flash-forward. Student practice: given two passages, identify which is
an explicit flash-forward and which is genuine subtle foreshadowing.

### TA-2 — Suspense vs. Dramatic Irony (MC-B)

Using the live-game-vs-recording anchor, teach identifying whether the reader shares the character's
uncertainty (suspense) or has information the character lacks (dramatic irony). Student practice:
given two tense scenes, classify each as suspense or dramatic irony.

### TA-3 — Identifying Foreshadowing's Retrospective Payoff

Extend TA-1: given a full short story, identify an early subtle detail and the later event that
reveals its significance, explaining how the detail only became meaningful in retrospect.

### TA-4 — Full Application: Analyzing Foreshadowing and Suspense in a New Text

Give the student a new short story excerpt. They must identify any genuine foreshadowing (excluding
explicit flash-forwards) (TA-1/TA-3), and classify any tense moments as suspense or dramatic irony
(TA-2) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** A short mystery excerpt, fully worked: an early detail (a character
nervously checking their watch, unexplained) identified as genuine foreshadowing once its
significance (a hidden deadline) is revealed later; a tense scene where neither the reader nor the
character knows if the deadline will be met, classified as suspense (shared uncertainty), contrasted
with a separate tense scene where the reader already knows a trap is set but the character doesn't,
classified as dramatic irony. Annotation confirms both the foreshadowing-vs-flash-forward distinction
(fixing MC-A) and the suspense-vs-dramatic-irony distinction (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** A new short excerpt. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("Does this early
detail tell you directly what will happen, or does it only make sense once you've read further? In
this tense moment, does the reader know something the character doesn't, or is the reader just as
uncertain?") to complete the analysis.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new short excerpt, unaided. Student identifies genuine foreshadowing and
classifies tense moments as suspense or dramatic irony.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Distinguishing Foreshadowing from an Explicit Flash-Forward**
Given two passages, identify which is an explicit flash-forward and which is genuine subtle
foreshadowing.

**MP-2 [P36] — Classifying Suspense vs. Dramatic Irony**
Given two tense scenes, classify each as suspense (shared uncertainty) or dramatic irony (reader
knows more than the character).

**MP-3 [P36] — Identifying a Foreshadowing Detail's Retrospective Payoff**
Given a full short story, identify an early subtle detail and the later event that reveals its
significance.

**MP-4 [P36] — Explaining the Information Structure of a Tense Scene**
Given a tense scene, explain specifically what the reader knows relative to what the character
knows, and classify the scene accordingly.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Analysis of Foreshadowing and Suspense in a New Text**
Given a new short story excerpt, identify any genuine foreshadowing and classify any tense moments
as suspense or dramatic irony.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student correctly sorts explicit flash-forwards from foreshadowing on provided pairs (MP-1)
  but misidentifies a subtle detail's foreshadowing role in a new full story (MP-3), provide a
  mandatory "does this make sense NOW, or only once I know what happens later?" check for every
  candidate detail.
- If the student's suspense/dramatic-irony classification is strong on isolated scenes (MP-2) but
  falters in a longer new text with multiple tense moments (MP-5), require an explicit
  "what does the reader know here, compared to the character?" statement for every tense moment
  before classifying it.
- If the student struggles to notice subtle foreshadowing details at all, flag the specific detail
  explicitly before asking them to explain its later significance.
- Students with strong performance in `eng.literature.plot-structure`'s climax-identification work
  should transfer readily to recognizing suspense's buildup toward that climax, since both require
  tracking how tension develops across a story's shape — draw this parallel explicitly if useful.
