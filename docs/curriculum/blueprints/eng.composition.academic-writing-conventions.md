**Concept:** Academic Writing Conventions — the formal conventions of academic prose, including
objectivity (minimizing unsupported personal opinion), hedging (qualifying claims appropriately
rather than overstating certainty), and disciplinary style expectations.

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Research Paper Writing (prerequisite) teaches
synthesizing multiple sources into an original argument. Academic Writing Conventions teaches the
specific FORMAL REGISTER that argument should be expressed in within academic contexts — objective
framing, careful hedging of claims based on the strength of evidence, and avoiding casual or
overconfident language. This concept unlocks `eng.composition.plagiarism-and-citation-ethics`
(understanding proper academic voice is part of understanding what constitutes appropriately
acknowledged versus misappropriated work), `eng.composition.editing-for-style` (revising toward a
consistent register requires knowing what that register demands), and
`eng.communication.academic-writing-advanced` (more sophisticated academic conventions build on
these foundational ones).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) identify overly casual, overly personal, or
overconfident language in a piece of writing and revise it toward objective, appropriately hedged
academic prose, (b) select hedging language (e.g., "suggests," "may indicate," "the evidence points
to") that accurately reflects the actual strength of the evidence behind a claim, rather than either
overstating certainty or hedging so much the claim becomes meaningless, and (c) explain why academic
objectivity does not mean having no argument or opinion, but rather grounding that argument
transparently in evidence rather than personal assertion.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-ACADEMIC-OBJECTIVITY-MEANS-HAVING-NO-OPINION-OR-ARGUMENT-AT-ALL

**Trigger signal:** Student believes "being objective" in academic writing means avoiding taking any
clear position or making any argument at all, producing flat, purely descriptive writing with no
actual claim or thesis — e.g., writing "There are many different views on climate policy" instead of
taking and defending a specific, evidence-based position.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two academic-style paragraphs on the same topic: one
that avoids any claim entirely ("There are different perspectives on renewable energy policy"), and
one that takes a clear, evidence-grounded position using appropriately objective, hedged language
("The evidence suggests that subsidy-based renewable energy policies produce faster short-term
adoption than carbon-tax-based approaches, though long-term effects remain less certain"). Ask:
"Which one is actually making an academic ARGUMENT, and which one is just avoiding having a
position?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Academic objectivity doesn't mean having no opinion — it means having an
opinion that is grounded in evidence and expressed with appropriate confidence, rather than personal
assertion or overstatement. A judge in a courtroom reaches a clear verdict — they don't refuse to
decide just to seem 'neutral' — but they base that verdict transparently on the evidence presented,
not personal feelings. Academic writing works the same way: take a position, but ground it in
evidence and express it with appropriate certainty."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Academic objectivity means: (1) having a clear, specific claim, (2)
grounding that claim in evidence rather than personal assertion, and (3) expressing your confidence
in the claim honestly, based on how strong the evidence actually is. It does NOT mean avoiding having
a position."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "There are many views on this topic." (no claim at all, avoids taking a position) vs. "The
   evidence suggests that X is more effective than Y for this specific population, though further
   research on long-term effects is needed." (a clear, evidence-grounded, appropriately hedged
   claim).
2. A student who believes academic writing forbids arguing for any position (over-correction into
   flatness) vs. a student who understands academic writing requires a clear position, expressed
   with evidence-appropriate confidence, not personal certainty (correct understanding).

**s6_path:** If the student continues avoiding any clear claim after the bridge, have them identify
the actual thesis buried in their overly-neutral draft (there is usually an implicit position even in
"balanced" writing) and make it explicit, then add appropriate hedging language to match its evidence
strength rather than removing the claim altogether.

### MC-B-HEDGING-LANGUAGE-MEANS-BEING-VAGUE-OR-WEAK-ABOUT-EVERYTHING

**Trigger signal:** Student either omits hedging language entirely (stating every claim with absolute
certainty regardless of evidence strength) or, once taught to hedge, over-hedges every single
statement uniformly regardless of how strong the underlying evidence actually is — treating hedging
as a blanket softening habit rather than a precise tool for matching language to actual evidence
strength.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student three claims with genuinely different evidence
strength behind them (a well-replicated scientific finding, a single preliminary study, and a
widely-debated theoretical claim), each written with IDENTICAL uniform hedging ("It may possibly be
the case that X could potentially occur"). Ask: "Does this uniform hedging accurately represent that
these three claims have very different amounts of supporting evidence?" Then show the same three
claims with hedging calibrated to their actual evidence strength (a confident statement for the
well-replicated finding, moderate hedging for the preliminary study, strong hedging for the debated
theory).

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Hedging language is like a volume dial, not a mute button — it should be set
to match how loud (confident) the evidence actually allows you to be, not turned down uniformly on
every single statement regardless of how strong the evidence is. A well-established, heavily-
replicated finding deserves confident language; a single preliminary study deserves more caution;
these should sound different, not identically vague."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before hedging a claim, ask: how strong is the actual evidence behind
this specific statement? Match your hedging language precisely — confident language ('demonstrates,'
'establishes') for strong, well-supported evidence; moderate hedging ('suggests,' 'indicates') for
reasonably solid but less conclusive evidence; strong hedging ('may,' 'a preliminary study found')
for weak or contested evidence. Uniform hedging on everything hides real differences in evidence
strength from your reader."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. Identical hedging phrases used for both a well-replicated finding and a single preliminary study
   (uniform, evidence-blind hedging) vs. confident language for the well-replicated finding and
   cautious language for the preliminary study (calibrated, evidence-sensitive hedging).
2. A claim stated with total certainty despite weak, contested evidence (no hedging where hedging is
   needed) vs. the same claim hedged appropriately to reflect its actual uncertainty (correctly
   calibrated).

**s6_path:** If the student continues to hedge uniformly (or not at all) after the bridge, have them
rank three source claims by actual evidence strength first (strongest to weakest, based on the type
and amount of supporting evidence), and only then assign hedging language to each — forcing the
evidence assessment to happen before the language choice, not simultaneously or ignored.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.composition.research-paper-writing`):** "Synthesize these two source excerpts
into one sentence supporting an original claim." A student who cannot perform basic source synthesis
lacks the foundation this concept builds on — route to `eng.composition.research-paper-writing`
review first.

**PD-2 (checks basic register awareness, informal pre-check):** "Rewrite this casual sentence
('I think homework is basically pointless') in a more formal, academic style." A student who cannot
attempt any register shift needs a brief primer before Component 3.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Judge's-Verdict-and-Volume-Dial Anchor.** Combine two images: a judge delivering a clear
verdict while explicitly citing the specific evidence and its weight (not refusing to decide, and not
overstating certainty beyond what the evidence supports) — seeding the fix for MC-A, since the judge
takes a position without abandoning objectivity. Second, a volume dial that can be set to different
levels rather than a mute button that's either fully on or fully off — seeding the fix for MC-B,
since hedging should be calibrated precisely to evidence strength, not uniformly applied or uniformly
absent.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Objectivity as Evidence-Grounded Argument, Not No Argument (MC-A)

Using the judge's-verdict framing, teach that academic objectivity requires a clear claim grounded in
evidence, not the absence of a claim. Student practice: given three "balanced" but claim-less
passages, identify the implicit position and rewrite each with an explicit, evidence-grounded thesis.

### TA-2 — Recognizing Evidence Strength

Teach students to assess how strong a piece of evidence actually is (well-replicated vs. single-study
vs. contested/theoretical) as a distinct, prior step to choosing hedging language. Student practice:
given four source claims, rank them by evidence strength.

### TA-3 — Calibrating Hedging Language to Evidence Strength (MC-B)

Using the volume-dial anchor, teach a graduated set of hedging phrases matched to evidence strength
levels. Student practice: given the four ranked claims from TA-2, assign appropriately calibrated
hedging language to each.

### TA-4 — Avoiding Casual Register in Academic Prose

Teach recognition of overly casual diction, contractions, first-person overuse, and informal
transitions that don't fit academic register, alongside appropriate alternatives. Student practice:
given a casually-written paragraph, revise it toward academic register while preserving the original
claim and appropriately calibrated hedging.

### TA-5 — Full Application: Objective, Calibrated, Formally-Registered Prose

Give the student a set of source materials with varying evidence strength and a casually-drafted
paragraph. They must revise it into fully academic prose: an explicit, evidence-grounded claim (TA-1),
calibrated hedging (TA-2, TA-3), and formal register (TA-4) — the full synthesis task.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Draft: "I think social media is definitely bad for teenagers, everyone knows
this." Fully worked: revised into "Research consistently links heavy social media use to disrupted
sleep patterns in adolescents, though the relationship between social media use and broader mental
health outcomes remains more contested, with some studies finding modest effects and others finding
none." Annotation confirms a clear claim is retained (not abandoned into vagueness, fixing MC-A), and
hedging is calibrated differently for the well-supported sleep-disruption claim versus the more
contested mental-health claim (fixing MC-B).

**WE-2 (Guided):** New casual draft on a different topic (school start times) with a mix of strong
and weak evidence available. Student is prompted with guiding questions ("What is this draft's actual
claim, once we strip away the casual phrasing? Which parts of the evidence are strongly supported,
and which are more preliminary? How should the hedging language differ between them?") to revise
toward calibrated academic prose.

**WE-3 (Independent):** A new casual draft with source materials of varying strength, unaided.
Student revises into fully objective, evidence-grounded, appropriately calibrated, formally
registered academic prose.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Identifying the Implicit Claim in "Balanced" Writing**
Given a claim-avoiding, falsely neutral passage, identify the implicit position and rewrite it as an
explicit, evidence-grounded claim.

**MP-2 [P36] — Ranking Evidence Strength**
Given four source claims, rank them from strongest to weakest evidence support, justifying the
ranking.

**MP-3 [P36] — Calibrating Hedging Language**
Given the four ranked claims, assign hedging language appropriately calibrated to each one's evidence
strength, avoiding uniform hedging.

**MP-4 [P36] — Revising Casual Register**
Given a casually-written academic paragraph, revise it toward formal academic register while
preserving its claim and appropriate hedging.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Academic Revision**
Given a casual draft and source materials of varying evidence strength, produce a fully revised
academic paragraph with an explicit claim, calibrated hedging, and formal register.

the mastery probe stage (S9), route by failure: MP-1 failure indicates residual MC-A; MP-2 failure
alone indicates the evidence-ranking step (TA-2) needs review, distinct from either misconception;
MP-3 failure alone indicates residual MC-B; MP-4 failure alone indicates the register-specific
extension (TA-4); MP-5 failure with MP-1 through MP-4 passing indicates an integration gap — provide
one more fully-guided revision example before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student can rank evidence strength and assign hedging correctly in isolation (MP-2, MP-3)
  but reverts to uniform hedging under the combined cognitive load of MP-5, this is expected at first
  — provide a structured checklist (claim, then evidence-rank, then hedge, then register) as
  scaffolding before removing it.
- If the student overcorrects after MC-A into overstated, unhedged confidence (swinging from "no
  claim" to "absolute claim" without calibration), address this explicitly as a distinct step — having
  a claim and calibrating its confidence are two separate skills that must both be present.
- If the student's revisions toward formal register (TA-4) strip out genuinely useful hedging or
  claim language along with the casual phrasing, clarify that formality and hedging/claim strength are
  independent dimensions — one can be perfectly hedged and still sound casual, or vice versa.
- Students who have strong evidence-evaluation instincts from `eng.composition.rhetorical-analysis`
  should transfer that skill readily to TA-2; if this transfer doesn't occur, revisit whether that
  prerequisite skill was genuinely internalized or only surface-level.
