## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-CLAUSE-WITH-A-SUBORDINATING-WORD-ISNT-A-CLAUSE
- **Trigger signal:** Student assumes that adding a subordinating word (because, although, when, if) to a subject-verb group somehow removes its "clause" status, treating dependent clauses as not genuinely being clauses at all — missing that a dependent clause STILL contains a subject-verb pair (the defining feature of a clause) and is only "dependent" in the sense that it cannot stand alone as a complete sentence, not in the sense that it lacks clause structure.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "In 'because she was tired,' is there still a subject ('she') and a verb ('was')? Does adding 'because' remove that subject-verb pair, or does it just change whether this group of words can stand alone as a complete sentence?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "A dependent clause STILL genuinely contains a subject-verb pair — that's what makes it a clause at all, just like an independent clause. The word 'dependent' refers specifically to its INABILITY TO STAND ALONE as a complete sentence (because of the subordinating word), not to any lack of clause structure. 'Because she was tired' is absolutely a clause (subject 'she' + verb 'was'), just a dependent one."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Check for a subject-verb pair to confirm clause status first — then separately determine whether it can stand alone (independent) or not (dependent) due to a subordinating word."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "She was tired" (independent clause — subject-verb pair, stands alone) vs. "because she was tired" (dependent clause — SAME subject-verb pair, still a genuine clause, just can't stand alone due to "because").
  - "The dog barked" (independent) vs. "when the dog barked" (dependent — still has "dog" + "barked," a genuine clause).
- **s6_path:** "It's a very reasonable assumption that adding a word like 'because' somehow changes the fundamental nature of the group of words — but the subject-verb pair is still there, making it genuinely a clause; the subordinating word only affects whether it can stand alone."

### MC-INDEPENDENT-CLAUSE-MEANS-SIMPLE-SENTENCE-ONLY
- **Trigger signal:** Student assumes "independent clause" is synonymous with "simple sentence" (a single independent clause with no other clauses attached), missing that independent clauses can also appear WITHIN more complex sentence structures — a compound sentence has TWO (or more) independent clauses joined together, and a complex sentence has an independent clause combined with one or more dependent clauses; the term "independent clause" describes a STRUCTURAL UNIT, not a whole-sentence category.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "In 'I was tired, so I went to bed,' are there two separate independent clauses joined together ('I was tired' and 'I went to bed'), or is this just one single simple sentence made of one independent clause?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "'Independent clause' describes a STRUCTURAL UNIT (a subject-verb group that COULD stand alone), not a whole-sentence category. A single sentence can contain multiple independent clauses (a compound sentence: 'I was tired, so I went to bed' — TWO independent clauses joined by 'so'), or an independent clause plus a dependent clause (a complex sentence: 'I went to bed because I was tired'). 'Simple sentence' specifically refers to a sentence with ONLY ONE independent clause and no dependent clauses — a narrower, more specific category."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Recognize that a sentence can contain multiple independent clauses (compound) or a mix of independent and dependent clauses (complex) — 'independent clause' is a building-block unit, not a synonym for 'whole sentence.'"
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "I was tired, so I went to bed" (compound sentence — TWO independent clauses joined by "so") vs. "I went to bed" alone (a simple sentence — ONE independent clause).
  - "I went to bed because I was tired" (complex sentence — one independent clause + one dependent clause) vs. treating this as containing only one clause total.
- **s6_path:** "It's a reasonable simplification to think of 'independent clause' as just another name for 'a whole simple sentence,' since a standalone independent clause IS a complete simple sentence — but recognizing that independent clauses can also combine with other clauses within larger sentence structures is the key insight for understanding compound and complex sentences."

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The stand-alone test**

> Write several subject-verb groups on cards, some with subordinating words attached ("because she was tired") and some without ("she was tired"). For each, physically test: "Can this card, alone, be a complete sentence someone could say by itself?" Sort into "Yes, stands alone" (independent) and "No, needs something else" (dependent) piles — while explicitly confirming BOTH piles genuinely contain a subject-verb pair (both are clauses).

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Identifying the Subject-Verb Pair in Any Clause [C]**

Present a mixed set of independent and dependent clauses; student identifies the subject-verb pair in each, confirming both types are genuine clauses.

**TA-2 — Independent Clauses (Standing Alone) [C]**

Present independent clauses; student confirms they can stand alone as complete sentences.

**TA-3 — Dependent Clauses (Cannot Stand Alone) [C→P]**

Present dependent clauses (with subordinating words); student confirms they contain a subject-verb pair but cannot stand alone, directly targeting MC-A-CLAUSE-WITH-A-SUBORDINATING-WORD-ISNT-A-CLAUSE.

**TA-4 — Independent Clauses Within Compound and Complex Sentences [P]**

Present compound sentences (two independent clauses) and complex sentences (independent + dependent clause); student identifies each clause within the larger sentence structure, directly targeting MC-INDEPENDENT-CLAUSE-MEANS-SIMPLE-SENTENCE-ONLY.

**TA-5 — Building Sentences by Combining Clauses [P]**

Student combines given independent and dependent clauses into compound and complex sentences, applying correct combination and punctuation.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — subject-verb pair in both clause types)**

> "Identify the subject and verb in 'although it rained' and in 'we went outside.'"

Target response: "although it rained" — subject "it," verb "rained"; "we went outside" — subject "we," verb "went." Both are genuine clauses.

**WE-2 (Foundational — independent vs. dependent)**

> "Can 'we went outside' stand alone as a complete sentence? Can 'although it rained'?"

Target response: "we went outside" can stand alone (independent); "although it rained" cannot (dependent, due to "although").

**WE-3 (Intermediate — clauses within a larger sentence)**

> "In 'Although it rained, we went outside,' how many clauses are there, and what type is each?"

Target response: two clauses — "Although it rained" (dependent) and "we went outside" (independent) — combined into one complex sentence.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Subject-verb pair in dependent clause, novel example**
"Identify the subject and verb in 'when the bell rang.'"
*Target:* subject "bell," verb "rang" — confirming it's a genuine clause despite being dependent.

**MP-2 [P36] — Independent vs. dependent, novel example**
"Can 'since she arrived late' stand alone as a sentence?"
*Target:* no — dependent, due to "since," despite containing a subject-verb pair ("she arrived").

**MP-3 [P36] — Clauses within a compound sentence**
"In 'The rain stopped, and the sun came out,' how many independent clauses are there?"
*Target:* two — "The rain stopped" and "the sun came out," joined by "and."

**MP-4 [P36] — Clauses within a complex sentence**
"In 'She finished her homework before she went to bed,' identify the independent and dependent clauses."
*Target:* "She finished her homework" (independent); "before she went to bed" (dependent).

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain clauses**
"In your own words: does adding a word like 'because' or 'when' to a subject-verb group mean it's no longer a clause, and is 'independent clause' just another name for a whole simple sentence?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that dependent clauses still contain a genuine subject-verb pair (they're real clauses, just unable to stand alone), and that independent clauses are structural units that can combine with other clauses to form compound or complex sentences, not simply a synonym for "simple sentence."

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Subject-verb pair confirms clause status regardless of subordinating words**: always require this check first, directly countering the misconception that "because"/"although"/etc. remove clause status.
- **Independent clause is a structural building block, not a synonym for "sentence"**: explicitly teach that sentences can contain multiple clauses (compound, complex), reinforcing "independent clause" as a component, not a whole-sentence category.
- **This concept is foundational for sentence-combining and comma-usage skills**: for all learners, especially S9 learners, ensure genuine mastery here, since the downstream concepts (simple sentences, comma usage, syntax theory) all depend directly on the independent/dependent clause distinction established here.
- **Reuse the phrase/clause subject-verb-pair test as a direct extension**: explicitly connect this concept's foundational test back to the prerequisite phrases concept, building continuity rather than presenting this as an unrelated new skill.
