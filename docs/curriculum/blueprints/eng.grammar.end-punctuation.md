## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-EXCLAMATION-POINT-MEANS-LOUD-NOT-EMOTIONAL
- **Trigger signal:** Student uses the exclamation point purely to indicate LOUDNESS (shouting) rather than recognizing it signals STRONG EMOTION or emphasis more broadly (excitement, surprise, urgency, fear — not necessarily loud volume), and conversely fails to use it for quietly-but-intensely emotional statements, or overuses it on sentences with no genuine strong emotion.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said exclamation points mean someone is shouting loudly. Could someone whisper 'I can't believe it!' with genuine shock or excitement, even quietly? Would that still deserve an exclamation point?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "An exclamation point signals STRONG EMOTION or emphasis (excitement, surprise, urgency, fear, strong command) — not specifically loudness or volume. A quietly whispered 'I can't believe it!' can still carry genuine strong emotion deserving an exclamation point, while a loudly spoken but emotionally flat statement typically doesn't need one."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Use the exclamation point to signal genuine strong emotion or emphasis, regardless of whether the statement would be spoken loudly or quietly."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - A whispered "I can't believe it!" (quiet but genuinely emotional, deserves exclamation) vs. a loudly-stated but emotionally flat "I am going to the store" (no exclamation needed, despite volume).
  - "Watch out!" (urgent warning, strong emotion, deserves exclamation) vs. "I am fine." (no strong emotion, period is correct even if said firmly).
- **s6_path:** "Loudness and exclamation points often go together in everyday examples, which makes the connection feel natural — but the real signal is EMOTIONAL INTENSITY, not volume, and separating these two ideas helps use exclamation points more precisely."

### MC-ANY-SENTENCE-STARTING-WITH-A-WH-WORD-IS-A-QUESTION-MARK
- **Trigger signal:** Student automatically adds a question mark to any sentence beginning with a wh-word (what, who, where, when, why, how), without checking whether the sentence is GENUINELY interrogative (asking something) or is actually declarative/exclamatory despite starting with a wh-word (e.g., "What a beautiful day!" is exclamatory, not a real question, despite starting with "What"; "I wonder what time it is." is declarative — it reports a thought, ending in a period, not a question mark, even though it contains "what").
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You put a question mark after 'What a beautiful day.' But is this sentence actually ASKING something, or is it expressing strong feeling about the day? Does it need an answer?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "The presence of a wh-word doesn't automatically make a sentence a question requiring a question mark — check the sentence's actual FUNCTION: is it genuinely asking something that needs an answer (interrogative, question mark), or is it expressing strong feeling (exclamatory, exclamation point) or reporting a thought/statement (declarative, period), even though it contains a wh-word?"
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Check the sentence's actual function (genuinely asking vs. expressing emotion vs. stating a fact) rather than automatically using a question mark whenever a wh-word appears."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "What a beautiful day!" (exclamatory — expresses feeling, not a genuine question) vs. "What day is it?" (interrogative — genuinely asking).
  - "I wonder what time it is." (declarative — reports a thought, ends in a period) vs. "What time is it?" (interrogative — a direct question, ends in a question mark).
- **s6_path:** "Since wh-words so often DO signal questions, it's a very reasonable shortcut to expect a question mark whenever one appears — but checking the sentence's true function (asking vs. exclaiming vs. stating) catches the genuine exceptions."

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The punctuation-mark match**

> Present a set of sentence cards representing declarative, interrogative, and exclamatory sentences (mixed, including tricky wh-word exclamatory examples like "What a surprise!"). Match each card to the correct end-punctuation mark card (period, question mark, exclamation point), discussing the sentence's actual FUNCTION each time, not just surface features like the presence of a wh-word.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Periods for Declarative and Imperative Sentences [C]**

Present statements and commands; student applies the period correctly.

**TA-2 — Question Marks for Genuine Interrogative Sentences [C]**

Present genuine questions (both wh-questions and yes/no questions); student applies the question mark correctly, including checking for wh-word sentences that are NOT actually questions, directly targeting MC-ANY-SENTENCE-STARTING-WITH-A-WH-WORD-IS-A-QUESTION-MARK.

**TA-3 — Exclamation Points for Strong Emotion (Not Just Loudness) [C→P]**

Present sentences varying in emotional intensity, including quiet-but-emotional and loud-but-flat examples; student applies the exclamation point based on emotional content, directly targeting MC-EXCLAMATION-POINT-MEANS-LOUD-NOT-EMOTIONAL.

**TA-4 — Mixed Sentence Set Requiring Function-Based Judgment [P]**

Present a mixed set including tricky cases (wh-word exclamations, quiet strong emotions); student applies the correct end punctuation based on genuine sentence function.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — period)**

> "Punctuate: 'The sun is shining'"

Target response: "The sun is shining." — declarative, period.

**WE-2 (Foundational — question mark)**

> "Punctuate: 'Where are you going'"

Target response: "Where are you going?" — genuine question, question mark.

**WE-3 (Intermediate — exclamation point vs. wh-word trap)**

> "Punctuate: 'What an amazing performance' and 'What time does it start'"

Target response: "What an amazing performance!" (exclamatory, strong feeling, despite starting with "What") and "What time does it start?" (genuine question).

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Period, novel example**
"Punctuate: 'Please close the door'"
*Target:* "Please close the door." — imperative, period.

**MP-2 [P36] — Question mark, novel example**
"Punctuate: 'Did you finish your homework'"
*Target:* "Did you finish your homework?" — genuine yes/no question.

**MP-3 [P36] — Exclamation point for quiet emotion**
"Punctuate this whispered statement of shock: 'I never expected that'"
*Target:* "I never expected that!" — exclamation point, since the emotional intensity (shock) matters, not volume.

**MP-4 [P36] — Wh-word exclamatory trap**
"Punctuate: 'How wonderful this is' and 'How does this work'"
*Target:* "How wonderful this is!" (exclamatory, despite "How") and "How does this work?" (genuine question).

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain end-punctuation rules**
"In your own words: does an exclamation point mean something was said loudly, and does every wh-word sentence need a question mark?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that exclamation points signal strong emotion/emphasis (not necessarily loudness), and that sentence function (genuinely asking vs. exclaiming vs. stating) — not the mere presence of a wh-word — determines the correct end punctuation.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Function over surface cues (loudness, wh-words) is the central lesson for both misconceptions**: consistently require checking the sentence's genuine communicative function before selecting punctuation, rather than relying on volume associations or word-presence shortcuts.
- **Wh-word exclamatory sentences are a specific, teachable exception category**: "What a...!" and "How...!" exclamatory constructions deserve explicit, repeated practice since they're a common, systematic source of the wh-word-means-question-mark error.
- **Emotional intensity, not volume, is the exclamation-point test**: use quiet-but-emotional and loud-but-flat contrasting examples explicitly to break the loudness association.
- **Lower session_cap (4 TAs) reflects the concept's genuine scope**: this is a smaller, single-hour concept per the KG's `estimated_hours: 1` — the TA sequence is proportionally shorter, consistent with the established convention.
