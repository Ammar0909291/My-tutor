## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-INTERJECTIONS-MUST-BE-EXCLAMATIONS-WITH-EXCLAMATION-POINTS
- **Trigger signal:** Student assumes interjections always require an exclamation point and express only strong/loud emotion (Wow! Ouch!), missing that interjections can also express mild, quiet emotions or reactions and can appear with a comma and no exclamation point at all in more subdued contexts (e.g., "Well, I suppose that's true." or "Oh, I didn't know that.").
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "Look at 'Well, I suppose that's true.' Is 'well' expressing a big, loud emotion with an exclamation point, or a mild, thoughtful reaction with just a comma? Is it still an interjection?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Interjections express a range of emotions or reactions — from strong and sudden (Wow! Ouch!) to mild and conversational (well, oh, hmm) — and can be punctuated with either an exclamation point (for strong emotion) or a comma (for milder, more conversational reactions). Both are genuine interjections; the punctuation matches the intensity, not a fixed rule requiring exclamation points always."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Check whether a word stands independently expressing emotion/reaction, regardless of whether it's followed by an exclamation point or a comma — both signal interjection use depending on intensity."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "Wow! That's amazing!" (strong emotion, exclamation point) vs. "Well, that's interesting." (mild reaction, comma) — both "wow" and "well" are interjections.
  - "Ouch! That hurt!" (strong, exclamatory) vs. "Hmm, let me think about that." (mild, contemplative) — both function as interjections.
- **s6_path:** "It's natural to associate interjections with loud, exciting exclamations since those are the most memorable examples — but quieter conversational interjections (well, oh, hmm) are just as real and common, especially in everyday speech."

### MC-INTERJECTIONS-ARE-JUST-FILLER-WITH-NO-GRAMMATICAL-STATUS
- **Trigger signal:** Student dismisses interjections as meaningless "filler noise" with no real grammatical identity, rather than recognizing them as a genuine word class that stands grammatically independent from the rest of the sentence (not connected by normal syntax to a subject/verb) but still carries real communicative/emotional meaning.
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** "You said 'oh' and 'wow' are just meaningless sounds, not real words. But do they communicate something specific — surprise, realization, pain? Is that meaningless, or does it carry real information about how the speaker feels?"
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Interjections ARE a genuine word class — they just work differently from other classes: they stand grammatically independent (not tied into the sentence's normal subject-verb structure) rather than functioning as a subject, verb, object, or modifier. But they still carry real, specific communicative meaning (surprise: 'wow'; pain: 'ouch'; realization: 'oh'; hesitation: 'um') — they are not meaningless filler."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Recognize interjections as a real word class with genuine communicative meaning, even though they stand independently from a sentence's normal grammatical structure."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
  - "Ouch!" (a real interjection conveying pain, grammatically independent) vs. a true meaningless filler sound with no communicative content.
  - "Wow, that's incredible!" ("wow" independently conveys surprise, distinct from the main sentence structure) vs. treating "wow" as equivalent to random noise.
- **s6_path:** "Because interjections don't fit into the usual subject-verb sentence pattern, it's easy to dismiss them as unimportant extras — but they're a real, named word class doing real communicative work, just with a different grammatical role than the other seven classes."

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**Anchor scene — The emotion-word cards**

> Present a set of cards, each with an interjection and a matching facial-expression/scenario picture (Wow! + amazed face; Ouch! + hurt expression; Oh! + surprised face; Well... + thoughtful pause). Student matches each interjection to its emotional meaning and practices saying it with appropriate tone, discussing both strong (exclamation point) and mild (comma) intensity examples. This anchors interjections as genuine, meaningful emotional expressions tied to real communicative contexts.

---

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

**TA-1 — Strong/Exclamatory Interjections [C]**

Present sentences with strong interjections (Wow! Ouch! Hooray!); student identifies them and their emotional meaning.

**TA-2 — Mild/Conversational Interjections [C]**

Present sentences with mild interjections (well, oh, hmm, um) using commas rather than exclamation points; student identifies these as interjections too, directly targeting MC-INTERJECTIONS-MUST-BE-EXCLAMATIONS-WITH-EXCLAMATION-POINTS.

**TA-3 — Interjections' Grammatical Independence [C→P]**

Student examines sentences with interjections removed (e.g., "Wow, that's incredible!" → "That's incredible!") noting that the remaining sentence is still grammatically complete — demonstrating the interjection's independent status while still carrying real meaning, directly targeting MC-INTERJECTIONS-ARE-JUST-FILLER-WITH-NO-GRAMMATICAL-STATUS.

**TA-4 — Choosing Appropriate Interjections for Context [P]**

Student selects an appropriate interjection for various described scenarios (stubbing a toe, hearing surprising news, considering a request thoughtfully), matching the interjection's specific communicative meaning to context.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Foundational — strong interjection)**

> "In 'Ouch! That really hurt!' what's the interjection, and what does it express?"

Target response: "Ouch" — expresses pain/sudden discomfort.

**WE-2 (Foundational — mild interjection)**

> "In 'Well, I suppose we could try that,' what's the interjection, and how is it punctuated differently from 'Ouch!'?"

Target response: "Well" — a mild, thoughtful interjection, followed by a comma rather than an exclamation point.

**WE-3 (Intermediate — grammatical independence)**

> "Remove the interjection from 'Wow, the view is beautiful.' Is the remaining sentence still complete?"

Target response: "The view is beautiful" — yes, still a complete sentence; "wow" was grammatically independent but still added real emotional meaning.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Strong interjection, novel example**
"In 'Hooray! We won!' what's the interjection, and what does it express?"
*Target:* "Hooray" — expresses joy/celebration.

**MP-2 [P36] — Mild interjection, novel example**
"In 'Hmm, I'm not sure about that,' what's the interjection?"
*Target:* "Hmm" — a mild, contemplative interjection.

**MP-3 [P36] — Grammatical independence check**
"Remove the interjection from 'Oh, I forgot my keys.' Is the rest still a complete sentence?"
*Target:* "I forgot my keys" — yes, complete; "Oh" was independent.

**MP-4 [P36] — Choosing an appropriate interjection**
"You just heard some very surprising news. What interjection might you use?"
*Target:* any appropriate surprise interjection (e.g., "Wow," "Oh my," "Whoa").

**MP-5 [P36] — Explain interjections**
"In your own words: what are interjections, and do they only show strong emotion with exclamation points?"
*Target:* states or demonstrates that interjections express emotion/reaction (strong or mild), stand grammatically independent from the rest of the sentence, and can be punctuated with either an exclamation point or a comma depending on intensity.

S9 (L1 has different interjections/exclamations that don't map directly to English ones, e.g. different culturally-specific reaction words): explicitly teach English-specific interjections as new vocabulary items with their specific emotional associations, rather than assuming direct translation from L1 equivalents (which often carry different connotations or don't exist as direct parallels).

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- **Mild interjections deserve equal attention to exclamatory ones**: explicitly teach comma-punctuated, conversational interjections (well, oh, hmm) alongside vivid exclamatory ones, directly countering the exclamation-point-only default.
- **Grammatical independence does not mean meaninglessness**: reinforce that interjections carry real, specific communicative content despite standing outside the sentence's normal syntax — this is the key distinction from true meaningless filler.
- **Culturally-specific interjection vocabulary for L1 learners**: for S9 learners, treat English interjections as new vocabulary items to learn directly, since interjections often don't translate cleanly and carry culture-specific connotations.
- **Lower session_cap (4 TAs) reflects the concept's genuine scope**: interjections is a smaller, single-hour concept per the KG's `estimated_hours: 1` — the TA sequence is proportionally shorter than richer 2-3 hour concepts, consistent with the established convention (cf. eng.phonics.rhyming's similar scaling).
