## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-IDENTIFYING-A-DEVICE-BY-NAME-IS-THE-GOAL-OF-LITERARY-ANALYSIS
- **Trigger signal:** Student treats "spotting" and correctly naming a literary device (e.g., "that's a metaphor!") as the endpoint of analysis, without going on to explain WHY the author used that specific device and WHAT EFFECT it creates — mistaking device-identification (a labeling task) for device-analysis (an interpretive task).
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present a metaphor ("Her anger was a wildfire, spreading fast and burning everything in its path.") and two student responses: one that just says "that's a metaphor" and stops, and one that says "this metaphor compares anger to a wildfire, emphasizing how the anger spreads uncontrollably and destructively, making the emotion feel dangerous and overwhelming rather than just intense." Ask which response demonstrates actual literary analysis.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Naming a device correctly is just the first step, like correctly naming a tool before using it. Real literary analysis requires the next step: explaining what specific effect the device creates and why the author likely chose it here rather than a plainer alternative. Stopping at the label is like stopping halfway through the job."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "After identifying a device, always push further: what specific effect does it create in this passage, and why might the author have chosen it here instead of stating the idea directly? A label alone isn't analysis — the effect-explanation is the actual analytical work."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Naming-as-analysis (correctly identifying and labeling a device completes the analytical task) vs. (b) Naming-as-first-step (identifying a device is only the starting point; explaining its specific effect and purpose is the actual analytical work).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1 re-check, then re-run TA-1's name-then-explain practice before continuing.

### MC-LITERARY-DEVICES-ARE-DECORATIVE-EXTRAS-SEPARATE-FROM-MEANING
- **Trigger signal:** Student treats literary devices as optional decorative flourishes layered on top of a story's "real" meaning, rather than recognizing that devices are often integral tools authors use to actually construct and deliver meaning, theme, and effect — removing or ignoring the device can genuinely lose part of what the passage communicates, not just its "prettiness."
- **Conflict evidence [P28]:** Present a passage using a specific device (e.g., irony: a character celebrating a "victory" that the reader can see is actually a devastating loss) and ask the student what would be lost if the ironic framing were removed and the passage just stated events plainly. Confirm that removing the device removes the passage's actual meaning/effect (the reader's awareness of the gap between the character's perception and reality), not just decorative flair.
- **Bridge text [P30]:** "Literary devices aren't decoration sitting on top of the 'real' meaning — they're often HOW the meaning gets built and delivered in the first place. Removing the device (like irony, or a controlling metaphor) doesn't just make the passage 'less pretty' — it can genuinely remove or flatten the actual meaning the author was constructing."
- **Replacement text [P31]:** "Test whether a device is 'just decoration' by imagining the passage without it: does the meaning stay exactly the same, or does something genuinely get lost? If meaning is lost, the device was doing real interpretive work, not just adding decoration."
- **Discrimination pairs [P33]:** (a) Devices-as-decoration (literary devices are optional stylistic extras separate from a work's actual meaning) vs. (b) Devices-as-meaning-construction (literary devices are often integral tools that construct and deliver meaning; removing them can genuinely lose part of what's communicated).
- **s6_path:** If detected, drop to PD-1, then re-present the Concrete Anchor's structural-beam framing before continuing to TA-3.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

### PD-1: Theme-and-Message Readiness
- **Tests:** Can the student already derive a story's theme from its plot and character evidence — the interpretive endpoint literary devices are typically tools FOR building, making device-effect analysis meaningful only once theme-derivation is already solid?
- **Probe:** Give a short passage and ask the student to state its theme with supporting evidence.
- **Pass condition:** Produces a reasonable theme statement with evidence.
- **Fail routing:** If theme-derivation is weak, route to `eng.literature.theme-and-message` review — device analysis connects specific techniques to the larger meaning theme identification already established.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**"The Structural Beam, Not the Wallpaper."** Present a model house with visible structural beams (holding up the roof — remove one and the roof genuinely sags or falls) versus decorative wallpaper (remove it and the room looks plainer, but the structure is unaffected). Have the student sort 4 device examples into "beam" (removing it changes the actual meaning/structure) versus initially-assumed "wallpaper," then test each by imagining removal — most genuine literary devices turn out to be beams, not wallpaper, directly seeding MC-LITERARY-DEVICES-ARE-DECORATIVE-EXTRAS-SEPARATE-FROM-MEANING's fix. Separately, present a "tool ID card" (correctly naming a hammer) next to a "tool USE demonstration" (showing what the hammer actually accomplishes — driving a nail) — confirming that naming a tool is not the same as using/explaining it, seeding MC-IDENTIFYING-A-DEVICE-BY-NAME-IS-THE-GOAL-OF-LITERARY-ANALYSIS's fix.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1: Name-Then-Explain Practice
Present 4 passages, each containing one device, and have the student first name the device, then push to the required second step: explaining the specific effect it creates and why the author likely chose it. Directly counters MC-IDENTIFYING-A-DEVICE-BY-NAME-IS-THE-GOAL-OF-LITERARY-ANALYSIS.

### TA-2: The Removal Test for Meaning
Using the discrimination pairs from MC-LITERARY-DEVICES-ARE-DECORATIVE-EXTRAS-SEPARATE-FROM-MEANING, present a device-containing passage and have the student imagine/write the passage with the device removed, confirming what meaning is genuinely lost.

### TA-3: Categories of Literary Devices
Introduce the three broad categories (figurative — metaphor, simile, personification; structural — foreshadowing, flashback, framing; sound-based — alliteration, rhyme, onomatopoeia) and have the student sort 6 device examples into their correct category.

### TA-4: Connecting Devices to Theme
Given a passage's already-identified theme, student identifies one device that helps construct or reinforce that specific theme, explaining the connection.

### TA-5: Full Device Analysis of a Novel Passage
Student analyzes a new passage: identifies 2 devices, names their category, explains their specific effect, and connects at least one to the passage's theme — the generative capstone.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

### WE-1: Name-Then-Explain (Scaffolded)
**Passage:** "The old house groaned under decades of silence." (personification)
**Walkthrough:** Name: personification (the house is given a human-like ability to "groan"). Explain: this creates a sense of the house as almost alive, burdened and weary, deepening the atmosphere of neglect and age beyond a plain description.

### WE-2: Removal Test (Guided)
**Passage with device:** "He wore his loneliness like a second skin." (simile) **Without device:** "He was very lonely, and it seemed like a permanent part of him."
**Walkthrough:** Confirm the simile version conveys the inescapable, close, almost physical nature of the loneliness in a way the plain version loses — the device was doing real meaning-work, not decoration.

### WE-3: Full Device Analysis (Independent)
**Passage:** A short passage using both foreshadowing (an ominous early detail) and irony (a character's confident statement the reader knows will be proven wrong), connected to a theme about the danger of overconfidence.
**Walkthrough:** Student identifies both devices, categorizes them (structural: foreshadowing; and irony as a distinct device type), explains their specific effects, and connects the irony directly to the overconfidence theme.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Push past naming to explain effect**
Given a passage with a device, student names it AND explains its specific effect and likely purpose, not stopping at the label. *(Directly targets MC-IDENTIFYING-A-DEVICE-BY-NAME-IS-THE-GOAL-OF-LITERARY-ANALYSIS.)*

**MP-2 [P36] — Apply the removal test**
Given a device-containing passage, student applies the removal test and confirms specific meaning that would be lost without the device. *(Directly targets MC-LITERARY-DEVICES-ARE-DECORATIVE-EXTRAS-SEPARATE-FROM-MEANING.)*

**MP-3 [P36] — Categorize devices**
Given 4 unlabeled device examples, student correctly sorts each into figurative, structural, or sound-based categories.

**MP-4 [P36] — Connect a device to theme**
Given a passage's theme, student identifies and explains how one specific device reinforces that theme.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full novel-passage device analysis**
Given a new passage, student identifies 2 devices, categorizes them, explains their effects, and connects at least one to the passage's theme.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** At session open (S0), confirm PD-1 passes — device analysis connects specific techniques to a work's larger theme, so weak theme-derivation undermines the connective analytical task. During instruction (S1), watch for both misconception patterns: stopping at naming (MC-IDENTIFYING-A-DEVICE-BY-NAME-IS-THE-GOAL-OF-LITERARY-ANALYSIS) and treating devices as decorative (MC-LITERARY-DEVICES-ARE-DECORATIVE-EXTRAS-SEPARATE-FROM-MEANING) both under-value the actual interpretive work devices require — redirect both to the Structural Beam/Tool-Use anchor. If the student stops at labeling or dismisses a device's meaning-function during TA-1/TA-2 (S6), drop back to the Concrete Anchor's demonstrations before returning to independent analysis. At mastery-probe stage (S9), do not certify mastery unless MP-1 and MP-2 both pass — a student who categorizes devices correctly (MP-3) but still stops at naming or treats devices as decorative has not learned the core analytical judgment this concept requires.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student stops at naming a device without explaining its effect, weight remediation toward MC-IDENTIFYING-A-DEVICE-BY-NAME-IS-THE-GOAL-OF-LITERARY-ANALYSIS's bridge text and re-run TA-1's name-then-explain practice before returning to independent analysis.
- If the student treats devices as decorative rather than meaning-constructing, weight remediation toward MC-LITERARY-DEVICES-ARE-DECORATIVE-EXTRAS-SEPARATE-FROM-MEANING's bridge text and TA-2's removal-test practice specifically.
- This concept feeds three direct downstream unlocks (`eng.literature.metaphor-and-simile`, `eng.literature.irony`, `eng.literature.poetry-basics`) — do not certify mastery without TA-5's full passage analysis, since each downstream concept deepens one specific device category established here.
- Reuse the "structural beam, not wallpaper" framing explicitly across all three downstream device concepts — each teaches that its specific device family is a genuine meaning-construction tool, not decoration.
