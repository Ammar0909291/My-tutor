**Concept:** Rhetorical Appeals — the three classical modes of persuasion Aristotle named: ethos
(appeals to the speaker's credibility), pathos (appeals to the audience's emotion), and logos
(appeals to logic and evidence).

**Why this concept exists in the sequence:** Audience and Purpose (prerequisite) teaches the student
to identify WHO they're writing for and WHY. Rhetorical Appeals teaches the specific persuasive
LEVERS a writer pulls once persuasion is the chosen purpose — a student who already knows their
audience is skeptical parents can now choose whether to build credibility (ethos), stir concern
(pathos), or marshal facts (logos), and combine them deliberately. This concept unlocks
`eng.composition.rhetorical-devices` (specific language techniques that execute these appeals, like
anaphora for pathos or statistics for logos) and `eng.composition.persuasive-techniques` (applied
combinations of all three in real persuasive writing).

## Learning Objective

By the end of this concept, the student can (a) correctly identify which of the three appeals — ethos,
pathos, or logos — a given persuasive passage is primarily using, with textual evidence, (b) explain
that most effective persuasive writing combines more than one appeal rather than relying on just
one, and (c) draft short persuasive passages that deliberately deploy a specified appeal (or
combination) for a stated audience and purpose.

## Component 1 — Misconception Register

### MC-A-PATHOS-MEANS-JUST-BEING-EMOTIONAL-OR-DRAMATIC

**Trigger signal:** Student equates pathos with any emotionally charged or dramatic language,
regardless of whether it's relevant to the argument — e.g., inserting an unrelated sad story into a
persuasive essay about recycling policy simply because "it has emotion," without connecting the
emotion to the actual claim being argued.

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two passages that both use emotional language about
the same topic (school funding cuts): one links the emotion directly to the argument (a student
describing losing access to counseling services because of a specific proposed cut, tied explicitly
to why the cut should be reversed), and one uses emotional language that is disconnected from the
actual claim (a vague, generic statement about "how sad budget cuts are" with no connection to the
specific policy argument). Ask: "Both use emotion. Which one actually makes you more likely to agree
with a specific position, and which just makes you feel bad in general?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Pathos isn't just 'add emotional language' — it's connecting a real feeling
to the SPECIFIC claim you're making, so the emotion does persuasive work. Think of it like a lawyer's
closing argument: a lawyer who cries about 'how sad the whole situation is' in general convinces no
one. A lawyer who makes the jury feel the specific weight of a specific piece of evidence is using
pathos correctly — the emotion is aimed at something that matters to the verdict."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before adding emotional language, ask: does this emotion connect
directly to the claim I'm making, or is it just general sad/happy/angry language floating nearby? If
a reader could feel the emotion and still not know what you want them to DO or BELIEVE, it's
decoration, not pathos."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "It's just so sad how things have changed over the years." (generic emotional language, unclear
   what claim it serves) vs. "When the after-school program closed, twelve students lost their only
   safe place to do homework before a parent got home from work — that's the direct cost of this
   budget line." (emotion tied explicitly to the specific claim about the budget line).
2. Inserting an unrelated tragic anecdote into an essay about traffic light timing (emotion
   disconnected from the actual argument) vs. describing a specific near-miss accident caused by the
   exact intersection's current timing, connected directly to the proposed change (emotion in
   service of the specific claim).

**s6_path:** If the student still adds disconnected emotional language after the bridge, have them
draw a literal arrow from the emotional sentence to the specific claim sentence it's supposed to
support — if no arrow can honestly be drawn, the sentence must be revised or cut.

### MC-B-LOGOS-MEANS-JUST-USING-NUMBERS-OR-STATISTICS

**Trigger signal:** Student equates logos with the mere presence of any numbers or statistics,
regardless of whether the numbers are relevant, sufficient, or logically connected to the claim —
e.g., citing an irrelevant or cherry-picked statistic as if its mere numerical nature made an
argument "logical."

**Conflict evidence [P28]:** Show the student two passages using statistics about the same claim
(that a new park would benefit the community): one uses a directly relevant statistic connected by
clear reasoning (a specific figure on how many families in the surrounding neighborhood currently
lack access to any green space within walking distance, explicitly reasoned to the benefit claim),
and one uses an irrelevant or misleading statistic (a national average that has nothing to do with
the specific community in question, presented as if its mere existence proves the point). Ask:
"Both have numbers. Which one actually gives you a REASON to believe the claim, and which one is
just a number sitting next to the claim?"

**Bridge text [P30]:** "Logos isn't just 'include a statistic' — it's building a chain of REASONING
where evidence logically connects to a conclusion, the way a bridge's support beams have to
actually connect to both sides to hold weight. A number floating near a claim with no explained
connection is like a support beam propped up in mid-air, touching nothing — it looks like structure,
but it isn't holding anything up."

**Replacement text [P31]:** "Before using a statistic, ask: have I explained WHY this number
supports my specific claim, or did I just place it nearby and hope the reader connects the dots? A
number needs an explicit 'because' bridging it to your claim, or it's not doing logical work."

**Discrimination pairs [P33]:**
1. "Studies show 73% of people like parks." (vague, unsourced, not connected to the specific
   community or claim) vs. "A neighborhood survey found 68% of families within a half-mile of the
   proposed site currently travel over twenty minutes to reach any green space — this specific gap is
   what the new park would close." (specific, sourced, explicitly reasoned to the claim).
2. Citing a national statistic about crime rates in general to argue for a specific local streetlight
   proposal (mismatched scope, no explained connection) vs. citing the specific number of
   after-dark incidents reported on the exact stretch of road in question, reasoned directly to the
   proposal (matched scope, explicit connection).

**s6_path:** If the student still treats any number as automatically "logical" after the bridge,
have them write out the missing reasoning step explicitly as a sentence starting with "This number
matters to my claim because..." — if they cannot complete that sentence honestly, the statistic
does not belong in the argument as currently framed.

## Component 2 — Prerequisite Diagnostic Block

**PD-1 (checks `eng.composition.audience-and-purpose`):** "For this writing prompt, name a specific
audience and the persuasive purpose driving it." A student who cannot commit to a specific audience
and persuasive purpose needs `eng.composition.audience-and-purpose` reviewed before continuing,
since rhetorical appeals only make sense in service of persuading a defined reader.

**PD-2 (checks basic claim recognition, informal pre-check):** "Underline the sentence in this short
passage that states what the writer wants the reader to believe or do." A student who cannot locate
the underlying claim needs a brief primer on claim identification before Component 3, since all
three appeals exist to support a claim, not to stand alone.

## Component 3 — Concrete Anchor [P06]

**The Three-Legged Stool Anchor.** Draw or build a simple three-legged stool, labeling each leg
Ethos, Pathos, and Logos. Show that a stool missing one leg (or with one leg much shorter than the
others) wobbles or tips — a persuasive argument leaning ENTIRELY on one appeal is similarly unstable
(all pathos and no logos feels manipulative; all logos and no pathos feels cold and unpersuasive to
many audiences; all ethos and no logos/pathos feels like an empty appeal to authority). This directly
seeds fixes for both misconceptions: it shows that each "leg" must be load-bearing and properly
connected to the seat (the claim) — a pathos leg that's just decorative emotional language (MC-A)
or a logos leg that's just a floating, disconnected statistic (MC-B) doesn't actually hold the stool
up, it just LOOKS like a leg.

## Component 4 — Conceptual Development Sequence

### TA-1 — Identifying Ethos

Teach ethos as an appeal to the speaker's credibility, expertise, or trustworthiness. Show examples
(citing credentials, acknowledging counterarguments fairly, using measured and careful language).
Student practice: identify the ethos-building move in three short passages.

### TA-2 — Identifying Pathos (Connected to Claim)

Teach pathos using the lawyer's-closing-argument bridge text, emphasizing the connection test:
emotion must link explicitly to the specific claim. Student practice: given five passages using
emotional language, sort into "connected to the claim" and "disconnected decoration" using the
arrow-drawing test from the s6_path.

### TA-3 — Identifying Logos (Connected to Claim)

Teach logos using the support-beam bridge text, emphasizing the "because" bridge test between
evidence and claim. Student practice: given five passages using statistics or facts, sort into
"explicitly reasoned to the claim" and "floating, disconnected numbers."

### TA-4 — Combining Appeals Strategically

Teach that strong persuasive writing typically layers more than one appeal, chosen deliberately
based on audience (an audience that already trusts the writer needs less ethos-building; a highly
analytical audience may need more logos than pathos). Student practice: given an audience profile
and a claim, plan which one or two appeals would be most effective and justify the choice, then
draft a short passage using the planned combination.

## Component 5 — Worked Examples

**WE-1 (Scaffolded):** Prompt: "Persuade the school board to add a mental health day to the
calendar." Fully worked: ethos move — cites a school counselor's professional observation; pathos
move — a specific student's account of feeling unable to ask for help during a stressful week,
explicitly tied to the claim that a dedicated day would have helped; logos move — the specific
percentage of students at the school who reported feeling overwhelmed in a recent internal survey,
reasoned directly to the proposal. Annotation labels each sentence with its appeal and confirms each
passes the "connected to the claim" test.

**WE-2 (Guided):** Same prompt type, new topic ("Persuade classmates to support a school recycling
initiative"). Student is given one appeal already drafted (a logos sentence citing a specific waste
statistic) and prompted with guiding questions ("What ethos move could establish your credibility on
this topic? What specific, connected pathos moment could you add — not generic 'caring about the
planet' language, but something tied directly to this school?") to complete the passage.

**WE-3 (Independent):** New prompt ("Persuade the city council to build a bike lane on a specific
street"). Student plans and drafts a full passage using at least two appeals, unaided, labeling each
appeal used and justifying the combination for the stated audience.

## Component 6 — Mastery Probe Set

**MP-1 [P36] — Appeal Identification**
Given three short passages, each primarily using a different appeal, identify which appeal
(ethos, pathos, or logos) each uses and cite the specific textual evidence.

**MP-2 [P36] — Pathos Connection Test**
Given two passages using emotional language on the same topic, determine which one passes the
"connected to the claim" test and explain why the other is decorative rather than persuasive.

**MP-3 [P36] — Logos Connection Test**
Given two passages using statistics on the same topic, determine which one passes the "because"
bridge test and explain why the other is a floating, disconnected number.

**MP-4 [P36] — Strategic Combination Planning**
Given an audience profile (a skeptical, highly analytical audience) and a claim, plan which one or
two appeals would be most effective and justify the choice in two sentences.

**MP-5 [P36] — Full Persuasive Draft**
Given a new persuasive writing prompt with a stated audience, draft a short passage using at least
two appeals, label each appeal used, and confirm each passes its respective connection test.

**Protocol routing paragraph:** On entry (S0), run PD-1 and PD-2; an audience/purpose gap routes

failure alone indicates residual MC-A; MP-3 failure alone indicates residual MC-B; MP-4 failure with
MP-1 through MP-3 passing indicates a strategic-planning gap (TA-4) rather than a recognition gap;
MP-5 failure alone indicates a production/transfer gap — provide one more guided combination example
before re-probing.

## Component 8 — Adaptive Flags

- If the student can correctly IDENTIFY appeals in others' writing (MP-1 through MP-3) but cannot
  PRODUCE connected appeals of their own (WE-3, MP-5), treat this as a production gap and provide an
  additional guided drafting example before independent work.
- If the student defaults to pathos-heavy writing regardless of audience (even for highly analytical
  audiences where logos would be more persuasive), flag this as an audience-sensitivity gap — revisit
  the audience-profiling connection from the prerequisite concept alongside TA-4.
- If the student produces technically connected appeals that feel manipulative or dishonest (e.g.,
  exaggerated emotional claims, cherry-picked statistics), flag this as an ethical-use concern
  distinct from mechanical correctness — note for the downstream `persuasive-techniques` concept
  rather than re-teaching this concept's connection tests.
- Students with strong logical/analytical tendencies may over-rely on logos and resist adding pathos
  even when justified — use the three-legged stool anchor concretely to show that a stool missing
  the pathos leg still wobbles, even if the logos leg is strong.
