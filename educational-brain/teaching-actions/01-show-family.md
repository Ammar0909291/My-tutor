# SHOW Family

Actions where the tutor presents something for the learner to observe.
SHOW actions are the cheapest-setup, fastest-to-deploy family — which is
exactly why they are the default first move in `../decision-engine/01`'s
DEMONSTRATION state and why Filter 6 (load fit) favors them under any
load signal ("the low-load end of the surviving families wins —
demonstration over simulation").

The family-wide rule that applies to every action below: **prediction-
first, always, when a reveal is coming** (`../foundations/04-universal-
teaching-principles.md` Principle 19's SHOW-family application — watching
alone risks the illusion of competence; committing to a prediction first
converts passive watching into a real stake). And the family-wide risk
every action below must guard against: **SHOW alone never certifies
learning** — every SHOW action needs a DO or TEST-THINKING action after
it before mastery is claimed (Universal Principle 19).

## 1. Demonstration

**What it is**: the tutor performs the procedure, runs the causal
sequence, or executes the physical action, live, in front of the learner.

**Fits**: procedure (the canonical use — `../decision-engine/01`'s
DEMONSTRATION state is built around this action), causal system (showing
the mechanism run), physical procedure (showing the motor sequence).

**Setup cost**: LOW — needs no materials beyond what the concept
requires, no advance preparation of variants.

**Procedure**:
1. Elicit a prediction before the reveal ("what do you think happens
   if...?").
2. Perform the action or run the sequence, narrating minimally.
3. Exaggerate the critical instant on first pass if this is a first
   viewing (`../decision-engine/01` DEMONSTRATION → EXPLANATION rule:
   two instances before naming).
4. On a second confusion signal at the same instance, do not re-run
   identically — this triggers Escalation Rung 1 (change frame, not
   volume; `../decision-engine/05`).

**Failure mode**: running the demonstration at full speed with no
prediction ask turns it into television — visually engaging, cognitively
inert.

## 2. Worked Example

**What it is**: a fully solved instance of a procedure, shown step by
step, with the reasoning for each step made explicit — not just the
mechanical moves but why each move is taken.

**Fits**: procedure. This is the canonical opening move for any multi-
step procedure with establishable sub-steps (`../decision-engine/01`
GUIDED's "worked-example fading" sub-ladder for established learners).

**Setup cost**: LOW.

**Procedure**: show the complete solution once, narrating the REASON for
each step (not just the step); then immediately transition to Completion
Problem (`03-do-family.md #10`) — a worked example that is never faded
into learner production is passive SHOW with no DO follow-through, which
violates the family-wide certification rule above.

**Failure mode** (the gap the Architecture Audit's Missing Knowledge
finding named, `../validation/05-missing-knowledge.md`): worked examples
generated without a self-explanation prompt attached, or introduced too
late for a learner who already has partial procedural fluency (expertise
reversal — established learners should enter the fading ladder further
along, not at step 1; `../decision-engine/01` GUIDED).

## 3. Contrasting Cases

**What it is**: two (or more) instances placed side by side that differ
in exactly the dimension that matters for the concept, so the learner's
attention is drawn to the distinguishing feature by the contrast itself
rather than by being told what to look for.

**Fits**: concept (`../decision-engine/04` filter 2's "concept → analogy,
contrasting cases, classification, drawing" row).

**Setup cost**: MEDIUM — requires selecting or constructing a genuine
minimal-pair (differing in only the load-bearing dimension; a pair that
differs in several dimensions at once teaches nothing about which one
matters).

**Procedure**: present both cases together, ask "what's different?"
before any explanation. The learner's own noticing is the teaching; a
tutor who names the difference before asking has pre-empted the
discovery the action exists to produce.

**Failure mode**: a "contrasting pair" that actually differs in
irrelevant surface features (different numbers, different names) instead
of the concept's load-bearing dimension teaches surface pattern-matching,
not the concept.

## 4. Simulation-Observed

**What it is**: a dynamic system (physical, digital, or narrated) that
the learner watches run, with parameters visible but not yet learner-
controlled — the watched form of simulation; see `03-do-family.md #14`
for the learner-manipulated form.

**Fits**: causal system.

**Setup cost**: MEDIUM — parameter spaces themselves are load
(`../decision-engine/04` filter 6: "under any load signal: no
simulations... parameter spaces are load"). This action is legal only
with full cognitive headroom; under any load signal, step down to plain
Demonstration.

**Procedure**: elicit a prediction about the outcome before running;
run; compare the outcome to the prediction explicitly — the gap between
predicted and observed IS the teaching moment, more than the simulation
itself.

**Failure mode**: running a simulation with several parameters changing
at once when the learner has not yet built a mental model of the system
with one parameter varying — this compounds load past what the family's
LOW/MEDIUM-cost siblings would have cost, for no additional teaching
value at that stage.

## 5. Model/Object Inspection

**What it is**: the learner examines a physical or virtual artifact
(a manipulative, a diagram, a labeled specimen, a working object) that
embodies the concept, without the tutor performing an action on it —
distinguished from Demonstration by the absence of a performed sequence;
this is examination, not enactment.

**Fits**: concept, physical procedure (inspecting the tool or apparatus
before using it).

**Setup cost**: LOW, when the artifact already exists (a physical
manipulative, an existing diagram); MEDIUM if a new artifact must be
constructed for this concept.

**Procedure**: hand over (physically or attentionally) the artifact with
one directed question ("what do you notice about the bottom number?"),
not an open "look at this." Undirected inspection wastes the action's
main advantage — a learner's own eyes finding a feature they were pointed
toward remembers it better than being told the feature exists.

**Failure mode**: inspection with no directing question collapses into
passive looking, indistinguishable from decoration.
