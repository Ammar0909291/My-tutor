# TEST-THINKING Family

Actions that make the learner's own reasoning process the object of
attention — not just producing an answer (DO) but examining, committing
to, or interrogating the thinking that produces it. This family is where
misconceptions surface most cheaply and where advanced learners are
tested past mere correctness. `../decision-engine/04` filter 2 names this
family's canonical use directly: "live misconception → prediction +
collision + error analysis."

## 15. Prediction

**What it is**: the learner commits to an expected outcome before
observing what actually happens — the cheapest action in this family,
"compatible with everything and costs nothing" (`../decision-engine/01`
DEMONSTRATION state, citing this action by name).

**Fits**: causal system, concept — legally attachable to almost any SHOW
action as a mandatory prefix, per the family-wide SHOW rule
(`01-show-family.md`).

**Setup cost**: LOW.

**Procedure**: ask for the prediction BEFORE any reveal, always — a
prediction requested after the answer is already visible is not a
prediction, it's a rationalization. If the learner predicts wrong, do not
correct before the reveal; let the mismatch itself (Escalation and
Decision Matrix both govern what happens after — `../decision-engine/03
§2` MISCONCEIVING-during-DEMONSTRATION cell: "lock that in warmly... let
the mismatch sit in silence for a full beat").

**Failure mode**: treating a wrong prediction as something to correct
immediately rather than as the stake that makes the coming reveal
meaningful — a prediction whose wrongness is instantly softened loses
its diagnostic and motivational value in the same stroke.

## 16. Thought Experiment

**What it is**: a hypothetical scenario reasoned through verbally,
without any physical or simulated apparatus — "what would happen if
there were no friction at all?"

**Fits**: causal system.

**Setup cost**: MEDIUM — requires meta-language the learner may not yet
have; `../decision-engine/04` filter 4 explicitly excludes this action
below roughly age 10, where the abstraction of a purely hypothetical,
non-enacted scenario outpaces the learner's capacity to reason about it.

**Procedure**: pose the counterfactual precisely (changing exactly one
variable from the real, already-understood case), let the learner reason
aloud, and resist supplying the answer even when the reasoning stalls —
supplying it converts the thought experiment into Direct Telling wearing
a question mark.

**Relationship to Simulation**: a thought experiment is what a Simulation-
Manipulated action (`03-do-family.md #14`) becomes when no apparatus is
available or appropriate — same cognitive structure (vary one parameter,
observe consequence), executed in reasoning alone. Prefer the physical or
simulated form when one exists; the thought-experiment form is the
fallback when the scenario cannot be built or run (a frictionless surface
cannot be truly demonstrated, only imagined).

## 17. Error Analysis

**What it is**: presenting a wrong solution and asking the learner to
find the flaw and name the misconception behind it — "here is a wrong
solution — find the flaw" (`../assessment/07 §2`).

**Fits**: live misconception (the canonical use — the sharpest
diagnostic and repair instrument this catalog has for an active
misconception), procedure (finding a procedural error).

**Setup cost**: MEDIUM — the wrong solution must be authored to embody a
SPECIFIC, plausible error, not an obviously-broken strawman; a strawman
error teaches nothing because no real learner would make it.

**The stability guard** (critical, already cited as a named rule
— `../assessment/06 §2`): **only plant a flaw after the correct schema
is stable in this learner, or the flaw itself may be learned.** Error
Analysis run too early — before the learner has a solid correct model to
compare against — risks installing the wrong model rather than
inoculating against it. This is why `../first-lesson/03` states plainly
that Error Analysis "is an advanced learner's instrument and appears
nowhere in lesson one": a complete beginner has no correct schema yet
for the flawed example to be contrasted against.

**Procedure**: present the flawed solution; ask "what's wrong here?"
before offering any hint; once found, ask the learner to name the
underlying misconception in their own words, not just the surface error
— naming the mechanism is what generalizes the repair to future
instances of the same error.

## 18. Self-Explanation Prompt

**What it is**: asking the learner to explain, in their own words, why a
step (of a Worked Example, a Demonstration, or their own correct answer)
works — distinguished from Error Analysis by being applied to CORRECT
material, and from Teach-Back (`06-social-family.md #25`) by having no
external audience — this is private, reflective narration, not
performance.

**Fits**: concept, procedure.

**Setup cost**: LOW.

**Procedure**: attach directly to a Worked Example or Completion Problem
— "why did we do that step there, and not earlier?" — rather than
running it as a separate action afterward. This closes exactly the gap
`../validation/05-missing-knowledge.md` named: worked examples generated
without a self-explanation prompt attached are common in the runtime
today and produce shallower encoding than the same example with this
prompt attached.

**Failure mode**: accepting a restated procedure ("first you do this,
then you do that") as a self-explanation — that describes WHAT happened,
not WHY; the prompt must specifically target the reason, and a purely
descriptive answer should be redirected once ("right, and why THAT
step?") before moving on.

## 19. Generation

**What it is**: the learner constructs their own example, counterexample,
or instance of the concept, rather than working with one the tutor
supplies — "construct your own example" (`../decision-engine/07 §5`'s
stretch-item form), or its harder inversion, constructing a
counterexample to a proposed boundary.

**Fits**: concept.

**Setup cost**: MEDIUM.

**Procedure**: pose the generation task precisely ("give me a fraction
that's between 1/2 and 3/4" or "can you think of a case where this rule
would break?"), and treat any valid instance the learner produces as
success — generation tasks legitimately admit many correct answers,
unlike most other actions in this catalog, and the tutor must not
silently expect the one example they had in mind.

**Why it is diagnostically stronger than solving a given problem**
(`../assessment/07 §2`): "generation is a harder and more honest test
than solution — you cannot construct a counterexample to a boundary you
can't see." A learner who can solve supplied problems but cannot generate
their own instances has pattern-matched the supplied form without
holding the underlying concept.
