# SOCIAL Family

Actions that involve an audience, an opponent, a collaborator, or a
performance element beyond the learner working alone with the tutor.
SOCIAL is the highest-setup-cost family on average and the family with
the sharpest documented failure mode — the "chocolate-covered-broccoli"
illusion, where the social/game framing produces engagement and apparent
mastery that evaporates the instant the framing is removed.

## 24. Game

**What it is**: a competitive or scored format wrapped around practice —
racing against a timer, beating a score, a turn-based challenge.

**Fits**: procedure (fluency games — repeated practice of an already-
partially-fluent skill), fact/convention (recall games).

**Setup cost**: HIGH — requires rules, scoring, and often materials, and
carries the family's signature risk requiring active management.

**The chocolate-covered-broccoli guard** (named directly,
`../assessment/08 §4`): "mastery of the game mistaken for mastery of the
concept... performance that collapses without the game skin is common
and disqualifying." Every use of Game as a teaching action must be
followed, before certifying any mastery, by the SAME content assessed
**outside the game format entirely** — plain and unskinned. A learner who
excels at a fractions racing game but cannot solve the identical problem
presented as a plain worksheet item has learned the game, not the
fractions.

**Persona note**: strong-affinity default for competitive personas; not a
default for the fearful or anxious (public scoring raises stakes exactly
where they need lowered); private, self-record framings substitute for
competitive learners who would otherwise be compared against others
rather than their own past performance.

**When it fits**: reserved for practice/consolidation of already-
partially-established content (fluency-building), never for first
teaching of a new concept — the game's cognitive overhead (rules,
scoring, competitive pressure) is itself load, and stacking new-concept
load on top of game-mechanic load is the multi-step-game restriction
Filter 6 already names (`../decision-engine/04`: "under any load signal:
... no multi-step games").

## 25. Teach-Back

**What it is**: the learner explains the concept to an audience (the
tutor, a stated hypothetical student, or literally a peer) as if
teaching it themselves — the Feynman-technique action.

**Fits**: concept, procedure. Explicitly named as the evidence
requirement for the top rung of the knowledge ladder: "EXPERT needs a
teach-back. No same-session shortcut exists" (`../student-state/02 §4`).

**Setup cost**: MEDIUM.

**Procedure**: give the framing explicitly ("explain this like you're
teaching someone who's never seen it") and then genuinely stay in the
role of the naive listener — asking the clarifying questions a true
novice would ask, not questions that test whether the "teacher" already
knows the answer. A tutor who slips out of the naive-listener role
mid-teach-back collapses the action back into ordinary Q&A.

**Diagnostic value**: gaps in a teach-back are the most honest evidence
available that a concept is genuinely held rather than performed —
a learner can often produce correct ANSWERS without being able to teach
the mechanism to someone else, and the inability to teach it is itself
the finding, not a failure of the action.

**Relationship to Self-Explanation Prompt** (`04-test-thinking-family.md
#18`): Teach-Back is the audience-facing, performative form; Self-
Explanation Prompt is the private, reflective form. Use Self-Explanation
during consolidation; reserve Teach-Back for near-mastery certification,
where a genuine audience (even hypothetical) raises the stakes enough to
reveal gaps that private reflection alone would not surface.

## 26. Role-Play-as-Performance

**What it is**: the learner adopts a role or persona to work through the
concept — distinguished from Enactment (`03-do-family.md #13`), which is
physical procedure practice with no persona involved; Role-Play here
specifically involves adopting a character or perspective (playing "the
electron," arguing a historical figure's position, acting out a word
problem's characters).

**Fits**: concept, physical procedure (when the role itself embodies a
mechanism, e.g. physically moving as "the electron" through a circuit
diagram drawn on the floor).

**Setup cost**: HIGH — requires setup of the scenario and often
willingness to perform in front of an audience.

**Persona note** (already load-bearing, `../decision-engine/04` filter
4): "no public role-play for the fearful." This is a hard veto, not a
soft preference — a fearful learner offered public role-play should
receive a private variant (tutor-only audience) or a different action
from this family entirely, never a gently-encouraged version of the
public form.

**Failure mode**: treating enthusiasm for the performance itself as
evidence of conceptual engagement — a learner can enjoy playing a
character while the underlying mechanism passes by unexamined; the role-
play needs a directed question inside it ("as the electron, what happens
to you when you reach the resistor?") for the performance to carry
teaching content rather than just entertainment value.

## 27. Collaborative/Peer Framing

**What it is**: working the concept through with (real or narratively
framed) another learner rather than alone with the tutor — paired
problem-solving, "explain it to your study partner," or a narratively
framed "let's help this student who's stuck" framing when no real peer
is present.

**Fits**: procedure, concept.

**Setup cost**: MEDIUM.

**Procedure**: when a real peer is available, structure genuine turn-
taking (not one learner doing the work while the other watches) — the
same production-over-reception rule (Universal Principle 19,
`../foundations/04-universal-teaching-principles.md`) applies inside
collaboration exactly as it does to individual work. When no real peer
exists, the narrative framing ("this other student got stuck right
here — what would you tell them?") borrows the SOCIAL family's framing
benefit (lower stakes than direct self-assessment — critiquing a
fictional other's error feels less exposing than admitting one's own)
while requiring no actual second person.

**Relationship to Error Analysis** (`04-test-thinking-family.md #17`):
the narrative form of Collaborative Framing ("help this stuck student")
is frequently the delivery wrapper for an Error Analysis action — the
"stuck student's" wrong work IS the planted flaw, and the stability guard
from Error Analysis applies identically: only use this wrapper once the
learner's own correct schema is solid enough that engaging with someone
else's wrong model doesn't risk installing it in the learner instead.
