# TELL Family

Actions where the tutor states something verbally rather than showing or
having the learner produce it. TELL is the family `../decision-engine/01`'s
EXPLANATION state draws from, and it inherits that state's burst limits
directly: ≤2 sentences for beginners, ≤4 for established learners, then
the learner acts. TELL actions never legally repeat themselves in the
same frame on failure (Universal Principle 2,
`../foundations/04-universal-teaching-principles.md`) — a failed TELL
action escalates to a changed frame within TELL once, then out of the
family entirely to SHOW (`../decision-engine/05` rung 1→2).

## 6. Analogy

**What it is**: mapping the new concept onto a structure the learner
already owns, so the existing structure's logic carries over.

**Fits**: concept (`../decision-engine/04` filter 2).

**Setup cost**: LOW, if a good analogy already exists in the concept's
authored library (`../concepts/TEMPLATE.md`'s analogy-library section);
MEDIUM if one must be improvised, because an improvised analogy has not
been checked for a documented breaking point.

**Procedure**: state the mapping in one sentence, then let the learner
run it once themselves ("so if half a pizza is like this, what would a
third look like?") before moving on — an analogy stated and never
exercised is decoration, not teaching.

**The anti-analogy rule** (inherited directly, not restated in full):
every analogy has a scope of validity (Universal Principle 6) and every
concept entry names its ANTI-analogies — mappings that actively install
misconceptions for this specific concept. Never deploy an analogy the
concept's entry has flagged as a backfire, even if it is a well-known
general-purpose analogy elsewhere.

**Failure mode** (Escalation Rung 1→2, `../decision-engine/05` §2): an
analogy that needs defending twice ("well, ignore that part...") has a
broken mapping for this learner — the correct response is swapping to a
genuinely different source domain, never patching the same analogy a
third time.

## 7. Story

**What it is**: embedding the fact, convention, or concept inside a
narrative — a sequence of events with characters or a situation, rather
than a bare statement.

**Fits**: fact/convention (`../decision-engine/04` filter 2's "fact/
convention → story, direct telling, retrieval schedule" row).

**Setup cost**: LOW.

**Procedure**: the story carries the content; it is not decoration wrapped
around a separately-stated fact. A story followed by "and that's why
X is true" (restating the fact anyway) means the story wasn't doing the
work — the fact should be recoverable FROM the story alone.

**Persona note**: stories are a strong-affinity format for young children
and for learners whose behaviour profile shows story-responsiveness
(`../student-state/05 §3`); they are a weak default for learners who have
shown low affinity to narrative formats — this is measured per learner,
never assumed by age alone past early childhood.

## 8. Direct Telling

**What it is**: stating the fact, definition, or convention plainly, with
no wrapper — the simplest, cheapest action in the entire catalog.

**Fits**: fact/convention.

**Setup cost**: LOW.

**When it wins**: for genuine conventions (why a symbol is written a
particular way, what a term is called) there is no discovery to be had —
the convention is arbitrary by definition, and Direct Telling is the
honest, efficient choice (`../concepts/TEMPLATE.md`'s discovery-lesson
section: "an explicit statement of why direct instruction wins here —
convention?"). Dressing an arbitrary convention up as a discovery-worthy
mystery wastes the learner's effort on a search that has no findable
structure.

**Failure mode**: using Direct Telling for something that IS discoverable
(a genuine causal mechanism, a pattern the learner could find) skips the
understanding that construction would have built, trading a faster turn
for a shallower one.

## 9. Real-World Example Narrative

**What it is**: grounding a concept or convention in a specific, concrete
real-world situation the learner can picture — distinguished from Story
by being a single situated instance rather than a sequence with
narrative arc, and distinguished from Analogy by using a genuine instance
of the concept itself (a real fraction situation) rather than a
structurally similar but different domain.

**Fits**: concept, fact/convention.

**Setup cost**: LOW.

**Procedure**: choose a situation from the learner's own plausible life
context (their stated interests, age, or the concrete situations named
in the concept's authored library) rather than a generic textbook
scenario — a real-world example that feels arbitrary to the learner
carries little more weight than an abstract statement would have.

**Relationship to Analogy**: Real-World Example Narrative is preferred
over Analogy whenever a genuine instance of the concept exists in
ordinary life (fractions in cooking, ratios in maps) — Analogy is reached
for when no direct instance is accessible and a structurally similar
different-domain mapping is the only option. Prefer the real instance
first; it needs no scope-of-validity warning because it IS the concept,
not a stand-in for it.
