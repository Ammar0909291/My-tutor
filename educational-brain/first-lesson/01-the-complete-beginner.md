# What a Complete Beginner Actually Is

## 1. The founding correction: "knows nothing" is never literally true

A complete beginner has **zero usable formal knowledge in this domain** —
no vocabulary, no notation, no prior lessons, no educational jargon. But no
learner arrives knowing *nothing*: every human arrives with spoken language
(some), life experience, and enormous informal competence — children
fair-share sweets before any fraction lesson, produce and hear speech sounds
before any phonics lesson, and brace against bus-brakes before any physics
lesson.

This matters because **you cannot teach to nothing** — new knowledge only
attaches to existing knowledge. The complete beginner's tutor has exactly one
foundation available: *unnamed everyday experience*. The whole craft of
lesson one is finding that experience and naming it. So the operational
definition:

> A complete beginner is a learner whose relevant knowledge is entirely
> informal, entirely unnamed, and entirely unaware of itself.

The tutor's first job is not delivering content; it is locating the informal
competence that is already there and building the first formal step on it.

## 2. What may never be assumed

For a complete beginner, the assumption list is empty. Concretely banned
assumptions, each observed failing in real sessions:

- **Subject vocabulary** — including words adults consider ordinary:
  "vowel", "equation", "unit", "digit", "sentence". If lesson one needs a
  word, lesson one teaches the word (within the vocabulary limit, 02 §2).
- **Meta-educational vocabulary** — "concept", "topic", "practice",
  "assessment", "prerequisite". The tutor speaks in plain words: "thing",
  "try it", "let's see".
- **Format familiarity** — how a quiz works, what a number line is, what
  "fill in the blank" means, what the app's buttons do. The interface is a
  prerequisite too.
- **Stamina** — the ability to sit with a subject for 20 minutes is itself
  learned. Lesson one is short by design (02 §2).
- **The belief that they can learn this** — the deepest missing
  prerequisite, and the one lesson one exists to install.

## 3. Detection and verification

Do **not** run placement machinery on a claimed complete beginner. Binary
search (../assessment/02) needs a frontier to find; a true beginner's
frontier is the origin, and probing them with items is quizzing the
unteachable — the exact anti-pattern that opens file 06. Instead:

```
COMPLETE-BEGINNER CHECK (3–4 minutes, invisible)
1. CONVERSATION (2 min): "Have you ever done any of this before —
   school, home, videos, anything?" Take the history. Warm, brief,
   no follow-up interrogation.
2. ONE floor check, disguised as talk, BELOW the subject:
   - reading → oral language comfort: a 20-second chat; can they
     hear and repeat a two-syllable word? ("say 'window'")
   - mathematics → counting words: "count these for me" (3 objects
     on screen / fingers) — watching for the count-list and
     one-to-one pointing, not correctness pressure
   - physics → everyday object talk: "which of these is heavier,
     do you think?" — any engaged answer passes
3. ONE recognition probe AT the subject's first node, framed as a
   game, expected to fail softly: "do you know what sound this
   letter makes? No? Perfect — that's exactly where we start."
   The words "perfect — that's exactly where we start" are
   mandatory: the learner's first experience of not-knowing must
   be relief, not exposure.
4. Done. Start lesson one. Total questions asked: ≤3, none graded,
   none open-ended.
```

Verification is *behavioral and ongoing*, not an upfront test: lesson one
itself verifies the classification within ten minutes (see §4).

## 4. False beginners

A false beginner claims zero and has some — the anxious adult ("I'm
terrible at maths, start from nothing"), the returning school-leaver, the
self-deprecating perfectionist. Signatures during lesson one:

- **Recognition without production**: they light up at things shown but
  claim they couldn't produce them ("oh I've seen that").
- **Compressed struggle**: steps that hold true beginners for minutes clear
  in seconds; echo turns into unprompted production almost immediately.
- **Vocabulary slips**: the "complete beginner" says "denominator".

Handling rule: **run lesson one anyway, at the pace the learner sets, and
let the pace do the promotion.** Lesson one for a false beginner costs ten
pleasant minutes and ends with "you know more than you told me — that's
very common, and it means tomorrow we start further along." Never say
"you're not really a beginner" during the lesson (it reads as an accusation
of lying or as raised stakes). The under-claiming itself is calibration
data — record the direction (../assessment/04 §3); these learners
under-claim everywhere.

## 5. Returning and forgotten learners

The learner who once knew: storage strength survives disuse even when
retrieval strength is gone (Delivery 2 §8) — relearning is dramatically
faster than learning. Distinguishing them from true beginners matters
because re-teaching from zero is slow and quietly insulting:

- **Probe with cued retrieval, not questions**: give the first piece and
  watch what returns unbidden ("this letter says /m/ — and this one
  says...?"). A returning learner unlocks in cascades; a true beginner
  echoes each piece individually.
- **Say the mechanism out loud** — it is the kindest sentence they will
  hear: "this is still in there — it's rusty, not gone. Rusty comes back
  fast." Expect sudden unlocks mid-lesson and let the plan collapse
  forward when they come.
- **The forgotten learner's shame** ("I learned this twice already") is
  met with the truth: forgetting unpracticed things is what every brain
  does; relearning speed is the proof nothing was wasted.

## 6. Adults vs children as complete beginners

Same standard, different fragilities:

**The adult complete beginner** (learning to read at 40, first math since
school): the dominant force is **shame** — they have spent years hiding
this. Consequences: absolute privacy; dignity in every word (no child-voiced
praise, no cartoon framing); *name the courage once, early, plainly*:
"most people never dare to start — you did." Their asset is enormous
informal knowledge (money, time, speech, work) — lesson one anchors to it
relentlessly. Their risk is quitting after one bad session, silently;
the close script (04 §4) matters more than any content decision.

**The child complete beginner**: no shame yet — protect that; the danger is
*installing* it via visible failure, comparison, or an exasperated tone.
Assets: play as a native mode, no unlearning needed. Constraints: minutes
of stamina, not tens of minutes; concrete/physical only; behavioral
confidence signals only (they cannot self-report — ../assessment/04 §2,
07 §2). A child's lesson one that ends in laughter has succeeded even if
the sound taught was forgotten by dinner — the return visit will catch it;
the affect cannot be re-run.
