# First Lesson Principles and Hard Limits

## 1. The never-rules of lesson one

Each rule carries its why. These are absolute for lesson one; several relax
in later lessons (noted where so).

1. **Never quiz first.** Nothing has been taught; there is nothing to ask
   (Universal Principle 1). To a complete beginner every question is a test,
   and every test is failable, and lesson one has no failure budget. The
   first several minutes contain zero questions — the tutor demonstrates
   and the learner watches, then echoes. (Relaxes from lesson two: opening
   retrieval of the *previous* session becomes the norm.)
2. **Never ask open-ended questions.** "What do you know about letters?"
   asks a beginner to publicly display the size of their ignorance — the
   cruelest question format available, and the most common AI opener.
   Lesson-one questions, when they finally come, are echo ("say it with
   me"), recognition ("is this the one that says /m/?"), or two-choice.
   All three are near-unfailable and all three still carry information.
3. **Never assume vocabulary** — subject words, meta words, or format
   words (01 §2). Every term used must have been given *in this lesson*,
   inside the vocabulary budget below.
4. **Never use unexplained terminology, even in passing.** A single casual
   "vowel" or "equals" plants confusion that the learner, being a
   beginner, cannot even formulate a question about. LLM register drift is
   the observed mechanism (06 §10) — the standard here is a hard ban.
5. **Never stack concepts.** ONE new thing per lesson one. Not "letters
   have names and sounds", not "this is a 3, and 3 is odd". The Rule of
   One (Delivery 2 §5) is advisory later; in lesson one it is law —
   including its subtle violation, the forward reference ("we'll see later
   how this connects to...") which injects a second concept in a
   subordinate clause (06 §4).
6. **Never skip the demonstration.** Beginners cannot build from words
   alone — words point at experiences, and the beginner hasn't had the
   experience yet. In lesson one, demonstration precedes explanation
   without exception (04 §1). An explanation-first lesson one is a lecture
   to someone who doesn't yet speak the language.
7. **Never overload** — the numeric limits in §2 below are ceilings, not
   targets. When in doubt, halve.
8. **Never rush silence.** The beginner's pauses are longer than any other
   learner's — everything is being assembled from scratch. Wait time
   floors: 3 seconds always, 5+ after anything new (03 §4).
9. **Never mark a first attempt wrong.** At lesson one the learner cannot
   yet distinguish "my answer was wrong" from "I am wrong for this
   subject." First attempts are redirected, never graded: "almost — listen
   once more: /mmm/. Your turn." (03 §6.)
10. **Never end without a win, and never end on a failure state.**
    Universal Principle 14, with zero exceptions at lesson one: the final
    90 seconds are always an engineered success plus the close script
    (04 §4).
11. **Never let lesson one run long.** Ending while it's still fun is a
    feature: stamina is built across sessions, not within one, and the
    open loop ("next time — two more sounds") is worth more than a fourth
    activity.

## 2. The hard limits (cognitive ceilings for lesson one)

These are the Cognitive Load Engine's numbers (Delivery 2 §5) evaluated at
the beginner's boundary condition — with one critical correction: **treat
working memory as 2 slots, not 3–4.** Everything is novel to a complete
beginner — the subject, the tutor, the format, the app itself — and that
novelty overhead is paid from the same capacity before any content loads.

| Dimension | Lesson-one ceiling | Why this number |
|---|---|---|
| New concepts | **1** | The Rule of One, as law (§1.5) |
| New vocabulary | **≤3 words**, each spoken and used ≥3 times | a word met once is a word lost; three uses across the lesson is the minimum for next-session survival |
| Explanation burst | **≤2 sentences / ~30 seconds**, then the learner does something | speech is transient; two sentences is what 2 slots hold while also acting |
| Examples before any question | **≥2** | one example is an event; two is a pattern — and pattern-recognition is the only inference a beginner can run |
| Questions total | **≤6**, none open-ended, none before the first demonstration | each question, however gentle, spends attention and risk budget |
| Activities | **1–2** | switching costs are load; the second activity exists only to vary the channel on the SAME concept |
| Session length | **child: 5–10 min · adult: 15–20 min** | stamina is a prerequisite lesson one doesn't have (01 §2) |
| Visible failure budget | **1** — and lesson one is engineered so even that one is optional | ../assessment/02 §6 gives ordinary sessions two; lesson one has no trust reserve to spend |
| New-to-familiar ratio | every new item lands on a named familiar thing | teaching to nothing is impossible (01 §1); the anchor is not decoration, it is the attachment point |

## 3. The first-minute rule

**The learner does something successfully within the first 60–90 seconds.**
An echo counts ("say it with me: /mmm/ — that's it, that's the sound").
The first minute sets the session's emotional sign; a learner who has
already succeeded listens differently for the remaining minutes. Observed
AI failure: spending the first three minutes on framing, options, and
throat-clearing prose — by which point the nervous beginner's imagination
has filled the silence with dread.

## 4. What lesson one optimizes for

Priority order, explicit, because trade-offs happen mid-session:

1. **The learner returns.** (Affect, safety, the close script.)
2. **The learner believes "I can learn this."** (The first solo success.)
3. **One thing is actually learned.** (The concept, surviving to next
   session's opening retrieval.)

When forced to choose — session running long, a failure state fired,
energy fading — sacrifice from the bottom. A lesson one that taught
nothing but ended warm, with the learner intending to come back, is a
partial success and fully recoverable. A lesson one that covered its
content over a learner's quiet distress is a failure that may never get
another chance to be recovered.
