# Subject Adaptations: the First Lesson Standard Applied

The Standard applied to the three live curricula, anchored to each
subject's REAL zero-prerequisite entry nodes (verified against the live
canonical KGs — these are the nodes a complete beginner actually lands on):

| Subject | Entry node(s) (`requires: []`) | KG name |
|---|---|---|
| English | `eng.phonics.print-concepts`, `eng.phonics.phonemic-awareness` | Print Concepts; Phonemic Awareness |
| Mathematics | `math.found.mathematical-thinking` | Mathematical Thinking |
| Physics | `phys.meas.units` | SI Units and Measurement |

Full per-concept entries for these nodes belong in `../concepts/` (per its
TEMPLATE); this file defines the lesson-one treatment those entries must
honor, and what the Standard changes per subject.

## 1. English — first lesson

**The truth about English's lesson one: it is ORAL, and print is
optional.** Both entry nodes are pre-reading: phonemic awareness is
entirely by ear and mouth (no letters exist yet), and print concepts is
about the *object* of a book/text (it has a front; the squiggles carry the
words; they run left-to-right) — knowledge adults forget is knowledge.
The observed platform failure ("could not reliably teach the first lesson
of English") begins exactly here: an AI that opens English with letters,
definitions, or — worst — grammar vocabulary has skipped both real entry
nodes and started mid-curriculum.

- **Anchor**: the learner's own speech — they already produce every sound
  of their language. "You know how the m-m-moon starts? That hum? Today is
  that hum."
- **Demonstrate**: the tutor plays with a sound audibly: stretches it
  ("mmmm"), finds it in words ("mmmoon... mmmilk"), enjoys it. Twice.
- **One concept**: one sound (/m/ is the canonical first: visible on the
  lips, stretchable, hummable — it cannot be schwa-contaminated easily).
  NOT a letter — the letter is a later lesson; binding sound to symbol
  on day one stacks two concepts (AL-9) and risks the letter-name
  misconception before sound-awareness exists.
- **Guided → solo**: echo the hum → find it ("does mmmoon have the hum?
  does sun?") → the solo: learner produces a /m/-word of their own, or
  catches the hum in a word the tutor says.
- **Voice is the WHOLE channel** — this is the tree's flagship
  voice-required territory: every success and every failure is audible
  and nothing is writable. The tutor's own modeling (clipped, honest,
  unhurried sounds) is the curriculum (see the schwa discipline at
  `../concepts/english/eng.phonics.letter-sound-correspondence.md`, which
  governs from the moment letters arrive in later lessons).
- **What changes vs the universal Standard**: nothing structural — this IS
  the Standard's home ground. The vocabulary budget is spent on zero
  technical words ("sound" and "hum" are plain speech; "phoneme" never).

## 2. Mathematics — first lesson

**The entry node is `math.found.mathematical-thinking` — and for a
complete beginner the honest floor below it is counting-with-meaning**:
the count-word list, one-to-one pointing, and the last-word-is-the-answer
insight (cardinality). A six-year-old complete beginner's lesson one is
counting three real things correctly. An adult complete beginner's lesson
one for the same node is noticing-and-continuing a pattern — the node's
actual content (mathematical thinking = noticing, patterning, reasoning)
at adult dignity.

- **Anchor (child)**: their fingers and their snacks — counting already
  half-lives in their world. "How old are you? Show me on your fingers —
  you just counted!"
- **Demonstrate**: the tutor counts three objects SLOWLY, touching each
  one, and lands hard on the insight: "one... two... three. THREE
  crackers." (The touch-count models one-to-one; the emphatic last word
  models cardinality — both invisible to adults, both the actual
  content.)
- **One concept**: counting tells you HOW MANY. Not digits, not writing
  numbers, not "3 comes after 2" — those are other lessons (AL-9).
- **Manipulables are mandatory, and fingers are legitimate equipment** —
  never shamed, at any age: finger-counting is one-to-one correspondence
  running on built-in hardware, and shaming it removes the beginner's
  only tool.
- **Guided → solo**: count together touching each → learner counts with
  tutor silent → solo: "how many?" with three NEW objects — and the
  answer wanted is the cardinal ("three!"), not the recount.
- **What changes vs the Standard**: the demonstration must be *physical/
  visual* (objects, touching) where English's was purely auditory; the
  confirm step listens for the cardinality insight specifically (a
  learner who recounts when asked "so how many?" hasn't landed it — go
  back one step, no comment). Adult variant swaps objects for patterns
  but keeps every limit identical.
- **Imported dread is the default here** (05 §1 "I hate maths" —
  imported history): assume it silently even when unstated; the
  externalization line is pre-loaded, and lesson one's job is one
  unmistakable, private win.

## 3. Physics — first lesson

**The entry node is `phys.meas.units` (SI Units and Measurement) — and
taught as named, it is a trap.** "Today: SI units" opens with two
unexplained abstractions (a unit; a system of them) — AL-6 and AL-10 in
one move. The Standard's anchor rule demands the need arrive before the
names: measurement exists because bare human judgment disagrees, and
*that dispute is recreatable in 30 seconds*.

- **Anchor / need**: "How tall are you? ... How do you KNOW? If your
  friend says they're taller, how would you settle it — for sure, so
  nobody can argue?" The learner re-invents the need for an agreed
  standard before any vocabulary arrives (this is the Discovery
  Framework's need-creation step, run in miniature — full discovery
  design belongs to this node's future `../concepts/physics/` entry).
- **Demonstrate**: measure a real thing, twice, with two different
  informal units — hand-spans, then a shoe: "eight of MY hands... but
  eleven of YOUR hands? Same table! Who's right?" The problem is now
  FELT: results that depend on whose hands means nobody can agree.
- **One concept**: measuring = comparing to an agreed same-for-everyone
  thing. THAT'S a unit. The word "metre" may arrive as the name of one
  such agreement — "SI" does not arrive at all in lesson one (vocabulary
  budget: "measure", "unit", "metre" is already the full 3-word
  allowance, 02 §2).
- **Guided → solo**: measure together with an agreed unit → learner
  measures a second object → solo: learner measures one thing alone and
  states the result AS a comparison ("four pencils long").
- **What changes vs the Standard**: physics' lesson one leans hardest on
  live demonstration-with-real-objects and on need-creation-first — its
  entry concept is an *agreement*, and agreements only make sense after
  the dispute they solve. The vocabulary ceiling strains hardest here
  (three technical words is the entire budget and it is exactly spent);
  discipline is tightest.

## 4. What stays universal (all three subjects, unchanged)

- The corrected flow and its time boxes (04 §1) — anchor first,
  demonstrate before explain, echo before solo, exactly one solo,
  invisible confirm, scripted close.
- Every hard limit (02 §2): one concept, ≤3 words, 2-sentence bursts,
  ≥2 examples, ≤6 questions, failure budget of 1.
- The first-minute rule, the tutor behaviour standard (03), all
  failure-state handling (05), the entire anti-library (06).
- The completion criteria C1–C4 including next-session survival as the
  formal gate (04 §3).
- The priority inversion: return > self-belief > content (02 §4).

What adapts is only: the anchor's source domain, the demonstration's
channel (ear / hands / objects), how the vocabulary budget is spent, and
which imported feelings to expect at the door (dread for maths, shame for
adult reading, "is this hard?" wariness for physics).
