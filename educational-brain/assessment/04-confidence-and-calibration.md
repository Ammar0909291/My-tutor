# Confidence Estimation and Calibration

## 1. Knowledge state is two-dimensional

Correctness alone is half a measurement. The real state space is
**correctness × confidence**, and the four quadrants demand four different
teaching responses:

| | **Confident** | **Hesitant** |
|---|---|---|
| **Correct** | MASTERED (for this item type): the only quadrant that advances difficulty | FRAGILE: knowledge exists, trust in it doesn't. Consolidate — confirm warmly, give one more same-type item immediately; do NOT advance. Advancing on fragile knowledge builds towers on glass. |
| **Wrong** | MISCONCEPTION: the dangerous quadrant — invisible to correctness-only assessment until it's tested, and self-defending when challenged. Route to detection (03) and repair (Delivery 2 §4). Never "correct" it in passing. | AWARE GAP: the healthiest failure state — the learner's self-model is accurate. Teach the gap; *praise the calibration explicitly* ("you knew you weren't sure — that's a skill"). |

The two off-diagonal quadrants are where correctness-only assessment lies:
hesitant-correct gets falsely certified and collapses later; confident-wrong
gets missed entirely and metastasizes (Delivery 2 §4).

## 2. Estimating confidence

### Behavioral signals (free, continuous, hard to fake)

- **Response latency** — the primary signal. Fast = the answer came from a
  model or a chunk; slow = reconstruction, search, or doubt. Interpretation
  is always *joint* with correctness (the grid above).
- **Prosody** (voice-first advantage, Delivery 1): rising intonation on a
  statement ("...twelve?") is a confidence report attached to the answer at
  zero cost. Flat, quick delivery = confidence — even when wrong,
  *especially* when wrong.
- **Hedging language**: "maybe", "I think", "probably", "is it...?",
  "something like". Count and weigh; hedges on correct answers signal
  fragility.
- **Answer-changing and confirmation-seeking**: switching answers or
  checking the tutor's face before committing = low confidence regardless of
  the words.
- **Hesitation location**: mid-answer restarts localize *which part* of the
  knowledge is unsure — finer-grained than any numeric rating.

### Explicit elicitation

- **"How sure are you?"** on a simple scale (sure / think so / guessing)
  works from roughly age 8–9 upward. Younger children cannot introspect
  confidence reliably — use behavioral signals only, and use the child-safe
  framing "would you bet your points on it?" if any explicit read is needed.
- **Wager framing** for competitive personas: "how many points do you want
  to stake?" converts confidence into a number they enjoy providing.
- Explicit ratings are themselves trainable and gameable; behavioral signals
  stay honest. Where the two disagree, trust behavior.

### Elicitation discipline

Ask for confidence **before** revealing correctness, always. Confidence
reported after the reveal is hindsight, worthless for calibration data and
worthless for the learner's own calibration practice.

## 3. Calibration — the alignment of confidence with reality

A learner is calibrated when their confidence *predicts* their correctness:
of the answers they call "sure", ~all are right; of their "guessing" answers,
performance sits near chance. Calibration is measured by simply **recording
(confidence, correctness) pairs** across normal work and comparing claimed
confidence with achieved accuracy per confidence level.

Two miscalibration signatures, both persistent traits worth recording at
placement (02 §5) and both trainable:

- **Overconfidence** — "sure" answers frequently wrong. Sources: fluency
  illusions (recognition felt as knowledge), fast intuitive models
  (misconceptions are *confident* by nature), and personas that experience
  doubt as weakness. Risk: stops checking, stops reviewing ("I know this"),
  resists repair.
- **Underconfidence** — "guessing" answers frequently right. Sources:
  anxiety, repeated past failure, perfectionism. Risk: wasted review on
  known material, avoidance of attempts, chronic hedging that hides real
  knowledge from the tutor *and from the learner*.

## 4. Calibration training

Calibration is a teachable skill, and one of the highest-value ones — it
transfers to everything the learner will ever study.

1. **Predict-with-confidence loops**: before every reveal, a one-word
   confidence call; after the reveal, nothing more is needed — the
   juxtaposition itself trains. (This drops out of prediction-first teaching,
   Delivery 2 §3, for free.)
2. **Show the learner their own record.** The single most powerful
   intervention, one sentence long:
   - To the underconfident: "When you say you're guessing, you're right
     70% of the time. Your 'guesses' are knowledge. Trust them more."
     For an anxious learner this is life-changing, and it is *evidence*,
     not reassurance — it cannot be dismissed the way comfort can.
   - To the overconfident: "When you feel surest on fractions, that's
     exactly where the errors hide. On this topic, 'sure' means
     check once more." Attach the check to the *feeling* of sureness —
     the feeling becomes the trigger.
3. **Celebrate accurate hedges as loudly as correct answers.** "You said
   'not sure' and it was wrong — your radar is working perfectly" rewards
   the calibration even when the content failed. This is what makes honest
   "I'm guessing" utterances survive (Recovery Engine, Delivery 1).
4. **Never punish disclosed low confidence** with easier material as a
   visible demotion; the learner learns to hide doubt, and the confidence
   channel — the tutor's best instrument — goes dark.

## 5. Confidence rules for the assessment engine

1. Advancement requires **confident-correct**, not correct (Universal
   Principle 22; the fluency gate of 02 §2 and 05 §3).
2. Every **confident-wrong** answer is a first-class event: route to
   misconception detection immediately; never spot-correct and move on.
3. **Hesitant-correct** schedules consolidation: one immediate same-type
   item, and the concept stays at its current review interval instead of
   expanding (memory scheduling, Delivery 2 §8).
4. Calibration data is part of the mastery picture: a concept is not fully
   mastered while the learner's confidence on it is miscalibrated (05 §3 —
   verification-of-death for repaired misconceptions already requires
   confident-correct-fast).
5. Confidence signals are *per-domain*: a learner can be overconfident in
   arithmetic and underconfident in reading. Track calibration per subject
   branch, not as one global trait.
