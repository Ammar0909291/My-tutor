# Assessment Rubrics

Free responses — explanations, drawings, performances, teach-backs — carry
the richest evidence and the highest scoring subjectivity. Rubrics convert
that judgment into criteria: consistent across sessions, explainable to the
learner, and usable as *teaching targets* in their own right.

## 1. Rubric design rules (apply to every rubric below)

1. **Observable behaviors only.** "Understands deeply" is not a criterion;
   "states the mechanism and gives a self-generated example" is. If two
   honest observers could disagree, the descriptor is not finished.
2. **Levels are qualitatively distinct**, never adverb ladders.
   "Somewhat clear / clear / very clear" is one criterion wearing three
   hats; real levels describe *different behaviors*.
3. **The top level is expert behavior, not perfection.** Rubric ceilings of
   flawlessness teach perfectionism and are never reached; ceilings of
   "what an expert actually does" are reachable and honest.
4. **Show the rubric before assessing** (age-appropriately). Hidden rubrics
   measure guess-the-teacher; visible rubrics ARE instruction — the learner
   aims at the described behavior. (For young children the rubric appears
   as the tutor's modeled examples, not a table.)
5. **Analytic for teaching, holistic for gating.** Analytic (per-dimension)
   scores say *what to teach next*; a holistic judgment says *pass/not-yet*.
   Use each for its job; don't average analytic scores into meaninglessness.
6. **Every level maps to a next move.** A rubric level that doesn't tell
   the tutor what to do next is decoration.

## 2. Explanation Quality Rubric (the workhorse)

Scores any "explain it in your words" response, spoken or written, any
subject. Target for mastery gates: **level 3+** (05 §3).

| Level | Behavior | Signature sound | Tutor's next move |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Nothing, or restates the question | "It's... the thing where you..." | Not an explanation failure — a knowledge absence. Back to teaching. |
| 1 | Keyword fragments, no connections | "Denominator... flip it?" | Words without a model. Rebuild from a representation, not from vocabulary. |
| 2 | Correct **procedure** description, no mechanism | "You flip the second one and multiply." | The right-answer-wrong-depth trap: works until any variation. Ask the why-question; teach toward mechanism. |
| 3 | **Mechanism** present — says *why* it works | "Dividing by a half asks how many halves fit inside — that's why it gets bigger." | Gate-eligible. Push toward boundaries and self-generated examples. |
| 4 | Mechanism + **boundary** + own example | "...and it only reads that way because the wholes match — with different wholes you can't compare. Like, half my pizza vs half yours." | Expert behavior: knows why, where it stops, and generates instances. Certify; invite teach-back. |

Scoring notes: score the *best level consistently shown*, not the average;
vocabulary sophistication counts for nothing (a level-3 in playground words
beats a level-2 in Latin); interviewer prompts ("can you say why?") are
allowed once — the level is where the learner lands after one prompt.

## 3. Self-Explanation Rubric (during worked examples)

Scores the learner's running commentary while studying a worked solution
(08 §7). Predictive: level here forecasts isomorph success before it's tried.

- **L0 — Silent/none**: "okay... okay..." → prompt with "why that step?"
- **L1 — Paraphrase**: restates what the step did ("they multiplied both
  sides") → the step is seen, not understood; ask "why *that* move?"
- **L2 — Local why**: justifies the step from the previous line → good;
  probe whether the *goal* is visible.
- **L3 — Goal-directed why**: connects the step to the solution strategy
  ("they're isolating x, so the 3 has to go") → ready for the completion
  problem.
- **L4 — Anticipation**: predicts the next step before reading it → ready
  to skip rungs on the fading ladder.

## 4. Drawn-Model Rubric (visual assessment, 06 §2)

Four analytic dimensions, each 0–2. Score the model, never the artwork.

| Dimension | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Parts** | key components missing | components present, some invented/extra | all and only the relevant components |
| **Relations** | parts floating, unconnected | some correct connections/directions | structure right: connections, directions, proportions where they matter |
| **Labels/narration** | can't say what parts are | names parts | names parts AND their roles |
| **Predictive use** | drawing is static decoration | can point to "what happens next" somewhere | uses the drawing to run a prediction ("so if this increases, this...") |

The fourth dimension is the discriminating one: a drawing the learner can
*run* is a model; a drawing they can only *describe* is a picture. Planted
misconceptions surface here more reliably than anywhere else (03 §6).

## 5. Teach-Back Rubric (the strongest evidence format, 05 §2 level 11)

The learner teaches the concept; the tutor plays a student and, at the right
moment, **performs the concept's #1 documented misconception**.

| Dimension | Not yet | Gate-worthy |
|---|---|---|
| **Accuracy** | teaches errors | content correct throughout |
| **Completeness** | fragments; assumes the hard part | covers the load-bearing ideas in a workable order |
| **The planted misconception** | misses it, or "no, that's wrong" with no repair | *catches it AND repairs it* — explains why it's tempting and where it breaks (this is Delivery 2 §4's contrast step, performed live: the single strongest mastery behavior observable) |
| **Adaptation** | one fixed script | notices the "student's" confusion and changes representation |

## 6. Practical / Procedure Rubric (structure)

Built per-procedure from this frame; scored live via observation checklist
(06 §3):

- **Preparation** (0–2): gathers/checks what's needed; zeroes/calibrates
  instruments unprompted.
- **Execution** (0–2): steps in workable order; technique within tolerance;
  self-corrects drift.
- **Controls & safety** (0–2): keeps the constants constant; safety
  behaviors unprompted. *Safety criticals are pass/fail overrides* — one
  miss fails the practical regardless of the rest (06 §3).
- **Interpretation** (0–2): reads the result correctly; answers one
  step-why probe (08 §6) at explanation level ≥2.

## 7. Calibration Rubric (04, made scoreable)

Judged over a *window of items* (≥10 with confidence calls), not per item:

- **L0 — Uncalibrated**: confidence and correctness unrelated.
- **L1 — Directionally calibrated**: "sure" outperforms "guessing", with a
  large persistent bias (over- or under-confidence; record direction).
- **L2 — Calibrated**: confidence bands roughly match accuracy; hedges are
  informative.
- **L3 — Actively calibrated**: uses their own confidence as a *tool* —
  flags their own "sure but should check" topics, requests review where
  they feel decay. This level is a metacognitive mastery signal worth
  recording subject-wide.

## 8. Retell / Comprehension Rubric (language & story-rich subjects, 07 §3)

- **L0**: isolated details, no thread.
- **L1**: sequence of events, surface-complete ("and then... and then").
- **L2**: sequence + causal links ("she hid it *because*...").
- **L3**: causal retell + inference beyond the text (motive, prediction,
  theme) — answers questions the text never states directly.
- **L4**: L3 + stance: evaluates, connects to other texts/experience.
  Recall questions ("what color was the door?") never rise above L0–L1
  evidence and must not be scored as comprehension.
