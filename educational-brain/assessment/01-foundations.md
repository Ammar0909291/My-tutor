# Assessment Foundations

## 1. The five purposes — every item must know which one it serves

Assessment answers exactly one of five questions. An item that serves none is
waste; an item that tries to serve two usually serves both badly.

| # | Question | Name | Typical moment |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | *Where* is this learner? | Placement / diagnostic | New learner, new subject, return after absence |
| 2 | Is the *floor* solid? | Prerequisite verification | Before teaching anything that builds on it |
| 3 | What *wrong model* is running? | Misconception detection | Fast-wrong answers; systematic errors; before repair |
| 4 | Did the teaching *work*? | Formative check | During and immediately after teaching (measures the teaching, not the learner) |
| 5 | Is it *durable and transferable*? | Mastery verification | Delayed, disguised, mixed — never same-session |

The distinction between 4 and 5 is the most commonly collapsed and the most
expensive to collapse: a formative check passed five minutes after an
explanation certifies **nothing** about durable knowledge (see the
performance/learning dissociation, Delivery 2 §8). Purpose-4 items inform the
tutor's next move; only purpose-5 items may open mastery gates.

A sixth activity looks like assessment but is teaching: **retrieval practice**.
It uses the same instruments (questions) for a different goal (strengthening,
not measuring). It must be framed to the learner as practice, never judgment,
and its results feed review scheduling, not certification.

## 2. The evidence model

An assessment item is an **instrument that converts a hidden mental state into
an observable behavior**. The learner's knowledge cannot be seen; only
behavior can. Item quality is therefore exactly one thing: *how much the
observed behavior constrains the hypothesis about the hidden state.*

Consequences of taking this seriously:

- An item both the correct model and the misconception answer identically has
  **zero diagnostic value**, however "on-topic" it looks. (If the learner
  believes "multiplication makes bigger" and you ask 3 × 4, both the correct
  model and the misconception output 12. You learned nothing.)
- A *wrong* answer should tell you **why** it is wrong. Items whose wrong
  answers are uninterpretable ("not 12" — but holding which wrong model?)
  measure pass/fail when they could have measured the model. See distractor
  design (03).
- The best single probes are those where every plausible mental state produces
  a **different** observable answer — the "golden probe" ideal (03 §5).
- Behavior includes more than the answer: latency, prosody, hesitation
  location, self-correction, and drawn artifacts are all observations that
  constrain the hidden state, often more cheaply than another question
  (04, 06).

## 3. Quality metrics — how to judge any assessment item or session

1. **Validity** — does it measure the target, and only the target? The
   classic corruptions: a mathematics item that actually measures reading
   ability; a science item that measures vocabulary; an oral item that
   measures shyness; a timed item during acquisition that measures anxiety.
   Rule: strip every demand that is not the target (short stems, small
   numbers unless magnitude *is* the target, familiar contexts unless
   transfer *is* the target).
2. **Reliability** — the same learner in the same knowledge state should get
   the same result. Threats: ambiguous wording, items whose difficulty swings
   with incidental slot values (see item templates, 03 §4), mood- and
   fatigue-sensitivity of long sessions.
3. **Diagnosticity** — wrong answers map to named causes. The metric: what
   fraction of possible wrong responses can be attributed to a specific
   misconception or gap? (Multiple-choice with mapped distractors: high.
   "Show your work" production items: highest, but costlier to interpret.)
4. **Sensitivity** — the item distinguishes the states you actually care
   about. An item every learner passes, or every learner fails, has zero
   sensitivity regardless of its elegance.
5. **Efficiency** — information per question and per second. Binary-search
   placement (02) exists because of this metric; so does the stop rule
   (§ heading of this library: once the decision is clear, stop).
6. **Affect cost** — anxiety induced per item. This is a real currency: an
   item that frightens the learner *changes the state it is measuring*
   (anxiety consumes the working memory the answer needed), and it spends
   trust. Every assessment session has an affect budget (09 §5).
7. **Transfer distance** — how far the item's surface is from the taught
   surface. Distance must be *chosen*, not accidental: near for formative
   checks, far for mastery gates (05 §2).
8. **Gaming resistance** — can the item be passed by test-savviness alone?
   Tells that leak the answer (longest option, grammatical agreement,
   "all of the above") are gaming invitations; see anti-patterns below.

## 4. Failure modes — the ways assessment lies

### False mastery (the assessment says "knows"; the learner doesn't)

- **Recognition inflation**: multiple-choice and re-shown examples measure
  familiarity, which feels like and scores like knowledge but is neither
  producible nor transferable.
- **Pattern matching without model**: the learner solves the item by surface
  similarity to a remembered example ("it looks like the one where you
  flip"). Detection: change the surface, hold the structure.
- **Lucky guessing**: a 4-option item is passed by chance 25% of the time —
  and far more when distractors are weak (see anti-patterns). Confidence
  tagging (04) and two-tier items (03 §3) are the antidotes.
- **Teaching to the item**: the learner has seen this exact item (or its
  answer) before. Never reuse a taught example as an assessment item; never
  reuse a failed item in the same session (09 §4).
- **Same-session inflation**: everything answered within minutes of teaching
  is retrieval-strength performance, not storage (Universal Principle 17).

### False failure (the assessment says "doesn't know"; the learner does)

- **Vocabulary/reading barrier**: the learner has the concept and cannot
  parse the question. Especially common for young readers and second-language
  learners; the fix is oral delivery or simplified stems (06).
- **Format unfamiliarity**: first-ever encounter with a format (grid-in,
  two-tier, "select all that apply") measures format skill once, knowledge
  only afterward. Introduce formats with a throwaway practice item.
- **Anxiety flooding**: fear consumes the working memory the item needed.
  Signature: performance collapses under assessment framing but the same
  content succeeds in casual conversation. Fix: invisible assessment (07 §2)
  and the fearful-learner adaptations (07 §4).
- **Working-memory overload masquerading as ignorance**: a multi-step item
  can fail from load, not from any missing piece. Diagnosis: decompose; if
  every step passes in isolation, the failure was load — teach chunking and
  step-recording, don't reteach content.
- **Ambiguity and item defects**: the learner answered a different (valid)
  reading of the question. *Always autopsy the item before the learner*
  (09 §2).

### Measurement-corrupting effects

- **Observer effect**: assessment changes the state it measures — retrieval
  strengthens (good, but now the next measurement is of a different state),
  failure demoralizes (bad, and it contaminates the next items in the same
  session — order effects are real; put the most important probe early).
- **Fatigue**: item quality of *responses* decays across a session
  independently of knowledge. Late-session failures are ambiguous; weight
  them less, or better, keep sessions short.
- **Practice effects on reused items**: any reused item measures memory of
  the item after first use. Isomorph templates (03 §4) exist to solve this.

## 5. The anti-pattern catalog

Each entry: the practice, why it fails, what to do instead.

1. **"Do you understand?" / "Any questions?"** — Yes-bias plus the
   metacognitive trap: the learners most in need are the least able to know
   they don't understand. *Never ask; sample behavior instead* — one small
   production item outperforms any self-report.
2. **The pop-quiz ambush** — unannounced graded assessment converts every
   future lesson into a threat environment; the affect cost compounds
   forever. Retrieval practice may be spontaneous, but it is *never graded
   and never framed as a test*.
3. **Grading during acquisition** — Universal Principles 1 and 15. First
   contact with a concept is practice by definition.
4. **Trick questions** — items engineered to catch the unwary measure
   suspicion, not knowledge, and teach the learner that the tutor is an
   adversary. (Distinguish from *misconception probes*, which look "tricky"
   but are honest: they present a natural case where the wrong model fails,
   not a linguistic trap.)
5. **Double-barreled items** — two concepts in one item make failure
   undiagnosable. One concept per item, always (03 §4).
6. **Negatively-worded stems** — "Which of these is NOT..." adds a working
   memory inversion that measures load capacity, not the concept. If the
   boundary matters, ask for it positively ("find the non-example" with the
   category named).
7. **"All of the above" / "None of the above"** — destroys distractor
   diagnosticity (a choice of "all" tells you nothing about which model
   produced it) and invites test-savviness.
8. **Throwaway distractors** — absurd options nobody would choose silently
   convert a 4-option item into a coin flip and destroy the guessing
   correction. Every distractor must earn its slot by being *attractive to a
   named wrong model* (03 §2).
9. **Longest-answer / grammatical tells** — the key is longer, more
   qualified, or grammatically agrees with the stem. Audit for tells by
   asking: could a learner with zero content knowledge beat chance? If yes,
   the item leaks.
10. **Reusing the taught example verbatim** — measures episodic memory of
    the lesson. Assessment items are always isomorphs or transfers, never
    replays.
11. **Infinite same-item retries** — by attempt three the learner is
    memorizing the answer, not exercising the skill. Retry with isomorphs
    only, after intervention (09 §4).
12. **Assessing into frustration** — a flooded mind fails items it knows.
    The result is uninterpretable AND harmful. The Recovery Engine
    (Delivery 1) preempts assessment unconditionally.
13. **Public results for fearful learners** — assessment data is private by
    default; publicity is an opt-in for competitive personas only (07 §4).
14. **Speed pressure during acquisition** — speed is a *fluency-phase*
    measure (05 §3). A clock on first learning converts a comprehension task
    into a threat task.
15. **The coverage illusion** — one item per topic on a syllabus map reads
    as "everything measured." One item measures almost nothing (guessing,
    slips, luck). Depth per decision beats breadth per session: measure what
    the next teaching decision needs, well.
16. **Confusing the learner's failure with the item's failure** — items
    have defects at meaningful rates. The autopsy order is: item first,
    then affect, then knowledge (09 §2).
