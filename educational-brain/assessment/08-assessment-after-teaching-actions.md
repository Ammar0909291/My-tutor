# Assessment After Teaching Actions

**The principle**: every teaching action creates its own characteristic
*illusion of learning* — a way the learner (and the tutor) can believe
learning happened when it didn't. The assessment that follows an action must
be designed to puncture *that action's specific illusion*. Generic follow-up
questions test the wrong thing with high confidence.

**The timing rule, applying to all of them**: immediate post-action assessment
measures performance (inflated by retrieval strength and context); the honest
read is **next-session retrieval**. Schedule both; steer teaching with the
first; certify with only the second (05 §2, level 8).

## 1. After an ANALOGY

- **Illusion**: fluency with the analogy mistaken for fluency with the
  concept; and silent import of unmapped features (analogy overextension —
  misconception birth type 6, Delivery 2 §4).
- **Assess with**:
  - *Mapping items*: "in the water analogy, what plays the role of
    voltage?" — an unmapped analogy is decoration, not understanding.
  - *Boundary items*: "where does the analogy break?" — knowing the breaking
    point is part of the analogy's safe operation (every analogy ships with
    one; Delivery 1 anti-library discipline).
  - **The overextension probe** — the decisive item: a case where the
    analogy gives the WRONG answer and the concept gives the right one
    (water "runs out"; current doesn't). Passing means the learner now uses
    the concept, not the analogy. This item type is the *only* direct test
    of overextension.

## 2. After a STORY

- **Illusion**: narrative memory mistaken for structural memory — the
  learner remembers the plot vividly and the point not at all (seductive
  detail at the whole-story scale).
- **Assess with**:
  - *Strip the story*: same structure, new characters and setting — near
    transfer with a full surface swap. Structure that survives the swap was
    learned; structure that dies with the characters was entertainment.
  - *"What was the story showing?"* — the point, not the plot. Plot recall
    with no extractable point = the story needs redesign (its mechanics
    didn't carry the concept), which is a lesson-design finding, not a
    learner finding.

## 3. After a DEMONSTRATION

- **Illusion**: spectacle memory — "the can crushed itself!" remembered for
  years with zero mechanism attached.
- **Assess with**:
  - *Varied-parameter prediction*: change one thing and ask for a
    prediction — "what if the can were steel? what if we didn't heat it?"
    Mechanism answers; spectacle cannot.
  - *Mechanism explanation*, rubric-scored (10 §2): "why did it crush?" —
    scored for the causal chain, not the vocabulary.
  - The **predict–observe–explain** cycle makes this structural: the
    assessment after this demo IS the prediction before the next one.

## 4. After an ANIMATION

- **Illusion**: smooth passive comprehension — animations *feel* maximally
  clear while their transience leaves little behind (Delivery 2 §5); the
  feeling of clarity is the specific danger.
- **Assess with**:
  - *Stop-frame prediction*: pause it — "what happens next, and why?"
  - *Re-sequencing*: stills from the animation, shuffled; the learner
    orders them and justifies the order.
  - *Muted narration*: replay without sound; the learner narrates. The
    animation carried them, or they can carry the animation — this
    distinguishes which.
  - *Key-frame drawing*: draw the single most important moment from memory,
    and say why it's the important one.

## 5. After a SIMULATION

- **Illusion**: knob-fiddling mistaken for model-building; or a *local*
  overfit ("more X → more Y", learned as universal from a narrow explored
  range).
- **Assess with**:
  - *Committed prediction before the next run*: predict → run → compare.
    A learner who cannot predict has been playing, not modeling (which is
    fine as exploration — but it isn't evidence).
  - *Extreme-case items*: "what happens at zero? at the maximum?" — probes
    the model beyond the explored range, where the local overfit fails.
  - *Inverse items* — the strongest: "what settings would produce THIS
    outcome?" Inversion cannot be answered from memory of past runs; it
    requires running the model backward, which requires having a model.

## 6. After an EXPERIMENT

- **Illusion**: ritual completion — steps followed, notebook filled,
  nothing understood; or outcome-fixation ("it worked!") with no grasp of
  what the outcome *means*.
- **Assess with**:
  - *Step-why items*: "why did we keep the water amount the same?" —
    understanding of controls IS the experimental concept.
  - *Counterfactual reading*: "what would it have meant if the result had
    come out the other way?" — a learner who can't say didn't understand
    what the experiment was asking.
  - *Design-a-variation* (advanced): "how would you test whether it was the
    heat or the light?" — experimental design is the transfer form of
    experimental understanding.

## 7. After a WORKED EXAMPLE

- **Illusion**: the illusion of competence from watching — the strongest
  and best-documented of all (Universal Principle 19). Watching a smooth
  solution *feels* exactly like being able to produce one.
- **Assess with**:
  - *Completion problem immediately*: a new partial solution; the learner
    finishes it (the fading ladder's own next rung is the assessment).
  - *Full isomorph* after that: same structure, new surface, unassisted.
  - *Self-explanation during the example* is itself assessable ("why did
    that step happen?") — silence or "because that's next" predicts the
    isomorph will fail before it's even attempted.

## 8. After a GAME

- **Illusion**: mastery of the game mistaken for mastery of the concept —
  the chocolate-covered-broccoli failure (Delivery 2 §6).
- **Assess with**: the same content **outside the game format entirely**,
  plain and unskinned. Performance that collapses without the game skin is
  a *game-design verdict*: the mechanic did not embody the concept. Fix the
  game, don't blame the learner. (Performance that survives de-skinning
  validates the game as a teaching action for this concept — record that.)

## 9. After VISUAL TEACHING (diagrams, visual proofs)

- **Illusion**: the picture memorized *as a picture* — surface-locked to
  its exact orientation, labels, and colors.
- **Assess with**:
  - *Re-draw from memory, with narration* — reproduces the structure, or
    the decoration?
  - *Transformed-figure items*: rotate the triangle, relabel the axes, swap
    the colors — structure survives transformation; surface-lock dies by it.
  - *Cross-modal restatement*: say the visual proof in words (or write the
    verbal argument as a picture). Understanding lives in the translation
    (06 §2).

## 10. After GUIDED DISCOVERY

- **Illusion**: the fragile private insight — vivid in the discovery
  context, unavailable outside it (why formalization is mandatory,
  Delivery 2 §2 step 5).
- **Assess with**:
  - *Formalization check*: state the general rule, in words, unprompted by
    the playground materials.
  - *Beyond-the-playground application*: one case unlike anything in the
    discovery environment.
  - *The collision case, cold, days later*: re-present the case that broke
    their first invention. Handling it cold proves the resolution was
    internalized, not situational.

## Summary dispatch table

| Action | Its illusion | The puncturing item |
|---|---|---|
| Analogy | analogy-fluency ≠ concept-fluency | case where analogy answers WRONG |
| Story | plot memory ≠ structure memory | surface-swap; "what was it showing?" |
| Demonstration | spectacle ≠ mechanism | varied-parameter prediction |
| Animation | felt clarity ≠ retention | stop-frame prediction; muted narration |
| Simulation | exploration ≠ model | inverse item; extreme case |
| Experiment | ritual ≠ understanding | step-why; counterfactual reading |
| Worked example | watching ≠ producing | completion → full isomorph |
| Game | game skill ≠ concept | de-skinned same content |
| Visual | picture ≠ structure | transformed figure; cross-modal restatement |
| Discovery | insight-in-context ≠ owned rule | formalization; cold collision case |
