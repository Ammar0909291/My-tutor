# Assessment by Modality: Oral, Visual, Practical

The governing rule for every modality choice: **never let modality skill
contaminate content measurement** (validity, 01 §3.1). A shy learner failing
an oral item, a clumsy drawer failing a visual item, and a slow reader
failing a written item are all the same defect: the instrument measured the
channel, not the concept.

## 1. Oral / voice assessment

Voice is the richest assessment channel that exists: every answer arrives
with its own confidence report and affect report attached (the latency ×
correctness grid, prosody, hedging — Delivery 1; 04 §2). For some content it
is the *only* valid channel: decoding, pronunciation, oral fluency in a
language — a child can read "cat" aloud wrongly, and no typed interface can
even see the error.

### Techniques

- **Think-aloud protocol** — the gold standard for *process* assessment:
  "solve it out loud, tell me everything you're thinking." Reveals the
  strategy, the step where load spikes (hesitation location), the exact wrong
  move — a full model X-ray from one item. Cost: it adds load itself, so
  never combine think-aloud with time pressure or with new content.
- **Explain-back** — "tell me how it works, in your words." Scored against
  the explanation rubric (10 §2); the phrase "in your words" is load-bearing,
  since verbatim replay is recognition, not understanding.
- **Teach the confused character** — for children: the tutor voices a
  puppet/character who holds a planted misconception; the child corrects it.
  Assessment disguised as play — the child never experiences a test, and
  correcting a planted error is level-11 evidence in miniature (05 §2).
- **Oral counting / decoding runs** — voice-required content (Delivery 1,
  seed concept 2): nonsense-word reading, stretched blending, counting past
  boundary numbers ("...29, 30" vs "...29, twenty-ten").

### Design rules for voice items

- **Short stems.** Speech is transient; a two-clause question is held in the
  learner's working memory while they think — that's the tutor spending the
  learner's slots (Delivery 2 §5). One clause, one question.
- **No multi-part questions.** "And also..." in a voice item guarantees the
  first part is lost.
- **Announce the answer format**: "just the number", "one word". Removes
  format-guessing load and makes latency interpretable.
- **Repetition on request is always free** — and is itself a signal:
  occasional repeats = load; systematic repeats = stalling for time
  (disambiguate by pattern and by what follows).
- **The 3-second wait applies doubly.** In assessment, barging into thinking
  silence doesn't just steal thinking — it corrupts the latency measurement.
- **What voice measures poorly**: multi-step written manipulation, spelling,
  notation fluency, anything needing persistent external state. Route those
  to visual/written; a voice tutor that forces algebra through the ear is
  choosing its convenience over validity.

## 2. Visual assessment

- **Learner-drawn diagrams** — the model X-ray (Delivery 2 §6, Drawing):
  "draw what's happening." Misconceptions appear in drawings that speech
  conceals (current drawn as dots shrinking along the wire; fractions drawn
  as unequal pieces; force arrows on a resting book). Scored with the
  drawn-model rubric (10 §4). Guard: the learner narrates the drawing —
  score the *model*, never the draftsmanship.
- **Re-draw from memory** — after visual teaching: reproduces the *structure*
  or just the picture? (08 §9).
- **Point-to / annotate** — "point to where it breaks", "mark the error in
  this diagram", "label the part that does X". Near-zero production load,
  high diagnosticity; ideal for young learners and low-affect sessions.
- **Representation matching and translation** — graph ↔ equation ↔ words
  ↔ table. Translation between representations is where understanding lives
  (Delivery 2 §6, Matching); one-way success with reverse failure is a
  precise, common finding (can plot from equation, cannot read equation from
  plot) and names exactly what to teach next.
- **Error-spotting in a provided visual** — a diagram with one planted flaw.
  Detecting others' errors requires running the correct model (level-11
  logic in visual form) — but only plant flaws AFTER the correct schema is
  stable, or the flaw may be learned (Delivery 2 §6, Error Analysis guard).

## 3. Practical assessment

For physical procedures — lab technique, instrument handling, construction,
measurement — practical performance is the **only** valid assessment. You
cannot quiz your way to knowing whether someone can titrate, saw straight, or
measure with a ruler (the classic failure: students who ace measurement
quizzes and start measuring from the ruler's edge, not the zero mark).

- **Performance tasks** with an observation checklist: the checklist scores
  *process points* (did they zero the scale? check units? repeat the
  measurement?), not just the product. Products can be right by luck;
  process is the knowledge.
- **Rubric-scored** (10 §6): observable behaviors per level, shown to the
  learner beforehand.
- **The "why" interleave**: during the performance, one or two "why did you
  just do that?" probes — practical skill with no mechanism behind it is
  ritual, and rituals break the first time conditions vary (08 §7).
- **Safety-critical steps are gate items**: a single missed safety behavior
  fails the practical gate regardless of everything else — the one place
  this library permits a single item to be decisive.

## 4. Choosing the modality

```
MODALITY DISPATCH
├─ Is the content voice-required (decoding, pronunciation,
│  oral fluency)?            → ORAL, no substitute
├─ Is it a physical procedure? → PRACTICAL, no substitute
├─ Is the target a MODEL of a system/structure?
│                            → VISUAL (drawn model) + oral narration
├─ Is it a multi-step symbolic procedure?
│                            → WRITTEN/VISUAL (persistent state),
│                              with think-aloud if process matters
├─ Is it conceptual understanding?
│                            → ORAL explain-back (rubric-scored),
│                              cross-checked once in a second
│                              modality (§ below)
└─ Default for continuous micro-diagnosis: ORAL — cheapest,
   richest signals, no context switch out of conversation.
```

**The cross-modal check**: mastery evidence should include at least one item
in a modality *other than the teaching modality*. Knowledge that answers only
in the channel it was taught in is surface-locked (08 §9) — a real and common
finding, and detecting it costs exactly one item.

**Modality accommodation vs measurement**: switching modality to *accommodate*
(oral for the dyslexic reader, written for the anxious speaker) is validity
protection. But when the modality *is* the target (reading assessment for the
dyslexic reader), accommodate everywhere else and measure the target honestly,
gently, and privately — never quietly drop the target itself.
