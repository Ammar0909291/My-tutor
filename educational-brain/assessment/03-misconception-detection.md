# Misconception Detection, Distractor Design, and Question Generation

## 1. The probe principle

A misconception probe is an item on which **the correct model and the
misconception produce different answers**. That single sentence is the entire
science; everything else is engineering.

Design procedure, always in this order:

1. Open the concept's misconception library (per the Canonical Per-Concept
   Schema, Delivery 1; birth taxonomy in Delivery 2 §4).
2. For each misconception M, *run M mentally* on candidate situations until
   you find one where M's output diverges from the correct output.
3. That situation is the probe. If one situation separates several
   misconceptions at once — each producing a *distinct* wrong answer — you
   have found a **golden probe** (§5).

An item authored content-first ("write a question about fractions") instead of
divergence-first will, with high probability, be answered identically by the
right model and the common wrong models — an item with zero diagnostic value
that still *looks* rigorous (01 §2).

## 2. Distractor design — the science of wrong options

In selected-response items, the distractors are not filler; **the distractors
are the diagnosis**. Rules:

1. **One distractor = one named misconception.** Every wrong option is the
   answer some documented wrong model actually produces, and choosing it is
   recorded as evidence *for that model* — this is what turns practice into
   free continuous diagnosis (02 §7).
2. **Source distractors from real learner errors**, never from imagination.
   The misconception library, learner work, and the birth taxonomy
   (overgeneralization, intuition, language contamination, notation-induced,
   instruction-induced, analogy overextension) are the quarries. Imagined
   distractors are reliably too absurd to attract anyone.
3. **The attractiveness criterion**: to a learner *holding* misconception M,
   the M-distractor should look **more right than the key**. A distractor
   that even the misconceiving learner avoids has failed at its one job.
4. **No throwaway options.** Each absurd option converts the item toward a
   coin flip and destroys interpretability (01 §5.8). Three strong options
   beat four with a dud.
5. **No tells**: uniform option length, grammatical agreement with the stem
   for all options, no "all/none of the above", key position randomized.
6. **Plausible-to-the-wrong, transparent-to-the-right**: the ideal item is
   one the correct model answers instantly and confidently — difficulty
   should come from the concept, never from decoding the question.

### Worked example of a fully mapped item

Concept: fraction magnitude. Documented misconceptions: (a) "bigger
denominator = bigger fraction" (whole-number reasoning transferred), (b)
"fewer pieces = bigger" over-applied without size-of-whole care, (c) gap
thinking ("1/3 and 1/4 are close because 3 and 4 are close").

> Which is the largest? **A.** 1/3 **B.** 1/4 **C.** they are equal
> **D.** it depends on the whole
>
> - Key: A (for the same whole).
> - B ← misconception (a): 4 > 3, so 1/4 "must" be bigger. Choosing B is
>   evidence of whole-number reasoning — route to the repair path for (a).
> - C ← misconception (c): "they're basically the same." Route to magnitude
>   work on the number line.
> - D ← a *sophisticated* distractor: it is the truth when wholes differ, and
>   selecting it invites a follow-up ("we're sharing the same cake") that
>   distinguishes deep understanding from evasion. Advanced learners choosing
>   D with that justification are demonstrating mastery, not error — the
>   follow-up disambiguates.

## 3. Two-tier items — catching right-answer-wrong-reason

Tier 1 asks the question; tier 2 asks **why**, from a menu of reasons that is
itself misconception-mapped. This catches the large population that reaches
correct answers through wrong models (pattern-matching, lucky heuristics,
"the bigger number goes on top because it looks right").

> Tier 1: Does a heavy ball fall faster than a light one (no air)?
> — No (correct).
> Tier 2: Why? **A.** gravity pulls all objects equally hard **B.** gravity's
> extra pull on the heavy ball is exactly eaten by its extra inertia
> **C.** air pushes them the same
>
> A learner answering "No" + A holds a wrong model that will fail later
> (gravity pulls harder on heavier things — B is why they fall together).
> Correctness-only assessment certifies them; the two-tier item catches them.

Use two-tier items at mastery gates (05) and after misconception repair
(the "contrast" step's understanding is exactly tier-2 content).

## 4. Question-generation principles (authoring at scale)

1. **Evidence target first.** Before writing a word: *which two mental states
   must this item distinguish?* If the answer is "none in particular", the
   item should not exist.
2. **One concept per item.** Compound items are undiagnosable (01 §5.5). To
   assess integration of two concepts, that integration must itself be the
   named target, with single-concept items available to decompose a failure.
3. **Minimal incidental load.** Short stems; familiar vocabulary; small
   numbers unless magnitude is the target; context only when context is the
   point. Every incidental demand subtracts validity (01 §3.1).
4. **Production beats selection when interpretation is affordable.** Free
   response reveals the model in the learner's own structure; selection
   scales and self-diagnoses via distractors. Standing pattern: **selection
   for continuous diagnosis, production for gates** (05).
5. **Isomorph templates.** Author items as schemas with slots — contexts,
   names, numbers — yielding unlimited fresh isomorphs (solves item reuse,
   01 §4). CONSTRAINT DISCIPLINE: slot values change difficulty. Crossing a
   place-value boundary, producing a negative intermediate, a zero, or a
   carry can turn an easy item hard. Every template lists its
   difficulty-relevant slot constraints explicitly, or reliability dies
   (01 §3.2).
6. **Surface rotation by design.** Templates carry several unlike surface
   contexts (money, sport, cooking, travel) so transfer distance is a
   *dial*, not an accident: near-surface for formative checks, far-surface
   for gates (05 §2).
7. **Never the taught example.** The lesson's example is burned for
   assessment forever; it now measures episodic memory only.
8. **Write the follow-up with the item.** Every diagnostic item ships with
   its one-line follow-ups: what to ask on each distractor, what to ask
   after a correct-but-slow answer. Items without follow-ups produce
   verdicts; items with follow-ups produce diagnoses.

## 5. Golden probes

A golden probe is a single item that near-perfectly separates the correct
model from the dominant misconceptions — every plausible mental state gives a
different answer. They are rare, precious, and worth deliberate search for
every load-bearing concept.

Canonical exemplars:

- **8 + 4 = ☐ + 5** (equals sign). Correct model → 7. Operator
  misconception → 12 ("compute the left side"). "Add everything" variant →
  17. Three states, three answers, one item. (Delivery 1, seed concept 1.)
- **Reading the nonsense word "zat"** (decoding). Decodes → blending works,
  regardless of vocabulary. Refuses or substitutes a real word → guessing
  habit or blending gap. One utterance separates decoding from memorization.
  (Delivery 1, seed concept 2.)
- **"A bat and a ball cost £1.10; the bat costs £1 more..."** —
  the general shape: intuition shouts a confident wrong answer (10p) while
  the model computes 5p. Any item with this shape probes whether the learner
  *checks intuition against the model* — a metacognitive golden probe.

Hunting heuristic: golden probes live where the misconception's output is
*specific* (a number, a word), not just "wrong". Ask: "what exactly would the
wrong model say here?" — when the answer is a crisp, distinct value, you have
a probe; when it's vague, keep hunting.

## 6. Detection beyond items — the free channels

Misconceptions leak constantly through channels cheaper than questions:

- **Fast-wrong answers** (latency grid): a confident error is *never* a slip;
  it is a model's output. Every fast-wrong answer routes to detection.
- **Systematic error patterns** in ordinary work: the same wrong move
  repeated across varied items is a model; scattered unrelated errors are
  slips. Only the first deserves a probe.
- **Explanation language**: "the current gets weaker along the wire", "the
  answer to 8+4", "multiply makes it bigger" — misconception phrases said
  aloud during normal talk are detections, zero items required. The
  per-concept libraries should list each misconception's *characteristic
  phrases* for exactly this purpose.
- **Drawn models** (06 §3): a learner's diagram of "what's happening" shows
  the model directly — dots getting smaller along the wire is the
  used-up-current misconception, visible.
- **Self-corrections in speech**: "it's nine— wait, six" exposes the
  competing models mid-switch; log both the error and the repair.

Every detection, from any channel, routes to the same place: the concept's
misconception entry, then the 7-step repair sequence (Delivery 2 §4), then
delayed disguised re-verification (05 §4, 09 §4).
