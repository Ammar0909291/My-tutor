## Learning Objective

The learner can: (1) identify which of the 5 mathematical-thinking moves
(Notice, Conjecture, Test, Counterexample, Argument) they are using at a
given moment; (2) apply at least one move explicitly to a novel,
non-numeric situation; (3) distinguish mathematical THINKING from
mathematical CONTENT RECALL when given paired examples — a learner who
produces correct numeric answers without naming a single thinking move
has not achieved mastery (per the Blueprint's own observable criterion).

## Core Understanding

Mathematics is not a body of memorized facts and procedures; it is a
disciplined process — noticing a regularity, conjecturing that it always
holds, testing the conjecture against new cases, hunting for a
counterexample that breaks it, and constructing an argument for why it
must hold universally. Formulas and theorems are the OUTPUT of this
process, not its starting material. A learner who has memorized every
formula in a textbook but never noticed a pattern, formed a conjecture,
or asked "is this always true?" has not yet done mathematics — they have
only stored its residue.

## Mental Models

- **Beginner model — "math is content I recall"**: mathematics is a
  collection of facts and procedures to be remembered and applied. This
  is the arriving (pre-instruction) model for most learners, not a
  tutor-installed first step — it is actively wrong and must be
  replaced, not built upon. Shelf-life warning to deliver at
  installation: "everything you already know — times tables, formulas —
  stays true. We're adding a new layer underneath it: where did those
  facts come from?"
- **Intermediate model — "math is noticing + guessing + checking"**: the
  learner can run the Notice → Conjecture → Test loop on a concrete
  sequence, but does not yet distinguish testing a conjecture from
  proving it. Upgrade trigger: the first time a conjecture survives many
  tests but the learner is asked "are you SURE it always holds?" and has
  no answer beyond "I tried a lot of cases."
- **Advanced model — "conjectures need proof or a counterexample, not
  just testing"**: the learner holds all 5 moves as a genuine toolkit,
  understands that no finite number of confirming tests proves a
  universal claim, and actively hunts for counterexamples as a distinct,
  valuable move (not a failure).
- **Expert model — "content is a compressed record of thinking"**: every
  theorem the learner ever memorizes is now read as a compressed trace of
  someone else's Notice-Conjecture-Test-Counterexample-Argument cycle;
  new content is evaluated by asking "what pattern led here?" rather than
  accepted as a given.
- **Do not upgrade early**: a learner who cannot yet reliably notice a
  pattern in a concrete sequence should not be pushed into constructing
  formal arguments — the Blueprint's own Protocol A sequence (concrete
  pattern experience before naming the moves, before formalization)
  reflects this ordering discipline directly.

## Why Students Fail

Nearly every learner arrives with years of schooling that rewarded
correct answers and fast recall, never asked "why is this always true,"
and never modeled a working mathematician's actual process. The failure
is not a knowledge gap — it is a category error the learner doesn't know
they're making: they believe they are already doing "all of mathematics"
whenever they compute correctly, because no one ever separated the
process (thinking) from its product (content) in front of them. This
means the concept cannot simply be added to what the learner knows; the
mis-set category itself (math = content) must be surfaced and replaced
before the 5-move process can land as something new rather than
something already covered.

## Analogies

- **Best analogy — a mathematician vs. a formula-user facing the same
  new problem** (the Blueprint's own TA-B02 comparative): both know
  equally many formulas; only the one who notices, conjectures, and
  tests can make progress on a problem with no matching formula.
  Breaking point: over-applied, this analogy can make formula-knowledge
  sound worthless — it isn't; formulas are still useful, they are just
  not the thinking itself.
- **Alternative — detective vs. suspect list**: a detective who only
  checks names off a pre-given suspect list (recall) versus one who
  notices an inconsistency and forms a hypothesis (conjecture) then
  looks for evidence that breaks it (counterexample-hunting). Breaking
  point: real detective work doesn't have a clean "argument" stage
  analogous to mathematical proof — retire before formalizing Move 5.
- **ANTI-ANALOGY — do NOT frame the 5 moves as "steps in a recipe" to
  follow in fixed order**: this directly re-installs MC-4 (one correct
  method). The Blueprint is explicit that the moves are tools usable in
  any order, not a sequence — an anti-analogy this entry flags because
  the "recipe" framing is a natural, tempting shorthand for a tutor under
  time pressure.

## Discovery Questions

The Blueprint's own TA-A02→TA-A03 sequence IS the discovery design for
this concept: **Need** — "what's next after 1, 3, 6, given no rule
stated?" **Playground** — the learner examines differences between
consecutive terms. **Invention** — the learner proposes a next-term
guess with a reason. **Collision** — a wrong guess is met with the
actual Group 4 count (10), forcing reconciliation. **Formalization** —
naming what was just done as "Notice" and "Conjecture." **Compression** —
immediate reapplication to a fresh sequence. This is a genuine discovery
concept: the 5 moves themselves are best discovered by using them, not
told about in advance.

## Teaching Sequence

Concrete pattern experience must precede formal naming of the 5 moves —
the Blueprint's own Protocol A never states "Notice, Conjecture, Test..."
before the learner has already noticed, conjectured, and tested on the
triangular-dot sequence (TA-A02→TA-A03 precede TA-A04's naming). The
diagnostic battery (DQ-1 through DQ-3) must run in full before the
5-move model is revealed at all — revealing the model early would let a
learner with MC-1 active simply recite the moves without believing them,
producing a false-positive mastery signal. This is the concept's
governing sequence logic, one level above the specific Tutor Actions
dispatch below: diagnose → experience → name → formalize → apply, never
name-then-experience.

## Tutor Actions

From `../../teaching-actions/`, in the order the Blueprint's Protocol A

5-move model is ever revealed).

## Voice Teaching Notes

Listen for MC-1 phrasing surfacing unprompted in casual conversation
("I'm just not good at remembering formulas") — this is a stronger and
earlier signal than any formal probe. Latency on "when you picture a
mathematician working, what are they doing?" (Blueprint DQ-1) is itself
diagnostic: a fast, confident "solving equations" answer is a more
entrenched MC-1 than a hesitant one. The load-bearing sentence: "formulas
are the OUTPUT of mathematical thinking, not the thinking itself" —
slow, once, and returned to whenever a repair stalls.

## Tutor Recovery Strategy

Likeliest utterance: "I'm not a math person" (MC-3's verbal signature) —
this is the S6 Protocol's entry condition, not a standalone recovery
event; per the Blueprint, replace P28 (productive struggle) with P54
(worked-example provision) throughout, and never use misconception-naming
language (P27) for an S6+MC-3 learner unless they explicitly assert it as
a factual claim. The concept-specific smaller question when a learner
freezes on "notice something": "what do you notice about your
surroundings right now — anything at all" (Blueprint TA-D01) — the
lowest possible floor for the Notice move, deliberately content-free.

## Transfer Connections

- **Near**: a fresh sequence or situation requiring the same 5-move
  cycle (Notice, Conjecture, Test, Counterexample, Argument).
- **Far**: recognizing the same 5-move structure in a domain that looks
  nothing like a mathematics classroom — the Blueprint's own P76 probe
  (a biologist observing every mammal is warm-blooded and hypothesizing
  it holds for all mammals) is Notice→Conjecture performed outside
  mathematics entirely.
- **Real-world**: any claim encountered in daily life ("this always
  happens when...") treated as a conjecture to be tested rather than
  accepted, per the Blueprint's own everyday-life generation probe
  (P77).
- **Expert transfer**: the learner spontaneously asks "is this always
  true, or just in the cases I've seen?" of ANY claim — mathematical or
  not — without being prompted; this is the strongest evidence this
  root node ever produces, since it shows the 5-move disposition has
  become a general habit of mind rather than a mathematics-classroom
  skill.
