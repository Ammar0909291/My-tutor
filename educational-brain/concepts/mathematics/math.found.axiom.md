# Axiom — `math.found.axiom`

## Identity

- **Concept ID**: `math.found.axiom` (canonical mathematics KG)
- **Curriculum location**: mathematics / foundations
- **Prerequisites**: `math.found.logic` — the load-bearing part is the
  notion of a truth value (TRUE/FALSE) and of proof; an axiom is
  specifically a logical statement whose truth value is STIPULATED true
  by assumption rather than established by proof.
- **Unlocks** (from KG): `math.found.theorem`, `math.found.axiomatic-
  system` — a theorem is a statement proven FROM axioms; an axiomatic
  system is the whole structure of a starting axiom set plus everything
  derivable from it.
- **Difficulty**: developing · **Bloom**: understand · **Mastery
  threshold**: 0.80 · **Est. hours**: 3

## Learning Objective

The learner can: state that an axiom is a statement accepted as true
without proof, functioning as a starting point for a mathematical
system rather than a conclusion; explain why an axiom does not need to
be "obvious" to be legitimate (only useful and consistent with the
system's other axioms); and distinguish an axiom from a theorem (which
requires proof) even when both are equally certain within the system.

## Core Understanding

An axiom is not a fact so self-evident it doesn't need proof — it is a
STARTING POINT, chosen (not discovered) as the foundation a mathematical
system builds upward from. Whether an axiom "seems obvious" is
irrelevant to its logical role; what matters is that the system's
theorems are proven FROM it, and that it is never itself the target of
a proof within that system. This is why Euclid's notoriously
non-obvious fifth postulate (the parallel postulate) is just as
legitimate an axiom as his first four intuitively "self-evident" ones —
and why replacing it with a different postulate produces an equally
legitimate, different geometry (spherical or hyperbolic), rather than a
"wrong" one.

## Mental Models

- **Beginner model — "an axiom is a statement so obvious it needs no
  proof"**: the learner expects every axiom to be immediately,
  intuitively self-evident, matching how school geometry historically
  presented Euclid's first four postulates. This is the arriving,
  incomplete default rather than a useful first step. Shelf-life
  warning: "some axioms will feel completely obvious — but you're about
  to meet one, just as legitimate, that doesn't feel obvious at all."
- **Intermediate model — "an axiom is a stipulated starting point,
  obviousness optional"**: the learner accepts that an axiom's role
  (unproven foundation) doesn't require it to feel intuitively true, and
  can identify axioms that are genuinely non-obvious (the parallel
  postulate) as still fully legitimate. Upgrade trigger: presented with
  the parallel postulate's relative complexity compared to Euclid's
  other four.
- **Advanced model — "axioms are chosen, not proved, and changing them
  changes the system"**: the learner understands that axioms are a
  DELIBERATE CHOICE defining which mathematical system is being built —
  and that choosing a different, equally consistent axiom (e.g. a
  different parallel postulate) produces a different, not wrong, system.
- **Expert model — "axioms are evaluated by consistency and
  usefulness, never by 'truth'"**: the learner understands axioms are
  judged by whether the resulting system is internally consistent
  (never derives a contradiction) and mathematically fruitful, not by
  correspondence to an external "truth" — the same understanding this
  domain's `math.found.definition` concept establishes for definitions,
  now applied to axioms.
- **Do not upgrade early**: a learner who still expects every axiom to
  be obvious (beginner model) should not be pushed into comparing
  different axiom CHOICES for the same role (advanced model) — the
  Blueprint's own Protocol A sequence addresses the
  AXIOM-AS-OBVIOUS misconception (MC-1) before any axiom-choice
  reasoning is introduced.

## Why Students Fail

The dominant failure, per the Blueprint's own MAMR-foundational
misconception, is a direct consequence of how school geometry is
historically taught: Euclid's first four postulates ARE genuinely
intuitive (a straight line can be drawn between any two points, etc.),
and when these are the only axioms a learner has ever seen, "axioms are
obviously true statements" becomes an entirely reasonable induction —
one that fails the moment a learner meets an axiom (like the parallel
postulate, or any of a modern axiom system's less intuitive members)
that isn't obvious at all.

## Misconceptions

Reused from the Blueprint's Component 2 Misconception Registry (MC-1
through MC-3) by reference, with birth-type classification added:

- **MC-1 — "AXIOM-AS-OBVIOUS" (Type 1, overgeneralization from Euclid's
  first four postulates, genuinely intuitive, extended to axioms in
  general)**: MAMR-foundational. Full trigger/root-cause/error-pattern:
  Blueprint Component 2, MC-1 (the parallel-postulate rejection
  pattern).
- **MC-2 — "AXIOM-AS-PROVABLE" (Type 1, overgeneralization — the
  learner's general expectation that mathematical statements are always
  eventually provable, extended past where the axiom/theorem boundary
  holds)**: full content: Blueprint Component 2, MC-2.
- **MC-3 — "AXIOM-AS-THEOREM" (Type 6, analogy/prior-experience
  overextension — axioms and theorems are both "true mathematical
  statements" on the surface, and the learner extends theorem-like
  reasoning to axioms)**: full content: Blueprint Component 2, MC-3.
  MAMR note (Blueprint's own): MC-2 and MC-3 are addressed FIFO after
  MC-1 is cleared, since both depend on the same foundational repair.

## Analogies

- **Best analogy — the starting square of a board game**: every game
  needs an agreed starting position that isn't itself a MOVE within the
  game — it's simply where play begins, by agreement, not derived from
  earlier moves. An axiom is a mathematical system's starting position.
  Breaking point: game starting positions are usually arbitrary by
  design; axioms are chosen deliberately for their downstream
  mathematical consequences, not arbitrarily.
- **Alternative — the rules of a legal system's founding constitution**:
  a constitution's provisions aren't "proven" from more basic law — they
  are the foundation everything else is judged against. Breaking point:
  constitutions can be amended by processes the document itself
  specifies; axioms, by contrast, define the system — changing one
  produces a genuinely DIFFERENT system, not an amended version of the
  same one.
- **ANTI-ANALOGY — do NOT say "an axiom is just a really strong, really
  certain fact"**: "certain fact" reintroduces exactly the AXIOM-AS-
  OBVIOUS framing (MC-1) — an axiom's status comes from its STRUCTURAL
  ROLE as an unproven starting point, not from any special degree of
  certainty or obviousness.

## Demonstrations

- **Euclid's-five-postulates demonstration**: presenting all five side
  by side, with the learner rating each for "obviousness" — the first
  four score high, the fifth (parallel postulate) scores noticeably
  lower, directly surfacing the MC-1 collision.
- **Non-Euclidean substitution demonstration**: showing that replacing
  the parallel postulate with a different one produces a different,
  internally consistent geometry (rather than a broken one) — grounds
  the advanced "axioms are chosen, not discovered" model concretely.
- **Axiom-vs-theorem sorting**: a mixed list of true mathematical
  statements from a system, sorted by whether each is a foundational
  axiom or a derived theorem — directly tests MC-3.

## Discovery Questions

**Need** — given Euclid's five postulates and asked to rate each for
intuitive obviousness, the learner rates the first four high and the
fifth noticeably lower, then is asked: "if axioms must be obvious, is
the fifth one even a legitimate axiom?" **Playground** — the learner
considers what would happen to geometry without the fifth postulate.
**Invention** — the learner proposes that the fifth postulate might
still be a legitimate starting assumption even though it doesn't feel
as obvious. **Collision** — shown that mathematicians for 2,000 years
tried and failed to PROVE the fifth postulate from the other four
(exactly what the AXIOM-AS-PROVABLE misconception would predict should
be possible), and that it was eventually shown independent — genuinely
unprovable from the others. **Formalization** — naming what makes a
statement an axiom (stipulated, not proven, a starting point) explicitly,
independent of obviousness. **Compression** — sorting a fresh set of
statements from an unfamiliar axiom system into axioms vs. theorems.

## Teaching Sequence

MC-1 (axiom-as-obvious) must be repaired before MC-2 and MC-3 are
addressed, per the Blueprint's own MAMR ordering — a learner who still
expects axioms to be self-evidently true has no stable concept of
"stipulated starting point" to build the provability distinction (MC-2)
or the axiom/theorem distinction (MC-3) on top of.

## Tutor Actions

From `../../teaching-actions/`: **Demonstration** (the five-postulates
obviousness-rating task) → **Worked Example** (the 2,000-year failed-
proof-attempt history of the parallel postulate, used to defeat MC-2
concretely) → **Classification/Sorting** (axiom-vs-theorem sorting,
targeting MC-3). **What doesn't fit**: introducing non-Euclidean
geometry's full technical content — this concept only needs the fact
that a different, consistent geometry results from substituting the
axiom, not the geometry itself.

## Voice Teaching Notes

Listen for "that's not really an axiom, it's too complicated" applied to
a non-obvious axiom — this is MC-1's clearest verbal signature. A
learner who asks "but couldn't we prove that from something simpler?"
about a genuinely independent axiom is showing MC-2. The load-bearing
sentence: "an axiom's job is to be a starting point, not to be obvious."

## Assessment Signals

The Blueprint's own P41 detection probes and mastery gate (P91,
Component covering TA-A05) are the concrete item bank — not re-authored
here. Diagnostic interpretation this entry adds: a learner who correctly
identifies the parallel postulate as a legitimate axiom (MC-1 cleared)

non-provability are separable steps — both must be checked
independently before certifying full mastery.

## Tutor Recovery Strategy

Likeliest utterance: "but WHY do we just assume that's true?" facing a
non-obvious axiom — the concept-specific smaller question: "does every
starting point in a system need a reason outside the system, or can a
system simply choose where it starts?" — reframes the question from
seeking a justification (which axioms structurally don't have) to
recognizing the nature of a starting choice.

## Memory Hooks

**Type**: concept (a structural-role recognition skill: axiom vs.
theorem, regardless of content). Review form: sorting fresh statements
from unfamiliar axiom systems, never rote recall of a specific axiom
list. Interleaving partners: `math.found.definition` and
`math.found.theorem` (once available) — all three are "kinds of
mathematical statement" with genuinely different structural roles, and
mixed sorting practice across all three (as already recommended in the
`math.found.definition` entry) is the strongest test the categories
haven't blurred.

## Transfer Connections

- **Near**: sorting a fresh set of statements by axiom vs. theorem
  status within a given system.
- **Far**: recognizing "founding, unquestioned assumptions" in
  non-mathematical formal systems (a legal constitution's provisions, a
  game's starting rules).
- **Real-world**: noticing when an everyday disagreement is actually
  about differing unstated starting assumptions rather than a
  disagreement about derived conclusions.
- **Expert transfer**: the learner, meeting an unfamiliar mathematical
  system, spontaneously asks "what are its axioms?" before asking what
  its theorems say — recognizing that the axioms determine everything
  else.
