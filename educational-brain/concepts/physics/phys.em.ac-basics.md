# AC Basics: RMS Values and Phasors — `phys.em.ac-basics`

## Identity

- **Concept ID**: `phys.em.ac-basics` (canonical physics KG)
- **Curriculum location**: physics / electromagnetism — dependency level 7.
- **Prerequisites**: `phys.em.ohms-law`, `phys.em.faradays-law` — AC circuit
  analysis applies Ohm's-law-style reasoning to time-varying voltages/currents
  generated by the same electromagnetic-induction mechanism already
  established in Faraday's law (e.g. a rotating coil generator).
- **Unlocks** (from KG): `phys.em.lc-circuits` — a genuine hub concept for
  the AC-circuits sub-branch.
- **Difficulty**: proficient · **Bloom**: apply · **Mastery threshold**: 0.80
  · **Est. hours**: 4

## Learning Objective

The learner can: (1) correctly explain why RMS (root-mean-square) voltage,
NOT the simple average, is the physically meaningful "effective" AC
voltage — because a full AC cycle's plain average is exactly zero (positive
and negative half-cycles cancel), while RMS (square, then average, then
root) captures the voltage that delivers the same average power as an
equivalent DC voltage; (2) correctly compute V_rms = V_m/√2 for a sinusoidal
waveform; (3) correctly explain that component voltages in an AC circuit
must be combined as PHASORS (accounting for phase difference), not added
arithmetically — so V_R, V_L, V_C can each individually exceed the total
supply voltage without violating any conservation law.

## Core Understanding

An AC voltage/current oscillates continuously, so its plain average over a
full cycle is exactly zero — clearly not a useful "effective size" measure
(it would wrongly suggest a lightbulb sees no voltage at all). RMS
(root-mean-square: square the waveform first — making every value
positive — then average, then take the square root) fixes this by
producing V_rms = V_m/√2 ≈ 0.707V_m for a sinusoid, a value defined
specifically so that P_avg = V_rms²/R gives the same average power a
DC voltage of that same value would produce in a resistor — a physically
meaningful, not arbitrary, definition. In a series AC circuit containing
resistance, inductance, and capacitance together, the resistor's voltage
(V_R) is IN PHASE with current, while the inductor's (V_L) and capacitor's
(V_C) voltages are 90° out of phase with current in OPPOSITE directions
(hence 180° out of phase with each other) — so component voltages must be
combined as PHASORS (vector addition accounting for phase), giving
V_total = √(V_R² + (V_L−V_C)²), NOT the arithmetic sum V_R+V_L+V_C. This
genuinely allows V_L and V_C individually to be much larger than the total
supply voltage (they can nearly cancel each other in the phasor sum) — a
real, non-paradoxical consequence of the phase relationships, not a
violation of any conservation law.

## Mental Models

**Beginner**: "RMS voltage is just the average of the AC wave — for a sine
wave, that's V_m/2." Upgrade trigger: computing the actual average of a
full sine cycle (exactly zero) directly confronts the false equivalence
between "average" and "RMS."

**Intermediate**: "V_rms = V_m/√2, defined so P = V_rms²/R matches the
average AC power; component voltages must add as phasors, not
arithmetically, since V_L and V_C are 90° out of phase with current." This
model correctly captures both core results, but sometimes still writes
V_R+V_L+V_C = V_total out of habit from DC KVL reasoning.

**Advanced**: "Instantaneous voltages DO add arithmetically at every
instant (KVL always holds instant-by-instant) — but PEAK or RMS values, which
represent different instants for out-of-phase quantities, require phasor
(vector) addition to combine correctly."

**Expert**: complex impedance Z = R + j(X_L−X_C), unifying phasor addition
into a single complex-number framework — not required for mastery, a
natural extension toward `phys.em.lc-circuits`.

## Why Students Fail

The dominant failure is conflating RMS with a simple average, since
"average" is the more familiar, everyday-language concept, and the
squaring/rooting procedure feels like an arbitrary extra step rather than a
physically motivated one; a second, distinct failure is applying DC-style
arithmetic addition (V_R+V_L+V_C=V_total) to AC circuits without accounting
for phase differences.

## Misconceptions

Blueprint (`docs/curriculum/blueprints/phys.em.ac-basics.md`, Misconception
Engine) documents two; reused by reference with birth-type added.

- **MC-RMS-IS-THE-AVERAGE-OF-THE-WAVEFORM**: belief RMS voltage is simply
  the average of the AC waveform (V_m/2 for a sine). **Birth type**:
  language contamination (Type 3) — the everyday word "average" is loosely
  applied to RMS's technical square-mean-root definition, since both are
  colloquially "a typical value representing the whole wave." Probe: "What
  is the plain average (not RMS) of a full cycle of a sine wave — zero,
  V_m/2, or V_m/√2? Now what is its RMS value?"
- **MC-VOLTAGE-ACROSS-COMPONENTS-ADDS-ARITHMETICALLY-IN-AC**: writing
  V_R+V_L+V_C=V_total (arithmetic sum) for an AC series circuit, or being
  puzzled when component voltages individually exceed the supply voltage.
  **Birth type**: overgeneralization (Type 1) — DC circuit KVL (arithmetic
  voltage-drop summation) is correctly learned first and incorrectly
  extended to AC without accounting for phase. Probe: "In a series RLC
  circuit supplied by 50V, V_L and V_C both measure 1000V. Does this
  violate any conservation law?"

## Analogies

**Best**: adding two forces at right angles — F_net = √(F_x²+F_y²), NOT
F_x+F_y — directly targets
MC-VOLTAGE-ACROSS-COMPONENTS-ADDS-ARITHMETICALLY-IN-AC by giving a
familiar vector-addition precedent (forces) that maps directly onto
phasor voltage addition.

**Anti-analogy**: do NOT say "RMS is basically the average voltage" as an
informal shorthand — this directly installs
MC-RMS-IS-THE-AVERAGE-OF-THE-WAVEFORM by conflating two genuinely
different quantities (a true average of zero vs. RMS's nonzero
square-mean-root value).

## Demonstrations

Physical/conceptual: compute the true average (zero) versus RMS (V_m/√2)
for the same sine waveform, side by side — directly targets
MC-RMS-IS-THE-AVERAGE-OF-THE-WAVEFORM with concrete numbers.
Worked-example: the Blueprint's WE-3 (V_L = V_C = 1000V in a 50V-supply
circuit), computing the phasor sum √(V_R²+(V_L−V_C)²) = 50V, explicitly
showing V_L and V_C cancel — directly targets
MC-VOLTAGE-ACROSS-COMPONENTS-ADDS-ARITHMETICALLY-IN-AC.

## Discovery Questions

Discovery-style: "if V_L and V_C in a series AC circuit each measure 1000V,
but the supply is only 50V, has some law of physics been broken?" —
learner reasons through the phase relationship, discovering the phasor
cancellation resolves the apparent paradox, directly confronting
MC-VOLTAGE-ACROSS-COMPONENTS-ADDS-ARITHMETICALLY-IN-AC.
