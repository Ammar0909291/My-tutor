# Hamilton's Equations of Motion — `phys.mech.hamiltons-equations`

## Identity

- **Concept ID**: `phys.mech.hamiltons-equations` (canonical physics KG)
- **Curriculum location**: physics / mechanics — dependency level 13.
- **Prerequisites**: `phys.mech.hamiltonian` — Hamilton's equations are the
  direct dynamical consequence of the already-secure Hamiltonian
  formulation, giving the equations of motion in phase space.
- **Unlocks** (from KG): `phys.mech.poisson-brackets` — a genuine bridge
  concept into the algebraic structure of Hamiltonian mechanics.
- **Difficulty**: expert · **Bloom**: apply · **Mastery threshold**: 0.85
  · **Est. hours**: 7

## Learning Objective

The learner can: (1) correctly write Hamilton's equations with their
CORRECT, ASYMMETRIC signs — q̇ᵢ=+∂H/∂pᵢ (positive) and ṗᵢ=−∂H/∂qᵢ
(negative) — never both positive, since the minus sign on the momentum
equation is FORCED by the Legendre transform relating H to L, not an
arbitrary convention; (2) correctly explain that Hamilton's TWO equations
are NOT two redundant ways of writing the same single equation — q̇=∂H/∂p
governs how position evolves (the "velocity equation") while ṗ=−∂H/∂q
governs how momentum evolves (the "force equation"), and BOTH are
required together to fully determine the trajectory (q(t),p(t)) in phase
space, since Hamiltonian mechanics uses only first-order equations
(unlike Newtonian mechanics's single second-order q̈ equation).

## Core Understanding

Hamilton's equations are q̇ᵢ=+∂H/∂pᵢ and ṗᵢ=−∂H/∂qᵢ — note the
ASYMMETRIC signs: PLUS for the position equation, MINUS for the momentum
equation. This is NOT an arbitrary convention that could equally well be
written with both positive — the minus sign is FORCED by the underlying
mathematics: starting from the Euler-Lagrange equation ṗᵢ=∂L/∂qᵢ (a
direct consequence of the Lagrangian formulation) and the Legendre
transform H=Σpq̇−L (which gives ∂H/∂qᵢ=−∂L/∂qᵢ, a minus sign appearing
specifically because qᵢ is held fixed while transforming to momentum
variables), substituting shows ṗᵢ=−∂H/∂qᵢ necessarily. A concrete check:
for a spring system, H=p²/(2m)+½kx² — if the momentum equation incorrectly
used a plus sign, ṗ=+∂H/∂x=+kx would predict a force in the +x direction
when the spring is stretched to +x — an ANTI-restoring force, the wrong
physics; only the correct minus sign, ṗ=−∂H/∂x=−kx, gives the physically
correct restoring force. A second, equally important point: Hamilton's
two equations are NOT two redundant expressions of one underlying
equation — they serve genuinely DIFFERENT roles and BOTH are required to
solve the dynamics. q̇=∂H/∂p is the "velocity equation," telling you how
the position coordinate evolves in time; ṗ=−∂H/∂q is the "force
equation," telling you how the momentum coordinate evolves. In phase
space, position and momentum are treated as independent variables — using
only the momentum equation (e.g. ṗ=−kx for a spring) leaves you with one
equation in two unknowns (x and p); you need the position equation
(ẋ=p/m) as well to fully determine the trajectory. Together, the two
first-order equations are equivalent to — and just as complete as — the
single second-order Newtonian equation (e.g. combining both spring
equations recovers ẍ=−(k/m)x), but expressed as a coupled first-order
system in phase space rather than one second-order equation in
configuration space.

## Mental Models

**Beginner**: "Both Hamilton's equations have plus signs: q̇=+∂H/∂p and
ṗ=+∂H/∂q; also, deriving just one of the two equations is enough — the
other one is basically trivial/redundant." Upgrade trigger: checking the
spring example with a plus sign on the momentum equation (getting an
anti-restoring force, wrong physics) directly confronts the both-positive
assumption; attempting to solve a spring's trajectory using only the
momentum equation (finding one equation, two unknowns, stuck) directly
confronts the redundancy assumption.

**Intermediate**: "The correct pair is q̇=+∂H/∂p (velocity equation) and
ṗ=−∂H/∂q (force equation), asymmetric signs forced by the Legendre
transform; both equations are independently necessary, together fully
determining (q(t),p(t)) in phase space." This model correctly captures
both results, but sometimes still needs to explicitly re-derive the sign
via the Legendre transform when working an unfamiliar system.

**Advanced**: "Always write both of Hamilton's equations explicitly at
the start of a problem, boxed, before proceeding — never rely on memory
for the sign pattern without being able to re-derive it from H=Σpq̇−L if
needed; always verify both equations are being used together to solve
for the full trajectory."

**Expert**: the symplectic structure underlying Hamilton's equations
(the equations as flow generated by H on phase space, preserving phase-
space volume per Liouville's theorem) and the Poisson bracket
reformulation, q̇ᵢ={qᵢ,H} and ṗᵢ={pᵢ,H} — a natural consolidation
directly connecting to the KG unlock `phys.mech.poisson-brackets`, not
required for mastery.

## Why Students Fail

The dominant failure is writing both of Hamilton's equations with the
same (positive) sign, missing that the minus sign on the momentum
equation is mathematically forced by the Legendre transform, not
arbitrary; a second, distinct failure is treating the two equations as
redundant and deriving/using only one, missing that both are
independently necessary to determine the full phase-space trajectory.

## Misconceptions

Blueprint (`docs/curriculum/blueprints/phys.mech.hamiltons-equations.md`,
Misconception Engine) documents two; reused by reference with birth-type
added.

- **MC-SIGN-BOTH-POSITIVE**: believing "both Hamilton's equations have
  plus signs: q̇=+∂H/∂p and ṗ=+∂H/∂q." **Birth type**: overgeneralization
  (Type 1) — the visual symmetry of q and p as conjugate variables is
  incorrectly extended to symmetric signs in their equations of motion,
  missing that the Legendre transform specifically introduces a minus
  sign for the momentum equation. Probe: "For a spring, H=p²/(2m)+½kx².
  If ṗ=+∂H/∂x=+kx, does this correctly predict a restoring force when
  the spring is stretched to +x?"
- **MC-TWO-EQUATIONS-REDUNDANT**: believing "Hamilton's two equations are
  just two ways of writing the same equation," deriving only one and
  stopping. **Birth type**: perceptual intuition (Type 2) — the compact,
  parallel-looking notation of the two equations suggests they carry the
  same information, missing that q and p are independent phase-space
  variables each requiring their own equation of motion. Probe: "Using
  only ṗ=−kx for a spring, can you solve for both x(t) and p(t), or are
  you stuck with one equation and two unknowns?"

## Analogies

**Best**: a two-lane highway where one lane's traffic flow depends on the
other lane's density and vice versa — you need BOTH lanes' equations to
predict the full traffic pattern, just as you need both q̇=∂H/∂p and
ṗ=−∂H/∂q to predict the full phase-space trajectory — directly targets
MC-TWO-EQUATIONS-REDUNDANT by giving a concrete image of two necessarily
coupled, non-redundant equations.

**Anti-analogy**: do NOT say "Hamilton's equations are symmetric in q and
p" as an unqualified statement — while q and p play structurally
parallel roles, this phrasing directly invites MC-SIGN-BOTH-POSITIVE by
suggesting their equations of motion look identical (they don't — the
sign differs).

## Demonstrations

Worked-example: check the spring system's momentum equation with both a
plus and a minus sign, showing only the minus sign gives the physically
correct restoring force — directly targets MC-SIGN-BOTH-POSITIVE.
Worked-example: attempt to solve the spring's trajectory using only
ṗ=−kx, showing it's underdetermined without also using ẋ=p/m — directly
targets MC-TWO-EQUATIONS-REDUNDANT.

## Discovery Questions

Discovery-style: "if ṗ=+∂H/∂x for a spring stretched to positive x, what
direction does the resulting force point — restoring (back toward
equilibrium) or anti-restoring (away from equilibrium)?" — learner
computes and confronts the wrong-physics result, directly confronting
MC-SIGN-BOTH-POSITIVE.

Listen for "both equations are positive" or "I only need one of the two
equations" — the two direct misconception tells. Load-bearing sentence:
"the position equation is plus, the momentum equation is minus — that
minus sign isn't optional, it's forced by the math — and you need BOTH
equations together to solve for where things go." Channel-reality

MC-TWO-EQUATIONS-REDUNDANT (full repair via the underdetermined-system

their proper asymmetric signs, AND correctly using both together to

## Transfer Connections

Near: any phase-space equations-of-motion problem. Far: quantum mechanics
(the correspondence between Poisson brackets and quantum commutators,
{q,p}→[q̂,p̂]/iℏ, directly built on this classical structure) and chaos
theory (Hamiltonian flow and phase-space volume preservation underlying
the study of chaotic dynamical systems). Real-world: understanding why
numerical simulations of planetary orbits and molecular dynamics use
symplectic integrators specifically designed to respect Hamilton's
equations' structure. Expert: the symplectic/Poisson-bracket
reformulation of Hamiltonian mechanics.
