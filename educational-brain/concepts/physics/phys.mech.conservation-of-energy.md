# Conservation of Mechanical Energy — `phys.mech.conservation-of-energy`

## Identity

- **Concept ID**: `phys.mech.conservation-of-energy` (canonical physics KG)
- **Curriculum location**: physics / mechanics — dependency level 8.
- **Prerequisites**: `phys.mech.kinetic-energy`, `phys.mech.potential-energy`
  — mechanical energy conservation directly combines these two
  already-secure energy forms into one conserved total, KE + PE = constant.
- **Unlocks** (from KG): `phys.mech.bernoulli`, `phys.mech.conservative-forces`,
  `phys.mech.escape-velocity` — a genuine hub concept.
- **Difficulty**: proficient · **Bloom**: analyze · **Mastery threshold**: 0.85
  · **Est. hours**: 5

## Learning Objective

The learner can: (1) correctly state that KE + PE = constant ONLY when
NO non-conservative forces (friction, air resistance) do work — and
correctly identify when this condition is violated, refusing to apply
the conservation equation to a friction-involving scenario; (2) correctly
explain that mechanical energy conservation, when it applies, predicts
the SAME final speed regardless of the specific path taken between two
heights — path-independence is a genuine, verifiable consequence, not an
approximation.

## Core Understanding

Mechanical energy (KE + PE) is conserved — remains constant throughout
motion — ONLY when no non-conservative force (friction, air resistance,
applied forces doing net work) acts on the system; whenever friction is
present, some mechanical energy is converted to heat, and KE_i + PE_i ≠
KE_f + PE_f in general (using the conservation equation anyway produces a
systematically wrong answer, since it silently assumes zero energy loss).
When mechanical energy genuinely IS conserved (no friction), a resulting
consequence is PATH-INDEPENDENCE: an object sliding from height h to
height 0 via ANY frictionless path (straight ramp, curved ramp, several
different curves) arrives with the identical final speed, v = √(2gh),
regardless of the path's shape or length — this is not approximately
true or true "on average," but an exact consequence of gravity being a
conservative force (its work depends only on the start/end heights, never
on the path taken).

## Mental Models

**Beginner**: "Energy is always conserved, so KE_i+PE_i=KE_f+PE_f
regardless of friction; a longer/curvier path gives a different final
speed than a straight path." Upgrade trigger: comparing predicted vs.
actual final speed for a friction scenario (mismatch reveals the
conservation-equation misapplication) and comparing final speeds for
different frictionless paths to the same height (identical, confronting
path-dependence).

**Intermediate**: "KE+PE=constant only holds without friction; when it
holds, final speed depends only on height dropped, not on path shape."
This model correctly captures both core distinctions, but sometimes still
hesitates on genuinely path-independence-preserving but visually
complex paths (loops, S-curves).

**Advanced**: "Before applying conservation, explicitly check for any
non-conservative force in the scenario; when confirmed absent, trust
path-independence completely — even for a wildly curved frictionless
track, final speed depends ONLY on net height change."

**Expert**: mechanical energy conservation as a specific case of the
general work-energy theorem, where W_net splits cleanly into
-ΔPE (conservative forces) + W_nonconservative — reducing to KE+PE=const
exactly when the second term is zero — not required for mastery, a
natural derivation-level consolidation.

## Why Students Fail

The dominant failure is applying the conservation equation unconditionally,
without first checking whether friction or another non-conservative force
is actually present in the scenario; a second, distinct failure expects a
longer or more curved (but still frictionless) path to produce a
different final speed than a straight path to the same height.

## Misconceptions

Blueprint (`docs/curriculum/blueprints/phys.mech.conservation-of-energy.md`,
Misconception Engine) documents two; reused by reference with birth-type
added.

- **MC-FRICTION-CONSERVES**: applying KE_i+PE_i=KE_f+PE_f even when
  friction is present in the scenario. **Birth type**: overgeneralization
  (Type 1) — "energy is always conserved" (true for TOTAL energy
  including heat) is incorrectly extended to MECHANICAL energy alone,
  which specifically excludes the heat generated by friction. Probe: "A
  block slides down a ROUGH ramp. Does KE_initial + PE_initial equal
  KE_final + PE_final exactly, or is something missing from that
  equation?"
- **MC-PATH-MATTERS**: believing a longer or more curved (but still
  frictionless) path between two heights produces a different final
  speed than a straight path. **Birth type**: perceptual intuition (Type
  2) — a longer, more convoluted path visually "looks like more effort/
  distance," intuitively suggesting a different outcome, even though
  gravity's work depends only on height change, not path length. Probe:
  "A ball slides down a straight ramp and, separately, down a long curvy
  frictionless ramp — both starting and ending at the same two heights.
  Does the curvy ramp give a different final speed?"

## Analogies

**Best**: a bank account with and without a "leak" (friction) — with no
leak, the balance formula (deposits=withdrawals) holds exactly; with a
leak, some money silently disappears and the naive balance equation gives
a wrong prediction unless the leak is explicitly accounted for — directly
targets MC-FRICTION-CONSERVES by making the "silent loss" concrete.

**Anti-analogy**: do NOT say "energy is conserved" as a bare, unqualified
statement when discussing MECHANICAL energy specifically — without the
non-conservative-force caveat, this directly installs
MC-FRICTION-CONSERVES.

## Demonstrations

Physical/data-based: compare predicted (conservation equation) vs.
measured final speed for a block sliding down a visibly rough ramp,
showing the mismatch — directly targets MC-FRICTION-CONSERVES.
Conceptual: compute final speed v=√(2gh) for a straight ramp and a
curved (but frictionless) ramp to the identical height, showing identical
results — directly targets MC-PATH-MATTERS.

## Discovery Questions

Discovery-style: "does a ball rolling down a long, curvy (but
frictionless) slide arrive at the bottom faster, slower, or the same
speed as one going down a short straight ramp to the same height?" —
learner predicts, then the height-only dependence resolves it, directly
confronting MC-PATH-MATTERS.

## Teaching Sequence

Blueprint's assessment component cited by reference.
MC-FRICTION-CONSERVES is addressed first (establishing WHEN conservation
applies), before MC-PATH-MATTERS, since confirming conservation's
validity condition (no friction) is the prerequisite for then exploring
its strongest, most counterintuitive consequence (path-independence).

## Tutor Actions

## Voice Teaching Notes

Listen for conservation applied without checking for friction, or an
expectation that path shape affects final speed — the two direct
misconception tells. Load-bearing sentence: "conservation only holds when
nothing eats the energy — no friction, no air resistance; and when it
holds, only the HEIGHT dropped matters, never the path's shape."

## Assessment Signals

A learner applying conservation in a friction scenario without adjustment
signals MC-FRICTION-CONSERVES (MISCONCEIVING, full repair via the
predicted-vs-measured mismatch). A learner predicting different final
speeds for different frictionless paths signals MC-PATH-MATTERS (full
repair via the straight-vs-curved comparison). Mastery trigger: correctly
identifying when conservation applies (checking for non-conservative
forces first), AND correctly predicting identical final speed for any
frictionless path to the same height. Blueprint's assessment component
cited for the full item bank.

## Transfer Connections

Near: any frictionless energy-conservation problem, including roller
coasters and pendulums. Far: fluid dynamics (Bernoulli's equation as
mechanical energy conservation applied to flowing fluids, the direct KG
unlock) and orbital mechanics (escape velocity derived from energy
conservation, another direct KG unlock). Real-world: understanding why
roller coaster loop heights are always lower than the initial drop height
(some mechanical energy is always lost to friction/air resistance in
practice, even though the ideal frictionless prediction assumes
equality). Expert: mechanical energy conservation as the friction-free
special case of the general work-energy theorem.
