# The Standard Model — `phys.particle.standard-model`

## Identity

- **Concept ID**: `phys.particle.standard-model` (canonical physics KG)
- **Curriculum location**: physics / particle physics — dependency
  level 23 (the domain's terminal capstone node).
- **Prerequisites**: `phys.particle.hadron-quark-model`,
  `phys.particle.gauge-bosons`, `phys.particle.higgs-mechanism`,

  synthesis of the domain's matter particles, force carriers, mass

- **Unlocks** (from KG): none — the Particle Physics domain's capstone.
- **Difficulty**: expert · **Bloom**: evaluate · **Mastery threshold**:
  0.70 · **Est. hours**: 10

## Learning Objective

The learner can: (1) correctly assemble the complete 17-particle
Standard Model roster (6 quarks, 6 leptons, 4 gauge bosons, 1 Higgs
boson) with its generational structure; (2) correctly explain why
extraordinary precision within the Standard Model's scope does not
imply completeness beyond it; (3) correctly identify and distinguish at
least three major open questions (gravity, dark matter, dark energy,
neutrino mass mechanism, matter-antimatter asymmetry), and specifically
distinguish dark matter from dark energy as genuinely different
mysteries.

## Core Understanding

The Standard Model organizes all known quarks, leptons, gauge bosons,
and the Higgs boson into a single, unified theoretical framework,
allowing any known particle to be classified by its interactions and
quantum numbers — standing as the single most rigorously tested theory
in the history of physics, while still leaving several major phenomena
unexplained. Every concept studied throughout this domain is not a loose
collection of separate facts but specific pieces of one internally
consistent structure. A first persistent error believes the Standard
Model is "the theory of everything," missing that it explicitly excludes
gravity and does not explain dark matter or dark energy, which together
dwarf ordinary matter in the universe's total mass-energy budget. A
second error assumes extreme precision-testing success means no major
gaps remain, missing that precision within scope and completeness beyond
scope are logically independent properties. A third error conflates dark
matter and dark energy as "the same mystery, just undiscovered
particles," missing that they have genuinely different leading
hypotheses — dark matter as a likely new particle, dark energy as more
likely a property of space itself.

## Mental Models

**Beginner**: "The Standard Model explains everything in physics; a
theory tested this precisely can't have any major unresolved gaps; dark
matter and dark energy are basically the same mystery, both just
undiscovered particles." Upgrade trigger: the actual cosmological
mass-energy pie chart (~5% ordinary matter, ~27% dark matter, ~68% dark
energy) directly confronts the theory-of-everything assumption; the
precision-vs-completeness two-column chart (confirmed core alongside
explicitly acknowledged open questions) directly confronts the
precision-implies-completeness assumption; stating the differing leading
hypotheses (dark matter: likely new particle; dark energy: likely a
property of space) side by side directly confronts the conflation.

**Intermediate**: "The Standard Model is an extraordinarily successful,
precisely tested description of three of the four fundamental forces
and all currently confirmed matter particles — genuinely complete within
its explicitly bounded scope, while leaving major, acknowledged gaps for
physics beyond it; dark matter and dark energy are distinct open
problems with different leading hypotheses." This model correctly
captures the three core distinctions but may still need practice
recalling the full generational structure of the 17-particle roster
fluently.

**Advanced**: always frame the particle roster as a structured pattern
(three generations each of quarks and leptons, each heavier and less
stable than the last, plus exactly four gauge bosons and one Higgs
boson) rather than an arbitrary list, and always cite the specific
5%/27%/68% breakdown when asked to characterize the Standard Model's
scope.

**Expert**: the connection to ongoing experimental programs (dark matter
direct-detection experiments, neutrino mass mechanism proposals, Grand
Unified Theory candidates, quantum gravity approaches) as the genuine
frontier of particle physics today, and to the philosophy-of-science
point that mature scientific practice involves precisely bounding what a
successful theory does and does not explain — not required for mastery,
the domain's ultimate takeaway as its capstone concept.

## Why Students Fail

The dominant failure is believing the Standard Model is a complete
"theory of everything," missing that gravity, dark matter, and dark
energy remain entirely outside its framework; a closely related failure
assumes extraordinary precision-testing success means no major gaps
remain, missing that precision within scope and completeness beyond
scope are logically independent; a further failure treats the
17-particle roster as an arbitrary, unmemorable list rather than a
structured, generational pattern; a final failure conflates dark matter
and dark energy as the same kind of open question, missing their
genuinely different leading hypotheses and research approaches.

## Misconceptions

Blueprint (`docs/curriculum/blueprints/phys.particle.standard-
model.md`, Section 4 Misconception Library) documents three; reused by
reference with birth-type added.

- **MC-1 ("the Standard Model is a complete 'theory of everything,'
  explaining all of physics")**: believing "the Standard Model explains
  everything in physics." **Birth type**: overgeneralization (Type 1) —
  the Standard Model's genuinely remarkable, precisely-tested success
  leads students to over-extend its scope to "explains literally
  everything." Probe: "Does the Standard Model explain gravity? Does it
  explain dark matter and dark energy, which make up most of the
  universe's total mass-energy content?"
- **MC-2 ("a theory this precisely tested can't have any major
  unresolved gaps")**: believing "it's been tested so precisely, it
  must be basically finished/complete." **Birth type**: overgeneralization
  (Type 1) — conflating "extremely precisely tested within its domain"
  with "has no domain left to explore." Probe: "If the Standard Model's
  predictions have been confirmed to a trillion decimal places of
  precision, doesn't that mean it must be a complete theory with no
  major gaps?"
- **MC-3 ("dark matter and dark energy are basically the same open
  question, both just 'undiscovered particles'")**: believing "dark
  matter and dark energy are basically the same thing — just particles
  we haven't found yet." **Birth type**: language contamination (Type 3)
  — both terms share the word "dark" and are often mentioned together in
  popular science without distinguishing their very different natures.
  Probe: "Are dark matter and dark energy the same kind of mystery —
  both just a matter of finding the right undiscovered particle?"

## Analogies

**Best**: an enormous, beautifully completed jigsaw puzzle — every piece
present fits together perfectly, forming a coherent, precisely detailed
picture (three of the four forces, all confirmed matter particles) —
but one corner is explicitly, visibly empty, clearly marked as missing
pieces (gravity, dark matter, dark energy) nobody has yet found.
Explicitly breaks down at one point: a jigsaw puzzle's missing pieces
are presumed to exist somewhere, simply not yet found; it remains a
genuinely open question whether gravity/dark matter/dark energy will
ultimately take the form of new particles fitting neatly into the
existing framework, or require a fundamentally different theoretical
structure entirely.

**Anti-analogy**: do NOT say "the Standard Model is basically finished,
just missing a few small details" — this dramatically understates the
scale of what remains unexplained (95% of the universe's total
mass-energy content is not described by the Standard Model at all),
reinforcing MC-1 and MC-2.

## Demonstrations

Worked-example: build the full 17-particle Standard Model chart live
(three generations of quarks and leptons, four gauge bosons, one Higgs
boson), having the learner identify the generational and functional
structure rather than memorizing an arbitrary list. Worked-example:
present the actual cosmological mass-energy breakdown (~5% ordinary
matter, ~27% dark matter, ~68% dark energy) as a pie chart — directly
targets MC-1 by making the scale of what remains unexplained viscerally
concrete.

## Discovery Questions

Discovery-style: "You've now studied every piece of one of the most
successful theories in the history of science — the Standard Model.
Before I tell you how successful it is, I want you to guess: what
fraction of the universe's total mass and energy do you think this
theory actually explains?" — learner discovers (through the mass-energy
pie-chart reveal, likely far lower than guessed at ~5%) that
extraordinary success within scope and vast unexplained territory beyond
it coexist, directly confronting MC-1 and MC-2 together.

## Transfer Connections

Near: applying the same "confirmed core + explicitly bounded open
questions" evaluative framework to any other major scientific theory
encountered later (evolutionary theory, general relativity). Far: the
philosophy and history of science broadly — recognizing that mature
scientific practice involves precisely bounding what a rigorously tested
theory does and does not explain, one of the most transferable habits of
mind in science education. Real-world: ongoing dark matter

Grand Unified Theory candidates as the genuine frontier of particle
physics today. Expert: the general principle that a theory can be
simultaneously the most rigorously confirmed framework in its field and
explicitly, honestly incomplete beyond its own bounded scope.
