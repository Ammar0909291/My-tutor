# Newton's First Law — Inertia — `phys.mech.newtons-first-law`

## Identity

- **Concept ID**: `phys.mech.newtons-first-law` (canonical physics KG;
  aliases: law of inertia, first law)
- **Curriculum location**: physics / classical mechanics
- **Prerequisites** (from KG `requires`, with the load-bearing part):
  - `phys.mech.force` — the load-bearing part is force as an *interaction
    between two things* (a push or pull from something, on something) and
    the idea of a NET force. The learner does not need force units or
    F=ma; they need "forces can cancel."
- **Unlocks** (from KG): `phys.mech.equilibrium` — and conceptually it is
  the gate to all of dynamics: a learner who still believes motion needs
  force will misread F=ma, friction, projectiles, and orbits. **This is
  physics' single most important misconception battleground**, and the KG's
  own related-link to `phys.mech.newtons-second-law` marks the pairing.
- **Difficulty**: developing · **Bloom**: understand · **Mastery
  threshold**: 0.75 (KG-authored) · **Est. hours**: 3 ·
  **Reference (KG)**: NCERT Physics Class 9 Ch. 9
- **Learning objectives** — the learner can:
  1. Predict correctly what happens to a moving object when ALL forces on
     it stop (it keeps moving, same speed, same direction).
  2. Explain why moving things around us DO stop (friction/air are forces
     — the everyday world hides the law rather than showing it).
  3. Identify, for a given scenario, whether a net force is acting — from
     the *motion's change*, not from whether it "looks pushed."
  4. State the law's both halves (rest stays rest / motion stays motion)
     and why they are one statement, not two.

## Mental models

- **Beginner (arriving, wrong) — the "impetus" model**: motion is a
  substance the object *contains*; pushes pour it in, it leaks out over
  time, and things stop when it runs out. This is nearly every learner's
  starting model at every age — it is 2,000 years old (it is Aristotle's),
  it is rebuilt from scratch by every child watching balls roll to a stop,
  and it is *correct-looking for everything they have ever seen*. It is not
  taught out by one lesson; it is inhibited, slowly (see misconceptions).
- **Intermediate model — "friction is a thing"**: motion persists; stopping
  is CAUSED by named forces (friction, air). The learner can run "remove
  the friction" thought experiments but still slips under pressure
  ("what keeps it moving?" still feels like a fair question).
- **Advanced model — "change needs cause, motion doesn't"**: force causes
  *acceleration*, not velocity. The question "what keeps it moving?" is
  recognized as malformed — nothing needs to; the real question is always
  "what is changing its motion?"
- **Expert model — frames and equivalence**: rest and uniform motion are
  the same state seen from different frames; the first law defines
  *inertial frames* (its deep job — it is not a special case of the second
  law but the statement of where the second law applies). Boundary
  knowledge: knows the law's frame-dependence and where Newtonian thinking
  itself has a shelf life.
- **Versioning note**: unusually, the FIRST model here is not
  tutor-installed but learner-native and wrong. There is no "good first
  model" to install gently — the work IS the replacement. The versioning
  speech instead honors the old model: "your brain built the exact model
  Aristotle built, from the same evidence. It took humanity 2,000 years to
  find the flaw — we get to do it in an afternoon."

## Why beginners fail here

Every moving thing the learner has ever released has stopped. Their model
(motion runs out) is a *correct induction from a biased sample* — the bias
being that friction attended every observation of their life (birth type 2:
perceptual intuition, the deepest kind). Instruction fails when it announces
the law instead of engineering the collision: told "motion continues
forever," the learner memorizes the sentence for tests and keeps the impetus
model for life — the textbook answer and the gut model coexist for decades
(experts still show the interference under time pressure). The teaching
problem is not transmitting the law; it is making friction VISIBLE as a
force, because the law only looks true once the hidden force is seen.

## Misconception library

**M1 — Motion needs force ("what keeps it moving?") — the impetus model**
- *Why*: perceptual intuition (type 2); universal, pre-verbal, self-rebuilding.
- *Symptom / phrases*: "the force of the throw is still in the ball";
  "it stops when the force runs out"; "nothing is pushing it, so it slows."
- *Detection probe (verbatim)*: "I slide a puck on perfect, frictionless
  ice — nothing touches it ever again. What does it do?" Impetus answers:
  "slows down and eventually stops." Correct: "keeps going, same speed,
  forever." Fast-wrong here is the expected default, not a surprise.
- *Recovery*: never announce — collide. The friction-dial sequence
  (demonstrations below): same push, rougher → smoother → smoothest
  surface; the learner PREDICTS each distance, sees the trend, and is asked
  the killer extrapolation: "where does it stop if the surface is perfectly
  smooth?" The learner's own trend line answers "never" — the law comes out
  of their mouth, not the tutor's. Then the contrast step: "why did 'it
  runs out' feel so right?" (answer they must reach: because friction was
  invisibly present in every experience they've ever had).
- *Verification*: delayed + speeded + disguised (this misconception's
  regrowth under time pressure is the best-documented in all of education):
  "a spaceship's engine cuts out in deep space — what happens?" (fast,
  days later); the tier-2 item "the ball slows down because ___" with
  impetus-mapped distractors.

**M2 — "No motion means no force" (the resting book)**
- *Why*: forces are imagined as motion-makers only; balanced forces are
  invisible (type 2).
- *Symptom / phrase*: "nothing is pushing the book, it's just sitting there."
- *Detection probe*: "This book rests on the table. Are any forces acting
  on it?" — "no" or "only gravity" (missing the table's push) = M2.
- *Recovery*: the hand-as-table demonstration — hold the book on your flat
  palm: "is your hand pushing up?" (they feel it) — "the table is doing
  exactly what your hand is doing, it's just better at hiding the effort."
- *Verification*: force-identification items on static scenarios, delayed;
  drawn force arrows on a resting object (drawn-model rubric).

**M3 — "Heavier objects are harder to KEEP moving" (inertia confused with
weight/friction)**
- *Why*: conflation of mass-inertia with weight-driven friction — in daily
  life heavy things ARE harder to keep sliding (more friction).
- *Symptom*: correct answers on Earth scenarios, wrong in space ("the heavy
  crate drifts to a stop faster").
- *Detection probe*: "Two crates drift in empty space, one heavy, one
  light, same speed, engines off. Which stops first?" (Correct: neither,
  ever.)
- *Recovery*: separate the two ideas by name — inertia (resists CHANGE) vs
  friction (a force that happens to scale with weight on Earth). Thought
  experiment: pushing the crates sideways in space — the heavy one is
  harder to START and harder to STOP, but costs nothing to KEEP going.
- *Verification*: space-context items mixed among Earth-context items
  (the discrimination is the mastery).

**M4 — "The law is about rest" (half-law learning)**
- *Why*: instruction-induced (type 5) — "objects at rest stay at rest" is
  the memorable half; the motion half evaporates because it contradicts M1.
- *Symptom*: recites the rest half; hesitates or omits the motion half.
- *Detection*: "say the law" — listen for which half arrives first and
  whether the second arrives at all.
- *Recovery*: the equivalence framing — "rest is just motion at zero
  speed; the law is ONE sentence: velocity doesn't change without a net
  force." (This also pre-seeds the expert frame model.)
- *Verification*: state-the-law items scored for both halves; better,
  prediction items that only the motion half answers.

## Explanation library

- **Age 8–11 (story frame)**: "Things are lazy. Not lazy like they want to
  stop — lazy like they hate CHANGE. A parked ball hates starting. A
  rolling ball hates stopping. If nothing bothers them, they keep doing
  exactly what they were doing. The only reason rolling balls stop is that
  the ground keeps bothering them — that bothering is called friction."
- **Age 12+ (mechanism)**: "Force doesn't cause motion — force causes
  CHANGE of motion. Keeping-going is free; starting, stopping, and turning
  are what cost. Everything you've ever seen stop was being acted on by a
  force you didn't see. The law just says: no net force, no change."
- **Returning adult**: "You memorized 'objects in motion stay in motion'
  and it never felt true — because on Earth it never LOOKS true. The
  missing piece is that friction and air are forces, as real as a push.
  Once you count them, every 'thing slowing down' you've ever seen becomes
  the law working, not the law failing."
- **Visual frame**: three motion strips (photos at equal time gaps):
  equal spacing = no net force; growing/shrinking spacing = force at work.
  "Read the spacing, find the force."
- **Logical frame (advanced entry)**: "The first law isn't a special case
  of F=ma with F=0 — it's the claim that there EXIST frames where force-free
  objects move uniformly. It tells you where the rest of mechanics is
  allowed to be used. That's why it's first."
- **Anxious learner**: start from their competence: "You already know this
  law in your body — you brace when the bus brakes. Your body predicted
  that IT would keep going when the bus stopped. We're just writing down
  what your body knows."

## Analogy library

1. **The bus-brake lurch** (BEST — it is not an analogy but a felt
   instance): passengers keep going when the bus stops; nothing pushed
   them forward — they simply weren't stopped yet. *Breaking point*: none
   as an instance; as an intuition pump it doesn't cover the at-rest half.
2. **Air hockey / ice rink**: the closest everyday approach to
   frictionlessness. *Breaking point*: even these stop eventually — use as
   the second point on the friction-dial, not as "frictionless."
3. **Space probe** (Voyager, coasting for decades, engines off): the
   real frictionless case. *Breaking point*: gravity still curves its
   path — fine at this node, becomes the teaching point at orbits.
4. **Story analogy**: the lazy king who continues whatever he was doing —
   feasting or sleeping — until a messenger (a force) interrupts; the
   kingdom's couriers (friction) constantly interrupt, so nobody has ever
   seen him left alone.
5. **Visual analogy**: the motion-strip spacing (above) — equal-spacing =
   left alone.

**ANTI-ANALOGIES**:
- **"The ball uses up its energy"** — energy language at this node
  *re-installs the impetus model with scientific vocabulary* (motion as a
  consumable). Energy is a later, different bookkeeping; here it is poison.
- **"Objects WANT to keep moving" / desire language** beyond the first
  playful introduction — reinforces animism that then blocks force-pair
  reasoning at the third law. Retire "lazy/wants" within the same lesson
  it's introduced ("that was our joke version — the real version: it just
  doesn't change unless changed").

## Demonstration library

- **The friction dial (the core demo, predict-first, home-doable)**: same
  coin, same flick, across sandpaper → wood/table → glass/ice tray. Predict
  each distance BEFORE each flick. Then the extrapolation question (see M1
  recovery). No equipment beyond a coin and surfaces.
- **Tablecloth pull / card-and-coin (at-rest half, home)**: coin on a card
  on a glass; flick the card — the coin drops IN, having "refused" to
  start moving. Predict first: "where does the coin go?"
- **The bus-brake enactment (physical activity)**: learner walks carrying
  a full cup, stops abruptly — the water continues. Their own body is the
  apparatus.
- **Teacher demo (spectacle with mechanism)**: heavy ball hung on a thread,
  identical thread dangling below — slow pull snaps the TOP thread
  (weight adds), jerk snaps the BOTTOM (inertia wins). Predict which,
  first, both times.
- **Digital/interactive**: friction-slider simulation — the one genuinely
  simulation-shaped question at this node ("set friction to zero — now
  predict, then run"); inverse items ("what friction setting made this
  motion strip?") per `../assessment/08 §5`.

## Discovery lesson (discoverable — fully designed)

1. **Need**: the friction-dial demo creates it — the learner's own
   predictions succeed for rough surfaces and the pattern demands
   completion: each smoother surface carries the coin further. What is the
   pattern's limit?
2. **Playground**: surfaces of graded roughness, one coin, a measuring
   tape. Constraint: same flick each time (calibrate by practice).
3. **Invention**: the learner states their own rule for "what makes it
   stop." (Expected first inventions: "it runs out further on smooth" —
   impetus with a friction knob. That's progress; friction is now visible.)
4. **Collision**: "Your rule says it runs out. But the smoother the floor,
   the LONGER it takes to run out. What would perfectly smooth do?" The
   invented rule must either add "...and with no roughness it never runs
   out" — at which point it has become the first law — or defend a stopping
   point with no stopper, which the learner feels collapse.
5. **Formalization**: "What you just concluded — nothing to stop it, so it
   doesn't stop — is Newton's First Law. Newton's version adds the mirror
   half: nothing to start it, so it doesn't start. One sentence: motion
   doesn't change by itself."
6. **Compression**: apply cold to the spaceship item and the resting book
   (both halves, both directions), same session; the versioning speech
   about Aristotle closes it.

## Teaching actions (dispatch for this concept)

In order: **prediction activity** (every demo here is prediction-first —
this concept without committed predictions is a recitation exercise) →
**demonstration** (friction dial) → **guided discovery** (above) →
**thought experiment** (deep space, frictionless ice — this concept is THE
natural home of the genre; the real experiment is impossible by definition)
→ **physical enactment** (bus-brake walk) → **simulation** (friction slider,
inverse items) → **error analysis** (a written "the force of the throw keeps
it going" answer to dissect) → **teach-back** with the tutor performing M1.
*Poor fits*: story as primary carrier (narrative causality actively feeds
animism here — story is garnish only); games (no natural mechanic embodies
"nothing happens without cause" — a game about nothing happening isn't one).

## Voice teaching

- **Listen for impetus vocabulary**: "the force of the throw", "its force
  runs out", "it loses its push" — each is M1 audible in ordinary talk;
  log without interrupting, collide later.
- **Characteristic hesitation**: the question "what keeps it moving?" —
  a learner mid-transition pauses long here (the correct answer, "nothing
  needs to", feels like insolence). The hesitation is the model changing;
  give it the full 3-second silence and more.
- **The load-bearing sentence** (slow, low, repeat): "Force doesn't make
  things move. Force makes things CHANGE how they move."
- **Prosody trap to avoid**: reciting the law rhythmically ("an object in
  motion stays in motion...") invites sing-song memorization — the learner
  chants it while believing the opposite. Break the rhythm: ask for it in
  their words, never in the jingle.
- Self-corrections ("it stops because— wait, WHAT stops it?") are the
  advanced model arriving; celebrate the question itself.

## Assessment

- **Golden probe**: the frictionless-puck item (M1: "slows and stops" /
  correct: "same speed forever" / M3 variant appears when masses differ).
  Secondary: the resting-book force count (M2).
- **Distractor-mapped item**: "A ball rolls across grass and stops. Why?"
  A. the force of the kick ran out (←M1) · B. friction from the grass
  acted on it (key) · C. balls naturally stop; that's what objects do
  (←M1, Aristotelian phrasing) · D. its energy was used up (←M1 in energy
  clothing — the anti-analogy as a distractor).
- **Guided practice**: force-or-no-force sorting on motion strips (equal
  vs changing spacing); "find the hidden force" scenarios.
- **Independent practice**: fresh-surface prediction items (curling stone,
  space debris, coin on dashboard during braking).
- **Mastery gate set** (per `../assessment/05 §3` + repaired-misconception
  additions): two production predictions on unlike surfaces (one Earth-
  with-friction, one space); one unannounced item mixed into later
  kinematics work; delayed next-session retrieval; the SPEEDED disguised
  re-probe is mandatory here (M1 is the canonical regrowing misconception
  — a slow correct answer is the old model being consciously overridden,
  which is progress but not yet mastery); explanation at rubric ≥3
  ("change needs cause, motion doesn't").
- **Transfer items**: near — new sliding scenarios; far — why seatbelts
  (the learner explains a law, not a rule); cross-subject — none via KG
  cross_links at this node, but forward: `phys.mech.equilibrium` (its
  unlock) is the at-rest half industrialized; real-world — "why do you
  stumble forward when the train brakes?"; expert — spontaneously asking
  "what's the force?" when anything changes speed in daily life.
- **Voice assessment**: oral prediction items (latency on the puck item is
  the misconception-regrowth meter — track it across weeks); confidence
  before every reveal. Calibration note: this concept's signature is
  *confident-wrong at entry, hesitant-correct for weeks after repair* —
  both quadrants are expected stations, not anomalies; certify only at
  confident-correct-fast.

## Recovery notes (concept-specific only — engine: Delivery 1)

- Likeliest utterances: **"but that's obviously wrong"** (the learner's
  intuition outranking the law — this is engagement, not defiance; route
  into the friction-dial rather than debating) and **"I get it but I don't
  believe it"** — the honest mid-transition state; the correct response
  validates it explicitly: "perfect — that's exactly the stage between the
  old model and the new one. Believing follows predicting; let's predict
  more."
- The concept-specific smaller question (for "I don't know"): "Push your
  pencil across the desk and let go. What stopped it — did something stop
  it, or did it just... stop?" — touchable, immediate, and already the
  whole lesson in miniature.

## Memory & review

- **Type**: concept, with a repaired-misconception overlay (M1) — which
  puts it on the LONGEST verification tail in the physics curriculum:
  speeded disguised re-probes at expanding intervals for months
  (`../assessment/05 §3`).
- **Review form**: fresh prediction scenarios (concept review = re-applying
  in new contexts), never re-stating the law.
- **Interleaving partners**: second-law items (force present → what
  changes?) and equilibrium items (forces balanced → nothing changes) —
  the three-way discrimination IS Newtonian mechanics; also mix Earth vs
  space surfaces (M3's discrimination).
- **Deviation from default**: never certify from slow-correct answers on
  this node (see calibration note) — hold at current interval until fast.

## Transfer map

- **Near**: any released/coasting object (curling, ice, casters, space).
- **Far**: seatbelts, headrests, tablecloth trick, why you can't throw
  darts from a moving carousel.
- **Cross-subject**: design of experiments — the first law is the template
  of "isolate the variable" thinking (what happens when NOTHING acts is
  the control condition of the universe).
- **Real-world**: vehicle safety reasoning; sports (why following through
  doesn't "add force after release" — the release ends the interaction).
- **Expert transfer**: the learner spontaneously reframes any "what keeps
  it going?" question — in economics, in habit formation, anywhere — into
  "what's acting to change it?"; the law as a thinking template.
