# Letter-Sound Correspondence — `eng.phonics.letter-sound-correspondence`

## Identity

- **Concept ID**: `eng.phonics.letter-sound-correspondence` (canonical
  english KG; aliases: grapheme-phoneme correspondence, GPC)
- **Curriculum location**: english / phonics
- **Prerequisites** (from KG `requires`, with the load-bearing part):
  - `eng.phonics.alphabet-recognition` — the load-bearing part is visual
    letter identity (telling b/d/p/q apart, recognizing a letter across
    fonts and cases), NOT alphabet-song order. Song fluency without visual
    identity is a common false floor — probe by pointing, never by singing.
  - `eng.phonics.blending-segmenting` — the load-bearing part is ORAL
    blending with no print ("/k/ /a/ /t/ — what word?"). A child who cannot
    blend sounds by ear cannot blend them by eye; letters added to broken
    oral blending produce double failure. This prerequisite is checked by
    voice alone.
- **Unlocks** (from KG): `eng.phonics.consonants`, `eng.phonics.short-vowels`,
  `eng.phonics.sight-words` — i.e., this node is the gate to reading itself.
  Its KG mastery threshold (0.85) is set high for exactly that reason, and
  this entry endorses it: partial GPC mastery produces guessing readers.
- **Difficulty**: foundational · **Bloom**: understand · **Mastery
  threshold**: 0.85 (KG) · **Est. hours**: 3 ·
  **References (KG)**: National Reading Panel (2000); CCSS Foundational
  Skills K-5
- **Learning objectives** — the learner can:
  1. Produce the SOUND (not the name) for each taught letter, clipped and
     schwa-free, on sight.
  2. Point to the letter that makes a spoken sound (sound → grapheme, the
     reverse direction — both directions are the objective).
  3. Use letter-sounds to decode a simple untaught CVC word by blending.
  4. Read a nonsense CVC word ("zat", "vop") — proof of correspondence use
     rather than word memory.
  5. State, in child terms, the difference between a letter's name and its
     sound, and when each is used.

## Mental models

- **Beginner model — "letters are keys that make sounds"**: each letter,
  pressed by the eye, plays one sound; words are sounds played in a row,
  left to right. Deliberately one-to-one at this stage. *Shelf-life warning
  (versioning, delivered to the child in child terms)*: "most letters play
  fair. A few letters are tricky and play different sounds in different
  words — when you meet one, that's not the rule breaking, that's a new
  rule arriving."
- **Intermediate model — "teams and choices"**: some sounds are made by
  letter TEAMS (sh, ch, ee), and some letters choose between sounds by
  context (c in cat/city). The key upgrade: the unit of the code is the
  grapheme (1+ letters), not the letter.
- **Advanced model — "an orthography with layers"**: English spelling
  encodes sound AND meaning AND history (sign/signal keep their g for the
  meaning link). Irregulars stop being betrayals and become fossils with
  stories.
- **Expert model — "code among codes"**: alphabetic writing as one solution
  (vs syllabaries, logographs); English as a deep orthography vs Spanish/
  Finnish shallow ones; why decoding instruction matters MORE in English,
  not less, because of that depth.
- **Upgrade discipline**: the one-to-one beginner model is installed FIRST
  and violations are deferred — early exceptions ("but 'the' doesn't sound
  like t-h-e!") are acknowledged, named as "tricky words we'll collect",
  and parked; the system must feel reliable before its edges are toured,
  or the child concludes the code is not worth trusting (the exact
  conclusion guessing-readers act on).

## Why beginners fail here

Four tangled mechanisms, all voice-visible and none age-shameful (Delivery 1,
seed concept 2, anchored here to the KG node): (1) **letter NAMES intrude**
on letter sounds — "ay" is not /a/, and blending names produces noise;
(2) **schwa contamination** — consonants taught as "kuh/tuh" make 3-sound
words mathematically unblendable ("kuh-a-tuh" is five sounds); (3)
**direction** — tracking right-to-left reverses words (a `print-concepts`
floor gap surfacing here); (4) **first-letter guessing** — the habit of
predicting the word from its first letter plus context, which actively
displaces decoding practice. A struggling reader at this node usually has
two or more at once; generic "practice more" fixes none.

## Misconception library

**M1 — "Letters say their names"**
- *Why*: instruction- and environment-induced (alphabet songs, toys, and
  well-meaning adults teach names first and hardest — names are the child's
  prior knowledge, arriving before the concept that needs sounds).
- *Symptom / sound*: "see-ay-tee" for cat; blending attempts that use
  name-fragments ("kay-at").
- *Detection probe (verbatim, voice)*: "What SOUND does this letter make?"
  while showing K — the name ("kay") in response = M1. Instant, one item.
- *Recovery*: explicit two-register framing: "Letters are like people — a
  NAME and a JOB. The name is for talking about the letter. The job — the
  sound — is for reading. When we read, letters do their jobs." Then the
  sorting game: tutor says either a name or a sound; child says "name!" or
  "sound!" — the distinction itself becomes the game.
- *Verification*: name/sound sorted correctly on demand across taught
  letters, delayed to next session; decoding attempt free of name intrusion.

**M2 — Schwa-contaminated consonants ("kuh", "tuh", "puh")**
- *Why*: instruction-induced — adults naturally voice stop consonants with
  a vowel tail because pure stops feel unpronounceable in isolation.
- *Symptom / sound*: blends "kuh-a-tuh" into "kuhatuh" and looks betrayed;
  the word never assembles.
- *Detection probe (voice-only, verbatim)*: "Make the /k/ sound by itself."
  An audible "uh" tail = M2. Also detectable in the ADULT helping at home —
  and worth coaching there (see recovery).
- *Recovery*: the whisper test — "whisper the sound; if you can SING it,
  you've added 'uh'." Stop consonants are clipped: /k/, /t/, /p/. For
  continuous sounds (/m/, /s/, /f/) the opposite skill: stretch them
  ("mmmm"). The two families are taught as "quick sounds" and "stretchy
  sounds". Parent-facing line (the highest-ROI sentence in home reading):
  "never say 'kuh' for K — the extra 'uh' is why blending isn't working."
- *Verification*: clean isolated sounds across the taught set, then a
  successful 3-sound blend; the specific listen: no schwa between phonemes
  during the blend.

**M3 — Blending as chopping (segment-then-stall)**
- *Why*: over-practice of segmentation (a spelling skill) applied to
  reading; sounds produced as separated beads that never join.
- *Symptom / sound*: "/k/ ... /a/ ... /t/" with full stops, then silence or
  a guess; can say all three sounds, cannot fuse them.
- *Detection*: audible in any decoding attempt — the stops between sounds.
- *Recovery*: continuous blending — "sing the word slowly without stopping:
  caaaat" (stretch, don't chop); successive blending as the ladder (/ka/,
  then /kat/); the finger slides under the letters WITHOUT lifting (the
  finger's continuity models the voice's).
- *Verification*: cold decode of an untaught CVC with continuous voicing;
  the finger observed not lifting.

**M4 — Right-to-left tracking (reads "tac")**
- *Why*: print-directionality floor gap (`eng.phonics.print-concepts` is
  a sibling foundation) surfacing at this node.
- *Symptom*: reversed decodes; starting at the wrong end; wandering entry
  point.
- *Detection*: watch the finger/eyes at the first letter — silent, instant.
- *Recovery*: physical — finger-tracking left to right under each letter,
  every time, until automatic; a green dot at the word's start as a
  temporary scaffold (with its fade planned, per scaffolding law).
- *Verification*: unprompted correct-direction attack on fresh words;
  scaffold removed.

**M5 — First-letter guessing ("horse" for "house", picture-assisted)**
- *Why*: habit — sometimes explicitly taught ("use the picture!"), always
  self-reinforcing because it often works in picture books, and it is
  faster than decoding while decoding is still slow.
- *Symptom / phrase*: fast whole-word guesses sharing the first letter;
  accuracy collapses when the picture is covered.
- *Detection probe*: nonsense words — "zat" cannot be guessed, only
  decoded (the node's golden probe, objective 4); also: cover the picture
  and re-try the same page.
- *Recovery*: make decoding the winning move — nonsense-word games where
  guessing is impossible by construction; explicit praise for SOUNDING OUT
  even when slower ("you worked it out — that's real reading"); pictures
  repositioned as after-reading confirmation, never before-reading clue.
- *Verification*: nonsense CVC read-rate at/near real-CVC rate; no
  first-letter substitutions across a delayed passage.

## Explanation library

- **To the child (5–7, the primary audience — story frame)**: "Letters are
  little sound-buttons. This one plays /k/. This one plays /a/. Press them
  in order, sliding, and they join into a word: caaat — cat! You just
  READ." (The word "read" said with ceremony — the identity moment
  matters.)
- **To the child, name-vs-sound (M1 framing)**: "Your name is {child's
  name}, but you can also jump. A letter's name is 'kay', but its JOB is
  /k/. Reading uses jobs, not names."
- **To the returning older struggler (8–11, dignity-first)**: "Reading is a
  code, and nobody ever showed you the two keys: letters have sounds (not
  just names), and the sounds slide together without stopping. You're not
  bad at reading — you were given the wrong keys. New keys, ten minutes."
- **To the parent/home helper (the force-multiplier explanation)**: "Two
  things fix most blending trouble at home: (1) never add 'uh' — whisper
  the quick sounds, K is /k/ not 'kuh'; (2) stretch, don't chop — 'caaat',
  not 'kuh. a. tuh.' And let pictures be the reward after the word, not
  the hint before it."
- **Visual frame**: three boxes under the word, one sound per box; the
  finger slides through all three boxes in one motion.
- **Logical frame (for the older learner who wants the system)**: "26
  letters, ~44 sounds — so it can't be one-to-one, and that's WHY there
  are teams and tricky words later. The system is a code with history,
  not a broken promise."

## Analogy library

1. **Train cars coupling** (BEST): each letter-car carries one sound;
   pushed together slowly they couple into one train (word). Models both
   the order and the continuity of blending. *Breaking point*: digraphs
   (sh = one sound, two letters — "two cars sharing one load") — upgrade,
   don't discard, at the teams stage.
2. **Sound-buttons** (alternative): letters as buttons that PLAY sounds —
   maps the sight→sound directionality cleanly. *Breaking point*: implies
   exactly one sound per button; retire at letter-choice (c, g).
3. **Story analogy**: shy animal sounds that only hold hands if you
   approach slowly and smoothly; stop suddenly and they let go (continuous
   blending as gentleness — useful for the M3 child who chops).
4. **Visual analogy**: pouring three colors slowly into one swirl — the
   blend as literally continuous.
5. **Magnet snap**: sounds pulled close enough SNAP together — good for
   the final fusion moment.

**ANTI-ANALOGIES**:
- **"Letters SAY their names"** in any phrasing (including toys/songs that
  do) — installs M1 directly. The tree's standing rule: letters *have*
  names and *make* sounds; only the sounds read.
- **"Sound it out letter by letter, one at a time"** as an instruction —
  the phrasing installs M3 (chopping). The correct instruction is "say it
  slowly and smoothly", never "one at a time".
- **Phonetic respelling / IPA for beginners** — visual noise that helps no
  child; sounds are taught by voice and example words only (this is also
  the platform's voice-first advantage: the sound channel IS the content).

## Demonstration library

- **Home (no equipment)**: sound boxes drawn on paper, a coin pushed into
  each box per sound, then one finger-slide to blend. Two minutes,
  every day beats twenty minutes weekly (memory engine: spacing).
- **Learner activity (production)**: "you stretch it, I guess your word" —
  the child produces stretched blends, tutor plays the guesser; production
  cements what reception introduced.
- **Teacher demo (voice mirror)**: tutor models the stretched blend
  ("caaat"), child echoes; tutor models a WRONG chop ("kuh. a. tuh — can
  you hear why the word can't find itself?") — the planted error made
  audible.
- **Interactive/digital**: letters as draggable train cars that play their
  pure clipped sound on touch, and the continuous blend on coupling —
  the interface enforcing schwa-free audio is the point (an app that says
  "kuh" is an M2-installation machine; this is a hard content-quality
  requirement for any digital asset at this node).
- **Physical activity**: hop-the-boxes — one floor tile per sound, walked
  smoothly while voicing, then "walked fast" into the whole word.

## Discovery lesson — direct instruction wins here (with one discovery moment)

Letter-sound mappings are **conventions** (Delivery 2 §2 boundary rule):
which squiggle makes which sound is a social agreement, undiscoverable by
any playground — a child cannot derive that ⟨k⟩ plays /k/. So the mappings
themselves are told, briefly, joyfully, and drilled to automaticity (this
node is a TOOL skill — see memory section). One genuine discovery moment is
preserved and staged: **the blending click** — given /k/ /a/ /t/ said
slowly-then-faster by their own voice, the child DISCOVERS the word
assembling ("...caaat... CAT!"). That click (need: "what word is hiding?" →
collision: chopping fails → resolution: smooth joining works) is the
emotional payload of the whole node and should be engineered for, protected,
and celebrated — it is the moment reading starts belonging to the child.

## Teaching actions (dispatch for this concept)

In order: **voice interaction** (this node IS voice — modeling, echoing,
producing; see below) → **physical enactment** (sound boxes, hop-the-boxes,
finger-tracking) → **game** (nonsense-word monster: feed the monster only
words you can READ — the mechanic makes decoding the winning move, M5's
counter) → **teacher demo with planted error** (the audible chop) →
**worked example** (tutor decodes a fresh word aloud, thinking audibly:
"quick sound /k/... stretchy /aaa/... slide...") → **drawing** (child draws
boxes under new words unprompted = the strategy internalizing).
*Poor fits*: thought experiments, concept maps, error analysis on paper
(all require meta-language the age group doesn't have); silent written
worksheets (the content is SOUND — silence removes the signal).

## Voice teaching

This is a **voice-required node** (the KG's whole phonics domain is): every
misconception above is audible and none is reliably visible in text.

- **Listen for**: schwa tails on stop consonants (M2); letter names inside
  decoding attempts (M1); full stops between sounds (M3); reversed order
  (M4); instant whole-word guesses (M5). Five failure modes, five distinct
  sounds — a 10-second decode attempt is a complete diagnostic.
- **Hesitation patterns**: >2s latency between letters = sound-recall gap
  (teach that letter, not blending); smooth stretch with the WRONG vowel =
  vowel confusion (route to `eng.phonics.short-vowels` minimal-pair
  listening: cat/cot/cut by ear first, no print).
- **Pronunciation stakes**: the TUTOR'S model is the curriculum — clipped
  stops, honest stretchy sounds, no schwa, unhurried. Accent variation is
  fine and said so ("people say sounds a little differently — your /a/ is
  yours"); contamination is not.
- **The load-bearing sentence** (slow, warm): "Say it slowly and smoothly —
  don't stop between the sounds."
- **Silence discipline**: after presenting a fresh word, the 3-second wait
  is sacred; the child's slow assembly IS the learning, and rescuing at
  second two steals the click.

## Assessment

- **Golden probe**: one nonsense CVC ("zat") — decodes it = correspondence
  + blending both work, regardless of vocabulary; refuses or substitutes a
  real word = M5 or a blending gap (follow the sound: which of M1–M4 was
  audible?).
- **Diagnostic set (voice, 60 seconds)**: "sound of this letter?" ×3
  (M1/M2), one oral blend with no print (floor check), one real CVC, one
  nonsense CVC, watch-the-finger (M4). Six observations, five
  misconceptions covered, one minute.
- **Guided practice**: boxes + coins with the tutor; successive blending
  ladder (/ka/ → /kat/); "which letter makes /m/?" (sound → grapheme,
  the reverse direction, objective 2).
- **Independent practice**: fresh CVC sets rotating all taught letters;
  nonsense words mixed 1-in-4 (keeps decoding honest, per M5).
- **Mastery gate set** (per `../assessment/05 §3`, TOOL-skill variant):
  production of sounds across the taught set, clipped, at automaticity
  (latency flatlined against the child's own baseline — this is a tool
  skill; correct-but-slow is NOT done, it will consume the working memory
  that comprehension needs later); three untaught real CVCs + two nonsense
  CVCs decoded cold; delayed next-session repeat of two of them; both
  directions (letter→sound and sound→letter); the name/sound sort passed
  once more, delayed. The KG's 0.85 threshold is honored as: no more than
  one miss across the gate set, and no misconception-typed miss at all.
- **Transfer items**: near — new CVCs with taught letters; far — the
  child's own name's first sound found in OTHER words ("you're M — find
  /m/ in 'mat'"); real-world — environmental print (cereal boxes, signs:
  "find a word you can read out there this week" — reading leaves the
  lesson); expert — spontaneous decoding attempts on unfamiliar words
  anywhere (the strongest evidence; celebrate every sighting).
- **Voice assessment / calibration**: all assessment at this node is oral;
  confidence read behaviorally only (this age can't self-report — Delivery
  1 grid + `../assessment/04 §2`); characteristic pattern: hesitant-correct
  dominates for weeks (normal — consolidating), and the correct response
  is one more same-type item, never advancement (04 §5).

## Recovery notes (concept-specific only — engine: Delivery 1)

- Likeliest utterances: **"I can't read"** (identity statement, age-adapted
  script: "you can't read YET — you already know three sound-buttons,
  that's the start of reading" — then prove it with a word made only of
  known buttons) and pre-verbal giving-up (silence, face-hiding — treat as
  "I give up": tutor takes over, reads a word aloud slowly with visible
  enjoyment, zero demand, then offers the child just the LAST sound of the
  next word — re-entry through the smallest possible door).
- The concept-specific smaller question (for "I don't know" on a word):
  drop from the word to ONE letter — "just this one: what's its sound?" —
  and rebuild the word from their success outward.

## Memory & review

- **Type**: TOOL skill — the automaticity target of the whole tree
  (Delivery 2 §8: tool skills must run below consciousness to free working
  memory for meaning; a child sounding out effortfully at age 9 pays a
  comprehension tax on every sentence for years).
- **Review form**: short daily fluency bursts (2–3 minutes, game-framed)
  until latency flatlines; then maintenance spacing. Letter-sound facts are
  the one legitimate flashcard kingdom in early English — as SOUNDS, oral,
  never silent cards.
- **Interleaving partners**: newly taught letters mixed with the oldest
  taught letters every burst (pure recency practice creates the illusion
  of the full set); real words mixed with nonsense words (M5 hygiene);
  letter→sound mixed with sound→letter (directional flexibility).
- **Deviation from default**: frequency-first letter order pays off —
  review weights letters by usefulness (s,a,t,p,i,n-style sets make words
  from week one), not alphabet order; the alphabet-order review set is an
  anti-pattern here (it delays word-making, which delays the click, which
  delays the identity: "I am someone who reads").

## Transfer map

- **Near**: every new CVC word; the child's own name deconstructed.
- **Far**: teams stage (sh/ch/ee) — the model upgrade this node's
  versioning line promised; writing (segmenting is this skill run in
  reverse — spelling and reading co-train).
- **Cross-subject**: mathematics symbol-reading (the meta-lesson "squiggles
  systematically mean things, and I can learn codes" transfers as a
  disposition); music notation later, for the same reason.
- **Real-world**: environmental print everywhere — the transfer target IS
  daily life, immediately, and every out-of-lesson decode is gate-grade
  evidence.
- **Expert transfer**: the older learner who understands WHY English
  spelling is layered (meaning + history) reads unfamiliar technical words
  by structure — the disposition this node plants at age five matures into
  morphological reading at fifteen.
