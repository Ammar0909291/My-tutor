## Learning Objective
Classify haloalkanes by degree of substitution (1°, 2°, 3°) and carbon count; use IUPAC nomenclature for haloalkanes and haloarenes; describe the effect of C–X bond polarity on physical properties (boiling point, solubility); and explain relative C–X bond reactivity in terms of bond enthalpy and polarizability.

## Core Understanding
**Classification of haloalkanes**:
Haloalkanes (alkyl halides) are compounds in which one or more hydrogen atoms of an alkane are replaced by halogen (F, Cl, Br, I). Classification by degree of substitution of the carbon bearing the halogen:
- **Primary (1°)**: C–X carbon bonded to one alkyl group (e.g., CH₃CH₂Cl, 1-chloroethane).
- **Secondary (2°)**: C–X carbon bonded to two alkyl groups (e.g., (CH₃)₂CHCl, 2-chloropropane).
- **Tertiary (3°)**: C–X carbon bonded to three alkyl groups (e.g., (CH₃)₃CCl, 2-chloro-2-methylpropane).
- Geminal dihalides: two halogens on the same carbon; vicinal dihalides: two halogens on adjacent carbons.

**Haloarenes**: aryl halides have the halogen bonded directly to an aromatic ring carbon (e.g., chlorobenzene, C₆H₅Cl; 2-chlorotoluene). Vinyl halides (halogen on C=C) and allylic halides (halogen on C adjacent to C=C) are named and behave distinctively.

**IUPAC nomenclature**:
- Name the longest C chain carrying the halogen as the parent alkane.
- Number to give the halogen the lowest locant.
- Name halogens as prefixes: fluoro, chloro, bromo, iodo — alphabetically before other substituents if equal priority.
- Examples: CH₃CHBrCH₃ = 2-bromopropane; CHCl₃ = trichloromethane (chloroform); CHBrClF = bromochlorofluoromethane (IUPAC: bromochlorofluoromethane).
- For haloarenes: the ring is the parent when halogen is directly bonded. Chlorobenzene (no locant needed for one substituent); 1,2-dichlorobenzene (o-dichlorobenzene); 1-bromo-4-chlorobenzene.

**Physical properties — effect of C–X bond polarity**:
- **Boiling points**: haloalkanes have higher boiling points than alkanes of similar molecular mass because the polar C–X bond contributes to permanent dipole-dipole interactions (in addition to London dispersion forces present in all molecules). Trend: bps increase with molecular mass (more electrons → stronger London forces); bps increase in the order F < Cl < Br < I at the same carbon skeleton because polarizability increases with increasing atomic size (London forces dominant over dipole-dipole as size increases).
- For monohalides at same carbon count, bp: CH₃F < CH₃Cl < CH₃Br < CH₃I (16 < −24 < 3.6 < 42.5 °C).
- **Density**: haloalkanes are denser than the corresponding alkanes; CCl₄ (1.59 g/mL), CHCl₃ (1.47 g/mL) are denser than water → sink. Fluoroalkanes may be lighter.
- **Solubility**: haloalkanes are generally INSOLUBLE in water (non-polar C chain cannot form H-bonds; C–X dipole-dipole alone insufficient to overcome the strong H-bonding network of water). Soluble in non-polar organic solvents (like dissolves like). Fluorinated solvents (e.g., fluorobenzene) are particularly dense and immiscible with water.

**C–X bond character and reactivity**:
- **Bond polarity**: C–X bond is polar (C^δ+ – X^δ−) because X is more electronegative than C. Polarity order: C–F > C–Cl > C–Br > C–I (decreasing electronegativity difference with increasing atomic number).
- **Bond enthalpy**: C–F > C–Cl > C–Br > C–I (bond energy decreases with increasing atomic radius; Cδ+ is held less tightly by F⁻ than by larger, more diffuse X⁻ anions). C–F bond is extremely strong (~485 kJ/mol); C–I bond is much weaker (~240 kJ/mol).
- **Polarizability**: the electron cloud around F is compact and hard to polarise; the cloud around I is large and soft. Large polarizability means the C–I bond responds more readily to an attacking nucleophile (the bond elongates more easily, lowering the activation energy for substitution or elimination).
- **Reactivity order**: R–I > R–Br > R–Cl >> R–F. Despite C–F being the most polar, C–F is also the STRONGEST and LEAST REACTIVE bond. The RATE of nucleophilic substitution and elimination is controlled by BOND ENTHALPY and POLARIZABILITY (bond-breaking ease), not by bond polarity alone.

**Haloarenes vs. haloalkanes reactivity**:
- Haloarenes (C₆H₅Cl): the C–Cl bond has partial double-bond character because the lone pair on Cl is conjugated into the ring (+M effect). This shortens and strengthens the C–Cl bond compared to haloalkanes; the carbon bearing Cl is less electrophilic (ring electron density). Haloarenes are therefore MUCH LESS REACTIVE in nucleophilic substitution than haloalkanes under normal conditions.
- Nucleophilic aromatic substitution (SNAr) requires strongly electron-withdrawing groups (e.g., –NO₂) ortho/para to the leaving group — these activate the ring for nucleophilic attack at the ipso carbon.

## Mental Models
**C–X bond as two competing factors**: reactivity is the NET of two competing factors — bond polarity (making C electrophilic, ENCOURAGING nucleophilic attack) vs. bond strength (resisting bond breaking, DISCOURAGING attack). For C–F, the enormous bond strength dominates → least reactive. For C–I, the bond is weak and highly polarizable → most reactive, even though C–I is the least polar. Two factors that pull in opposite directions — the bond-strength/polarizability factor wins for reactivity.

## Why Students Fail
Students rank reactivity in the order C–F > C–Cl > C–Br > C–I, following the polarity trend rather than the reactivity trend. They also confuse haloarene stability (conjugation with ring) with haloalkane reactivity, and do not recognise that haloarenes resist nucleophilic substitution under mild conditions.

## Misconceptions
- **MC-1 (Type 2 — perceptual intuition)**: "C–F compounds are the most reactive haloalkanes because C–F is the most polar bond." Probe: "Does C–F react faster with NaOH solution than C–I under the same conditions?" Characteristic phrase: "fluorine is most electronegative so C–F is most reactive." Intervention: the C–F bond is the MOST POLAR haloalkane bond (C^δ+ most electrophilic) BUT also the STRONGEST (highest bond enthalpy, ~485 kJ/mol). The rate of nucleophilic substitution depends on how easily the C–X bond can be BROKEN (activation energy for bond breaking). C–F is hardest to break → SLOWEST to react. C–I is easiest to break (weakest, most polarizable electron cloud, lowest activation energy) → FASTEST to react. Reactivity ORDER: I > Br > Cl >> F — opposite to polarity order. Polarity governs the thermodynamic driving force; bond strength and polarizability govern the kinetic barrier.
- **MC-2 (Type 3 — language contamination)**: "Haloarenes are more reactive than haloalkanes because aromatic systems are 'activated' for reaction." Probe: "What type of reaction does benzene preferentially undergo? Does it readily undergo nucleophilic substitution?" Characteristic phrase: "aromatic = activated = reactive." Intervention: benzene and haloarenes are activated for ELECTROPHILIC AROMATIC SUBSTITUTION, not for nucleophilic substitution. In fact, the C–Cl bond in chlorobenzene is HARDER TO BREAK than in chloroalkanes — the lone pair on Cl is delocalised into the ring (resonance/+M effect), partially double-bond character, shorter C–Cl bond. Chlorobenzene is far LESS reactive in nucleophilic substitution than chloroalkanes. "Aromatic activated" refers to EAS reactivity, not to any substitution at the sp² C–Cl.
- **MC-3 (Type 5 — instruction-induced)**: "All haloalkanes are insoluble in water, so they have no interaction with water at all." Probe: "CHCl₃ (chloroform) has a significant dipole moment. Does this affect its interaction with water in any way?" Characteristic phrase: "insoluble = zero interaction." Intervention: haloalkanes are POORLY SOLUBLE but not completely inert toward water. They may slowly undergo hydrolysis (especially at elevated temperature or with acid/base catalysis: R–Cl + H₂O → R–OH + HCl). The dipole-dipole interaction between C–X and H₂O contributes a small miscibility, especially for smaller molecules (CH₂Cl₂, CHCl₃ are partially miscible with water). "Insoluble" means a very low but non-zero solubility — in organic chemistry, solubility and reactivity are distinct concepts that must not be conflated.

## Analogies
**Valid**: the C–X bond reactivity series is like a combination lock — a more polar C–F lock looks "easier to turn" (highly polarised, obvious electrophilic centre) but the lock's spring (bond strength, 485 kJ/mol) is extremely stiff. The C–I lock looks less obviously positioned (less polar) but has an old, worn spring (weak bond, ~240 kJ/mol, large polarizable cloud) that opens quickly under attack.

## Demonstrations
**Reactivity order by silver halide precipitation**: mix separate solutions of C₂H₅F, C₂H₅Cl, C₂H₅Br, C₂H₅I with aqueous AgNO₃ (or warm with aqueous KOH then add AgNO₃). Rate of AgX precipitate: iodide precipitates first (yellow, fastest reaction), bromide next (pale yellow), chloride slowest (white, slowest), fluoride essentially no precipitate at room temperature. Direct observation of reactivity order.

**Immiscibility demo**: add 1-bromobutane (or CHCl₃) to water in a separating funnel — two distinct layers form, with the denser haloalkane below. This shows insolubility and higher density in one observation.

## Discovery Questions
1. "Rank in order of decreasing boiling point (with reasoning): CH₃Cl, CH₃Br, CH₃I, CH₃F. Then rank in order of decreasing reactivity toward KOH(aq) and explain the difference between the bp and reactivity rankings."
2. "Chlorobenzene cannot be hydrolysed by NaOH(aq) at room temperature and 1 atm, whereas 1-chlorobutane reacts readily. Account for this difference, citing bond character, resonance, and the nature of C–X bond in each molecule."

## Teaching Sequence
1. Classification: 1°/2°/3° haloalkanes; haloarenes (aryl halides). IUPAC naming rules with examples.
2. Physical properties: bp trend (alkyl chain length; halogen identity: F<Cl<Br<I by London forces); density (higher than alkane/water); water insolubility.
3. C–X bond: polarity vs. bond enthalpy vs. polarizability. Two competing trends.
4. Reactivity order: I > Br > Cl >> F. Rationale: bond enthalpy and polarizability dominate over polarity for rate. Practical implications.
5. Haloarenes: lone pair conjugation into ring → partial double bond character → less reactive in nucleophilic substitution → requires activated ring (SNAr) or special conditions.

## Tutor Actions
- **If student ranks reactivity C–F > C–Cl > C–Br > C–I**: ask "What is the C–F bond enthalpy? And C–I? Which bond is easier to break? What does 'breaking the bond' have to do with the rate of substitution?" (Breaking the C–X bond is the key step → weakest/most polarizable breaks fastest → I fastest.)
- **If student says haloarenes are more reactive**: ask "What type of reaction does benzene preferentially undergo?" (EAS, not nucleophilic.) "Is electrophilic substitution the same as nucleophilic substitution?" (No.) "So aromatic activation is for electrophiles, not nucleophiles — haloarenes are actually LESS reactive in nucleophilic substitution."
- **FRAGILE sign**: knows reactivity order I > Br > Cl >> F by memory but cannot explain WHY from bond enthalpy and polarizability.

## Voice Teaching Notes
The polarity vs. reactivity inversion for C–F is the signature learning objective of this concept. In voice: "I want you to tell me two properties of the C–F bond: its polarity, and its strength. Are these consistent or contradictory in terms of reactivity?" (Contradictory — most polar but least reactive.) "Which property wins for kinetics?" (Bond strength/polarizability.) Making the contradiction explicit and then resolving it is more memorable than presenting the result alone.

## Assessment Signals
- **Green**: classifies haloalkanes by degree correctly; names using IUPAC correctly; explains the C–F paradox (most polar but least reactive); correctly orders reactivity I > Br > Cl >> F with bond-enthalpy/polarizability justification; explains haloarene reduced reactivity via lone-pair conjugation; states haloarenes are much less reactive than haloalkanes in nucleophilic substitution.
- **Amber**: correct classification and naming but gives polarity as the explanation for reactivity (ranking F as most reactive); or cannot explain the haloarene/haloalkane reactivity difference.
- **Red**: says C–F is most reactive; says haloarenes are more reactive than haloalkanes (confusing EAS activation with nucleophilic substitution); says insoluble means zero interaction with water.
- **Probe**: "Three compounds: C₂H₅Cl, C₆H₅Cl, C₂H₅I. Rank them in order of increasing rate of nucleophilic substitution by NaOH(aq) at 25°C and 1 atm. Justify each step."

## Tutor Recovery Strategy
If student cannot explain haloarene reduced reactivity: ask them to draw the resonance structures of chlorobenzene. "How many of these resonance structures show C–Cl double bond character?" (3 of the resonance hybrids place positive charge on Cl — or equivalently, lone pair of Cl is in π system → partial C=Cl character.) "If the C–Cl bond has some double bond character, is it shorter or longer than C–Cl in a haloalkane?" (Shorter.) "Is it stronger?" (Yes.) "So is it easier or harder to break?" (Harder.) This resonance argument makes the lower reactivity structurally concrete.

## Memory Hooks
- **Reactivity order**: "I > Br > Cl >> F. Bond strength wins over polarity for kinetics."
- **C–F paradox**: "Most polar, strongest bond, LEAST reactive in substitution/elimination."
- **Boiling points**: "F < Cl < Br < I (increasing London forces with increasing size/polarizability of X)."
- **Haloarenes vs. haloalkanes**: "Haloarene C–X in π system (resonance/+M) → shorter, stronger, harder to break → much less reactive in nucleophilic substitution."

## Cross-Subject Connections
- **Environmental chemistry**: chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs, e.g., CCl₂F₂, Freon) are stable haloalkanes (strong C–F and C–Cl bonds) used as refrigerants; photodissociation in the stratosphere releases Cl• radicals that catalytically destroy ozone. The Montreal Protocol (1987) phased out CFCs, replaced by hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) and hydrofluoroolefins (HFOs) — the chemistry of C–X bond stability directly governs environmental persistence.
- **Pharmacology**: many drugs contain halogens (fluorine is found in ~20% of pharmaceuticals, e.g., fluoxetine, ciprofloxacin, atorvastatin). Fluorine's metabolic stability (strong C–F bond resists oxidative metabolism), its ability to mimic the C–H bond sterically, and its effect on lipophilicity are all exploited in medicinal chemistry.
