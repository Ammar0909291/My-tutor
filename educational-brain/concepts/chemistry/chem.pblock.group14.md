## Learning Objective
Describe the allotropy of carbon, the properties and industrial importance of CO and CO₂, the chemistry of Si as the basis of semiconductor technology and silicate materials, and the inert pair effect manifested in Sn and Pb.

## Core Understanding
Group 14: C, Si, Ge, Sn, Pb. Valence configuration: ns²np². Common OS: +4 (all four valence electrons involved); also +2 (inert pair effect, increasingly stable for Sn and Pb).

**Carbon allotropes**:
- Diamond: each C sp³ bonded to 4 others → rigid tetrahedral network; hardest natural substance; electrical insulator (no free electrons); transparent.
- Graphite: each C sp² bonded to 3 others in layers; the fourth electron forms a delocalised π system across each layer → electrical conductor; layers held by weak van der Waals forces → lubricant; opaque (absorbs visible light via π electrons).
- Graphene: single layer of graphite; zero-band-gap semiconductor/metallic character at Dirac points; Nobel Prize 2010.
- Buckminsterfullerene C₆₀: spherical cage molecule; each C is sp² with pentagonal and hexagonal faces; soluble in organic solvents (unlike diamond and graphite); superconducting when doped with alkali metals.

**Carbon chemistry**:
- CO: highly toxic (binds Fe²⁺ in haemoglobin, 200× stronger than O₂); reducing agent (removes O from metal oxides in smelting: Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂); Lewis base (donates lone pair from C to metal centres in carbonyls).
- CO₂: linear (O=C=O, sp hybridised C); acidic oxide (CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ HCO₃⁻ + H⁺); solid CO₂ (dry ice) sublimes at −78.5°C (no liquid phase at 1 atm due to phase diagram); greenhouse gas.
- Carbonic acid/bicarbonate buffer: relevant to blood pH (chem.equil.buffer).

**Silicon**:
- Semiconductor: band gap ~1.1 eV (visible-to-near-IR photons bridge it) → basis of solar cells and transistors.
- SiO₂: giant covalent (network) solid; high mp (quartz, sand); not acidic in water (unlike CO₂) but reacts with HF (SiO₂ + 4HF → SiF₄ + 2H₂O — the only acid that dissolves glass) and molten alkali (SiO₂ + 2NaOH → Na₂SiO₃ + H₂O).
- Silicates: SiO₄⁴⁻ tetrahedra linked in chains (pyroxenes), sheets (micas), 3D networks (feldspars, quartz); basis of Earth's crust minerals.
- Silicones: synthetic polymers (Si–O backbone with organic side chains); heat-resistant, water-repellent, biocompatible.

**Inert pair effect in Sn and Pb**:
- Sn: both +2 (SnO, SnCl₂ — reducing agent) and +4 (SnO₂, SnCl₄) stable; Sn²⁺ is reducing.
- Pb: +2 dominant (PbO, PbCl₂); Pb⁴⁺ is strongly oxidising (PbO₂ in lead-acid battery: PbO₂ + SO₄²⁻ + 4H⁺ + 2e⁻ → PbSO₄ + 2H₂O).

## Mental Models
**The allotrope property map**: C's three allotropes (diamond, graphite, C₆₀) differ ONLY in bonding arrangement (sp³ vs sp² vs sp² curved) — same element, vastly different properties. The bonding model predicts all observable differences.

**The diagonal C–Si comparison**: C and Si both have 4 valence electrons but differ fundamentally: C forms multiple bonds easily (π bonds) → organic chemistry; Si strongly prefers single bonds (sp³ → SiO₄ tetrahedra, silicate chains, NOT Si=O=Si chains) → silicate mineral chemistry.

## Why Students Fail
Students extend the CO₂ model to SiO₂ and expect SiO₂ to be a gas with acidic aqueous chemistry — SiO₂ is a giant covalent solid with no molecular form because Si does not form stable π bonds with O.

Students confuse CO (toxic reducing agent, Lewis base) with CO₂ (acidic oxide, not toxic in the same way).

## Misconceptions
- **MC-1 (Type 6 — analogy overextension)**: "SiO₂ behaves like CO₂ — it dissolves in water to form an acid." Probe: "Write the equation for SiO₂ dissolving in water." Characteristic phrase: "silicon dioxide + water = silicic acid." Intervention: SiO₂ is a network covalent solid; it does NOT dissolve in water at room temperature; the C→Si analogy breaks down because Si cannot form π bonds with O (unlike C=O); SiO₂ reacts with MOLTEN NaOH or HF, not with liquid water.
- **MC-2 (Type 2 — perceptual intuition)**: "Diamond conducts electricity because it is made of carbon like graphite." Probe: "Which allotrope of carbon conducts electricity — diamond or graphite?" Characteristic phrase: "carbon conducts electricity." Intervention: diamond has no delocalised electrons (all four valence electrons used in σ bonds to four neighbouring C atoms); graphite has a delocalised π system across its layers → conducts electricity; the BONDING arrangement, not the element, determines conductivity.

## Analogies
**Valid**: Graphite as a stack of A4 paper — within each sheet (layer), atoms are tightly bonded (like paper fibres); between sheets, only weak forces (like placing sheets on top of each other unglued) → easy to slide the sheets apart (lubricant), easy to conduct electrons within a sheet.

## Demonstrations
**Graphite conductivity**: connect graphite (pencil lead) between a battery and bulb — the bulb lights up. Contrast with diamond (if available) — insulator. Directly demonstrates allotrope-dependent properties from the same element.

**CO₂ acid test**: bubble CO₂ through limewater (Ca(OH)₂); white precipitate CaCO₃ forms (acidic oxide reacts with alkali → carbonate). Contrast with SiO₂ — no reaction with limewater — confirms network solid vs molecular gas distinction.

## Discovery Questions
1. "Both graphene (single layer of graphite) and diamond are pure carbon. One is a conductor and one is an insulator. Explain the difference using hybridisation and electron delocalisation."
2. "The lead-acid battery uses Pb(s)/PbSO₄ at the anode and PbO₂/PbSO₄ at the cathode. Using OS assignments, identify which electrode involves oxidation and which involves reduction on discharge."

## Teaching Sequence
1. Electronic configurations; +4 and +2 OS; inert pair trend down the group.
2. Carbon allotropes: structure → properties derivation for each; hybridisation connection.
3. CO vs. CO₂: structures, reactions, and uses; toxicity mechanism.
4. Silicon vs. carbon: no π bond argument; SiO₂ as network solid; silicates briefly.
5. Sn and Pb: oxidation state stability; Pb²⁺ dominant; PbO₂ in batteries.

## Tutor Actions
- **If student extends CO₂ analogy to SiO₂**: ask "what is the bonding in CO₂?" (molecular covalent, discrete molecules) vs "what is the bonding in SiO₂?" (giant covalent network, no discrete molecules); ask why Si does not form Si=O double bonds (cannot form stable π bonds with O due to size mismatch).
- **FRAGILE sign**: knows diamond is insulator and graphite is conductor but cannot explain why from their structures.

## Voice Teaching Notes
The allotrope discussion is visually striking — always use or show images of diamond, graphite, C₆₀, and graphene before introducing structures. The visual diversity from the same element motivates the structural explanation.

## Assessment Signals
- **Green**: explains all three C allotropes from structure; distinguishes CO from CO₂ in toxicity and reducing power; explains why SiO₂ is solid and CO₂ is gas.
- **Amber**: correct allotrope descriptions but cannot explain SiO₂ vs CO₂ structural difference.
- **Red**: claims SiO₂ dissolves in water like CO₂; confuses CO and CO₂ properties.
- **Probe**: "Why is graphite used as a lubricant? Why is diamond used as a cutting tool? Link each answer to atomic-level bonding."

## Tutor Recovery Strategy
If student cannot differentiate diamond from graphite electrically: return to band structure qualitatively — diamond has all electrons localised in σ bonds (all in valence band, large gap to conduction band); graphite has a delocalised π system (electrons partially in the conduction region at room temperature). No free electrons in diamond; mobile electrons in graphite.

## Memory Hooks
- **C allotrope properties**: "Diamond: sp³, hard, insulator. Graphite: sp², soft, conductor. C₆₀: sp², soluble, cage."
- **CO vs CO₂**: "CO = toxic (binds Fe in blood) + reducing (removes O from metal oxides). CO₂ = acidic oxide + greenhouse gas."
- **SiO₂ vs CO₂**: "Si can't make Si=O π bonds → giant solid. C can make C=O π bonds → molecular gas."
