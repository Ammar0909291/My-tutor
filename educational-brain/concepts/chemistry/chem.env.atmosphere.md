## Learning Objective

Students can name the four main atmospheric layers (troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere), describe their altitude ranges and temperature profiles; state the approximate percentage composition of dry air by volume (N₂ ~78%, O₂ ~21%, Ar ~0.9%, CO₂ ~0.04%); explain the natural greenhouse effect in terms of IR absorption by CO₂, H₂O, and CH₄; and distinguish the natural greenhouse effect from anthropogenic climate change.

## Core Understanding

Earth's atmosphere is a mixture of gases stratified by temperature profile into distinct layers. The **troposphere** (0–12 km) contains 75% of atmospheric mass and all weather; temperature decreases with altitude at ~6.5 K/km (lapse rate). The **stratosphere** (12–50 km) hosts the ozone layer; temperature increases with altitude due to UV absorption by O₃. The **mesosphere** (50–80 km) has the coldest atmospheric temperatures (~−90 °C). The **thermosphere** (>80 km) has high temperatures (hundreds to thousands of °C) due to high-energy solar radiation absorption, but very low density.

The **natural greenhouse effect**: solar radiation (mostly visible and UV) passes through the atmosphere and is absorbed by Earth's surface, which re-emits as longer-wavelength infrared (IR) radiation. Greenhouse gases (CO₂, H₂O vapour, CH₄, N₂O) absorb this IR, trapping heat and warming the surface by ~33 K above what it would be without the atmosphere. This is essential for life.

**Anthropogenic enhancement**: human emissions (fossil fuels, agriculture, deforestation) have increased CO₂ from ~280 ppm (pre-industrial) to ~420 ppm (2023), amplifying the natural greenhouse effect.

## Mental Models

**Temperature-altitude profiles as a jagged mountain range**: The atmosphere doesn't cool uniformly with altitude. Each layer has a different temperature gradient — the troposphere cools, the stratosphere warms (due to ozone UV absorption), the mesosphere cools again, the thermosphere warms dramatically but has negligible mass. The jags in the temperature profile define the layer boundaries.

**The greenhouse analogy (and its limits)**: CO₂ and H₂O act like the glass in a greenhouse — they are largely transparent to incoming visible light but absorb outgoing IR. However, a real greenhouse also traps warm air convectively; the atmospheric greenhouse effect is purely radiative. The analogy is useful for the one-way filter concept; incorrect for the mechanism beyond that.

**Air as a recipe with precise proportions**: Dry air at sea level is 78.09% N₂, 20.95% O₂, 0.93% Ar, 0.04% CO₂ by volume. Water vapour varies 0–4%. These proportions are stable over most of the troposphere because of atmospheric mixing.

## Why Students Fail

1. **Confusing layers by the wrong property**: Students confuse the stratosphere and mesosphere, or confuse temperature profile with altitude profile. The mnemonic must be paired with temperature gradient direction.
2. **Natural greenhouse effect as harmful**: Students believe the greenhouse effect is inherently bad because "enhanced greenhouse effect" is the climate-change problem. The natural greenhouse effect is what makes Earth habitable.
3. **CO₂ as the main greenhouse gas by effect**: CO₂ is the main anthropogenic concern, but water vapour is the most abundant and powerful greenhouse gas in the natural system.
4. **Composition percentages for wet vs. dry air**: Students confuse the percentage given for dry air with what's actually measured (humidity varies).

## Misconceptions

**MC-1 — The greenhouse effect is inherently harmful** (Type 5, instruction-induced)
- *Diagnostic probe*: "What would Earth's average temperature be without any greenhouse gases in the atmosphere? Is the greenhouse effect something we should eliminate?"
- *Characteristic phrase*: "The greenhouse effect is bad — it's causing climate change."
- *Repair*: Without the greenhouse effect, Earth's average temperature would be approximately −18 °C (compared to the current +15 °C). The natural greenhouse effect is essential. The *enhanced* greenhouse effect from anthropogenic emissions is the problem.

**MC-2 — CO₂ is the main greenhouse gas overall** (Type 5, instruction-induced)
- *Diagnostic probe*: "Which single gas accounts for the largest fraction of the natural greenhouse effect?"
- *Characteristic phrase*: "CO₂ is the most important greenhouse gas."
- *Repair*: Water vapour accounts for ~50% of the natural greenhouse effect; CO₂ accounts for ~20%. CO₂ is the most important *anthropogenic* greenhouse gas (the one humans can actually influence most directly), but not the most abundant in the natural system.

**MC-3 — Atmospheric layers are separated by composition** (Type 2, perceptual intuition)
- *Mechanism*: Students imagine each layer as chemically distinct (N₂ in one, O₂ in another). In fact, the troposphere, stratosphere, and mesosphere have similar bulk composition; the layers are defined by temperature gradient, not composition (except the ozone-rich stratosphere).
- *Diagnostic probe*: "If you collected air from the stratosphere (excluding the ozone layer), would its N₂:O₂ ratio differ significantly from tropospheric air?"
- *Repair*: Atmospheric layers are defined by temperature profile. The same molecular nitrogen and oxygen predominate in all three lower layers. The ozone layer within the stratosphere is the notable compositional exception.

## Analogies

**The glass pane model for IR absorption**: Imagine incoming sunlight as a stream of bouncy rubber balls (short wavelength — passes through glass easily). After striking the floor, they become slow-moving foam balls (long wavelength IR — absorbed by glass). The glass (greenhouse gases) lets the rubber balls in but catches the foam balls. Earth's surface warms as foam balls pile up.

## Demonstrations

**Demonstration 1 — CO₂ greenhouse effect simulation**
- Two sealed containers: one with air, one with CO₂-enriched air. Shine an IR heat lamp on both. Measure temperature rise with a thermometer. The CO₂-enriched container warms faster. Students connect the observation to the atmospheric greenhouse effect.

## Discovery Questions

1. "The stratosphere's temperature increases with altitude because ozone absorbs UV radiation. If all the ozone were removed, what would happen to the stratosphere's temperature profile?" (Targets: without UV absorption, temperature would likely decrease with altitude instead — the stratosphere would become an extension of the troposphere's lapse rate.)
2. "Farmers sometimes use reflective plastic covers over crops on cold nights. Is this exploiting the same physical principle as the greenhouse effect?" (Targets: yes — the plastic traps outgoing IR radiation from the warm soil, preventing radiative cooling of the soil and protecting crops from frost.)
3. "CO₂ concentrations have risen from 280 ppm to ~420 ppm since industrialisation. Why doesn't this 150 ppm increase in a gas that makes up only 0.04% of the atmosphere have a negligible effect on climate?" (Targets: CO₂ absorbs in specific IR bands that H₂O doesn't fully cover; even a small additional absorber in those specific bands can have a significant radiative forcing effect because the effect is logarithmic, not proportional.)

## Teaching Sequence

1. Present the atmosphere as a mixture (from chem.found.pure-substances): state its approximate composition (N₂, O₂, Ar, CO₂, trace gases, water vapour).
2. Introduce the four layers: altitude ranges, temperature profile. Sketch the temperature-altitude graph — the key visual for this topic.
3. Explain what drives each temperature gradient: troposphere (decreasing T due to decreasing pressure and solar radiation); stratosphere (increasing T due to ozone UV absorption); mesosphere (decreasing T, no UV absorber); thermosphere (very high T due to high-energy photon absorption, but low density).
4. Introduce the natural greenhouse effect: solar radiation → visible/UV → absorbed by Earth's surface → re-emitted as IR → trapped by greenhouse gases.
5. Distinguish natural vs. enhanced greenhouse effect explicitly (MC-1 repair built into the teaching sequence).
6. Present CO₂ ppm data: pre-industrial vs. current. Discuss the human-activity link.
7. Address MC-2: water vapour vs. CO₂ as greenhouse gases.

## Tutor Actions

- If student says greenhouse effect is inherently harmful → MC-1 repair: the −18 °C vs. +15 °C comparison.
- If student says CO₂ is the largest greenhouse gas contributor overall → MC-2 repair: water vapour comparison.
- If student confuses atmospheric layers by composition → MC-3 repair: define layers by temperature profile, not composition.
- Advance when student can draw the temperature-altitude profile, classify each layer, and explain the natural greenhouse effect mechanism with the correct assignment of who does what (surface absorbs visible → re-emits IR → greenhouse gases trap IR).

## Voice Teaching Notes

The temperature-altitude graph is the single most important visual for atmospheric layers. Describe it verbally as: "starts warm at the surface, cools until the tropopause, then warms again through the stratosphere, cools again through the mesosphere, then shoots up in the thermosphere." The word "jagged mountain range" helps students who cannot see the graph.

The natural/enhanced greenhouse distinction should be made explicitly and early in any discussion of climate change, to prevent MC-1 from forming in the first place. The phrasing "essential but now amplified" is useful.

## Assessment Signals

**Mastery gate**:
1. Student correctly names four layers in order of altitude, with temperature gradient direction for each.
2. Student explains the greenhouse effect mechanism in three steps: solar in → surface re-emit IR → greenhouse gases absorb IR.
3. Student correctly identifies water vapour as the largest natural greenhouse gas and CO₂ as the most important anthropogenic one.

**FRAGILE signal**: student names layers and their temperature trends correctly but cannot explain why each layer has its particular temperature gradient.

**MISCONCEIVING signal**: student says "the greenhouse effect is bad and we should remove it." Immediate MC-1 repair.

## Tutor Recovery Strategy

If stuck:
1. For layer confusion: reduce to two layers only (troposphere and stratosphere), master those completely, then add mesosphere and thermosphere.
2. For greenhouse effect mechanism: draw the one-way filter diagram (short wavelengths in → long wavelengths blocked). Ask the student to trace the path of a photon from the sun to the ground and back.
3. For CO₂ vs. H₂O greenhouse confusion: present the specific IR absorption bands of each molecule. CO₂ absorbs at 15 μm, H₂O at multiple bands. The fact that they cover different wavelength ranges makes both important.

## Memory Hooks

- **Atmospheric layers from bottom to top**: "The Stratosphere Makes Things Hazy" → Troposphere, Stratosphere, Mesosphere, Thermosphere.
- **Temperature gradient memory**: Troposphere decreases (T for temperature, T for tropics = warm at bottom); Stratosphere warms (ozone heats); Mesosphere decreases; Thermosphere warms dramatically.
- **Air composition**: "Nitrogen 78, Oxygen 21, Argon almost 1, CO₂ trace" — three numbers cover dry air.
