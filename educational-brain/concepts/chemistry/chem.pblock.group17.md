## Learning Objective
Describe the periodic trends in physical properties and reactivity of the halogens (F, Cl, Br, I, At), explain halogen displacement reactions as a practical application of reactivity trends, and describe the key chemistry of hydrogen halides and oxyacids of chlorine.

## Core Understanding
Group 17 (halogens): F, Cl, Br, I, At (radioactive). Valence configuration: ns²np⁵. One electron short of a noble gas configuration → strong tendency to gain one electron (highest electron affinity in their period). Common OS: −1 (halide X⁻); Cl, Br, I can also show +1, +3, +5, +7 (in oxyacids/oxyanions, expanding octet using d orbitals). F is always −1 (highest electronegativity, no d orbital, strongest oxidising halogen).

**Physical state at room temperature (and trend)**:
- F₂: pale yellow gas (bp −188°C)
- Cl₂: yellow-green gas (bp −34°C)
- Br₂: red-brown liquid (bp +59°C)
- I₂: grey-black solid (sublimes at 184°C, giving purple vapour)
- Trend: bp increases down the group because molecular mass increases → stronger London dispersion forces (no H-bonding or dipole forces between homonuclear diatomics).

**Reactivity trend**: DECREASES down the group. F₂ is the strongest oxidising agent (most reactive) because it has the most negative standard reduction potential (E° = +2.87 V) — a consequence of weak F–F bond (low bond enthalpy due to lone pair repulsion in the small F₂ molecule), very high IE₁ of the F atom it produces, and high hydration enthalpy of F⁻. Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂ in reactivity.

**Halogen displacement reactions**: a more reactive halogen (X₂) displaces a less reactive halide (Y⁻) from solution. Cl₂ displaces both Br⁻ and I⁻; Br₂ displaces I⁻ but not Cl⁻; I₂ cannot displace Cl⁻ or Br⁻. These reactions are redox reactions: X₂ (oxidant, reduced to X⁻) + Y⁻ (reductant, oxidised to Y₂). Practical test: extract the product into a non-polar solvent (cyclohexane) — Br₂ gives orange, I₂ gives purple organic layer; confirms identity of the product.

**Hydrogen halides (HX)**:
- Trend in acid strength: HF (weak acid, Ka = 7×10⁻⁴; pKa = 3.1) < HCl < HBr < HI (all three strong acids). HF is anomalously weak because the very strong H–F bond (568 kJ/mol, highest bond enthalpy in HX series) makes it harder to ionise; also H–F associates in solution (hydrofluoric acid forms HF₂⁻, H₂F₂⁺ at high concentrations).
- HF is a corrosive liquid that etches glass (SiO₂ + 4HF → SiF₄ + 2H₂O) — a unique property; HCl, HBr, HI are gases at RT.
- Preparation: HCl is a by-product of organic chlorination; HBr and HI cannot be made by reacting H₂SO₄ with the sodium salt (SO₄²⁻ would oxidise Br⁻ and I⁻); phosphoric acid is used instead.

**Oxyacids of chlorine** (increasing OS of Cl):
- HOCl (hypochlorous acid, Cl OS = +1): bleaching agent; disproportionates in alkali.
- HClO₂ (chlorous acid, +3): relatively unstable.
- HClO₃ (chloric acid, +5): strong acid.
- HClO₄ (perchloric acid, +7): the strongest simple oxyacid; fully dissociated.
- Acid strength increases with OS of Cl (more O atoms → more electron-withdrawing → O–H bond more polar → easier proton release).

**Fluorine anomaly** (covered in chem.period.electron-affinity): F < Cl in electron affinity because F's compact n=2 shell has higher electron–electron repulsion for the incoming electron. Despite this, F is a stronger oxidising agent than Cl due to the thermodynamic cycle (weak F–F bond + high hydration enthalpy of F⁻ combine to make the overall reduction potential of F₂ the highest of any element).

## Mental Models
**The electron greediness scale**: going up Group 17 (I → F), atoms are smaller and want the electron more urgently (higher EA, higher χ). F is the greediest electron taker in the periodic table. Reactivity tracks "how much each halogen wants another electron."

**The displacement hierarchy**: picture halogens as a single-file queue ranked by reactivity (F first). Only someone in a HIGHER position in the queue can displace someone in a lower position. Cl can displace Br and I (it's higher). I cannot displace Cl or Br (it's lower).

## Why Students Fail
Students confuse electron affinity (gas-phase, single-step) with overall reactivity (governed by the whole thermodynamic cycle including bond dissociation, hydration, and standard reduction potential). F has less negative EA than Cl but is MORE reactive because the overall Born-Haber-type cycle favours F₂ reduction.

Students incorrectly predict HF is a strong acid because "F is the most electronegative element." The bond enthalpy (not χ alone) determines acid strength: HF has the strongest H–X bond, which is actually hardest to break.

## Misconceptions
- **MC-1 (Type 6 — analogy overextension)**: "F₂ has the highest electron affinity in Group 17, so it must be the most reactive oxidising agent because EA measures how well it gains electrons." Probe: "Which has more negative EA: F or Cl?" Characteristic phrase: "most negative EA means most reactive." Intervention: Cl has MORE negative EA than F (F < Cl anomaly covered in electron affinity node); yet F₂ is more reactive. The full thermodynamic cycle (bond enthalpy of X₂ + hydration enthalpy of X⁻) determines the standard reduction potential; for F₂, the very weak F–F bond and very high hydration enthalpy of F⁻ together make E°(F₂/F⁻) = +2.87 V, the highest in the table.
- **MC-2 (Type 5 — instruction-induced)**: "HF is a strong acid because F is very electronegative and so the H–F bond polarity is high." Probe: "Measure the pH of 0.1 M HF — is it the same as 0.1 M HCl?" Intervention: HF is a WEAK acid (pH ≈ 2.1 for 0.1 M, vs pH = 1.0 for HCl); the very high H–F bond enthalpy (568 kJ/mol) makes ionisation harder than for HCl (431 kJ/mol); electronegativity creates bond polarity but bond enthalpy determines whether the bond actually breaks under standard conditions.
- **MC-3 (Type 1 — overgeneralization)**: "Halogen reactivity increases going down the group like metal reactivity." Probe: "Which is more reactive: Cl₂ or I₂?" Characteristic phrase: "down the group means more reactive." Intervention: METALS are more reactive going DOWN a group (lower IE, easier electron loss); NON-METALS (including halogens) are less reactive going down (lower EA, harder electron gain). The two trends are opposite and driven by opposite processes.

## Analogies
**Valid**: Halogen displacement reactions as a strict pecking order — in a flock, higher-ranking birds displace lower-ranking birds from a food source; Cl displaces Br and I from solution, Br displaces I, I displaces no-one.

## Demonstrations
**Displacement series experiment**: add Cl₂ water to solutions of KBr and KI; add Br₂ water to KI solution; shake with cyclohexane; observe orange (Br₂) and purple (I₂) organic layers. Simple, safe, visually striking, directly tests the reactivity hierarchy.

**HF etching glass** (video demonstration only): glass frosted by brief contact with HF solution; contrast with HCl, which does not etch glass. Demonstrates HF's unique reactivity with SiO₂.

**Cl₂ bleaching vs. SO₂ bleaching** (cross-reference with Group 16): bleach a dye with Cl₂, expose to light; compare with SO₂-bleached sample that recoloures. Reinforces oxidising vs. reducing bleach distinction.

## Discovery Questions
1. "Using standard reduction potentials, explain why fluorine cannot be prepared by electrolyzing an aqueous solution of fluoride salts, while chlorine can be prepared from aqueous NaCl electrolysis."
2. "Arrange HF, HCl, HBr, HI in order of increasing acid strength and explain the trend. Note that electronegativity ALONE does not explain the order."
3. "The ClO⁻/ClO₃⁻ disproportionation: 3ClO⁻ → 2Cl⁻ + ClO₃⁻. Assign OS to each Cl. Identify which Cl is oxidised and which is reduced."

## Teaching Sequence
1. Group overview: ns²np⁵; one-electron-short model; OS range (F always −1; Cl/Br/I up to +7).
2. Physical state and mp/bp trend: London dispersion forces increasing with molecular mass.
3. Reactivity trend: F > Cl > Br > I; explain from the thermodynamic cycle (F–F bond + hydration enthalpy).
4. Displacement reactions: three experiments; tabulate observations; derive the reactivity order.
5. Hydrogen halides: acid strength trend; HF as weak acid; HF glass etching.
6. Oxyacids of chlorine: four acids; OS of Cl; acid strength trend with OS.
7. F anomaly recap: why F < Cl in EA but F₂ > Cl₂ in reactivity.

## Tutor Actions
- **If student reverses halogen reactivity trend**: immediately ask "do halogens react by losing or gaining electrons?" (gaining); "going down the group, does the tendency to gain electrons increase or decrease?" (decrease: EA becomes less negative); reactivity towards electron gain decreases down the group.
- **If student says HF is a strong acid**: give pH evidence or Ka value; connect to bond enthalpy (not just electronegativity) as the determinant of acid strength.
- **FRAGILE sign**: performs displacement reactions correctly but cannot explain WHY Cl₂ can displace Br⁻ (using reduction potential or reactivity order reasoning).

## Voice Teaching Notes
"More reactive going down the group" is a very persistent misconception for halogens — students extend the alkali metal trend without checking. Address it with the "gains vs. loses electrons" discrimination at the very start.

The displacement series experiment is the best anchor point: observable colour changes in less than 2 minutes, no calculations required, directly tests the concept. If doing a live session, do the experiment conceptually first (predict colours) then check the predictions.

## Assessment Signals
- **Green**: correctly predicts displacement reaction outcomes for any pair; explains the F < Cl EA anomaly vs. F > Cl in reactivity; explains HF weak acidity from bond enthalpy; ranks oxyacid strength from OS.
- **Amber**: correct displacement predictions but cannot explain them from reduction potentials; or correct acid strength ranking but wrong explanation.
- **Red**: reverses halogen reactivity trend (claims I₂ > Cl₂); calls HF a strong acid; confuses displacement direction.
- **Probe**: "Will I₂ displace Cl⁻ from NaCl solution? Will Cl₂ displace F⁻ from NaF solution? Explain."

## Tutor Recovery Strategy
If student cannot apply the displacement hierarchy: strip to the single-step comparison Cl₂ vs. Br₂; use the experimental fact (add Cl₂ to KBr → orange Br₂ formed; add Br₂ to KCl → no reaction) as data; ask "what does this mean about which is the better oxidising agent?" Build the hierarchy from specific experimental observations, not from abstract memory.

## Memory Hooks
- **Physical state at RT**: "F and Cl = gases. Br = liquid. I = solid." (Order: gas, gas, liquid, solid down the group.)
- **Halogen reactivity**: "NON-METALS: reactivity DECREASES down the group (unlike metals). F > Cl > Br > I."
- **HX acid strength**: "HF is the WEAK one. HCl, HBr, HI are all strong — HF is different because its bond is too strong to break easily."
- **Oxyacid strength**: "More oxygen = more OS on Cl = stronger acid. HClO₄ (strongest) → HClO (weakest)."
