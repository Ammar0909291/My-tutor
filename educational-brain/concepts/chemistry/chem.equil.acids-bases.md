## Learning Objective

Students can state the Arrhenius, Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry, and Lewis definitions of acids and bases; identify conjugate acid‚Äďbase pairs; recognise amphoteric species; define Ka, Kb, and pKa; and apply the relationship Ka √ó Kb = Kw for conjugate pairs.

## Core Understanding

**Three theories ‚ÄĒ increasing generality**:

1. **Arrhenius** (narrowest): acid produces H‚Āļ(aq) in water; base produces OH‚ĀĽ(aq) in water. Limitation: applies only to aqueous solutions; cannot explain NH‚āÉ as a base (produces no OH‚ĀĽ directly), or acid‚Äďbase reactions in non-aqueous solvents.

2. **Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry** (broader): acid is a proton (H‚Āļ) donor; base is a proton acceptor. Every Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry acid reaction creates a conjugate base; every Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry base reaction creates a conjugate acid. The acid-base pair differing by one H‚Āļ is the conjugate pair. Example: HCl + H‚āāO ‚Üí Cl‚ĀĽ + H‚āÉO‚Āļ; HCl and Cl‚ĀĽ are a conjugate acid‚Äďbase pair; H‚āāO and H‚āÉO‚Āļ are a conjugate acid‚Äďbase pair. NH‚āÉ + H‚āāO ‚áĆ NH‚āĄ‚Āļ + OH‚ĀĽ; NH‚āÉ is the base, NH‚āĄ‚Āļ is its conjugate acid.

3. **Lewis** (broadest): acid is an electron-pair acceptor; base is an electron-pair donor. Includes reactions with no proton transfer. Examples: BF‚āÉ + :NH‚āÉ ‚Üí F‚āÉB‚ÜźNH‚āÉ (BF‚āÉ is Lewis acid, NH‚āÉ Lewis base). Metal ions in coordination chemistry are Lewis acids (accept lone pairs from ligands). Every Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry acid is also a Lewis acid; the reverse is not true.

**Conjugate pairs** (Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry):
Acid ‚áĆ H‚Āļ + Conjugate base (conjugate base has one fewer H‚Āļ)
Base + H‚Āļ ‚áĆ Conjugate acid (conjugate acid has one more H‚Āļ)
Examples: HCO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ/CO‚āÉ¬≤‚ĀĽ; H‚āāPO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ/HPO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ; NH‚āĄ‚Āļ/NH‚āÉ; H‚āāO/OH‚ĀĽ

**Amphoteric species**: a species that can act as EITHER an acid or a base depending on the reaction partner.
- Water is amphoteric: H‚āāO donates H‚Āļ to NH‚āÉ (acts as acid) and accepts H‚Āļ from HCl (acts as base).
- HCO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ is amphoteric: acts as acid ‚Üí CO‚āÉ¬≤‚ĀĽ; acts as base ‚Üí H‚āāCO‚āÉ.
- Amino acids (zwitterionic form) are amphoteric.

**Ka (acid dissociation constant)**:
For a weak acid HA ‚áĆ H‚Āļ + A‚ĀĽ:
Ka = [H‚Āļ][A‚ĀĽ]/[HA]
pKa = ‚ąílog Ka. Larger Ka (smaller pKa) ‚Üí stronger acid (more dissociated at equilibrium).

**Kb (base dissociation constant)**:
For a weak base B + H‚āāO ‚áĆ BH‚Āļ + OH‚ĀĽ:
Kb = [BH‚Āļ][OH‚ĀĽ]/[B]

**Conjugate pair relationship**:
Ka √ó Kb = Kw = 1.0 √ó 10‚ĀĽ¬Ļ‚Āī at 298 K
This means: strong acid ‚Üí weak conjugate base; weak acid ‚Üí strong(er) conjugate base.

**Strength ranking**:
- Strong acids (Ka ‚Üí ‚ąě, pKa << 0): complete dissociation. Conjugate bases are very weak (spectator ions in acid‚Äďbase reactions).
- Weak acids (Ka in range 10‚ĀĽ¬≤ to 10‚ĀĽ¬Ļ¬≤): partial dissociation. Conjugate bases are real bases.

## Mental Models

**The H‚Āļ-transfer model for Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry**: a Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry acid‚Äďbase reaction is always a proton-transfer: one species gives up H‚Āļ, another catches it. Draw an arrow from the H‚Āļ donor to the H‚Āļ acceptor in every reaction. This makes the conjugate pair visible: donor and its product (minus H‚Āļ) are the conjugate pair; acceptor and its product (plus H‚Āļ) are the conjugate pair.

**The electron-pair donor/acceptor model for Lewis**: Lewis acids have an empty orbital or a positive centre; Lewis bases have a lone pair. The reaction is an arrow from the lone pair to the empty orbital. This unifies coordination chemistry (metal + ligand) with classical acid‚Äďbase chemistry under one framework.

## Why Students Fail

1. **Arrhenius as the only definition**: students apply Arrhenius exclusively and cannot classify NH‚āÉ as a base or BF‚āÉ as an acid (no proton transfer, no OH‚ĀĽ produced).
2. **Conjugate pairs must differ by exactly one H‚Āļ**: students pair H‚āāCO‚āÉ with CO‚āÉ¬≤‚ĀĽ (differ by 2 H‚Āļ) as a conjugate pair, missing that HCO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ is the direct conjugate base of H‚āāCO‚āÉ.
3. **Stronger acid has larger pKa**: students confuse pKa with Ka ‚ÄĒ since pKa = ‚ąílog Ka, a larger Ka gives a SMALLER (more negative) pKa. Stronger acid = smaller pKa.
4. **Amphoteric species can only be one thing**: students classify H‚āāO as "a base" because it accepts protons from acids, not recognising it can also donate.

## Misconceptions

**MC-1 ‚ÄĒ Conjugate pairs differ by two protons or by OH** (Type 1, overgeneralization/carelessness)
- *Mechanism*: students pick species in the same reaction without checking the exact H‚Āļ difference.
- *Diagnostic probe*: "In H‚āāSO‚āĄ + H‚āāO ‚Üí HSO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ + H‚āÉO‚Āļ, identify BOTH conjugate pairs."
- *Characteristic phrase*: "H‚āāSO‚āĄ and SO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ are a conjugate pair" (differ by 2 H‚Āļ, not 1).
- *Repair*: H‚āāSO‚āĄ ‚Üí HSO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ (loses 1 H‚Āļ): conjugate pair 1 = H‚āāSO‚āĄ/HSO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ. H‚āāO ‚Üí H‚āÉO‚Āļ (gains 1 H‚Āļ): conjugate pair 2 = H‚āāO/H‚āÉO‚Āļ. The rule: conjugate pairs differ by EXACTLY one H‚Āļ. Never two H‚Āļ, never OH.

**MC-2 ‚ÄĒ Stronger acid has higher pKa** (Type 4, notation-induced: "p" prefix implies larger = stronger)
- *Mechanism*: students see pKa as a "strength scale" and assume larger pKa = stronger acid, by analogy with "higher score = better."
- *Diagnostic probe*: "Acetic acid has pKa = 4.76. Hydrofluoric acid has pKa = 3.17. Which is the stronger acid?"
- *Characteristic phrase*: "Acetic acid is stronger because pKa 4.76 > 3.17."
- *Repair*: pKa = ‚ąílog Ka. Larger Ka ‚Üí smaller pKa. HF (pKa = 3.17) has Ka = 10‚ĀĽ¬≥¬∑¬Ļ‚Ā∑ = 6.8 √ó 10‚ĀĽ‚Āī; acetic acid (pKa = 4.76) has Ka = 10‚ĀĽ‚Āī¬∑‚Ā∑‚Ā∂ = 1.7 √ó 10‚ĀĽ‚ĀĶ. HF is 40√ó stronger. Lower pKa = stronger acid. Use the phrase: "pKa down, strength up."

**MC-3 ‚ÄĒ Arrhenius base must contain OH** (Type 5, instruction-induced: first theory taught defines base as OH‚ĀĽ source)
- *Mechanism*: students learn Arrhenius first and permanently associate "base" with hydroxide.
- *Diagnostic probe*: "Is NH‚āÉ a base? Explain using Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry theory."
- *Characteristic phrase*: "NH‚āÉ is not a base because it contains no OH."
- *Repair*: Arrhenius is the narrowest theory. Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry: NH‚āÉ + H‚āāO ‚áĆ NH‚āĄ‚Āļ + OH‚ĀĽ ‚ÄĒ NH‚āÉ accepts a proton from H‚āāO ‚Üí it is a Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry base. The OH‚ĀĽ comes from water's donated proton, not from NH‚āÉ itself. Lewis theory additionally includes BF‚āÉ, AlCl‚āÉ, and metal ions as Lewis acids ‚ÄĒ none contain protons at all.

## Analogies

**The H‚Āļ currency analogy**: protons (H‚Āļ) are the currency in Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry acid‚Äďbase transactions. The acid is the payer (gives away H‚Āļ), the base is the receiver. Every transaction generates two new entities: the depleted payer (conjugate base) and the enriched receiver (conjugate acid). The currency doesn't disappear; it just changes hands.

**The spectrum-of-theories analogy**: Arrhenius, Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry, and Lewis are like zoom levels on a map. Arrhenius is street-level: highly detailed for water, can't see beyond city limits. Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry is city-level: covers all proton transfers. Lewis is country-level: covers electron-pair chemistry, including reactions with no H‚Āļ at all. A city-level map still shows all the streets, and a country-level map still shows all cities. Each theory contains the previous as a special case.

## Demonstrations

**Demonstration 1 ‚ÄĒ Universal indicator with NH‚āÉ and BF‚āÉ**
- Show NH‚āÉ(aq) turning universal indicator blue (basic). Ask: how does NH‚āÉ produce OH‚ĀĽ with no OH in its formula? Derive NH‚āÉ + H‚āāO ‚áĆ NH‚āĄ‚Āļ + OH‚ĀĽ using Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry. Then show the Arrhenius explanation fails (NH‚āÉ has no OH) but Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry succeeds. This distinguishes the two theories concretely.

## Discovery Questions

1. "For the reaction: HPO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ + H‚āāO ‚áĆ H‚āāPO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ + OH‚ĀĽ, identify the acid, base, conjugate acid, and conjugate base. Is HPO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ amphoteric?" (Targets: HPO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ is the base (accepts H‚Āļ from H‚āāO ‚Üí H‚āāPO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ is its conjugate acid); H‚āāO is the acid (donates H‚Āļ ‚Üí OH‚ĀĽ is its conjugate base). 2. "The Ka of acetic acid (CH‚āÉCOOH) is 1.8 √ó 10‚ĀĽ‚ĀĶ. Calculate Kb for the acetate ion (CH‚āÉCOO‚ĀĽ). Is acetate a stronger or weaker base than NH‚āÉ (Kb of NH‚āÉ = 1.8 √ó 10‚ĀĽ‚ĀĶ)?" 3. "Identify the Lewis acid and Lewis base in: AlCl‚āÉ + Cl‚ĀĽ ‚Üí AlCl‚āĄ‚ĀĽ. Does this reaction involve proton transfer?" (Targets: AlCl‚āÉ is the Lewis acid (Al has an empty 3p orbital, accepts the electron pair). Cl‚ĀĽ is the Lewis base (lone pair donor). No proton transfer occurs. This reaction cannot be described by Arrhenius or Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry theories ‚ÄĒ it requires the Lewis framework.)

## Tutor Recovery Strategy

If stuck:
1. For conjugate pairs: "Write the acid dissociation: HA ‚Üí H‚Āļ + A‚ĀĽ. The pair is HA/A‚ĀĽ. That's one H‚Āļ difference. Now write H‚āāA ‚Üí H‚Āļ + HA‚ĀĽ. The pair is H‚āāA/HA‚ĀĽ. What is the pair for HA‚ĀĽ? HA‚ĀĽ ‚Üí H‚Āļ + A¬≤‚ĀĽ. The pair is HA‚ĀĽ/A¬≤‚ĀĽ. Never jump two steps."
2. For Ka √ó Kb = Kw: "Ka = [H‚Āļ][A‚ĀĽ]/[HA]. Kb = [HA][OH‚ĀĽ]/[A‚ĀĽ]. Multiply: Ka √ó Kb = [H‚Āļ][OH‚ĀĽ] = Kw. The [A‚ĀĽ] and [HA] cancel. It's an algebraic identity ‚ÄĒ not a coincidence."
3. For Lewis acids: "Lewis acid needs to ACCEPT an electron pair. What does that require? An empty orbital. BF‚āÉ: boron has 3 bonds, 6 electrons, empty 2p orbital ‚Üí Lewis acid. Metal ions: d-block or Al¬≥‚Āļ with empty orbitals ‚Üí Lewis acids."

## Memory Hooks

- **Arrhenius: H‚Āļ in water (acid) / OH‚ĀĽ in water (base). Narrow ‚ÄĒ aqueous only.**
- **Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry: H‚Āļ donor (acid) / H‚Āļ acceptor (base). Conjugate pair: differ by 1 H‚Āļ.**
- **Lewis: e‚ĀĽ pair acceptor (acid) / e‚ĀĽ pair donor (base). Broadest ‚ÄĒ includes BF‚āÉ, AlCl‚āÉ, metal ions.**
- **Ka √ó Kb = Kw. Strong acid ‚ÜĒ weak conjugate base. pKa down = strength up.**
- **Amphoteric: can donate OR accept H‚Āļ. Water and HCO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ are the key examples.**
