## Learning Objective
Describe the electronic configurations, oxidation states, key reactions, and anomalous properties of Group 13 elements (B, Al, Ga, In, Tl), with emphasis on the boron-aluminium contrast and the inert-pair effect in heavier members.

## Core Understanding
Group 13 elements: B, Al, Ga, In, Tl. Valence configuration: ns²np¹. Common oxidation state: +3 (loss of both s and one p electron). Exception: Tl commonly shows +1 due to the inert pair effect (6s² electrons become increasingly stable due to relativistic effects in heavier elements, making them harder to remove — Tl⁺ is more stable than Tl³⁺).

**Boron (Z=5)**: unique in the group; a metalloid (semiconductor). Does NOT form B³⁺ ions (ionisation energies are too high for cationic chemistry). Forms only COVALENT compounds. Has only 3 valence electrons → electron-deficient (octet incomplete) in BCl₃, BF₃ → strong Lewis acid. Boron trifluoride BF₃: planar sp² geometry; accepts lone pairs (Lewis acid). Borates (B–O compounds): boric acid B(OH)₃ acts as a Lewis acid (accepts OH⁻ from water: B(OH)₃ + H₂O ⇌ B(OH)₄⁻ + H⁺), NOT a Brønsted acid. Borax (Na₂B₄O₇) in buffer solutions. Boron carbide (B₄C) and boron nitride (BN) are extremely hard high-tech ceramics.

**Aluminium (Z=13)**: the most abundant metal in Earth's crust; the archetype Group 13 metal. Amphoteric oxide: Al₂O₃ reacts with both acids (Al₂O₃ + 6HCl → 2AlCl₃ + 3H₂O) and bases (Al₂O₃ + 2NaOH + 3H₂O → 2Na[Al(OH)₄]). Self-passivating: a thin layer of Al₂O₃ protects Al from further oxidation; this layer is intact in air but dissolved by strong NaOH (explaining the hydrogen evolution when Al reacts with alkali). AlCl₃ is a Lewis acid catalyst (Friedel-Crafts reactions). Thermite reaction: 2Al + Fe₂O₃ → Al₂O₃ + 2Fe + heat (ΔT > 2000°C; Al displaces Fe from its oxide).

**Anomaly B vs. Al**: B has NO metallic character; Al is a reactive metal. B forms covalent compounds exclusively; Al forms ionic compounds in common states. This anomaly is predicted by periodic-properties: B is smaller, higher IE, higher χ than expected from its group position (diagonal relationship with Si).

**Inert pair effect (Ga, In, Tl)**: for Ga (+1 and +3 both known, +3 dominant), In (+1 and +3), Tl (+1 dominant, +3 possible); as you descend the group, the 6s² electrons become relativistically stabilised (inert pair), making the +1 state increasingly stable.

## Mental Models
**The Lewis acid socket**: Group 13 elements have an empty p orbital when they have 3 bonds and no lone pairs (BF₃, AlCl₃); this empty orbital is the Lewis acid acceptor site. Every Group 13 compound with a full octet needs either a fourth bond (forming [BF₄]⁻) or a coordinate bond from a Lewis base.

**The amphoteric oxide seesaw**: Al₂O₃ sits at the oxide seesaw's middle point — acidic enough to react with strong bases (acts as an acid), basic enough to react with strong acids (acts as a base). Na₂O (far left, strongly basic) and P₂O₅ (far right, strongly acidic) are at the extremes.

## Why Students Fail
Students predict B³⁺ ions by analogy with Al³⁺ and Na⁺ — but boron's high IE and small size prevent cation formation; boron chemistry is exclusively covalent.

Students claim Al dissolves only in acids — it is amphoteric and dissolves readily in concentrated NaOH too.

## Misconceptions
- **MC-1 (Type 1 — overgeneralization)**: "Group 13 elements always form M³⁺ ions." Probe: "What ion does thallium most commonly form?" Characteristic phrase: "Group 13 means +3 always." Intervention: the inert pair effect means Tl⁺ dominates for thallium; B forms no M³⁺ at all (covalent only). The +3 is dominant only for Al and Ga in common chemistry.
- **MC-2 (Type 5 — instruction-induced)**: "Aluminium reacts only with acids, not bases, because it is a metal." Probe: "What happens when aluminium foil is placed in concentrated NaOH solution?" Characteristic phrase: "metals react with acids, not bases." Intervention: Al₂O₃ and Al(OH)₃ are amphoteric; Al(OH)₃ + NaOH → Na[Al(OH)₄]; aluminium reacts vigorously with concentrated NaOH producing H₂ gas (2Al + 2NaOH + 2H₂O → 2NaAlO₂ + 3H₂).
- **MC-3 (Type 5 — instruction-induced)**: "Boric acid is a proton-donating Brønsted acid like other acids." Probe: "Write the ionization of B(OH)₃ in water." Intervention: B(OH)₃ acts as a Lewis acid by accepting a lone pair from H₂O (or OH⁻) to form [B(OH)₄]⁻, releasing H⁺ indirectly; it does NOT donate H⁺ from its own structure — this is a Lewis acid mechanism, not Brønsted-Lowry.

## Analogies
**Valid**: Boron as a parking space with only 3 cars but room for 4 — it actively looks for a fourth car (lone pair donor) to fill its empty spot (completing its octet). This is why BF₃ is such a strong Lewis acid.

## Demonstrations
**Thermite reaction** (video only, safety): Al powder + Fe₂O₃ ignited with a Mg ribbon; the intense light and molten Fe produced dramatically demonstrate Al's reducing power and the thermite principle.

**Aluminium amphoterism**: dissolve Al foil in both dilute HCl (forms AlCl₃ + H₂) and in concentrated NaOH (forms Na[Al(OH)₄] + H₂); identical endpoint from opposite conditions demonstrates amphoterism directly.

## Discovery Questions
1. "Why does BF₃ form a stable adduct with NF₃ even though N is in the same period as B and both elements have similar electronegativities? Which is the Lewis acid and which is the Lewis base?"
2. "Anhydrous AlCl₃ rapidly absorbs water from air and forms Al(OH)₃. Explain this in terms of Lewis acid behaviour and the hydrolysis mechanism."

## Teaching Sequence
1. Electronic configurations and common OS; introduce inert pair as a trend.
2. Boron: no B³⁺; Lewis acid BCl₃/BF₃; boric acid mechanism; boron compounds in materials.
3. Aluminium: properties; amphoteric oxide; passivation layer; thermite; AlCl₃ as a Lewis acid catalyst.
4. Inert pair trend: Ga/In/Tl +1 stability; relativistic effect briefly.
5. Diagonal relationship B/Si: both small, high χ, covalent chemistry dominant.

## Tutor Actions
- **If student predicts B³⁺**: ask "what are the first three ionisation energies of boron?" (very high for all three); "is it realistic to remove three electrons from such a small atom?" → B³⁺ never forms; redirect to covalent Lewis acid model.
- **FRAGILE sign**: knows Al is amphoteric but cannot write the equation for Al dissolving in NaOH.

## Voice Teaching Notes
The thermite reaction is a vivid memory anchor for Al's reducing power; introduce it before the formal chemistry. The amphoterism demonstration should always be done before the explanation — let the observation drive the question.

## Assessment Signals
- **Green**: explains B Lewis acid mechanism, Al amphoterism, and inert pair effect for Tl; writes correct equations for Al + HCl and Al + NaOH.
- **Amber**: understands Al amphoterism but cannot connect B's Lewis acidity to its incomplete octet.
- **Red**: predicts B³⁺ ions; claims Al reacts with acids only.
- **Probe**: "Explain why BCl₃ is planar but [BCl₄]⁻ is tetrahedral, using hybridization and Lewis acid-base reasoning."

## Tutor Recovery Strategy
If student cannot grasp B as Lewis acid: return to chem.bond.coordinate-bond; B with 3 bonds has an empty 2p orbital — that is the vacant orbital required for Lewis acid behaviour. Once the vacancy is identified, the rest follows.

## Memory Hooks
- **Group 13 +3 rule with exceptions**: "All +3 except B (covalent only) and Tl (+1 favoured — inert pair)."
- **Al amphoterism**: "Aluminium reacts with BOTH acid AND alkali — amphoteric like the word 'amphibious' (lives in two worlds)."
