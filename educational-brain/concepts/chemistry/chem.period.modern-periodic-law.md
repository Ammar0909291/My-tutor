## Learning Objective

Students can state the modern periodic law; identify the four blocks (s, p, d, f) of the periodic table and their electron configurations; explain the basis of period and group structure; describe the position of hydrogen; and explain why Mendeleev's original law (based on atomic mass) was superseded by atomic number.

## Core Understanding

**Modern periodic law** (Moseley, 1913):
"The physical and chemical properties of elements are a periodic function of their atomic number (Z)."

This supersedes Mendeleev's law (1869), which used atomic mass. Atomic number (number of protons) is the fundamental identity of an element — it determines electron configuration, which determines chemistry.

**Mendeleev's law vs. modern law**:
Mendeleev ordered by atomic mass — successfully predicted missing elements (Ga, Ge, Sc) and left gaps for them. However, anomalies arose where atomic mass order contradicted observed chemistry (Ar before K: Ar's mass is greater than K's, but placing them by mass would put Ar in the K group where it doesn't fit). Moseley's X-ray spectroscopy (1913) showed that atomic number, not mass, determines spectral lines and chemical behaviour. Reordering by Z resolved all anomalies.

**Structure of the long-form (modern) periodic table**:
- **Period**: horizontal row. Each period corresponds to filling one principal quantum number shell (n = 1 → period 1; n = 2 → period 2; etc.) — except d-block starts at n − 1 and f-block at n − 2.
- **Group (family)**: vertical column. Elements in the same group have the same valence electron configuration and similar chemical properties. IUPAC groups 1–18.

**The four blocks** (based on which subshell is being filled):
- **s-block** (Groups 1–2 + He): last electrons enter ns orbitals. Period n fills the ns subshell. Highly reactive metals (alkali, alkaline earth), plus H and He.
- **p-block** (Groups 13–18): last electrons enter np orbitals. Period n fills np. Non-metals, metalloids, noble gases.
- **d-block** (Groups 3–12, transition metals): last electrons enter (n−1)d orbitals. Period 4 fills 3d; period 5 fills 4d; etc.
- **f-block** (lanthanides and actinides, sometimes Groups 3 offset): last electrons enter (n−2)f orbitals. Periods 6 and 7 fill 4f and 5f respectively.

Reading an element's position gives its valence configuration:
- Group 1, period 3 → Na: 3s¹ (s-block)
- Group 17, period 3 → Cl: 3s²3p⁵ (p-block)
- Group 8, period 4 → Fe: [Ar]3d⁶4s² (d-block)

**Position of hydrogen**:
Hydrogen is uniquely placed — it fits Group 1 (1s¹, one valence electron, forms H⁺) but also resembles halogens (forms H⁻, shares one electron to reach a complete shell). In most long-form tables, H is placed above Li (Group 1) for convention, but its chemistry is distinct from alkali metals and from halogens. Some tables float H separately to emphasise its uniqueness.

**Why the periodic law works** (connection to electronic config):
Periodicity arises because valence electron configurations recur periodically as Z increases. Na (11) and K (19) both have ns¹ outer configurations → similar reactivity. The period length reflects the subshell filling: period 1 (2 elements — 1s); period 2/3 (8 elements — 2s2p / 3s3p); period 4/5 (18 elements — s + d + p); period 6/7 (32 elements — s + f + d + p).

## Mental Models

**The address model for the periodic table**: an element's position in the table is its "address." The row (period) is the city (which shell is being filled); the block (s, p, d, f) is the district; the group is the street. Na and K live on the same street (Group 1) in different cities (periods 3 and 4) — same neighbourhood character, different city. Reading the address tells you the valence electron configuration.

**The repeating-pattern model for periodicity**: imagine listening to a piece of music with a repeating motif. The periodic table is structured like music: every time the valence configuration ns¹ recurs (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr), a new verse begins. The pattern (valence electrons) creates chemical similarity; the increasing atomic number (moving to higher n) creates the variation.

## Why Students Fail

1. **Mendeleev's law is still the current law**: students learn Mendeleev in history and assume atomic mass is still the basis. They can't explain why Ar and K swap places by the modern law.
2. **Period number equals the principal quantum number for all blocks**: true for s and p blocks; NOT true for d-block (period 4 fills 3d, not 4d) or f-block (period 6 fills 4f, not 6f). Students apply n = period to d/f block elements and get the wrong subshell.
3. **All groups have the same number of valence electrons**: the d-block complicates this — Group 12 (Zn) has ns²(n−1)d¹⁰, and the d electrons are often counted as valence in transition metal chemistry.
4. **Hydrogen is an alkali metal because it's in Group 1**: hydrogen's placement is conventional, not chemical — it does not share the reactive metal character of Li, Na, K.

## Misconceptions

**MC-1 — Mendeleev's law (atomic mass) is the modern periodic law** (Type 5, instruction-induced: Mendeleev is taught first and students treat it as final)
- *Mechanism*: the historical narrative often presents Mendeleev as the culminating figure; Moseley's correction is mentioned briefly and not emphasised.
- *Diagnostic probe*: "Why is argon (Ar, Z=18, mass≈40) placed before potassium (K, Z=19, mass≈39) in the modern table?"
- *Characteristic phrase*: "The periodic table orders elements by increasing atomic mass."
- *Repair*: Mendeleev ordered by mass but was forced to swap Ar and K (and Te/I, Co/Ni) against mass order to match chemical properties. Moseley explained why: the fundamental quantity is atomic number Z (number of protons), not mass. Ordering by Z puts Ar (Z=18) before K (Z=19) correctly. The modern periodic law: properties are periodic functions of Z.

**MC-2 — The d-block fills the same n as the period number** (Type 5, instruction-induced from the n = period rule for s/p blocks)
- *Mechanism*: students learn "period 2 fills n=2" (2s, 2p) and apply "period 4 fills n=4" to d-block too, writing 4d for Fe instead of 3d.
- *Diagnostic probe*: "Fe is in period 4, Group 8, d-block. What subshell are its d electrons in?"
- *Characteristic phrase*: "Fe has electrons in 4d because it's in period 4."
- *Repair*: d-block elements fill (n−1)d, not nd. Period 4 fills 3d (plus 4s). Period 5 fills 4d. This is because 4s has lower energy than 3d at the time of filling (Aufbau: 4s fills before 3d). Fe's configuration is [Ar]3d⁶4s² — 3d, not 4d.

**MC-3 — Hydrogen is an alkali metal** (Type 3, language contamination: "Group 1" implies "alkali metal")
- *Mechanism*: students see H in Group 1 and apply the group label "alkali metal" to it.
- *Diagnostic probe*: "Is hydrogen an alkali metal? Justify your answer."
- *Characteristic phrase*: "Hydrogen is an alkali metal because it's in Group 1."
- *Repair*: alkali metals (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr) are soft reactive metals that react vigorously with water. Hydrogen is a non-metal gas at room temperature. H's placement in Group 1 reflects its 1s¹ electron configuration (one valence electron, like alkali metals), but its chemistry is fundamentally different — it also forms H⁻ (like halogens) and its ionisation energy (1312 kJ/mol) is much higher than any alkali metal. H is unique; some periodic tables place it separately for this reason.

## Analogies

**The hotel floor analogy**: the periodic table is a hotel. The period is the floor; the block is the wing (s-wing, p-wing, d-wing, f-wing). Elements on the same floor share the highest occupied principal quantum number; elements in the same wing share the type of subshell being filled. Elements in the same vertical column (same room number across floors) have the same number of valence electrons — similar chemistry regardless of floor.

**The Moseley X-ray key analogy**: Moseley discovered that each element's X-ray emission has a unique frequency proportional to Z². This "fingerprint" is determined by Z, not mass. Two elements with the same Z don't exist (they'd be the same element); two elements with the same mass do (isotopes). Z is the true identity; mass is a consequence of nuclear composition.

## Demonstrations

**Demonstration 1 — Block identification from configuration**
- Give students 10 elements with their configurations (mix of s, p, d, f). Students identify the block and the period for each, then locate them on the periodic table. Cross-check: does the period table position match? Emphasise the (n−1)d rule for d-block elements.

## Discovery Questions

1. "An element has the configuration [Kr]4d¹⁰5s²5p². Identify its period, block, and group. Name the element." (Targets: highest n = 5 → period 5. Last filled subshell = 5p → p-block. Electrons in 5p: 5p² → Group 14. Period 5, Group 14, p-block = Tin (Sn, Z=50).)
2. "Mendeleev placed tellurium (Te, mass ≈ 128) before iodine (I, mass ≈ 127) despite I having a smaller mass. Explain this anomaly using the modern periodic law." (Targets: by atomic mass, I (127) should come before Te (128). But by atomic number, Te (Z=52) comes before I (Z=53) — the correct modern order. Te's chemical properties (Group 16, like S/O) require placement in column 16; I's properties (Group 17, like F/Cl/Br) require column 17. Moseley confirmed: Z, not mass, determines chemistry.)
3. "Why does the 4th period of the periodic table contain 18 elements instead of 8 (like periods 2 and 3)?" (Targets: periods 2 and 3 fill only s (2 electrons) and p (6 electrons) subshells → 8 elements each. Period 4 fills 4s (2), 3d (10), and 4p (6) subshells → 18 elements. The d subshell (10 electrons, 5 orbitals) is added for the first time in period 4, expanding the period length. Periods 6 and 7 add the f subshell (14 electrons) → 32 elements each.)

## Teaching Sequence

1. Review from chem.period.classification: Mendeleev's original arrangement by mass; prediction of missing elements; chemical family concept.
2. Review from chem.atomic.electronic-config: Aufbau order; s/p/d/f subshells; orbital notation.

4. Modern periodic law: properties are periodic functions of Z. Define period and group.
5. The four blocks: s, p, d, f — which subshell is being filled. Define (n−1)d for d-block. Address MC-2. Work Discovery Question 1.
6. Period length: why 2/8/8/18/18/32/32. Count subshell electrons for each period type.
7. Position of hydrogen: anomalous, not alkali metal. Address MC-3.
8. Work Discovery Questions 2 and 3.

## Tutor Actions

- If student says atomic mass is the basis of the modern table → MC-1 repair: Moseley showed Z (proton number) is the fundamental property; cite the Ar/K and Te/I anomalies that atomic mass can't explain.
- If student assigns n = period to d-block → MC-2 repair: d-block fills (n−1)d; period 4 = 3d; check Fe's known configuration [Ar]3d⁶4s².
- If student calls H an alkali metal → MC-3 repair: alkali metals are soft reactive metals; H is a gas; H has anomalously high ionisation energy; H also forms H⁻ unlike any alkali metal.
- Advance when student can identify period, group, block, and valence configuration from any given Z up to Z = 54.

## Assessment Signals

**Mastery gate**:
1. Student correctly states the modern periodic law (properties periodic function of Z, not mass).
2. Student correctly identifies the four blocks and the subshell being filled for any given element.
3. Student correctly identifies period and group for an element given its electron configuration.
4. Student correctly explains why Ar is placed before K despite Ar's greater atomic mass.

**FRAGILE signal**: student correctly places elements but cannot explain WHY period 4 has 18 elements (no connection to the d-block's 10 extra electrons).

**MISCONCEIVING signal**: student says properties are periodic function of atomic mass. Address MC-1 with the Ar/K example before any block discussion.

## Tutor Recovery Strategy

If stuck:
1. For Mendeleev → Moseley: "Write Ar (mass 40, Z=18) and K (mass 39, Z=19). In Mendeleev's table, K would come before Ar by mass. But K is a reactive alkali metal; Ar is a noble gas. Chemistry says Ar comes first. Why? Because Z=18 (Ar) < Z=19 (K). The chemical character matches Z, not mass."
2. For d-block period rule: "The Aufbau rule tells us 4s fills BEFORE 3d. So in period 4, the first two elements (K, Ca) fill 4s. Then 3d fills (Sc through Zn). The d subshell has n−1 because 4s is lower energy than 3d during filling; the 3d electrons are n−1 for period 4."
3. For hydrogen: "Check three properties: (1) state at room temperature — alkali metals are solid metals; H is a gas. (2) Reactivity with water — alkali metals react vigorously; H₂ does not react with liquid water at room temperature. (3) Ionisation energy — Li: 520 kJ/mol; H: 1312 kJ/mol. H is fundamentally different."

## Memory Hooks

- **Modern periodic law: properties = f(Z). Z = atomic number. NOT atomic mass.**
- **Four blocks: s (Groups 1–2), p (Groups 13–18), d (Groups 3–12, fills (n−1)d), f (lanthanides/actinides).**
- **Period = n value for s/p blocks. Period = (n−1) value for d-block. Remember (n−1)d.**
- **Hydrogen: Group 1 by convention only. Not an alkali metal. Unique chemistry.**
- **Period 4 has 18 elements because 4s (2) + 3d (10) + 4p (6) all fill in period 4.**
