## Learning Objective
Identify and rank intermolecular forces present in a given substance; predict and explain trends in boiling point, melting point, solubility, and viscosity in terms of intermolecular force type and strength; state the requirements for hydrogen bonding and explain its exceptional effects on the properties of water, HF, and biological molecules.

## Core Understanding
**Types of intermolecular forces (weakest to strongest in typical molecules)**:

1. **London dispersion forces (van der Waals, induced dipole–induced dipole)**:
   - Present in ALL molecules, including non-polar ones — the only IMF in noble gases and symmetric non-polar molecules (CH₄, N₂, CCl₄).
   - Origin: instantaneous, temporary uneven electron distribution creates a momentary dipole; this induces a dipole in a neighbouring molecule; the two temporary dipoles attract.
   - Magnitude depends on: (a) NUMBER OF ELECTRONS (more electrons = larger, more polarisable electron cloud = stronger dispersion forces); (b) SURFACE AREA (more surface contact = more temporary dipoles interacting — this is why n-pentane has a higher bp than neopentane despite same molecular formula: n-pentane has more surface area).
   - Trend: increase down a group (more electrons) — explains why F₂ is a gas, Cl₂ a gas, Br₂ a liquid, I₂ a solid at room temperature.

2. **Permanent dipole–dipole interactions**:
   - Present in polar molecules (molecules with a net dipole moment, arising from polar bonds in asymmetric geometry).
   - Stronger than London forces at comparable molar mass because the dipoles are permanent, not instantaneous.
   - Example: CH₃Cl has a higher bp than the non-polar CCl₄ of similar molar mass because of permanent dipole interactions supplementing London forces.
   - Note: ALL polar molecules also have London forces — dipole-dipole is additional, not a replacement.

3. **Hydrogen bonding**:
   - The STRONGEST intermolecular force (roughly 5–40 kJ/mol; London forces between large molecules can compete but for small molecules, H-bonding dominates).
   - REQUIREMENTS: (1) H bonded DIRECTLY to F, O, or N (the donor — must be directly bonded, not just present); (2) a lone pair on F, O, or N in the acceptor molecule. Both must be present.
   - Origin: the extreme electronegativity of F/O/N, combined with small atomic size, creates a very polar bond with the H nucleus essentially exposed and able to interact closely with the lone pair on another F/O/N.
   - Examples: H₂O (bp 100°C, far above H₂S bp −60°C despite higher molar mass of H₂S); HF (bp 19.5°C, far above HCl bp −85°C); NH₃ (bp −33°C, higher than PH₃ bp −87°C).
   - Water has FOUR H-bond sites per molecule: 2 O–H donors + 2 lone pairs as acceptors → extensive 3D network, tetrahedral arrangement → explains: (a) unusually high bp for its molar mass; (b) high specific heat capacity (energy breaks H-bonds); (c) ice LESS DENSE than liquid water (open hexagonal lattice in ice — H-bonds maximally satisfied; partial collapse on melting → denser liquid water → water expands on freezing, ice floats, protecting aquatic life); (d) high surface tension; (e) high enthalpy of vaporisation.
   - HF: only ONE H-bond donor per molecule (one O–H equivalent: one F–H), two lone pairs, so forms chains, not 3D network — still strong but less than water per molecule.

**Biological H-bonding**:
- DNA double helix: adenine–thymine (2 H-bonds), guanine–cytosine (3 H-bonds) base pairs. The specificity is geometric (A-T geometry matches A-T, not A-G): H-bond donor/acceptor positions are complementary only for the correct pair.
- Protein secondary structure: α-helix (H-bond between N–H of residue n and C=O of residue n+4 along the same polypeptide chain) and β-sheet (H-bonds between adjacent strands, antiparallel or parallel).

**Comparing forces and physical properties**:
- Boiling point: higher bp → stronger IMF to overcome. Non-polar + small → only London; non-polar + large → strong London; polar + small → London + dipole; H-bonding molecules → highest bp for their size.
- Solubility ("like dissolves like"): polar/ionic solutes dissolve in polar solvents (H₂O can H-bond to alcohols, sugars, ionic species via ion-dipole forces); non-polar solutes dissolve in non-polar solvents (hexane dissolves fats).
- Viscosity: stronger or more numerous IMF → higher viscosity (glycerol: 3 OH groups → extensive H-bonding → very viscous).

## Mental Models
**The force hierarchy pyramid**: draw three tiers — base (London: in everything, weakest per unit size for small molecules), middle (dipole-dipole: only in polar asymmetric molecules, adds to London), top (H-bonding: only with F/O/N directly bonded to H, the strongest per unit size for small molecules). Every molecule starts at the base; it rises to middle or top only if it qualifies. Total IMF = sum of all tiers present.

**The H-bond lock-and-key**: hydrogen bonding requires BOTH a donor (H on F/O/N) and an acceptor (lone pair on F/O/N). If either is absent, no H-bond forms — like a lock needing both the keyhole and the key. CH₃OCH₃ (ether) has an acceptor lone pair on O but no O–H or N–H donor — it can H-bond with water (water donates H) but cannot H-bond with itself → much lower bp than CH₃OH (methanol) despite similar molar mass.

## Why Students Fail
Students say "H₂O has H-bonding because it contains hydrogen and oxygen" without specifying the structural requirement (H directly bonded to O). They also say CH₄ has no intermolecular forces at all (forgetting that London forces are universal). The ice-density anomaly frequently trips students who memorise "ice floats" without understanding that the tetrahedral H-bond network in ice is more open than liquid water's partially broken network.

## Misconceptions
- **MC-1 (Type 3 — language contamination)**: "Hydrogen bonding is just a strong version of a covalent bond." Probe: "Is the O–H···O interaction in water the same type as the O–H covalent bond within one water molecule?" Characteristic phrase: "hydrogen bonding = hydrogen covalent bond." Intervention: the covalent O–H bond (within a molecule) is ~464 kJ/mol. The O–H···O hydrogen bond (between molecules) is ~20 kJ/mol — about 20 times weaker. They are fundamentally different interactions: the covalent bond involves electron sharing; the H-bond involves electrostatic attraction between the partially positive H (its nucleus nearly exposed) and a lone pair on F/O/N. "Hydrogen bond" is unfortunately named — "hydrogen bridge" or "H-bond" better conveys the intermolecular nature.
- **MC-2 (Type 5 — instruction-induced)**: "Hydrocarbons have no intermolecular forces." Probe: "Why does hexane (C₆H₁₄) have a boiling point of 69°C when its intermolecular forces are supposedly nonexistent?" Characteristic phrase: "alkanes have no IMF." Intervention: London dispersion forces are UNIVERSAL — present in all matter including non-polar molecules and noble gases. Hexane has a bp of 69°C precisely because it has 50 electrons and an extended surface area, generating significant London forces. The correct statement is: non-polar molecules have ONLY London forces (no dipole-dipole, no H-bonding). "No IMF" is never correct.
- **MC-3 (Type 2 — perceptual intuition)**: "Ice sinks because it is a solid, and solids are always denser than liquids." Probe: "Ice floats on water. Explain this in terms of molecular structure." Characteristic phrase: "solids are always more dense than liquids." Intervention: for MOST substances, solid IS denser than liquid — the particles pack more tightly. Water is a famous EXCEPTION. In ice, every water molecule forms 4 H-bonds in a tetrahedral arrangement, creating an open hexagonal lattice with large open channels. When ice melts, some H-bonds break and molecules can pack more closely — liquid water is DENSER than ice (peak density at 4°C). The H-bond network's geometry is the exception; this specific structure is not found in most molecular solids.

## Analogies
**Valid**: London forces as static cling — the momentary separation of charge (like shuffling across a carpet) attracts a neighbouring molecule's electrons (the static pulls paper to your finger). It's fleeting, weak, but always present. The more electrons (the bigger the carpet), the stronger the static; the more surface area (more carpet), the more chances for static contact.

## Demonstrations
**IMF and boiling point trend**: show boiling points of the Group 17 hydrides (HF, HCl, HBr, HI) and Group 16 hydrides (H₂O, H₂S, H₂Se, H₂Te) on a graph. For Group 16: H₂S, H₂Se, H₂Te show an increasing bp trend (London forces increasing with molar mass) — extrapolating the trend downward would predict H₂O bp ≈ −70°C. The actual bp of 100°C is 170°C above prediction — the anomaly IS H-bonding.

**Miscibility demonstration**: water and hexane don't mix (different IMF); water and ethanol mix in all proportions (both H-bond). Salad dressing separation illustrates the principle without any equipment.

## Discovery Questions
1. "Arrange the following in order of increasing boiling point: CH₄, CH₃OH, CH₃F, C₂H₆. Justify each step, naming the IMF involved and explaining why it is stronger or weaker than the others."
2. "A student claims that NH₃ can act as a hydrogen bond donor but not as an acceptor. A second student claims it can be both. Who is correct? Use the definition of H-bond donor and acceptor to explain, and describe how NH₃ interacts with water."

## Teaching Sequence
1. Recap covalent bonding and polar molecules (what creates a dipole).
2. London dispersion forces: origin (temporary dipoles), universality, dependence on electron count and surface area.
3. Permanent dipole-dipole: polar molecules only; adds to London; examples.
4. H-bonding: requirements (F/O/N directly bonded to H, plus lone pair acceptor); strength; examples (H₂O, HF, NH₃); compare to non-H-bonding analogues.
5. Water's unique properties: H-bond network, tetrahedral geometry, ice density anomaly, high bp/Hvap/surface tension.
6. Biological H-bonding: DNA base pairing, protein secondary structure.
7. Physical property predictions: bp, mp, solubility, viscosity from IMF type and strength.

## Tutor Actions
- **If student says "alkanes have no IMF"**: ask "why does hexane have a bp of 69°C if there are no forces holding molecules together?" Then introduce London forces as universal.
- **If student says all polar molecules H-bond**: check their structural requirement — "does the molecule have H bonded directly to F, O, or N?" CH₃Cl has a polar C–Cl bond but no H on F/O/N → no H-bonding.
- **FRAGILE sign**: correctly identifies H-bonding molecules from a list but cannot explain the ice anomaly or DNA base-pairing in H-bond terms.

## Voice Teaching Notes
The H-bond structural requirement is the one concept students must state precisely, not approximately. In voice teaching, drill: "H directly bonded to F, O, or N" as a memorised phrase, then test it on edge cases (CH₃OCH₃: has O, has H, but H is on C not on O — no H-bonding within the ether). The distinction between "molecule contains O" and "molecule has O–H" is where most errors occur.

## Assessment Signals
- **Green**: names all three IMF types; states London forces are universal; states H-bond requirements precisely; explains water's anomalous bp, density anomaly, and high Hvap; connects H-bonding to DNA and protein structure; ranks molecules by bp using IMF argument.
- **Amber**: correct IMF identification but cannot explain water's anomalous properties from first principles; or forgets London forces in polar molecules.
- **Red**: says alkanes have no IMF; says H-bonding only requires H and O anywhere in the molecule; says ice is denser than water.
- **Probe**: "Explain why ethanol (C₂H₅OH, Mr = 46) has a higher boiling point than dimethyl ether (CH₃OCH₃, Mr = 46), using intermolecular forces. Can dimethyl ether form hydrogen bonds with water? Explain."

## Tutor Recovery Strategy
If student cannot rank IMF strengths: return to the definition of each IMF. Build a table: force type / origin / what molecules have it / approx. strength range / example. Then rank from the table, not from memory. The table makes the hierarchy explicit and tangible.

## Memory Hooks
- **IMF order**: "London (all) < dipole-dipole (polar) < H-bonding (F/O/N-H)."
- **H-bond requirement**: "H directly bonded to F, O, or N. Lone pair acceptor also needed."
- **Water anomaly**: "H-bonds form open tetrahedral lattice in ice → less dense than liquid → ice floats."
- **London trend**: "More electrons + more surface area = stronger London forces."
- **"Like dissolves like"**: "Polar/ionic → water. Non-polar → organic solvents."

## Cross-Subject Connections
- **Biology**: H-bonding holds the DNA double helix together (A-T: 2 H-bonds; G-C: 3 H-bonds — explains why G-C-rich DNA is more thermally stable); stabilises protein secondary structure (α-helices, β-sheets); essential for enzyme active-site geometry and antibody-antigen recognition.
- **Materials science**: viscosity differences (glycerol vs. water vs. hexane) trace directly to H-bond network density; understanding IMF is the basis for designing lubricants, adhesives, and polymer processing.
