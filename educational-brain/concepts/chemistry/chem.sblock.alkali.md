## Learning Objective
Describe the periodic trends in properties of Group 1 metals (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs), explain their reactions with water, oxygen, and halogens in terms of electronic configuration and ionisation energy, and identify the anomalous properties of lithium due to the diagonal relationship with magnesium.

## Core Understanding
Group 1 (alkali metals): Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr (radioactive). Valence configuration: ns¹. Only one electron in the outer shell, and it is well-shielded from the nucleus → lowest IE₁ in each period → lose this electron very readily → almost always +1 in compounds → very reactive metals.

**Trends down Group 1**:
- Atomic radius: increases (more shells added) → outer electron further from nucleus.
- IE₁: decreases (Li 519 → Na 496 → K 419 → Rb 403 → Cs 376 kJ/mol) — outer electron easier to remove → **reactivity increases down the group** (contrast with halogens/non-metals where reactivity DECREASES down group).
- Melting and boiling points: DECREASE (Li 181°C → Na 98°C → K 63°C → Cs 28°C) — metallic bonding (one delocalised electron per atom in a lattice) gets weaker as the atomic radius increases and electron density decreases.
- Density: INCREASES down the group, but all are less dense than most metals (Li 0.53, Na 0.97, K 0.86 g/cm³ — all float on water; K density < Na due to anomalous large atomic volume).
- Flame colours: Li (crimson red), Na (intense yellow-orange), K (lilac/violet), Rb (red-violet), Cs (blue) — from electron excitation to higher energy levels and emission on return.

**Reactions with water** (all vigorous; vigour INCREASES down group):
- 2M(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2MOH(aq) + H₂(g)
- Li: steady fizzing; Na: rapid, melts into a ball, yellow flame; K: catches fire spontaneously (pale violet flame); Rb/Cs: explosive even in small amounts.
- All produce alkaline solutions (MOH strong base, fully dissociates) → Group 1 metals named "alkali metals."

**Reactions with oxygen** (INCREASING complexity/OS of oxide down the group):
- Li: burns in air to form normal oxide: 4Li + O₂ → 2Li₂O (O²⁻, O OS = −2).
- Na: burns in air to form PEROXIDE: 2Na + O₂ → Na₂O₂ (O₂²⁻, O OS = −1); Na₂O also exists but less stable.
- K, Rb, Cs: form SUPEROXIDES in excess O₂: K + O₂ → KO₂ (O₂⁻, O OS = −½); these are paramagnetic (unpaired electron on O₂⁻).
- Na₂O₂ + H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂O₂; KO₂ + H₂O → KOH + H₂O₂ + ½O₂ (used in breathing apparatus — consumes CO₂ and releases O₂).

**Reactions with halogens** (all react vigorously, rate increases down Group 1):
- 2M + X₂ → 2MX (ionic solid; rate increases as IE₁ decreases; all Group 1 halides are ionic, all soluble in water except those of larger anions with very large cations).

**Other reactions**:
- With H₂: 2M + H₂ → 2MH (metal hydrides; H carries −1 OS — ionic hydride; react with water: MH + H₂O → MOH + H₂↑).
- With nitrogen: only Li reacts directly with N₂ at room temperature: 6Li + N₂ → 2Li₃N (lithium nitride, ionic). Other Group 1 metals do not react with N₂ readily.
- Na and NaOH industrial manufacture: chlor-alkali process — electrolysis of brine (NaCl(aq)): cathode 2H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂ + 2OH⁻; anode 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻; overall: 2NaCl + 2H₂O → Cl₂ + H₂ + 2NaOH. Three useful products from one process.

**Anomalous properties of lithium (diagonal relationship Li/Mg)**:
- Smaller atomic radius than expected for Group 1 (IE₁ = 519 kJ/mol — much higher than Na 496).
- High charge density (smallest ionic radius in Group 1) → forms covalent character in some compounds, similar to Mg²⁺.
- Forms normal oxide (Li₂O) like Mg (MgO) — not peroxide.
- Forms stable nitride (Li₃N) like Mg (Mg₃N₂) — unique in Group 1.
- LiF and Li₂CO₃ are relatively insoluble (like MgF₂ and MgCO₃) — higher lattice energy due to small ionic radius.
- Li has higher mp (181°C), hardness, and density than expected by extrapolation from Na/K.

## Mental Models
**The one-electron relay race**: every Group 1 metal is a runner carrying exactly one electron that it desperately wants to hand off to the nearest electron-acceptor. The runners at the bottom of the group (Rb, Cs) are faster (lower IE₁) and more desperate — they react more violently. Li is the slowest runner (highest IE₁ in group), yet still reactive compared to other metals.

**The oxide ladder**: going down Group 1, the product of burning in oxygen climbs an "oxidation ladder" for oxygen: normal oxide (Li₂O, O²⁻) → peroxide (Na₂O₂, O₂²⁻, O = −1) → superoxide (KO₂, O₂⁻, O = −½). More electron-rich later metals can stabilise higher OS anions (larger cations provide better lattice stabilisation for the less compact peroxide/superoxide anions).

## Why Students Fail
Students predict Group 1 oxide formation as "always Li₂O-type" by analogy with Group 1's ionic simplicity — they do not anticipate that K, Rb, Cs produce superoxides rather than normal oxides.

Students confuse the alkali metal trend (INCREASES down group, like metals generally) with halogen/non-metal trend (DECREASES down group). The key discriminator is "gaining or losing electrons?" — metals lose (easier down), non-metals gain (harder down).

## Misconceptions
- **MC-1 (Type 3 — language contamination)**: "Alkali metals are called 'alkali' because they dissolve in alkalis." Probe: "Why are Group 1 metals called alkali metals?" Characteristic phrase: "they like alkalis." Intervention: 'alkali' refers to the PRODUCT — all Group 1 metals react with water to produce strongly alkaline solutions (MOH, fully dissociating strong bases). The metals themselves are NOT alkalis; they produce alkalis. The name describes the product, not an affinity.
- **MC-2 (Type 1 — overgeneralization)**: "All Group 1 metals burn in oxygen to form M₂O." Probe: "Write the product of sodium burning in excess oxygen." Characteristic phrase: "sodium oxide forms." Intervention: Na forms the PEROXIDE Na₂O₂ (O OS = −1) not the normal oxide Na₂O; K/Rb/Cs form SUPEROXIDES (O OS = −½). The oxide type depends on which anion is stabilised by the particular cation — smaller Li²⁺ stabilises the compact O²⁻; larger cations stabilise the less compact O₂²⁻ and O₂⁻ better. This is an important exception to the "Group means predictable product" assumption.
- **MC-3 (Type 5 — instruction-induced)**: "Li is the most reactive because it's at the top of the group, like in the halogen series." Probe: "Which reacts more vigorously with water — Li or Cs?" Characteristic phrase: "higher up = more reactive for Group 1." Intervention: Group 1 REACTIVITY INCREASES DOWN the group (lower IE₁ → easier electron loss). Li is the LEAST reactive alkali metal (highest IE₁ in group, smallest radius, most charge-dense). This is the OPPOSITE of the halogen trend — metals react by LOSING electrons (easier going down); non-metals react by GAINING electrons (easier going up). Never apply one group's trend to the other.

## Analogies
**Valid**: Group 1 reactivity trend as a spring mechanism — each metal atom is a coiled spring holding one electron. Li's spring is tightest (highest IE₁); Cs's spring is almost uncoiled (lowest IE₁). The spring releases (the metal reacts) when it contacts water or another oxidant. The looser the spring, the more violently it releases — Cs's spring is so loose it fires even with traces of moisture.

## Demonstrations
**Reaction of Li, Na, K with water**: add small pieces successively to troughs of water with universal indicator; observe: Li (steady fizzing, no flame, solution turns blue-green → blue indicating MOH), Na (rapid, melts, yellow flame, vigorous bubbling), K (ignites immediately, purple-lilac flame, rapid). This single three-stage experiment directly reveals the reactivity trend and connects each metal to a flame colour. Safety: always use small pieces, open troughs, no lids, no K with large amounts of water; Rb/Cs shown by video only.

**Flame tests with salts** (Li, Na, K chloride solutions on nichrome wire): characteristic colours (crimson, yellow, lilac) make the concept of atomic emission spectra visible and tangible; reinforces the Group 1 identity for each metal.

## Discovery Questions
1. "Sodium peroxide Na₂O₂ is used in submarines and rescue breathing equipment to regenerate oxygen: Na₂O₂ + CO₂ → Na₂CO₃ + O₂; Na₂O₂ + H₂O → NaOH + H₂O₂. Assign OS to all species in the first equation and confirm it is NOT a redox reaction. Assign OS in the second equation and confirm H₂O₂ is produced."
2. "Li reacts with N₂ at room temperature (6Li + N₂ → 2Li₃N), but sodium does not. Relate this to the charge density and diagonal relationship of Li with Mg, which also forms Mg₃N₂."

## Teaching Sequence
1. Electronic configuration ns¹; IE₁ trend down group; why reactivity INCREASES (losing electrons becomes easier).
2. Reactions with water: write equations, predict products (all MOH + H₂), explain vigour trend.
3. Reactions with oxygen: three oxide types (Li₂O, Na₂O₂, KO₂); assign OS to O in each; explain cation size → anion stabilisation.
4. Reactions with halogens and H₂: straightforward ionic product; H⁻ in metal hydrides.
5. Li anomaly: smaller radius, higher IE₁ than trend, diagonal relationship with Mg, reactions with N₂, properties of LiOH/Li₂CO₃.
6. Chlor-alkali process: brine electrolysis, three products, economic importance.

## Tutor Actions
- **If student says Li is most reactive in Group 1**: ask "What determines metal reactivity?" (ease of electron loss); "What determines that?" (IE₁); "Which has the lower IE₁ — Li or Cs?" (Cs); "So which reacts more easily?" (Cs). Make the logic explicit before stating the conclusion.
- **If student predicts Na₂O from Na + O₂**: tell them the actual product (Na₂O₂); ask "what is the OS of O in Na₂O₂?" (–1, not –2); "what do we call O at OS –1?" (peroxide); introduce oxide ladder model.
- **FRAGILE sign**: knows the water reaction products but cannot explain why vigour increases, or cannot explain the OS of O in Na₂O₂.

## Voice Teaching Notes
The water reactions are the most visually impactful demonstrations in Group 1 — do them first (or show video immediately) to create the initial strong impression of reactivity increasing. Students remember Cs blowing up water forever; use that memory as the hook. Then work backward: "Why does Cs react more violently than Na? What's the one thing that changes as you go from Na to Cs?" (IE₁). This is the right inductive sequence.

The Li anomaly is often skipped in introductory courses but exam questions love it — the diagonal relationship to Mg is a high-value pattern. Connect it immediately to Group 2 (the next topic) so students see it as a conceptual bridge.

## Assessment Signals
- **Green**: writes correct balanced equations for M + H₂O, M + O₂ (all three product types), M + X₂; predicts flame colours; explains Li anomaly including Li₃N formation; describes chlor-alkali process products.
- **Amber**: correct water reactions and flame colours but wrong oxide products (especially Na₂O₂ vs Na₂O).
- **Red**: says Li most reactive; predicts all Group 1 metals form normal oxides; confuses alkali (product) with alkali (reagent).
- **Probe**: "Arrange Li, Na, K in order of increasing reactivity with water. Explain the order using IE₁. Then write the balanced equation for K reacting with water and predict the pH of the resulting solution."

## Tutor Recovery Strategy
If student cannot order the reactivity: strip to one pair (Na vs K), give the IE₁ values (Na 496, K 419 kJ/mol), ask "which loses its electron more easily?" (K); "which reacts more readily?" (K). Then extend to Li (IE₁ = 519 > Na > K). Pattern is established from specific numbers, not from a rule memorised without understanding. Once the pair comparison is mastered, the whole group order follows.

## Memory Hooks
- **Reactivity in Group 1**: "Group 1 reactivity INCREASES down (unlike Group 17). Li least reactive; Cs most reactive. Lower IE₁ = easier electron loss."
- **Oxide products**: "Li → normal oxide (Li₂O). Na → peroxide (Na₂O₂). K/Rb/Cs → superoxide (KO₂)."
- **Flame colours**: "LiCr-NaY-KLi" = Li Crimson, Na Yellow, K Lilac.
- **Li anomaly**: "Li behaves like Mg — forms nitride, normal oxide only, insoluble carbonate."

## Cross-Subject Connections
- **Biology**: Na⁺ and K⁺ are the primary intracellular/extracellular cations driving the action potential (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump); Li⁺ (as lithium carbonate) is used in treatment of bipolar disorder; K is an essential macronutrient for plants (fertilisers).
- **Industrial/environmental**: NaOH (chlor-alkali process) is one of the top-5 industrial chemicals worldwide (soap, paper pulp, PVC synthesis); Na in street lamps (low-pressure sodium → monochromatic yellow, high-pressure → broad spectrum); Cs atomic clock (Cs hyperfine transition = definition of the SI second).
