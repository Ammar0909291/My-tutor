## Learning Objective
Describe the composition of crude oil and the principle of fractional distillation; name the major fractions obtained and their approximate carbon ranges and uses; explain thermal and catalytic cracking and their industrial importance; and define octane number and explain how reforming improves fuel quality.

## Core Understanding
**Composition of crude oil**:
Crude oil (petroleum) is a complex mixture of hydrocarbons — predominantly alkanes (straight-chain, branched, and cycloalkanes) with smaller amounts of aromatic hydrocarbons, and trace impurities of sulfur, nitrogen, and oxygen compounds. It is a fossil fuel formed over millions of years from marine organisms under heat and pressure. The hydrocarbons range from methane (C₁) to heavy waxes (C₃₀+).

**Fractional distillation**:
Crude oil is separated into fractions by fractional distillation in a fractionating column. The principle relies on the different boiling points of the fractions — determined by molecular mass and chain length (longer chains → stronger London dispersion forces → higher bp).

- The crude oil is heated (350–400°C) and vaporised; vapours enter the fractionating column.
- Temperature decreases from bottom (hottest) to top (coolest).
- Fractions condense at different levels where the temperature matches their boiling point range and are drawn off as liquids.
- Fractions with the LOWEST boiling points exit at the TOP; fractions with the HIGHEST boiling points remain at the BOTTOM.

| Fraction | Approx. carbon range | Boiling point range | Main uses |
|---|---|---|---|
| Refinery gas | C₁–C₄ | < 25°C | LPG (liquefied petroleum gas), domestic fuel |
| Petrol (gasoline) | C₅–C₁₂ | 25–75°C | Motor fuel |
| Naphtha | C₅–C₁₂ | 70–120°C | Feedstock for chemicals, solvents |
| Kerosene (paraffin) | C₁₀–C₁₆ | 150–250°C | Jet fuel, domestic heating |
| Gas oil (diesel) | C₁₄–C₂₀ | 220–350°C | Diesel engines, central heating |
| Fuel oil | C₂₀–C₅₀ | 300–370°C | Ship fuel, power stations |
| Lubricating oil | C₂₀–C₅₀ | 350–500°C | Engine lubrication |
| Bitumen (asphalt) | C₅₀+ | > 500°C (residue) | Road surfacing, roofing |

**Demand mismatch and cracking**:
Consumer demand for petrol/kerosene is far greater than the natural fraction composition of crude oil. Heavy fractions are in excess; lighter fractions are in deficit. **Cracking** converts large, less-useful alkane molecules into smaller, more-valuable ones by breaking C–C bonds at high temperature (and catalyst).

- **Thermal cracking**: high temperature (~500°C) and pressure (~70 atm); homolytic C–C bond fission → FREE RADICAL mechanism; produces mainly straight-chain alkenes and alkanes; fast, gives a wide product distribution.
- **Catalytic cracking**: lower temperature (~450°C), lower pressure; uses zeolite (aluminosilicate) catalysts; CARBOCATION (carbonium ion) mechanism → produces BRANCHED alkanes and aromatic hydrocarbons preferentially (better for fuel); allows selectivity. Catalytic cracker regenerates catalyst by burning off deposited carbon (coke).
- Both types produce ALKENES (ethene, propene) as important petrochemical feedstock for plastics, polymers, and alcohols.
- **Hydrocracking**: catalytic cracking in the presence of H₂; saturates the alkenes produced, converting them to alkanes (better for fuel, not feedstock).

**Octane number and fuel quality**:
The octane number (octane rating) measures the resistance of a fuel to KNOCKING (premature, uncontrolled ignition in the cylinder, causing engine knock — loss of efficiency and engine damage).

- **Reference scale**: iso-octane (2,2,4-trimethylpentane) = 100 (best — highly branched, anti-knock); n-heptane = 0 (worst — straight-chain, knock-prone).
- Octane number of a fuel = percentage of iso-octane in an iso-octane/n-heptane mixture that knocks at the same rate as the fuel under test.
- **Structural factors**: branched alkanes > straight-chain alkanes; cycloalkanes > straight-chain; aromatic hydrocarbons > alkanes → branching and ring closure raise the octane number.

**Reforming (catalytic reforming)**:
Converts low-octane straight-chain alkanes (from naphtha) into high-octane branched alkanes and aromatic hydrocarbons using a platinum (or platinum-rhenium) catalyst at ~500°C and moderate pressure.

- **Isomerisation**: straight-chain alkanes → branched alkanes (e.g., n-hexane → 2-methylpentane).
- **Cyclisation and aromatisation (dehydrogenation)**: straight-chain alkanes → cycloalkanes → aromatic hydrocarbons (e.g., heptane → toluene + 4H₂). H₂ is a useful by-product.
- Reforming raises the octane number without adding lead (lead-free fuels — tetraethyllead [TEL] was banned because Pb poisons catalytic converters and accumulates in the environment).

**Oxygenate additives**: ethanol and MTBE (methyl tert-butyl ether) raise octane number in blended fuels; ethanol has a high octane number (~109) and is renewable.

## Mental Models
**Fractionating column as a reverse zipper**: the column is coldest at the top and hottest at the bottom. Molecules zip up to their boiling point level and then condense — short molecules make it to the top before condensing (low bp), long molecules fall back before reaching the top (high bp, condense lower down). Each rung of the zipper collects a fraction at its own temperature.

**Cracking as molecular surgery**: cracking is like cutting a long chain of paper clips into shorter pieces. The difference between thermal and catalytic cracking is the cutting tool — random scissor cuts (thermal, random radical mechanism, all shapes) vs. shaped scissors (catalytic, guided carbocation mechanism, preferentially gives branched shapes).

**Octane number as a fuel's composure under pressure**: an engine's cylinder compresses the fuel–air mixture before ignition. A fuel with LOW octane number ignites too early (before the spark, from heat alone) — like someone who flinches before the starting gun. A HIGH-octane fuel waits calmly for the spark — stays cool under compression. Branched molecules are more "composed" because their compact shape dissipates the radicals that initiate pre-ignition.

## Why Students Fail
Students reverse the fractionating column — saying the heaviest (highest-bp) fractions come off at the top. They confuse cracking (breaking C–C bonds into smaller molecules) with fractionation (separating already-existing molecules). They say reforming produces ethanol from alkanes (it does not — reforming produces branched alkanes and aromatics; ethanol is a separate renewable additive). They also confuse octane number with the number of carbons in the fuel molecule.

## Misconceptions
- **MC-1 (Type 2 — perceptual intuition)**: "Heavy fractions come off at the top of the fractionating column because they're denser and rise higher." Probe: "In which direction does density work in a gas — do denser gases rise or sink?" Characteristic phrase: "heavy things float to the top." Intervention: in the fractionating column, liquids (not gases) are what collect at each level. The vapours RISE from the heated bottom; as they rise, the temperature decreases. A fraction condenses (becomes liquid) at the level where the column temperature matches its BOILING POINT. The heaviest fractions (highest bp) condense FIRST, at the BOTTOM. The lightest fractions (lowest bp, most volatile) remain as vapour longest and only condense near the TOP of the column where it is coolest. Heavy = high bp = stays liquid at bottom; light = low bp = rises to top.
- **MC-2 (Type 3 — language contamination)**: "Cracking breaks down crude oil into its component fractions, just like distillation." Probe: "Does cracking require a new bond to be broken, or does it just separate molecules that were already mixed?" Characteristic phrase: "cracking and distillation both separate crude oil." Intervention: DISTILLATION separates molecules that already exist in the crude oil mixture — no chemical bonds are broken; it is a physical process. CRACKING is a CHEMICAL REACTION: it breaks C–C COVALENT BONDS in large alkane molecules to produce SMALLER, NEW molecules (alkenes + shorter alkanes). Cracking is done AFTER distillation, on specific fractions, to increase yield of more useful lighter hydrocarbons. They are sequential, not synonymous.
- **MC-3 (Type 5 — instruction-induced)**: "The octane number tells you how many carbon atoms are in the fuel." Probe: "Regular petrol has octane number 95. Does that mean it contains only molecules with 95 carbons?" Characteristic phrase: "95 octane = C₉₅ molecules." Intervention: the octane number is a RELATIVE PERFORMANCE SCALE, not a carbon count. It was defined by comparing a fuel's knocking tendency with a mixture of iso-octane (octane number 100, 8 carbons, NOT 100 carbons) and n-heptane (octane number 0). A fuel with octane number 95 knocks at the same rate as a mixture of 95% iso-octane and 5% n-heptane by volume — it says nothing about the actual carbon chain length of the fuel. Modern gasolines contain a wide mixture of hydrocarbons (C₅–C₁₂) along with additives; the octane number summarises their collective anti-knock performance.

## Analogies
**Valid**: fractional distillation is like a hotel's different floors for different-temperature guests — the boiling-point range determines which floor (temperature zone) each fraction checks in to. Hot guests (volatile, low bp) go to the cooler upper floors; cold guests (heavy, high bp) settle on the warm lower floors.

## Demonstrations
**Laboratory fractionation of a hydrocarbon mixture**: distil a mixture of hexane and decane through a fractionating column (even a short one packed with glass wool). Collect fractions at different boiling point ranges; show that the hexane fraction (bp 69°C) comes over at ~70°C, while decane (bp 174°C) requires much higher temperature. Illustrates selectivity by boiling point.

**Cracking of liquid paraffin (kerosene)**: pass paraffin vapour over broken ceramic pot (as catalyst) heated strongly in a hard-glass tube; collect the gas produced over water. Test: decolourises bromine water (alkene present) and burns readily. Confirms that larger molecules have been cracked into smaller unsaturated products.

## Discovery Questions
1. "Crude oil from two different wells has almost identical composition. Yet refinery A produces three times more petrol per barrel than refinery B using the same distillation. What process must refinery A be running that refinery B is not? Explain the chemistry."
2. "Straight-run petrol from distillation has an octane number of about 40. Reforming raises it to around 100. Explain using structural chemistry why straight-run petrol knocks so readily and why reforming improves this."

## Teaching Sequence
1. Crude oil composition: mixture of alkanes (C₁–C₃₀+), formed from fossil organisms, physical properties determine separation strategy.
2. Fractional distillation principle: boiling point vs. chain length (London forces); column temperature gradient; condensation at matching level; heaviest at bottom, lightest at top.
3. Major fractions: carbon range, bp range, uses (table). Demand mismatch: too much heavy oil, too little petrol/kerosene.
4. Cracking: purpose (balance demand); thermal (free radical, high T/P, wide products) vs. catalytic (zeolite, carbocation, branched/aromatic preference); alkene by-products as feedstock.
5. Octane number: definition via reference scale (iso-octane 100, n-heptane 0); structural factors (branched > straight, aromatic > alkane); knocking mechanism.
6. Reforming: isomerisation + aromatisation on Pt catalyst; raises octane number; H₂ by-product; replaces TEL additives.

## Tutor Actions
- **If student says heaviest fractions exit at the top**: draw the column with temperature labels (hot bottom, cool top) and ask "Where in the column is it hot enough for the high-boiling fraction to remain as vapour?" (At the bottom only — it condenses at the bottom.) "Where do low-boiling fractions condense?" (At the top, where it's cool enough.)
- **If student confuses cracking with fractionation**: ask "After fractional distillation, do you need to add anything or apply a chemical reaction to the naphtha fraction to get petrol?" (Yes — cracking breaks C–C bonds.) "Distillation just separates what's already there — true or false?" (True — no bonds broken.) Force the chemical-vs-physical distinction.
- **If student defines octane number as carbon count**: ask "Iso-octane has 8 carbons and an octane number of 100. If octane number = carbon count, what would octane number of 100 represent?" (100 carbons — clearly absurd.) "Octane number is a performance scale; 100 means it performs like pure iso-octane."
- **FRAGILE sign**: student knows fractional distillation order and can name fractions but cannot explain WHY the lighter fractions come off at the top, or cannot distinguish thermal from catalytic cracking mechanistically.

## Voice Teaching Notes
In voice, the distinction between physical separation (distillation) and chemical reaction (cracking) is the core concept to anchor first. Ask: "Is fractional distillation making new molecules or just sorting molecules that already exist in crude oil?" (Just sorting.) "Is cracking making new molecules?" (Yes — breaking bonds and forming new smaller ones.) Lock this distinction before naming any fractions or describing column operation. The octane number definition is counterintuitive and needs to be built from first principles (what is knocking, why is it bad, how do we measure anti-knock performance) rather than given as a definition to memorise.

## Assessment Signals
- **Green**: explains that heavier fractions condense lower in the column; names 4+ major fractions with approximate carbon ranges and uses; explains that cracking breaks C–C bonds to give smaller alkanes and alkenes; distinguishes thermal (free radical) from catalytic (carbocation, zeolite, branched products); defines octane number correctly as a performance reference scale (iso-octane=100, heptane=0); explains why branched alkanes have higher octane numbers; states what reforming achieves.
- **Amber**: knows fraction names and uses but cannot explain column operation (why heavier at bottom); or can define octane number but cannot explain the structural basis for anti-knock performance.
- **Red**: says heaviest fractions exit at the top; says cracking and distillation are the same process; says octane number = carbon count.
- **Probe**: "Explain why catalytic cracking is preferred over thermal cracking for fuel production, and name ONE advantage each produces as a feedstock product."

## Tutor Recovery Strategy
If student cannot explain why different fractions condense at different levels: build the argument from first principles. "Which physical property of a liquid determines when it boils?" (Boiling point.) "Does a long-chain alkane like C₂₀ boil at a higher or lower temperature than a short-chain C₅?" (Higher — more electrons, stronger London forces, more energy needed to escape the liquid phase.) "So in a column where temperature decreases from bottom to top, where does C₂₀ condense?" (Low down, where it's still hot enough to reach its bp — wait, no: it condenses LOWER because it needs a HIGHER temperature to remain as vapour. It becomes liquid SOONER as it rises and meets lower temperatures.) This bottom-up logical construction prevents simple reversal errors.

## Memory Hooks
- **Column order**: "Heaviest at the BOTTOM (highest bp, highest London forces). Lightest at the TOP (lowest bp, most volatile, exits as gas)."
- **Cracking**: "Cracking = CHEMICAL reaction (breaks C–C bonds). Distillation = PHYSICAL separation (no bonds broken). Both are needed to refine crude oil."
- **Thermal vs. catalytic cracking**: "Thermal = free radicals (hot, high pressure). Catalytic = carbocations (zeolite, milder, branched products preferred)."
- **Octane number**: "Iso-octane (8C, branched) = 100. n-Heptane (7C, straight) = 0. Higher number = less knocking = better fuel. NOT the carbon count."
- **Reforming**: "Reforming converts straight chains → branched and aromatic (Pt catalyst, ~500°C). Raises octane number. Produces H₂."

## Cross-Subject Connections
- **Environmental chemistry**: combustion of petroleum hydrocarbons produces CO₂ (greenhouse gas), NOₓ (from N₂ oxidation at high temperature; precursor to photochemical smog), and SO₂ (from sulfur impurities; cause of acid rain). Catalytic converters oxidise CO and hydrocarbons and reduce NOₓ — poisoned by Pb compounds, which is why tetraethyllead was banned. Life-cycle analysis of petrol vs. biofuels is a current environmental policy question.
- **Chemical engineering**: the fractionating column is a scaled-up application of the same principle used in laboratory fractional distillation — the industrial unit is 50–70 m tall with bubble-cap trays instead of glass beads. Column design, reflux ratio, and feed preheating are all engineering optimisations of the same underlying boiling-point physics.
