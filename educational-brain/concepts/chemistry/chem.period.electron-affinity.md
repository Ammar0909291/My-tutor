## Learning Objective

Students can define electron gain enthalpy (electron affinity) and explain the sign convention; describe and explain the general trend across a period and down a group; explain anomalies at Group 2/13 (less negative EA for Group 2), Group 15/16 (less negative EA for Group 15), and the anomalously low EA of F relative to Cl; relate electronegativity (Pauling and Mulliken scales) to electron affinity and ionization energy; and use electron affinity trends to predict bond character.

## Core Understanding

**Definition — Electron gain enthalpy (ΔegH, IUPAC) / Electron affinity (EA)**:
The enthalpy change when one electron is added to an isolated gaseous atom in its ground state:
X(g) + e⁻ → X⁻(g)    ΔegH = EA (first electron affinity)

Sign convention (two in use — students must recognise both):
- IUPAC / enthalpy convention: ΔegH is NEGATIVE for electron gain that releases energy (exothermic = energy released = electron is attracted to nucleus). More negative = greater tendency to gain electron = stronger non-metal.
- Older convention (common in many textbooks): EA is defined as the energy RELEASED (positive number). A "high EA" in this convention = a highly exothermic electron addition.
The two conventions have opposite signs: ΔegH(F) ≈ −328 kJ/mol (IUPAC) = EA(F) = +328 kJ/mol (older).

**First vs. second electron affinity**:
First EA: usually negative (energy released) for main-group non-metals — electron is attracted by the nucleus.
Second EA (e.g., O → O²⁻): always POSITIVE (endothermic) — adding a second electron to an already-negative ion requires energy to overcome electron-electron repulsion. The second electron must be forced on by a net energy input. O²⁻ is only stable in ionic crystal lattices (the lattice energy provides the energy needed).

**General trend across a period: EA becomes more negative (more exothermic)**:
Across a period, Z increases at the same n → Zeff increases → incoming electron is attracted more strongly to the nucleus → more energy is released → more negative ΔegH (stronger electron affinity). This parallels the IE trend (both driven by rising Zeff).

**Two anomalies in period 2 and 3 (same causes as IE anomalies)**:

*Anomaly 1 — Group 2 (Be, Mg) have less negative EA than Group 1 (Li, Na)*:
Group 2 has a completely filled s subshell (ns²). An added electron would enter the higher-energy np subshell, experiencing shielding from the filled ns². Less nuclear attraction felt → less energy released → less negative EA. Compare: EA(Na) ≈ −53 kJ/mol; EA(Mg) ≈ 0 (essentially no affinity).

*Anomaly 2 — Group 15 (N, P) have less negative EA than Group 16 (O, S)*:
Group 15 has a half-filled p subshell (np³, all singly occupied — Hund's rule). Adding an electron to a half-filled p subshell creates a doubly occupied orbital → spin-pairing repulsion → less energy released → less negative EA. Compare: EA(N) ≈ 0 (essentially no affinity); EA(O) ≈ −141 kJ/mol.

*Anomaly 3 — F has a less negative EA than Cl*:
Expected: EA(F) more negative than EA(Cl) because F has higher Zeff. Observed: EA(F) ≈ −328, EA(Cl) ≈ −349 kJ/mol — Cl's EA is MORE negative. Reason: F is the smallest atom among the halogens. Its 2p orbitals are very compact and already partially filled; the incoming electron experiences significant electron-electron repulsion in the crowded n=2 shell. Cl's larger 3p orbital accommodates the incoming electron with much less repulsion → more energy released → more negative EA.

**General trend down a group: EA becomes less negative**:
Down a group, the incoming electron enters a higher n shell (further from nucleus, more shielded) → less attracted → less energy released → less negative EA. However, the F anomaly means the most negative EA in Group 17 is Cl, not F.

**Electronegativity**:
Electronegativity (χ) is the tendency of a bonded atom to attract electrons toward itself in a bond. Two scales:
- *Pauling scale*: derived from bond dissociation energies. F is assigned χ = 4.0 (most electronegative). Electronegativity decreases down a group; increases across a period.
- *Mulliken scale*: χ_Mulliken = (IE₁ + EA)/2 (average of ionization energy and electron affinity; measures the average tendency to hold or attract electrons). Directly connects IE and EA to electronegativity.

Trend: electronegativity increases across a period and decreases down a group — same direction as both IE and EA (and opposite to atomic radius).

## Mental Models

**The gravity well model**: electron affinity is the depth of the "potential energy well" the incoming electron falls into. High Zeff atoms have a deeper well (more negative EA — more energy released). Noble gases have no well (full shell — closed door). Group 15 half-filled p: the well is partially blocked by repulsion. F's well is deep but tiny — the electron has trouble fitting in without bumping into others.

**The Mulliken electronegativity model**: imagine an atom at the boundary between two bonding partners. IE is how hard it grips its own electron. EA is how hard it grips an incoming electron from the other atom. Electronegativity = average of these two grips. F grips both sides hard → highest electronegativity.

## Why Students Fail

1. **Sign confusion: more negative ΔegH = "weaker" electron affinity**: students see −349 for Cl and −328 for F and conclude Cl has "weaker" EA because it's "more negative." The more negative the ΔegH (IUPAC convention), the more energy released, the more exothermic, and the STRONGER the electron affinity.
2. **F has a more negative EA than Cl**: the anomalous fact that Cl has a more negative EA than F is frequently missed; students apply the "smaller = stronger" rule from IE to EA.
3. **Second electron affinity of oxygen is negative (exothermic)**: students extend the first EA's exothermic logic to the second → they forget that O²⁻ requires endothermic energy input and is only stabilised by the ionic crystal lattice.

## Misconceptions

**MC-1 — More negative ΔegH means weaker tendency to gain an electron** (Type 4, notation-induced: negative = less, negative = unfavorable in everyday language)
- *Mechanism*: students interpret a negative sign as "unfavorable" or "less attraction." In thermodynamic convention, negative ΔH = exothermic = energy released = favourable = strong tendency.
- *Diagnostic probe*: "ΔegH for fluorine is −328 kJ/mol and for chlorine is −349 kJ/mol. Which element has a stronger tendency to gain an electron?"
- *Characteristic phrase*: "F has ΔegH = −328 which is less negative than Cl's −349, so F gains electrons less readily than Cl."
- *Repair*: negative ΔegH means energy is RELEASED when the electron is added — the process is exothermic and spontaneous. MORE negative = MORE energy released = STRONGER attraction for the electron. So Cl (−349) attracts an electron more strongly than F (−328) — a genuine chemical fact, one of the important anomalies. F is more electronegative overall because its IE is also very high (Mulliken: χ = (IE + EA)/2).

**MC-2 — The EA anomaly at F vs. Cl is the same as at N vs. O** (Type 5, instruction-induced: students learn two EA anomalies, sometimes conflate their mechanisms)
- *Mechanism*: students know "N has a less negative EA than O because of half-filled subshell" and "F has a less negative EA than Cl" and assume both have the same cause.
- *Diagnostic probe*: "Both N and F have less negative electron affinities than their right-side/down-side neighbors. Are these anomalies caused by the same effect?"
- *Characteristic phrase*: "F has a less negative EA than Cl because F's 2p subshell is half-filled."
- *Repair*: N's anomaly: half-filled 2p³ shell — adding an electron causes spin pairing repulsion in a singly-occupied orbital (same half-filled stability). F's anomaly: NOT because F is half-filled (F has 2p⁵ — one vacancy). F's anomaly is due to the exceptionally SMALL n=2 shell — the incoming electron must enter an already-crowded 2p orbital, experiencing unusually high electron-electron repulsion. Cl's 3p orbitals are larger, accommodate the electron more comfortably → more energy released → more negative EA.

**MC-3 — The second electron affinity of oxygen is negative (adding electrons is always exothermic for non-metals)** (Type 1, overgeneralization from the first EA)
- *Mechanism*: students correctly learn that halogens and oxygen readily gain electrons (negative first EA). They extend this to ALL electron additions for non-metals, including the formation of O²⁻.
- *Diagnostic probe*: "O²⁻ is found in ionic oxides. Is the second electron affinity of oxygen positive or negative? Explain."
- *Characteristic phrase*: "O²⁻ forms readily because oxygen has a high electron affinity and gains electrons easily."
- *Repair*: the second EA of oxygen is POSITIVE (≈ +780 kJ/mol) — adding a second electron to an already-negative O⁻ ion requires overcoming electron-electron repulsion. O²⁻ does not form spontaneously in the gas phase — it would be unstable. O²⁻ is stabilised in ionic crystal lattices (the lattice energy of MgO, CaO etc. — typically 3000–4000 kJ/mol — more than compensates for the endothermic second EA). Born-Haber cycle thinking is required to understand oxide formation thermodynamics.

## Analogies

**The landing-pad analogy**: electron affinity is the energy released when a ball (electron) lands on a trampoline (the atom). A deep, small trampoline (F — high Zeff, tiny 2p shell) should release a lot of energy, but the ball bounces off partly because the trampoline is already crowded with other balls. A slightly larger trampoline (Cl — slightly lower Zeff, bigger 3p shell) has room to absorb the ball fully → more energy released → more negative EA. The F anomaly is exactly this: the trampoline is deep but too crowded.

**The Mulliken electronegativity scale as an average grip**: IE measures how hard an atom grips its OWN electrons. EA measures how hard it grips INCOMING electrons. Mulliken χ = average of both grips. F has a very strong grip on its own electrons (high IE = 1681 kJ/mol) and a reasonably strong grip on incoming ones (EA = 328 kJ/mol) → average grip is the highest of all elements → highest electronegativity, despite Cl having a slightly stronger grip on incoming electrons alone.

## Demonstrations

**Demonstration 1 — Halogen reactivity series as indirect EA evidence**
- React Cl₂(g) with aqueous KBr, and Br₂(aq) with aqueous KI (or demonstrate by colour change: Cl₂ solution is pale yellow-green; Br₂ is orange-brown; I₂ is brown/purple). Cl₂ displaces Br⁻ and I⁻; Br₂ displaces I⁻ but not Br⁻; I₂ displaces nothing. The reactivity order (Cl > Br > I) reflects the EA trend (more negative EA down Group 17 up to Cl; F would displace all, but HF/F₂ use requires specialist lab conditions). This directly shows that EA decreases down the group (stronger electron-attracting power for the lighter halogen), though the F < Cl anomaly is not directly demonstrable here.

## Discovery Questions

1. "Explain why the electron affinity of nitrogen (ΔegH ≈ 0 kJ/mol) is much less negative than that of oxygen (ΔegH ≈ −141 kJ/mol), despite N being to the LEFT of O (lower Z)." (Targets: N has configuration 2s²2p³ — each 2p orbital is singly occupied (Hund's rule). Adding an electron to N forces a doubly occupied 2p orbital, creating spin-pairing repulsion that nearly cancels the nuclear attraction → very little energy released → ΔegH ≈ 0. O has 2s²2p⁴ — one 2p is already doubly occupied; the next electron pairs in another 2p that already has one electron → pairing repulsion exists but is already present before this step, and O has one more proton → net attraction outweighs repulsion → more negative ΔegH.)
2. "EA of F = −328 kJ/mol; IE₁ of F = 1681 kJ/mol. EA of Cl = −349 kJ/mol; IE₁ of Cl = 1251 kJ/mol. Using the Mulliken formula, calculate χ for F and Cl. Which is more electronegative?" (Targets: χ_Mulliken(F) = (1681 + 328)/2 = 1009/2 = 1004.5 kJ/mol. χ_Mulliken(Cl) = (1251 + 349)/2 = 1600/2 = 800 kJ/mol. F is more electronegative than Cl (1004.5 > 800), even though Cl has a more negative EA. F's much higher IE (it grips its own electrons far more tightly) pushes its Mulliken electronegativity above Cl's. This is why F is the most electronegative element even though Cl has the highest EA in Group 17.)
3. "The second electron affinity of oxygen is +780 kJ/mol, yet MgO (with O²⁻) is thermodynamically stable. Explain." (Targets: O(g) + 2e⁻ → O²⁻(g): total ΔH = first EA + second EA = −141 + 780 = +639 kJ/mol — endothermic overall. O²⁻ is NOT stable in the gas phase. MgO stability comes from the Born-Haber cycle: the large lattice energy of MgO (≈ −3795 kJ/mol) more than compensates for all endothermic steps (including IE of Mg and both EAs of O), making the overall formation of solid MgO strongly exothermic (ΔHf° ≈ −601 kJ/mol). O²⁻ is created and immediately stabilised within the crystal lattice.)

## Teaching Sequence

1. Review from chem.period.modern-periodic-law: Zeff; Hund's rule; subshell filling.
2. Define electron gain enthalpy and the sign convention (both IUPAC/enthalpy and older EA conventions). Address MC-1 sign confusion immediately.
3. General trend across a period: Zeff rises → EA more negative. Two anomalies: Group 2 (ns² completed → next electron in higher np) and Group 15 (half-filled np → pairing repulsion). Work Discovery Question 1.
4. Trend down a group: EA generally becomes less negative. F anomaly: crowded n=2 shell → Cl has more negative EA than F. Address MC-2.
5. Second EA: always positive (endothermic for the second electron). Why O²⁻ exists only in lattices. Address MC-3. Work Discovery Question 3.
6. Electronegativity: Pauling and Mulliken scales. Mulliken χ = (IE₁ + EA)/2. Work Discovery Question 2.

## Tutor Actions

- If student confuses negative ΔegH with weak EA → MC-1 repair: negative ΔH = energy released = exothermic = more favourable = stronger affinity. More negative = stronger.
- If student says F anomaly is due to half-filled shell → MC-2 repair: F has 2p⁵ (one vacancy, NOT half-filled). F's anomaly is electron-electron repulsion in the crowded, small n=2 shell.
- If student says second EA of O is negative → MC-3 repair: +780 kJ/mol (endothermic); O²⁻ only stable in crystal lattice via lattice energy.
- Advance when student can explain both EA anomalies with distinct mechanisms, correctly interpret sign of ΔegH, and apply the Mulliken formula.

## Voice Teaching Notes

"Sign of ΔegH: negative = energy OUT = good = strong affinity. More negative = stronger. Before any EA comparison question, say it: more negative → stronger affinity."

"F vs. Cl EA anomaly: F's 2p shell is tiny and crowded. Cl's 3p is more spacious. More room → less repulsion for incoming electron → more energy released → Cl has more negative EA. NOT because F is half-filled — F has 2p⁵, which is one electron short of full, not half-filled."

## Assessment Signals

**Mastery gate**:
1. Student correctly interprets ΔegH sign (negative = exothermic = strong affinity).
2. Student explains the Group 15 anomaly (half-filled p stability, pairing repulsion) and F/Cl anomaly (compact n=2 shell, crowding) with distinct mechanisms.
3. Student correctly states that second EA of oxygen is positive (endothermic) and explains why O²⁻ exists only in ionic lattices.
4. Student applies the Mulliken formula to compare electronegativities.

**FRAGILE signal**: student correctly states both anomalies but applies the same explanation (half-filled shell) to both, revealing that the F/Cl mechanism has not been separately learned.

**MISCONCEIVING signal**: student says more negative ΔegH = weaker affinity. Address MC-1 before any trend analysis.

## Tutor Recovery Strategy

If stuck:
1. For sign of ΔegH: "Think of ΔH = enthalpy change. Negative ΔH = heat released = system loses energy = stable = FAVOURABLE process. A more negative EA means more heat released when the electron is accepted = the atom holds the electron more strongly. Cl at −349 kJ/mol releases MORE heat than F at −328 kJ/mol → Cl has a stronger pull on incoming electrons."
2. For F anomaly: "Draw F's orbital diagram: [He]2s²↑↓ 2p↑↓ ↑↓ ↑. The incoming 7th electron goes into the last 2p orbital with one already there → they repel. Now draw Cl: [Ne]3s²3p↑↓ ↑↓ ↑. Same situation, but 3p is BIGGER (higher n → larger orbital). Less crowding = less repulsion when the extra electron arrives = more energy released = more negative EA. Different from N: N has three HALF-filled p orbitals; it's the half-filled stability that causes N's low EA, not orbital size."
3. For second EA: "O has gained one electron → O⁻. O⁻ is now negatively charged. You're trying to add ANOTHER negative charge to an already-negative ion. Like charges repel. It costs energy to push the second electron in against that repulsion. Second EA is always positive (endothermic). The energy for this step comes from the ionic lattice — the huge lattice energy of MgO pays for both EAs and then some."

## Memory Hooks

- **EA trend: more negative across a period (rising Zeff); less negative down a group (higher n, more shielded).**
- **Two EA anomalies parallel IE anomalies: Group 2 (full s → next electron in p) and Group 15 (half-filled p → pairing penalty).**
- **F anomaly: Cl has MORE negative EA than F. Cause: F's compact n=2 shell is crowded; incoming electron suffers more repulsion. Cl's larger 3p accommodates it more comfortably.**
- **Second EA always positive (endothermic). O²⁻ stable only in ionic crystal lattice.**
- **Mulliken χ = (IE₁ + EA)/2. F most electronegative despite Cl having the more negative EA in its group.**

## Transfer Connections

- **chem.period.ionization-energy**: the IE and EA anomalies share the same mechanisms (half-filled stability; subshell type); mastery of IE anomalies directly transfers. IE and EA together determine Mulliken electronegativity.
- **chem.bond.ionic-bonding**: the Born-Haber cycle (lattice energy + EA + IE + atomisation enthalpy) explains why ionic compounds with doubly charged anions (O²⁻, S²⁻) are thermodynamically stable despite endothermic second EAs. EA is one term in the full cycle.

## Cross-Subject Connections

- **Physics (photoelectron spectroscopy)**: PES directly measures IE (binding energies); electron affinity is the complementary quantity measured in electron attachment spectroscopy. Together they experimentally determine orbital energy levels.
- **Materials science (work function and electron emitters)**: electron affinity in solids (not the same as atomic EA, but related) determines which materials make good electron-emitting surfaces. Low-EA materials (certain oxides) are used in vacuum tubes and electron microscopy filaments.

## Curriculum Feedback

The F < Cl anomaly is the single most frequently examined EA fact in standardised curricula and is missed by the plurality of students who know the general trend but apply it without exceptions. The Mulliken electronegativity formula is an elegant synthesis of IE and EA that many curricula teach separately — presenting it as (IE + EA)/2 makes the connection explicit and gives the F > Cl electronegativity fact a mechanistic foundation. The second EA content (O²⁻ endothermic, lattice-stabilised) is required for the Born-Haber cycle (downstream) and should not be deferred.
