## Learning Objective
Apply IUPAC rules to name cationic, anionic, and neutral coordination compounds; assign the metal oxidation state from a formula; name common ligands correctly; and write the formula of a complex from its systematic name.

## Core Understanding
**IUPAC naming rules (2013 recommendations, standard exam level)**:

**Step 1 — Name the cation before the anion** (for ionic compounds): same rule as simple ionic salts. If the complex is the cation, name it first; if it is the anion, name the simple cation first.

**Step 2 — Within the complex**:
1. LIGANDS first, METAL last.
2. Ligands listed in ALPHABETICAL ORDER (ignoring multiplying prefixes di-, tri-, bis-, tris-).
3. Anionic ligands end in **-o**: Cl⁻ → chlorido, F⁻ → fluorido, CN⁻ → cyanido, OH⁻ → hydroxido, O²⁻ → oxido, SO₄²⁻ → sulfato, CO₃²⁻ → carbonato, NO₂⁻ → nitrito-κN (N-bound) or nitrito-κO (O-bound), NO₃⁻ → nitrato, SCN⁻ → thiocyanato-κS or thiocyanato-κN depending on donor atom. Note: older names (chloro, cyano, hydroxo) still widely used and acceptable in many contexts.
4. Neutral ligands retain their molecule name with important exceptions: H₂O → aqua; NH₃ → ammine (note double-m); CO → carbonyl; NO → nitrosyl; N₂H₄ → hydrazine; PH₃ → phosphane; organic ligands use their molecular names (py = pyridine; en = ethane-1,2-diamine; bipy = 2,2′-bipyridyl).
5. Multiplying prefixes: di-, tri-, tetra-, penta-, hexa- for SIMPLE ligands (NH₃, Cl⁻, H₂O); bis-, tris-, tetrakis-, pentakis-, hexakis- for COMPLEX ligand names (ethane-1,2-diamine, triphenylphosphine) to avoid ambiguity.
6. Metal oxidation state in ROMAN NUMERALS in parentheses immediately after the metal name — no space: …iron(III)…, …copper(II)…. If oxidation state is zero: …nickel(0)…, …iron(0)….
7. For ANIONIC complexes: metal name changes to Latin root + **-ate**: iron → ferrate; copper → cuprate; silver → argentate; gold → aurate; lead → plumbate; tin → stannate; cobalt → cobaltate (Latin = Co → cobaltate); chromium → chromate (already used for CrO₄²⁻ in simple nomenclature — in coordination chemistry context, chromate = chromium(n) complex anion). Other metals use the English name + -ate: manganese → manganate; nickel → nickelate; platinum → platinate; palladium → palladate; zinc → zincate.
8. CATIONIC and NEUTRAL complexes: use the English metal name unchanged: iron, copper, cobalt, chromium, nickel.

**Step 3 — Determine metal oxidation state**: use the known charges of ligands (anionic) and counterions, plus overall charge of the complex. Example: [Co(NH₃)₅Cl]Cl₂ → overall neutral compound; complex cation = [Co(NH₃)₅Cl]²⁺ (since two Cl⁻ outside); charge from ligands: 5(0) + (−1) = −1; therefore Co + (−1) = +2 → Co is +3 (cobalt(III)).

**Bridging ligands**: prefix **μ** (mu); example: [(NH₃)₅Co-μ-OH-Co(NH₃)₅]⁴⁺ has a bridging hydroxido ligand.

**Examples (built and named)**:
- [Co(NH₃)₆]Cl₃: hexaamminecobalt(III) chloride
- [Co(NH₃)₅Cl]Cl₂: pentaamminechloridocobalt(III) chloride
- [Co(en)₃]³⁺: tris(ethane-1,2-diamine)cobalt(III)
- [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻: hexacyanidoferrate(II) ion
- [Pt(NH₃)₂Cl₂]: diamminedichloridoplatinum(II) (cisplatin, if cis isomer)
- [Cr(H₂O)₄Cl₂]Cl: tetraaquadichloridochromium(III) chloride
- [Ni(CO)₄]: tetracarbonylnickel(0)
- K₂[PtCl₄]: potassium tetrachloridoplatinate(II)
- Na₃[Fe(C₂O₄)₃]: sodium tris(oxalato)ferrate(III)

**Formula from name**:
- Identify cation and anion; write the complex in square brackets; arrange ligands around the metal in the order: anionic ligands, then neutral ligands alphabetically is one convention (formula convention differs from naming convention — in formulae, metal is listed first, then ligands in a fixed order; in older usage, cationic ligands listed first). Standard IUPAC formula: metal first within brackets, then ligands.

## Mental Models
**The naming flow as a production line**: read a complex formula like an assembly line producing words: (1) start with "counter-ion/complex-ion order"; (2) at the complex, list ligand names in alphabetical order with multiplying prefixes; (3) end with metal name + oxidation state. Every step is rule-based with no ambiguity if the formula is clear.

**Oxidation state as a charge balance problem**: the complex has a known total charge (from the counterions or stated directly). Ligand charges are known. Metal OS = total charge − sum of ligand charges. This is algebra, not memory.

## Why Students Fail
Students invent the metal's oxidation state rather than calculating it from charge balance. They confuse the alphabetisation rule with "list by number" (listing ammine before chlorido because there are 5 of the former). They use "di-/tri-" prefixes for complex ligand names (e.g. "diethylenediamine" instead of "bis(ethane-1,2-diamine)"). They forget the -ate suffix for anionic complexes and use the wrong Latin root.

## Misconceptions
- **MC-1 (Type 5 — instruction-induced)**: "List the most common ligand first, then the others." Probe: "Name [Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]⁺." Characteristic phrase: "list by how many there are." Intervention: IUPAC rules require ALPHABETICAL order, not by quantity. Alphabetical: ammine (A) before chlorido (C) → tetraamminedichloridocobalt(III). Four ammines are listed before two chloridos because "a" comes before "c" in the alphabet. Multiplying prefixes (tetra-, di-) are IGNORED for the alphabetisation: it's the ligand name itself (ammine, chlorido) that is alphabetised.
- **MC-2 (Type 3 — language contamination)**: "The -ate suffix means the compound is an oxyanion like sulfate or nitrate." Probe: "What does the suffix -ate mean in the name hexacyanidoferrate(II)?" Characteristic phrase: "-ate means it's an oxygen-containing anion." Intervention: in COORDINATION nomenclature, -ate is added to the metal name (or its Latin root) when the COMPLEX ITSELF is the anion — it signals that the complex as a whole carries negative charge. Hexacyanidoferrate(II) means [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻ — the complex is anionic. The -ate says nothing about oxygen content; ferrate is iron (ferrum) in an anionic complex. Context matters: "chromate" in simple chemistry is CrO₄²⁻ (an oxyanion); "chromate(III)" in coordination chemistry is [Cr(ligand)ₙ]³⁻.
- **MC-3 (Type 4 — notation-induced)**: "di-ethylenediamine means two ethylene groups within one ligand." Probe: "Name [Ni(en)₂Cl₂]. Does the name include 'di-' or 'bis-'?" Characteristic phrase: "diethylenediamine." Intervention: "en" is ethane-1,2-diamine (or ethylenediamine) — ONE ligand, which already has "di-" in "diamine" as part of its own name. When we have TWO of this ligand, we use BIS to avoid confusion: bis(ethane-1,2-diamine). Using "diethylenediamine" is ambiguous — it could be read as one ligand with a diethylene backbone. Bis-, tris-, tetrakis- are always used for ligands whose own names already contain a multiplying prefix or have a complex name.

## Analogies
**Valid**: naming a coordination compound is like addressing an official letter. The outer address is the counter-ion: "Potassium" or "Chloride". The inner address is the complex: you list the people inside (ligands) alphabetically, then state who's in charge (metal + oxidation state). The envelope label (complex formula in brackets) is always opened in the same way.

## Demonstrations
**Build-a-name exercise**: provide formulas of five complexes (spanning cationic/anionic/neutral, mono/polydentate ligands, different metals). Students write the systematic name step-by-step on a structured worksheet showing: oxidation state calculation → alphabetised ligand list → multiplying prefixes → metal name/suffix → counterion. The worksheet makes the algorithm explicit and visible.

## Discovery Questions
1. "Write the systematic IUPAC name for each: (a) [Cr(H₂O)₅Br]SO₄; (b) [Fe(CN)₅(NO)]²⁻; (c) [Pt(en)₂Cl₂]Cl₂; (d) K₃[Fe(C₂O₄)₃]; (e) [Ni(CO)₄]."
2. "Determine the oxidation state of the metal in each: (a) [Co(NH₃)₅NO₂]Cl₂; (b) [Fe(CN)₅NO]²⁻; (c) [Ir(CO)₃Cl₃]. For (b), NO can be NO⁺ or NO⁻ — explain how the ambiguity affects the metal's assigned OS."

## Teaching Sequence
1. Recap Werner's theory (inner/outer sphere) and complex structure from chem.coord.werner.
2. Name building blocks: ligand names (anionic -o; neutral exceptions: aqua, ammine, carbonyl, nitrosyl).
3. Alphabetical order rule; multiplying prefixes (di-/tris- distinction).
4. Metal naming: cationic/neutral complexes (English name + Roman OS); anionic complexes (Latin root + -ate + OS).
5. Oxidation state calculation by charge balance (worked examples).
6. Full names: build 5–6 examples step by step.
7. Formula from name: reverse the algorithm.

## Tutor Actions
- **If student lists ligands by number not alphabet**: write ligand names in a column, alphabetise them together, then add prefixes. Force explicit alphabetisation before combining.
- **If student gets oxidation state wrong**: set up the charge balance algebraically: (complex charge) = (metal OS) + (sum of ligand charges). Substitute what is known, solve for metal OS.
- **FRAGILE sign**: names familiar complexes correctly from memory but cannot name an unfamiliar combination or cannot write a formula from an unfamiliar name.

## Voice Teaching Notes
In voice teaching, drill the two-step algorithm explicitly: "Step 1: alphabetise the ligands. Step 2: metal + OS + -ate if negative complex." Then test with one familiar and one unfamiliar complex. The unfamiliar complex tests the algorithm vs. memory. If the student hesitates on the unfamiliar one, they were relying on memory; walk them through the algorithm again.

## Assessment Signals
- **Green**: names any coordination compound (cationic, anionic, neutral) by systematic IUPAC rules; correctly uses -ate with the Latin metal root for anionic complexes; alphabetises ligands correctly; uses bis-/tris- for complex ligand names; calculates metal OS by charge balance; writes formula from name.
- **Amber**: correct names for common familiar complexes but errors on anionic complexes (forgets Latin root) or on complex ligands (uses di- instead of bis-).
- **Red**: lists ligands by quantity rather than alphabet; uses -ate for any complex; cannot calculate metal OS from charge balance.
- **Probe**: "Name [Co(en)₂(NO₂)Cl]Cl and calculate the oxidation state of cobalt. Then write the formula for potassium hexacyanidoferrate(III)."

## Tutor Recovery Strategy
If student cannot alphabetise: write the full ligand names on separate cards or lines, sort them physically/visually, then combine. The error is usually sorting by formula (Cl, NO₂, NH₃) rather than by name (chlorido, nitrito, ammine). Make the names explicit before sorting.

## Memory Hooks
- **Ligand order**: "Alphabetical by ligand NAME (ignore di-/tri-/bis-/tris-)."
- **Anionic ligands**: "Ends in -o. Cl⁻ = chlorido. CN⁻ = cyanido. OH⁻ = hydroxido."
- **Neutral exceptions**: "H₂O = aqua. NH₃ = ammine (double-m). CO = carbonyl. NO = nitrosyl."
- **Anionic complex**: "Metal name → Latin root → -ate. Fe → ferr → ferrate. Cu → cupr → cuprate."
- **Multiplying prefix**: "Simple ligand: di-/tri-. Complex ligand name: bis-/tris-."
- **OS**: "Charge balance: complex charge = metal OS + ligand charges."

## Cross-Subject Connections
- **Pharmacology**: platinum anticancer drugs (cisplatin = cis-diamminedichloridoplatinum(II), carboplatin = diammine[cyclobutane-1,1-dicarboxylato]platinum(II)) are named by IUPAC coordination nomenclature; understanding the name allows the drug mechanism (DNA crosslinking) to be read from the ligand set.
- **Industrial catalysis**: Wilkinson's catalyst (chloridotris(triphenylphosphane)rhodium(I)) and Vaska's complex (carbonylchloridotris(triphenylphosphane)iridium(I)) are identified by their systematic names in the catalysis literature.
