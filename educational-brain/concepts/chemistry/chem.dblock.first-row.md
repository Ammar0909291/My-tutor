## Learning Objective
Describe the specific chemistry of the first-row transition elements (Sc‚ÄďZn), including their common oxidation states, the colours and formulae of their aqueous ions, the reason for variable oxidation states and the stability trends, and selected reactions of Fe, Cu, and Mn as the most commonly examined elements.

## Core Understanding
First-row transition elements: Sc (d¬Ļ), Ti (d¬≤), V (d¬≥), Cr (d‚ĀĶ anomaly), Mn (d‚ĀĶ), Fe (d‚Ā∂), Co (d‚Ā∑), Ni (d‚Āł), Cu (d¬Ļ‚Āį anomaly), Zn (d¬Ļ‚Āį). Recall that the outer electron configuration is [Ar]3d‚ĀŅ4s¬Ļ for Cr and Cu, and [Ar]3d‚ĀŅ4s¬≤ for the rest.

**Variable oxidation states** arise because the 3d and 4s levels are close in energy ‚Üí multiple OS accessible without requiring too much ionisation energy. Higher OS require more energy but can be stabilised by electronegative ligands (F‚ĀĽ, O¬≤‚ĀĽ). OS stability patterns:
- Sc: +3 only (d‚Āį after ionisation, no further d electrons to lose without extreme energy cost).
- Ti: +4 most stable (d‚Āį again); +3 reducing.
- V: +2, +3, +4, +5 (VO‚āā‚Āļ blue/VO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ pale yellow).
- Cr: +2 (Cr¬≤‚Āļ reducing), +3 (Cr¬≥‚Āļ most stable, [Cr(H‚āāO)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ green), +6 (Cr‚āāO‚āá¬≤‚ĀĽ orange, oxidising in acid).
- Mn: +2 (Mn¬≤‚Āļ pale pink, most stable in acid), +4 (MnO‚āā black solid), +7 (MnO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ purple/violet, strong oxidiser).
- Fe: +2 (Fe¬≤‚Āļ pale green, reducing), +3 (Fe¬≥‚Āļ yellow-brown, most stable in air).
- Co: +2 (Co¬≤‚Āļ pink most stable), +3 (Co¬≥‚Āļ strongly oxidising in aqueous but stabilised by strong-field ligands e.g. [Co(NH‚āÉ)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ).
- Ni: +2 (Ni¬≤‚Āļ green, most stable).
- Cu: +1 (Cu‚Āļ unstable in water ‚ÄĒ disproportionates: 2Cu‚Āļ ‚Üí Cu + Cu¬≤‚Āļ; stable in insoluble compounds CuI, Cu‚āāO, or coordinated to soft ligands), +2 (Cu¬≤‚Āļ blue, most common in aqueous).
- Zn: +2 only (d¬Ļ‚Āį, closed d shell, no d‚Äďd transitions, colourless, not a transition element by strict definition).

**Aqueous ion colours** from d‚Äďd electronic transitions (d‚Āį and d¬Ļ‚Āį give colourless ions):
- Ti¬≥‚Āļ: purple; V¬≤‚Āļ: violet, V¬≥‚Āļ: green; Cr¬≤‚Āļ: blue, Cr¬≥‚Āļ: green; Mn¬≤‚Āļ: pale pink (very weak ‚ÄĒ d‚ĀĶ high-spin transitions spin-forbidden); Fe¬≤‚Āļ: pale green, Fe¬≥‚Āļ: yellow-brown; Co¬≤‚Āļ: pink; Ni¬≤‚Āļ: green; Cu¬≤‚Āļ: blue; Zn¬≤‚Āļ: colourless.

**Iron chemistry in detail**:
- Fe¬≤‚Āļ/Fe¬≥‚Āļ interconversion: 2Fe¬≥‚Āļ + Fe ‚Üí 3Fe¬≤‚Āļ (disproportionation type); Fe¬≤‚Āļ oxidised to Fe¬≥‚Āļ in air; Fe¬≥‚Āļ reduced to Fe¬≤‚Āļ by I‚ĀĽ (2Fe¬≥‚Āļ + 2I‚ĀĽ ‚Üí 2Fe¬≤‚Āļ + I‚āā).
- [Fe(SCN)]¬≤‚Āļ: blood-red complex ‚ÄĒ the standard test for Fe¬≥‚Āļ (add SCN‚ĀĽ ‚Üí deep red ‚Üí Fe¬≥‚Āļ present).
- [Fe(H‚āāO)‚āÜ]¬≤‚Āļ (pale green) vs. [Fe(H‚āāO)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ (yellow-brown).
- Fe¬≥‚Āļ hydrolyses in water: [Fe(H‚āāO)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ ‚Üí [Fe(OH)(H‚āāO)‚āÖ]¬≤‚Āļ + H‚Āļ (rusty-brown precipitate on addition of NaOH to Fe¬≥‚Āļ).

**Copper chemistry in detail**:
- [Cu(H‚āāO)‚āĄ]¬≤‚Āļ (blue) + excess NH‚āÉ ‚Üí [Cu(NH‚āÉ)‚āĄ(H‚āāO)‚āā]¬≤‚Āļ (deep royal blue ‚ÄĒ "Schweizer's reagent," can dissolve cellulose).
- Cu¬≤‚Āļ + 2OH‚ĀĽ ‚Üí Cu(OH)‚āā (blue-green precipitate); Cu(OH)‚āā + excess NaOH ‚Üí no reaction (not amphoteric, unlike Al(OH)‚āÉ).
- Cu¬≤‚Āļ + I‚ĀĽ: 2Cu¬≤‚Āļ + 4I‚ĀĽ ‚Üí 2CuI‚Üď (white) + I‚āā (brown) ‚ÄĒ Cu¬≤‚Āļ oxidises I‚ĀĽ to I‚āā and is itself reduced to Cu‚Āļ (CuI insoluble).
- Cu disproportionation: 2Cu‚Āļ(aq) ‚Üí Cu(s) + Cu¬≤‚Āļ(aq) ‚ÄĒ thermodynamically spontaneous in water (confirmed by E¬į values); Cu‚Āļ is stable only in insoluble or coordinated form.

**Manganese chemistry in detail**:
- MnO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ (purple) in acid ‚Üí Mn¬≤‚Āļ (almost colourless, pale pink) ‚ÄĒ the colour change in KMnO‚āĄ titrations.
- MnO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ in neutral/basic ‚Üí MnO‚āā (brown/black precipitate).
- MnO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ reduction by H‚āāO‚āā (in acid): 2MnO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ + 5H‚āāO‚āā + 6H‚Āļ ‚Üí 2Mn¬≤‚Āļ + 5O‚āā + 8H‚āāO ‚ÄĒ both are oxidised and reduced? No: MnO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ is reduced (Mn +7‚Üí+2); H‚āāO‚āā is oxidised (O ‚ąí1‚Üí0) to O‚āā.
- MnO‚āā + hot conc H‚āāSO‚āĄ + KI: MnO‚āā oxidises I‚ĀĽ to I‚āā.
- Manganate(VI) MnO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ (green) disproportionates: 3MnO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ + 4H‚Āļ ‚Üí 2MnO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ + MnO‚āā + 2H‚āāO.

**Chromium chemistry**:
- [Cr(H‚āāO)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ (green, Cr¬≥‚Āļ): Cr(OH)‚āÉ is amphoteric ‚ÄĒ dissolves in both acid and base (‚Üí [Cr(OH)‚āĄ]‚ĀĽ or chromite in excess NaOH).
- Cr‚āāO‚āá¬≤‚ĀĽ (orange) ‚áĆ CrO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ (yellow) equilibrium in acid/base: add acid ‚Üí Cr‚āāO‚āá¬≤‚ĀĽ; add base ‚Üí CrO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ.
- K‚āāCr‚āāO‚āá titrations (acidified, with FeSO‚āĄ, Fe¬≤‚Āļ ‚Üí Fe¬≥‚Āļ; Cr‚āāO‚āá¬≤‚ĀĽ ‚Üí Cr¬≥‚Āļ): colour endpoint orange ‚Üí green; use diphenylamine sulphonate indicator.

## Mental Models
**The d-electron bank account**: each d electron is a "resource" the transition metal can spend (lose) to reach a higher oxidation state, or keep (keep d electrons) to remain at a lower OS. The "value" of spending depends on what stabilises the higher OS (electronegative ligands, lattice energy). Mn can spend up to 7 electrons (all 3d + 4s = 7) to reach Mn(VII) in permanganate ‚ÄĒ but only when the gain from Mn‚ÄďO bonds compensates the ionisation cost.

**The colour wheel**: transition metal complex colour is the complement of the absorbed colour. [Cu(H‚āāO)‚āĄ]¬≤‚Āļ absorbs orange-red light (d‚Äďd transition) ‚Üí transmits blue. [Ti(H‚āāO)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ absorbs green-yellow light ‚Üí transmits purple. The absorbed wavelength tells you the d‚Äďd splitting energy; the transmitted colour is what you observe.

## Why Students Fail
Students memorise colours without connecting them to d‚Äďd transitions and fail to explain why Sc¬≥‚Āļ and Zn¬≤‚Āļ are colourless (d‚Āį and d¬Ļ‚Āį ‚ÄĒ no d‚Äďd transition possible).

Students confuse OS of Mn in MnO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ (+7) with the coordination number or ligand count ‚ÄĒ they are independent.

## Misconceptions
- **MC-1 (Type 1 ‚ÄĒ overgeneralization)**: "All transition metal ions are coloured." Probe: "What colour is a solution of Sc¬≥‚Āļ or Zn¬≤‚Āļ?" Characteristic phrase: "transition metals are always coloured." Intervention: colour requires a d‚Äďd transition. Sc¬≥‚Āļ is d‚Āį (no d electrons at all ‚Üí no transition), Zn¬≤‚Āļ is d¬Ļ‚Āį (all d levels filled ‚Üí no vacancy for excitation). Both are colourless. Also Ti‚Āī‚Āļ (d‚Āį) is colourless. The rule is: d‚Äďd transition needs at least one electron AND at least one vacancy in the d subshell.
- **MC-2 (Type 5 ‚ÄĒ instruction-induced)**: "Cu‚Āļ is more stable than Cu¬≤‚Āļ in aqueous solution because Cu has a filled d¬Ļ‚Āį configuration." Probe: "What happens when you dissolve CuI in water?" Characteristic phrase: "d¬Ļ‚Āį means Cu‚Āļ is stable." Intervention: the d¬Ļ‚Āį stability argument applies to isolated atoms/ions, but in WATER the Cu‚Āļ(aq) ion disproportionates spontaneously to Cu(s) + Cu¬≤‚Āļ(aq) because the hydration enthalpy of Cu¬≤‚Āļ (much higher, charge¬≤ effect) more than compensates for the extra ionisation energy. Cu‚Āļ is stable only in INSOLUBLE salts (CuI, Cu‚āāO, Cu‚āāS) or coordinated to soft ligands in non-aqueous environments.
- **MC-3 (Type 4 ‚ÄĒ notation-induced)**: "Fe¬≤‚Āļ has configuration [Ar]3d‚Āī because Fe is [Ar]3d‚Ā∂4s¬≤ and you remove 2 electrons from 3d." Probe: "Write the electron configuration of Fe¬≤‚Āļ." Characteristic phrase: "remove from 3d first." Intervention: IONISATION removes electrons from 4s FIRST (even though 4s filled before 3d in Aufbau). Fe: [Ar]3d‚Ā∂4s¬≤ ‚Üí Fe¬≤‚Āļ: [Ar]3d‚Ā∂ (remove 2 from 4s); Fe¬≥‚Āļ: [Ar]3d‚ĀĶ (remove 3rd from 3d). This is a known inconsistency in the Aufbau model ‚ÄĒ 4s is the outermost orbital energetically in the atom, so it loses electrons first on ionisation.

## Analogies
**Valid**: The d-electron configuration as a staircase ‚ÄĒ going from d‚Āį (Sc¬≥‚Āļ, top step) to d¬Ļ‚Āį (Zn¬≤‚Āļ, bottom step), each element occupies a distinct step. Colour requires "jumping between steps" (d‚Äďd transition). If there are no steps above (d‚Āį) or below (d¬Ļ‚Āį), no jump is possible ‚Üí colourless. Elements in the middle of the staircase (d¬≥‚Äďd‚Āł) have the richest colour chemistry because many transitions are possible.

## Demonstrations
**Dichromate‚Äďchromate equilibrium**: prepare K‚āāCr‚āāO‚āá solution (orange), add dilute NaOH ‚Üí solution turns yellow (CrO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ); add dilute HCl ‚Üí turns orange again (Cr‚āāO‚āá¬≤‚ĀĽ). The reversible colour change directly demonstrates the pH-dependent equilibrium and the OS of Cr (+6 in both) with changed anion aggregation.

**KMnO‚āĄ titration colour change**: add acidified KMnO‚āĄ (purple) from burette to Fe¬≤‚Āļ solution ‚ÄĒ purple disappears instantly as each drop is added (Mn‚Ā∑‚Āļ reduced to Mn¬≤‚Āļ, colourless), then at the endpoint the first permanent pink. The dramatic purple‚Üícolourless change makes this the most visually striking redox titration.

## Tutor Actions
- **If student claims Sc or Zn ions are coloured**: ask "what electron configuration does Sc¬≥‚Āļ/Zn¬≤‚Āļ have?" (d‚Āį/d¬Ļ‚Āį); "what is needed for a d‚Äďd transition?" (at least one electron AND at least one vacancy); "does d‚Āį/d¬Ļ‚Āį satisfy this?" (no); conclude colourless.
- **If student writes Fe¬≤‚Āļ as [Ar]3d‚Āī**: ask "which electrons are the outermost (highest energy) in the neutral Fe atom?" (4s); "which do you remove first in ionisation?" (4s first, then 3d if needed); Fe¬≤‚Āļ = [Ar]3d‚Ā∂ (both 4s removed), Fe¬≥‚Āļ = [Ar]3d‚ĀĶ.
- **FRAGILE sign**: lists oxidation states correctly but cannot predict the colour of a given ion's aqueous solution, or cannot write ionic equations for Fe¬≤‚Āļ/Fe¬≥‚Āļ interconversions.

## Voice Teaching Notes
First-row transition chemistry is often assessed through colour-change observations and ionic equations. Make the colour table a core memory deliverable ‚ÄĒ require students to recite ion colours for Fe, Cu, Mn, Cr in each OS without notes. The visual richness of these reactions (MnO‚āĄ‚ĀĽ purple ‚Üí Mn¬≤‚Āļ colourless, Cu¬≤‚Āļ blue ‚Üí [Cu(NH‚āÉ)‚āĄ]¬≤‚Āļ deep blue, Cr‚āāO‚āá¬≤‚ĀĽ orange ‚áĆ CrO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ yellow) makes them memorable once seen.

## Tutor Recovery Strategy
If student cannot remember which OS is most stable for Fe, Mn, Cr: teach the one-rule short-cut ‚ÄĒ "the mid-OS is usually the most stable for first-row elements (Fe¬≥‚Āļ for iron, Mn¬≤‚Āļ in acid, Cr¬≥‚Āļ for chromium)." Then qualify: higher OS (Mn‚Ā∑‚Āļ, Cr‚Ā∂‚Āļ, Fe‚Ā∂‚Āļ) are strong oxidising agents and unstable; lower OS (Mn¬≤‚Āļ, Fe¬≤‚Āļ, Cr¬≤‚Āļ) are reducing. Work from the "most stable mid-OS" as the anchor and classify everything as "more oxidising" above or "more reducing" below.
