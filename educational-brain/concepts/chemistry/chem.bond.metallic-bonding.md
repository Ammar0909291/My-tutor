## Learning Objective

Students can describe the electron-sea model of metallic bonding and explain how delocalised electrons account for the characteristic properties of metals: electrical and thermal conductivity, malleability and ductility, metallic lustre, and high melting and boiling points. Students can also give a qualitative account of band theory (overlapping orbitals → conduction and valence bands) and explain why band theory supersedes the electron-sea model for quantitative prediction.

## Core Understanding

**Electron-sea model (Drude model)**:
Metal atoms lose their valence electrons to form a lattice of positively charged metal cations (kernels) surrounded by a delocalised "sea" of mobile electrons shared by the entire crystal. The metallic bond is the electrostatic attraction between the cation lattice and the electron sea. It is non-directional (unlike covalent bonds) and non-localised (unlike ionic bonds) — electrons are free to move through the whole metal.

**Properties explained by the electron-sea model**:

*Electrical conductivity*: mobile electrons carry charge when a potential difference is applied. No bond-breaking required; electrons flow freely through the lattice.

*Thermal conductivity*: mobile electrons transmit kinetic energy rapidly (much faster than lattice vibration alone). Metals are better thermal conductors than non-metals for this reason.

*Malleability and ductility*: when the lattice is deformed (struck or drawn), cation layers slide past each other while remaining surrounded by the electron sea. No specific bond angles or directions are broken — the sea adjusts. This contrasts with ionic crystals (layer displacement brings like-charges together → electrostatic repulsion → brittle fracture) and covalent network solids (directional bonds must be broken → fracture).

*Metallic lustre*: mobile electrons interact with and re-emit visible light of all wavelengths → metals appear shiny and reflective.

*High melting and boiling points*: the electrostatic attraction between the electron sea and the cation lattice is strong. More valence electrons and/or higher charge density on the cation → stronger metallic bond → higher melting point. Example: Na (one valence electron, low charge density) vs. Fe (multiple d-electrons contributing, high charge density) — iron has a much higher melting point (1538°C vs 98°C).

**Factors affecting metallic bond strength**:
- Number of delocalised electrons per atom (Group 1 < Group 2 < d-block metals).
- Charge and size of the cation (smaller, higher-charge cation → stronger attraction).
Consequence: Group 1 metals are softer and have lower melting points than Group 2 or transition metals.

**Band theory (qualitative)**:
When N metal atoms come together, their atomic orbitals overlap to form N molecular orbitals that are so closely spaced they form a continuous band of allowed energies. For metals:
- The *valence band* (from valence orbitals) is only partially filled (e.g., the 3s band of Na holds 2N electrons but Na contributes only N electrons → half-full).
- The *conduction band* (the next available band) either overlaps the valence band (Na, Cu) or lies close enough that electrons can thermally access it (Mg: 3s and 3p bands overlap).
- Electrons at the Fermi level (top of the occupied states at 0 K) can move into the vast number of unoccupied states in the same band or the conduction band with very little energy → metallic conduction.
- Insulators: a large band gap separates the full valence band from the empty conduction band (electrons cannot cross).
- Semiconductors: a small band gap; thermal energy at room temperature promotes a small fraction of electrons into the conduction band.

Band theory explains why conductivity DECREASES with rising temperature for metals (more lattice vibrations scatter mobile electrons) but INCREASES with rising temperature for semiconductors (more electrons thermally promoted across the small gap).

## Mental Models

**The ocean-and-buoys model**: the metal cations are buoys anchored in the ocean (the electron sea). The ocean itself is what holds the buoys in place — not rigid cables between pairs of buoys. You can rearrange buoys (deform the metal) and the ocean adjusts without the structure collapsing. This captures both the non-directionality and the malleability explanation.

**The stadium wave analogy for band theory**: in an empty stadium (no electrons), there are many seats (orbital states). A partial crowd (half-filled band) can easily shift — one person stands, a ripple of shifting people travels around the stadium instantly. A completely full stadium (no empty seats) can't shift at all (insulator). A stadium with just a few empty seats near the top tier (small gap, semiconductor) can shift if people jump from their seats.

## Why Students Fail

1. **Metallic bonding is "just" covalent bonding of the whole crystal**: students see "electron sharing" and assimilate it into covalent bonding. The key distinction — complete delocalisation across the whole crystal vs. localised sharing between two atoms — is not emphasised.
2. **Conductivity disappears on melting**: students assume that melting destroys the electron sea. Liquid metals remain conductors because the electrons remain delocalised — the cation lattice is gone but the mobile electrons persist.
3. **Harder metal = stronger metallic bond**: hardness and bond strength are related but not identical. Diamond is harder than any metal yet is not metallic. Hardness also depends on crystal structure and lattice geometry.

## Misconceptions

**MC-1 — Metallic bonding is a form of covalent bonding** (Type 3, language contamination: "electron sharing" language applied to both)
- *Mechanism*: both models involve electrons shared between nuclei; students collapse the distinction.
- *Diagnostic probe*: "How does metallic bonding differ from covalent bonding? Why can metals be hammered flat but covalent network solids like diamond shatter?"
- *Characteristic phrase*: "Metallic bonding is covalent bonding across the whole metal crystal."
- *Repair*: covalent bonds are directional and localised between specific pairs of atoms; stretching or re-angling them breaks them → brittle. Metallic bonds are non-directional; electrons are not between specific pairs — they float everywhere. When the lattice deforms, the sea re-establishes everywhere, no bonds are broken → malleable.

**MC-2 — Metals lose conductivity when melted** (Type 1, overgeneralization: "ionic compound loses conductivity when solid, gains it on melting" applied backwards to metals)
- *Mechanism*: students learn ionic solids don't conduct (fixed ions) but molten ionic compounds do (free ions). They apply this pattern: solid metal → fixed electrons → no conduction, which is exactly wrong.
- *Diagnostic probe*: "Liquid mercury at room temperature is used in electrical switches. Why can it conduct electricity?"
- *Characteristic phrase*: "When sodium melts, the electron sea collapses so it stops conducting."
- *Repair*: metals conduct because of MOBILE ELECTRONS, not mobile ions. Melting breaks the cation lattice but the electrons remain mobile in the liquid state. Liquid metals remain excellent conductors. Mercury is liquid at room temperature yet highly conductive.

**MC-3 — More valence electrons always means stronger metallic bond** (Type 1, overgeneralization of the "more electrons = stronger" heuristic)
- *Mechanism*: students correctly learn that more delocalised electrons → stronger bond → higher mp. They then predict: Group 1 < Group 2 < Group 3… for ALL properties. But the trend is not monotonic across all groups (transition metal d-electrons contribute variably; other factors like atomic radius and crystal structure matter).
- *Diagnostic probe*: "Predict the relative melting points of Na, Mg, and Al. Is the trend the same for all metals across period 3?"
- *Characteristic phrase*: "Al always has stronger metallic bonding than Fe because Al has 3 valence electrons and Fe has 2 [counting only 4s²]."
- *Repair*: for period 3 s/p metals, Na < Mg < Al is roughly correct (1, 2, 3 delocalised electrons, similar radii). But transition metals contribute d-electrons to the sea as well: Fe has 2 (4s) + 6 (3d) = up to 8 electrons, giving Fe a much higher mp (1538°C) than Al (660°C). Simple valence-electron counting fails for d-block metals.

## Analogies

**The ball-bearing jar analogy for malleability**: fill a jar with ball bearings (the cations) and pour in water (the electron sea). Shake the jar — the ball bearings rearrange but remain in contact; they don't shatter. Now imagine a jar of interlocking rigid rods (ionic crystal): shaking them brings like-charged rods together → they repel → the arrangement flies apart. The ball bearings + water jar is the metallic crystal.

**The highway analogy for conductivity**: metallic conduction is an open highway with no toll booths — electrons flow freely from entry to exit. Ionic/aqueous conduction is a ferryboat system — ions move, but only when dissolved or melted (toll booths lifted). Covalent molecular conduction: no highway at all.

## Demonstrations

**Demonstration 1 — Malleability and ductility of lead vs. brittleness of ionic NaCl**
- Hammer a small piece of lead sheet between two metal blocks: it flattens without cracking. Attempt the same with a NaCl crystal: it cleaves along crystal planes and shatters. The contrast is immediate and dramatic. Ask students to connect the observed difference to the structural model: electron sea (non-directional, adjusts) vs. ionic lattice (layer displacement brings like charges together → electrostatic repulsion → fracture).

## Discovery Questions

1. "Explain why sodium (Group 1, period 3) has a much lower melting point (98°C) than magnesium (Group 2, period 3, 650°C) using the electron-sea model." (Targets: Mg has 2 delocalised electrons per atom vs. Na's 1. Mg also has a smaller, more highly charged cation (Mg²⁺ vs Na⁺) for a similar atomic radius. Both effects increase the electrostatic attraction between the electron sea and the cation lattice → stronger metallic bond → higher melting point for Mg.)
2. "Band theory distinguishes metals, insulators, and semiconductors. Describe in qualitative terms (a) what makes copper a metal and (b) why a semiconductor's conductivity increases with temperature while a metal's decreases." (Targets: (a) Cu has a 4s band that is only half-filled (Cu has 4s¹); the valence and conduction bands overlap — electrons require negligible energy to move to unoccupied states → free electron flow. (b) Metal: rising T increases lattice vibrations → electrons scattered more → conductivity decreases. Semiconductor: rising T promotes more electrons across the small band gap into the conduction band → more charge carriers → conductivity increases. The two effects are opposite because the dominant variable differs.)
3. "Arrange in order of increasing melting point: Na, Fe, NaCl, I₂. Justify each position." (Targets: I₂ (molecular crystal, weak London dispersion forces, ~114°C) < Na (metallic, 1 valence e⁻, 98°C — actually I₂ and Na are close; I₂ melts at 114°C, so Na < I₂) — actually Na (98°C) < I₂ (114°C) < NaCl (801°C, strong ionic lattice) < Fe (1538°C, strong metallic bond with d-electrons). This ranking forces students to compare bond types.)

## Teaching Sequence

1. Review from chem.period.modern-periodic-law: block structure, valence electron count, metallic character trend.
2. Introduce the electron-sea model. Contrast: covalent (localised, directional) vs. metallic (delocalised, non-directional). Address MC-1.
3. Derive each property (conductivity, malleability, lustre, high mp) from the model. Address MC-2 (conductivity on melting). Address MC-3 (period 3 trend vs. d-block complexity).
4. Work Discovery Question 1 (Na vs. Mg melting point).
5. Introduce band theory qualitatively. Map onto conductors/insulators/semiconductors. Work Discovery Question 2.
6. Work Discovery Question 3 (mixed bond type comparison).

## Tutor Actions

- If student says metallic bonding = covalent → MC-1 repair: directionality and localisation are the distinguishing features; malleability vs. brittleness is the observable consequence.
- If student says liquid metals don't conduct → MC-2 repair: melting breaks the lattice, not the electron sea; mobile electrons persist in the liquid state.
- If student over-extends the "more valence electrons → stronger bond" heuristic to d-block metals incorrectly → MC-3 repair: d-electrons also contribute; transition metals generally have stronger bonds than their period-3 s/p counterparts.
- Advance when student can explain all four metallic properties from the electron-sea model and can qualitatively compare conductors, insulators, and semiconductors using band theory.

## Voice Teaching Notes

"Before any metallic property question: electron sea, non-directional, non-localised. Those three words. Malleability → non-directional, sea adjusts. Conductivity → electrons are already mobile."

"Band theory: half-empty band = metal. Empty conduction band + small gap = semiconductor. Empty conduction band + big gap = insulator. Draw three boxes, label them."

## Assessment Signals

**Mastery gate**:
1. Student correctly describes the electron-sea model and the non-directional nature of the bond.
2. Student correctly explains malleability, conductivity, lustre, and high melting point in terms of the model.
3. Student correctly predicts the qualitative band structure for a metal vs. insulator vs. semiconductor.
4. Student correctly identifies that liquid metals remain conductors.

**FRAGILE signal**: student explains malleability correctly but cannot apply the model to explain why ionic crystals are brittle (no transfer to the contrast case — understanding is surface-level, not structural).

**MISCONCEIVING signal**: student says "metallic bonding is electron sharing, like covalent bonds." Address MC-1 with the malleability vs. brittleness contrast before any property questions.

## Tutor Recovery Strategy

If stuck:
1. For malleability: "Draw two layers of cations. The electron sea fills the space between them. Push one layer sideways relative to the other. Do the electrons flow to the new positions? Yes — the sea adjusts. Now draw two ionic layers and push: the + row meets the − row of the other side, then meets the + row. Repulsion. Crack."
2. For conductivity on melting: "What carries charge in metals? Electrons, not cations. When you melt the metal, the cation lattice collapses into a liquid — but the electrons don't go anywhere. They're still free. The highway is still open."
3. For band theory: "In a single Na atom the 3s orbital holds up to 2 electrons. In a crystal of N Na atoms, those N orbitals overlap to form N molecular orbitals — a 3s BAND. Na has 1 electron per atom → N electrons fill half of the N orbitals (the lower half). The top half of the band is empty. Any electron near the top can jump to an adjacent empty orbital with basically no energy cost. That's metallic conductivity."

## Memory Hooks

- **Electron sea: delocalised, non-directional, mobile → conductor, malleable, lustrous, high mp.**
- **More valence electrons + smaller, higher-charge cation → stronger metallic bond → higher mp. Group 1 < Group 2 < d-block (roughly).**
- **Malleability vs. brittleness: electron sea adjusts (metal) vs. layer displacement gives like-charge contact (ionic crystal → fracture).**
- **Liquid metals still conduct: melting breaks the lattice, NOT the mobile electrons.**
- **Band theory: partial (half-filled) or overlapping bands → metal. Large gap → insulator. Small gap → semiconductor (conductivity increases with T).**

## Cross-Subject Connections

- **Physics (solid-state electronics)**: band theory is the foundation of semiconductor physics — the same half-filled vs. gapped band distinction explains transistors, solar cells, and LEDs. The Fermi level concept introduced here is central to all of solid-state device physics.
- **Materials science / engineering**: alloy design exploits metallic bonding — replacing some lattice positions with different-size atoms disrupts cation-layer sliding, increasing hardness (solid-solution strengthening). Understanding why pure metals are ductile and alloys are harder requires this node.
