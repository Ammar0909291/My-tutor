## Learning Objective
Explain coordinate bond (dative covalent bond) formation as the donation of a lone pair from a Lewis base to a Lewis acid, identify Lewis acids and Lewis bases in a given reaction, and represent coordinate bonds in dot-cross diagrams and structural formulas.

## Core Understanding
A coordinate bond (dative covalent bond) is a covalent bond in which BOTH electrons in the shared pair come from ONE atom. The atom that donates the lone pair is the Lewis base (electron pair donor); the atom that accepts it is the Lewis acid (electron pair acceptor). Once formed, a coordinate bond is IDENTICAL in every property to any other covalent bond formed by equal sharing — the distinction is only in origin, not in nature.

**Lewis acid-base definition** (broader than Brønsted-Lowry):
- Lewis base: any species with a lone pair to donate (NH₃, H₂O, F⁻, Cl⁻, CN⁻, CO, PR₃, etc.)
- Lewis acid: any species with a vacant orbital to accept a lone pair (BF₃, AlCl₃, Fe³⁺, BCl₃, all metal cations, H⁺)

**Key examples**:
1. **NH₄⁺ formation**: NH₃ (Lewis base, lone pair on N) donates to H⁺ (Lewis acid, vacant 1s). The resulting N–H bond is a coordinate bond; once formed, all four N–H bonds in NH₄⁺ are equivalent.
2. **BF₃·NH₃ adduct**: BF₃ (Lewis acid, empty p orbital on B) accepts from NH₃ (Lewis base). B becomes tetrahedral. The B–N bond is coordinate.
3. **[Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺ complex**: six H₂O molecules each donate a lone pair from O to the vacant d orbitals of Cu²⁺. All six Cu–O bonds are coordinate bonds. This is the foundation of coordination chemistry.
4. **CO as a ligand**: CO donates a lone pair from C to a metal centre (σ donation), and the metal back-donates into the CO π* orbital (π back-donation) — a more advanced example used in organometallic chemistry (chem.dblock.organometallics).

**Notation**: in structural diagrams, a coordinate bond is sometimes shown as an arrow (→) pointing from donor to acceptor; in fully elaborated dot-cross or line structures, the arrow distinguishes the donation direction, though the bond itself is identical to any covalent bond.

**Oxidation state bookkeeping with coordinate bonds**: when assigning formal charges or OS, the lone pair is assigned back to the donor (Lewis base) by convention. Example: in NH₄⁺ each H has OS +1, N has OS −3 (same as in NH₃); the coordinate bond does not change the OS assignment rule.

## Mental Models
**The gift of electrons model**: in an ordinary covalent bond, each atom contributes one electron to the shared pair (a joint enterprise). In a coordinate bond, one atom brings both electrons (a gift). Once the gift is accepted, the recipient and the donor are indistinguishable to an outside observer — the bond looks the same.

**The socket and plug model**: the Lewis acid is a socket (vacant orbital, ready to receive); the Lewis base is a plug carrying two electrons. The socket doesn't care where the plug came from once the connection is made; the electrical function (the bond) is the same.

## Why Students Fail
Students believe coordinate bonds are weaker than ordinary covalent bonds because "one atom did all the work." The misconception arises from informal language about "unequal sharing." Once formed, coordinate and ordinary covalent bonds have the same properties and strengths.

Students fail to identify Lewis acids when no proton exchange occurs — they look for H⁺ donors/acceptors (Brønsted-Lowry) and miss examples like BF₃ or Fe³⁺.

## Misconceptions
- **MC-1 (Type 5 — instruction-induced)**: "Coordinate bonds are weaker than ordinary covalent bonds because only one atom supplied the electrons." Probe: "In NH₄⁺, which N–H bond is the coordinate bond and is it weaker than the other three?" Characteristic phrase: "the arrow bond is weaker." Intervention: all four N–H bonds in NH₄⁺ are experimentally identical in length, strength, and all other properties; the coordinate bond origin is a historical labelling convention, not a physical difference in the final bond.
- **MC-2 (Type 3 — language contamination)**: "Lewis acids are substances that donate electrons." Probe: "Is BF₃ a Lewis acid or a Lewis base?" Characteristic phrase: "acids give electrons." Intervention: Lewis acids ACCEPT electrons (they have a vacancy); Lewis bases DONATE electrons. The Arrhenius/Brønsted acid model (H⁺ donor) is a SUBSET of Lewis acid behaviour (the H⁺ ion accepts a lone pair from the base); but not all Lewis acids are proton donors — BF₃, AlCl₃, and metal cations are Lewis acids with no exchangeable proton.
- **MC-3 (Type 1 — overgeneralization)**: "Every reaction involving lone pairs forms coordinate bonds." Probe: "Is the reaction HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O a coordinate bond formation?" Intervention: coordinate bonds form specifically when a Lewis base donates a lone pair to a Lewis acid to form a NEW BOND — creating an adduct or complex; acid-base neutralisation where ions exchange without forming a new discrete bond is not the same thing.

## Analogies
**Valid**: Two people jointly buying a car (ordinary covalent bond: one electron each). One person buying the car alone and sharing it with a partner who didn't contribute (coordinate bond). Once the car is in use, no one can tell who paid — the partnership is identical.

**Anti-analogy**: Do NOT say "coordinate bonds involve unequal electron sharing" — sharing refers to the electron distribution in the final bond (which can still be polar due to electronegativity differences, as in any covalent bond), not to the origin of the electrons.

## Demonstrations
**Universal indicator test**: dissolve BF₃ etherate in water; the solution is acidic despite BF₃ having no proton to donate. This demonstrates Lewis acidity (BF₃ accepts a lone pair from H₂O, generating H₃O⁺ indirectly). Directly shows Lewis acid ≠ proton donor.

**Colour change on complex formation**: add a small amount of CuSO₄ solution (pale blue) to concentrated ammonia solution; the deep blue [Cu(NH₃)₄(H₂O)₂]²⁺ complex forms, demonstrating NH₃ acting as a Lewis base donating to Cu²⁺. The dramatic colour change is visually compelling.

## Discovery Questions
1. "AlCl₃ is used as a catalyst in Friedel-Crafts reactions. Using Lewis acid-base theory, explain what property of AlCl₃ makes it a suitable catalyst."
2. "In the reaction between SO₃ and H₂O to form H₂SO₄, identify the Lewis acid and the Lewis base, and describe which bond is the coordinate bond."
3. "CO is a better ligand than N₂ for most transition metals, despite N₂ also having a lone pair. What additional bonding interaction makes CO a superior ligand?"

## Teaching Sequence
1. **Review ordinary covalent bond**: each atom contributes one electron; recap from chem.bond.covalent-bonding.
2. **Introduce coordinate bond from lone-pair donation**: use NH₃ + H⁺ → NH₄⁺ as the first example; draw the dot-cross diagram before and after; show that all four N–H bonds become identical.
3. **Define Lewis acid and Lewis base**: contrast with Brønsted-Lowry; show BF₃ + NH₃ as an example where no proton is involved.
4. **Metal complexes as coordinate compound examples**: briefly introduce [Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺; each H₂O donates a lone pair from O to Cu²⁺; all six bonds are coordinate; this previews chem.coord.werner.
5. **Formal charge and OS**: confirm that the coordinate bond origin does not change OS assignment; NH₄⁺: OS(N) = −3, OS(H) = +1 — same as NH₃.

## Tutor Actions
- **If student says coordinate bonds are weaker**: give the NH₄⁺ experiment (all four N–H bonds measured identically by spectroscopy); ask "which N–H would you cut to break the coordinate bond — they all look the same."
- **If student identifies Lewis acids only when H⁺ is present**: present BF₃/NH₃ and ask "which has a vacant orbital?" — the vacant orbital criterion is the general Lewis acid test.
- **FRAGILE sign**: identifies Lewis acid/base correctly for taught examples but fails to identify metal cations as Lewis acids in novel examples.

## Voice Teaching Notes
The arrow notation (→) for coordinate bonds is context-dependent — some textbooks use it, others use a standard line once the bond is formed; acknowledge both conventions and clarify which the student's curriculum uses.

"Lewis acid accepts electrons" is counter-intuitive because acids are traditionally proton-donors; let students articulate the Brønsted-Lowry definition first, then show the Lewis definition as a generalisation.

## Assessment Signals
- **Green**: correctly identifies Lewis acid and base in novel examples (including metal cation complexes); draws NH₄⁺ and BF₃·NH₃ with correct dot-cross structures; explains why all NH₄⁺ bonds are equivalent.
- **Amber**: correct for examples involving H⁺ but cannot apply Lewis acid definition to metal cations or B compounds.
- **Red**: claims coordinate bonds are weaker; confuses Lewis acid with Lewis base.
- **Probe**: "In the formation of [Fe(CN)₆]³⁻: (a) which species is the Lewis acid and which is the Lewis base? (b) how many coordinate bonds are formed? (c) describe the lone pair donation using the Lewis model."

## Tutor Recovery Strategy
If student cannot identify Lewis acids without H⁺: begin with the vacancy criterion — "what does a Lewis acid need structurally?" (an empty orbital to receive electrons). Ask "does BF₃ have an empty orbital?" (yes, empty 2p on B after bonding). Build from structural feature to functional definition. Once vacant-orbital = Lewis acid is secure, extend to metal cations (metal ions always have vacant d or s orbitals).

## Memory Hooks
- **Lewis definition**: "Lewis base = lone pair DONOR (Bases are givers). Lewis acid = lone pair ACCEPTOR (Acids are takers)." The alliteration D-D (Donor/Donate) for base and A-A (Acceptor/Accept) for acid.
- **Coordinate bond origin**: "Both electrons from ONE atom — but once formed, the bond is no different from any other."

## Transfer Connections
- **chem.coord.werner**: Werner's coordination compounds are entirely built on coordinate (dative) bonds between metal ions (Lewis acids) and ligands (Lewis bases); this node is the necessary conceptual prerequisite.

## Cross-Subject Connections
- **Biology (haemoglobin oxygen binding)**: O₂ (Lewis base via lone pairs) coordinates to Fe²⁺ in haem (Lewis acid); CO poisoning works by CO forming a stronger coordinate bond to the same Fe²⁺ site (CO is a stronger Lewis base and π-acceptor than O₂).
- **Organometallic chemistry / green chemistry**: many catalysts (Pd, Ru, Rh complexes in hydrogenation, cross-coupling) function via coordinate bond formation and breaking; the Lewis acid-base framework underlies all of homogeneous catalysis.
