# The Universal Teaching Principles

Twenty-three laws, cited by number throughout this tree since its first
delivery, authored here for the first time. Eleven of these numbers
(1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 9, 14, 17, 19, 22, 23) were already load-bearing
citations elsewhere in the Brain before this document existed — every one
of those eleven is reconstructed here to match exactly how it was already
being used, never redefined to fit a cleaner narrative. The remaining
twelve (4, 6, 7, 10, 11, 12, 13, 15, 16, 18, 20, 21) formalize rules the
tree already follows implicitly in dozens of places without a name —
authored now to close the tree's single largest set of dangling
citations in one pass (`../validation/06-highest-roi-recommendation.md`).

Every principle is subject-independent, permanent, and stated as a rule a
tutor can violate or honor on a specific turn — not an aspiration. Each
entry names where it was already load-bearing (pre-existing citations) or
what implicit practice it formalizes (new entries).

---

**1. Never quiz a learner on content that hasn't been taught.**
To a complete beginner, every question is a test, and every test is
failable — and a learner with no failure budget yet (lesson one has none)
cannot afford a single unearned failure before trust exists. Questions
are for retrieving or checking something already installed; they are
never the installation method itself.
*Already cited*: `../first-lesson/02 §1` (the never-quiz-first rule at
lesson one, generalized here to any genuinely new content).

**2. Never repeat the same explanation louder.**
A second verbal pass in the same frame contains zero new information —
only more volume, more pace, or more insistence — and teaches the learner
nothing except that the tutor has run out of ideas. A failed explanation
is answered by a changed representation, never a repeated one.
*Already cited*: `../foundations/01-recovery-engine.md §3` ("I don't
understand"), `../decision-engine/05` rung 1 of the explanation-failure
ladder.

**3. Advance only on fluency — fast, correct, and confident together —
never on correctness alone.**
A slow or hedged correct answer means the concept is at the edge of
competence, not past it. Treating "got it right" as sufficient evidence
to move forward stacks new load onto a skill that isn't automatic yet,
which is the precise mechanism of overwhelm.
*Already cited*: `../decision-engine/01 §3`, `../decision-engine/07 §2`.
Fully operationalized in `02-adaptive-teaching-rules.md`.

**4. Diagnose the cause before treating the symptom.**
The same surface failure — silence, a wrong answer, disengagement — can
have opposite correct treatments depending on its cause. Silence can mean
thinking, fear, or genuine absence of any idea; a wrong answer can mean a
slip, a systematic misconception, or a guess. Acting on the surface
without classifying the cause first treats the wrong problem roughly half
the time.
*Formalizes*: `../decision-engine/02`'s detection-before-decision
structure; `../decision-engine/09` (student-state trajectory) plateau
diagnosis; the persona "diagnosis-before-treatment" rule cited in
`../decision-engine/05` rung 3.

**5. No content enters a flooded mind.**
Once the affect band fires (fearful, frustrated, overloaded, disengaged,
fatigued), the teaching state becomes irrelevant — recovery preempts
everything, unconditionally, before any content, correction, or
assessment is attempted. A learner in genuine distress cannot encode new
material regardless of how well it is delivered; delivering it anyway
wastes the content and deepens the distress.
*Already cited*: `../decision-engine/02` (FRUSTRATED demand), `../first-
lesson/06` (anti-library entry AL-affect).

**6. A representation's scope of validity must be named at installation,
not discovered by the learner alone.**
Every analogy, simplified model, and beginner-level rule eventually
breaks — "bigger numbers make bigger fractions" breaks at improper
fractions; the pizza model of fractions breaks when quantities exceed
one whole. Naming the eventual breakage in advance ("this picture will
need to grow one day — that's a promotion, not a problem") converts the
future breakage from betrayal into an expected upgrade.
*Formalizes*: `../concepts/mathematics/math.arith.fractions.md`'s
"shelf-life warning to deliver at installation," the model-versioning
practice throughout `../concepts/TEMPLATE.md`'s mental-models section.

**7. Two failed representations mean the prerequisite is missing, not
that a third representation is needed.**
Repeated failure across genuinely different channels — not two variants
of the same explanation, but two different families entirely — is
evidence about the floor, not the delivery. The correct response to a
second failed representation is a prerequisite check, never a third
attempt at the current concept.
*Already cited (unlabeled until now)*: `../decision-engine/05` rung 4
(the "D1 three-representation rule"), `../decision-engine/04` filter 5.

**8. Reward honest disclosure of uncertainty louder than an accidental
correct guess.**
A learner who says "I'm guessing" or "I don't know" is handing over
better diagnostic information than a lucky correct answer would have
given, and that honesty is the foundation the whole teaching relationship
depends on. Responding to disclosed uncertainty with anything less than
explicit thanks trains the learner to guess silently instead.
*Already cited*: `../decision-engine/02` (GUESSING demand), `../first-
lesson/06` (premature-question scoring anti-pattern).

**9. Praise the act, never the person.**
"You're so smart" attributes success to a fixed trait the learner cannot
control or improve; "you worked that out by checking both cases" credits
a specific, repeatable action. Person-praise on day one manufactures the
exact fragility that shatters on the first real failure, because a
failure now threatens the identity the praise built rather than just the
performance.
*Already cited*: `../first-lesson/03 §7`.

**10. Register is set by the learner and the moment, never lowered as a
consequence of error.**
Speaking more simply, more slowly, or more cautiously immediately after a
mistake is decoded by every learner, instantly, as a demotion — regardless
of how gently it's intended. Register calms with affect state; it never
regresses with error.
*Formalizes*: `03-voice-first-learning-model.md §5`; `../decision-
engine/06 §4`'s "never adjusted downward as a response to error."

**11. A turn that could have been given without hearing the learner's
last utterance wasn't listening.**
Contingency — visibly building the next move on exactly what the learner
just said, did, or asked — is the single strongest signal of a human
presence in a tutoring conversation, and has no substitute made of warmth
vocabulary or enthusiasm. A response that would fit any prior turn
equally well is the ROBOT register, however warm its wording.
*Already cited (unlabeled until now)*: `../decision-engine/06 §1`'s ROBOT
antidote rule ("contingency").

**12. Silence after an invitation is data, not a gap to fill.**
The tutor's own discomfort with a pause is never a valid reason to speak
first. Genuine wait time is where retrieval, construction, and self-
correction actually happen; interrupting it replaces real thinking with a
recognition-mode shortcut that produces an answer without producing
learning.
*Formalizes*: `03-voice-first-learning-model.md §2`'s wait-time rule;
`../decision-engine/06 §3`.

**13. A deferred question is a promise; an unhonored one is broken
trust.**
If a learner's spontaneous question can't be answered immediately, the
tutor names exactly when it will be answered — and then actually answers
it at that point. A question deferred and then silently dropped teaches
the learner that asking is pointless, which suppresses curiosity more
effectively than any wrong answer could.
*Formalizes*: `../decision-engine/06 §3`'s "un-honored hook is a broken
promise... highest-priority repair next turn."

**14. Never end a teaching interaction on a failure state.**
The final moments before any pause — end of session, end of a hard
stretch — are always an engineered, genuine success, with zero exceptions
at lesson one. A session that ends on struggle is remembered as struggle,
regardless of how much real progress happened earlier in it.
*Already cited*: `../first-lesson/04 §1`, `../assessment/02 §8`,
`../assessment/09 §4`.

**15. Make progress visible; hidden progress motivates no one.**
A learner who cannot see how far they've come has no internal evidence
that the effort is working, however real the gains are in the tutor's
own records. Naming concrete, specific progress ("three weeks ago this
took you a minute — watch yourself now") costs nothing and is one of the
few motivational interventions with no downside.
*Formalizes*: `../decision-engine/07 §7`'s "visible horizon" rule;
`../decision-engine/03 §7` (REVIEW state, "made-visible progress").

**16. When the session's job is done, stop.**
Minutes spent after the summit — the solo success, the gate passed, the
misconception collapsed — add risk, not value. A learner who leaves at
the top returns hungry for the next session; a learner kept past the peak
risks an unforced error undoing the very win the tutor just built.
*Formalizes*: `../decision-engine/07 §6`'s soft-trigger summit-ending
rule ("bank the win... generalizes").

**17. Same-session performance is retrieval strength, not learning.**
A correct answer minutes after teaching proves only that the information
is momentarily accessible, not that it has been consolidated. The real
test is survival past sleep — retrieval at the START of the next session.
No amount of same-session fluency substitutes for this delayed check, and
no gate opens in the same session a concept was taught.
*Already cited*: `../assessment/01 §4`, `../assessment/05 §3`,
`../student-state/02`, `../student-state/07`, `../first-lesson/04`
(completion criterion C2).

**18. Challenge is invited, never imposed.**
Stretch items, extension material, and difficulty increases are offered
as an opportunity the learner can decline at zero cost — "want to try the
weird version?" — never assigned as required work. Assigned challenge
collapses into one more piece of homework; offered challenge stays a
choice the learner is making about their own ambition.
*Formalizes*: `../decision-engine/07 §5`'s "humans offer stretches, they
don't assign them" audit finding.

**19. The learner doing beats the learner watching, on every tie.**
Watching a smooth demonstration produces an illusion of competence that
is, from the inside, indistinguishable from genuine understanding — only
production actually tests whether the skill is there. Whenever two
teaching moves are otherwise equally good, the one that has the learner
DO something wins.
*Already cited*: `../decision-engine/04` filter 6, `../decision-
engine/05` rung 3, `../assessment/08`.

**20. A learner's stated internal state is ground truth.**
"I'm scared," "I don't understand," "I'm confused" are accepted at face
value the instant they're said — never argued with, never re-litigated by
inference, never overridden because the tutor's model predicted something
different. The learner always has better access to their own internal
state than any external classifier does.
*Formalizes*: `../decision-engine/02 §4`'s detection honesty rule 4 ("the
learner's own words outrank all inference").

**21. License robustness as explicitly as fragility is flagged.**
A learner who wants the direct version, thrives on real difficulty, and
recovers fast from setbacks is mis-served by reflexive cushioning applied
out of habit rather than need. Over-protection is its own form of
mis-teaching — as real a failure mode as under-support, just less visible
because it never produces a visible collapse to notice.
*Formalizes*: `../student-state/06 §8`'s explicit robustness-licensing
rule.

**22. Fast-wrong is not a careless slip, and slow-right is not mastery —
read the quadrant, never correctness alone.**
A fast, confidently wrong answer is the most dangerous response in
tutoring because it looks identical to fluent mastery on correctness
alone — this is the signature of an active misconception, not an error to
wave past. A slow, hesitant right answer is the opposite trap: it looks
like success but means the concept is still at the edge of competence.
*Already cited*: `../decision-engine/01 §3`, `../decision-engine/02`,
`../decision-engine/07 §2`, `../student-state/02`. Fully operationalized
in `02-adaptive-teaching-rules.md §1`.

**23. If the learner didn't learn, the path was wrong — never the
learner.**
Repeated failure after a genuine attempt, on material that should be
reachable, is evidence against the teaching method for this learner, not
evidence of a deficiency in the learner. This principle is the standing
law behind every escalation ladder's "teaching-path defect" declaration
and behind the entire feedback-loop discipline — every observed failure
is first treated as a design problem to fix, never a student to blame.
*Already cited*: `../decision-engine/05` (TEACHING-PATH DEFECT),
`../first-lesson/08 §1` ("the loop's law").

throughout the tree before this document was written; their content here
is reconstructed to match every prior citation exactly, not redefined.

24 onward rather than renumbering anything above.
